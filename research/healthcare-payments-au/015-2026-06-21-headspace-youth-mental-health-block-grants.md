# Headspace and the PHN Block Grant Model: How Australia Funds Youth Mental Health Outside the MBS

**Report 015 — 2026-06-21**

**Summary:** headspace, Australia's National Youth Mental Health Foundation, delivers free mental health, physical health, alcohol/drug, and vocational support to 12–25 year olds through a network of approximately 164 centres nationally, funded via block grants flowing Commonwealth → Primary Health Networks → local lead agencies — a structural model sitting entirely outside MBS fee-for-service. Its strengths (free access, holistic care, youth focus) coexist with persistent weaknesses: PHN discretion producing unequal access across regions, rural coverage gaps, difficulty measuring cost-effectiveness, and an unresolved overlap with Better Access for the same age cohort.

## Background

### Origins and Purpose

headspace — formally the **National Youth Mental Health Foundation (NYMHF)** — was established in 2006 under the Howard Government as Australia's first dedicated national mental health and wellbeing service for young people aged **12 to 25**. It was conceived in response to evidence that mental disorders typically onset in adolescence and early adulthood, that young people were poorly served by adult mental health services and general practice, and that early intervention significantly changed long-term trajectories.

The design brief was distinctive: rather than bolting youth mental health onto the MBS fee-for-service system (which would charge gap fees, require GP gatekeeping, and provide single-clinician care), headspace would operate as a **"one-stop shop"** integrating:
- Mental health assessment and treatment
- Physical health care (including sexual health and GPs)
- Alcohol and other drug support
- Vocational and employment support

**At point of care, services are free.** Young people walk in, call, or connect online and are not billed. This is the single most important distinction from the Better Access scheme (MBS Items 80010, 80110 etc.) documented in Report 011, where gap fees averaged ~$92 per psychology session in 2023.

### The Governance Architecture: Three Layers

The headspace system operates across three distinct organisational layers that can cause confusion in policy discussion:

**Layer 1: headspace National (NYMHF)**
The national peak body, funded directly by the Commonwealth. Responsible for brand standards and licensing, clinical governance frameworks, training and development, data collection, research, and public communications. Essentially the "franchise franchisor" for the network.

**Layer 2: Primary Health Networks (PHNs)**
Since the commissioning transition in approximately 2015–17, PHNs have served as the Commonwealth's regional procurement agencies for headspace. The Department of Health and Aged Care provides **block grants** to PHNs (alongside other mental health service grants), and PHNs then sub-commission headspace centre operation from local providers. PHNs have discretion within their regional grants, which introduces variability in how much funding reaches headspace as opposed to other PHN-commissioned mental health services.

**Layer 3: Lead Agencies**
Local NGOs, community health centres, or other organisations that win PHN sub-contracts to operate headspace centres under the brand and quality framework. They employ the clinical and support staff. Their mix of services, staffing levels, and clinical governance can vary across the network.

This three-layer model is fundamentally different from MBS fee-for-service, where individual practitioners bill Medicare for individual consultations. There is no per-visit billing, no Medicare data trail per episode, and no direct incentive structure tying payment to volume of consultations.

## Key Findings

### 1. Scale of the Network

By 2023–24, headspace operated approximately **164 physical centres** across Australia, with an additional network of outreach and satellite services attached to main centres in some regions *[needs verification: confirm exact count from AIHW or headspace National]*. The network grew steadily from an initial cohort of 10 pilot sites in 2006 to 90 by 2013 and subsequently expanded through multiple budget commitments.

Beyond physical centres, **eheadspace** (launched approximately 2014) provides free online chat and phone counselling, typically available from 9am to 1am AEST, 7 days a week. eheadspace provides lower-intensity but nationally accessible support and is particularly important for young people in rural and remote areas without nearby physical centres.

**headspace School Support** is a separate rapid-deployment program activated when a school or community experiences a suicide or self-harm-related critical incident, deploying clinicians for immediate postvention support. This service is available nationally.

### 2. Clients Served

AIHW publishes annual data on headspace services. In recent years, headspace centres were estimated to serve approximately **100,000–120,000 young Australians per year** through face-to-face and digital centre contacts *[needs verification: check AIHW Mental Health Services in Australia, headspace chapter, for latest year data]*. eheadspace provides additional digital contacts.

The profile of those attending: presenting issues are predominantly **depression and anxiety** (consistent with the broader mild-to-moderate mental illness population seeking early intervention). A meaningful proportion present with at least moderate-severity symptoms. Data suggest the majority are first-time help-seekers, consistent with headspace's early intervention mandate.

### 3. Funding Quantum and Cost per Client

The total annual Commonwealth investment in headspace is not transparently broken out in budget papers because funding flows through PHN block grants (which cover all PHN-commissioned services) plus a separate headspace National grant. Published estimates have suggested the total system cost at approximately **$300–$500 million per year** when PHN-attributable funding, headspace National core funding, and program-specific grants are aggregated *[needs verification: this is an estimate; the true figure requires tracking PHN contracts and headspace National grants across all 31 PHNs]*. If approximately 120,000 young people are served annually, this implies a cost of roughly **$2,500–$4,000 per client per year** — substantially higher than the Medicare-only cost of ten Better Access psychology sessions (~$1,000 in government rebates), though the services are not directly comparable given headspace's multidisciplinary, holistic model.

Cost-comparison debates have been a persistent feature of headspace policy discussions. The overhead structure — national body, PHN administration layer, lead agency admin, centre facilities — adds costs not present in fee-for-service models. Critics have argued this overhead reduces value for money. Defenders argue that the holistic model, free access, and youth-appropriate environment produce outcomes not achievable through an MBS fee-for-service pathway alone.

### 4. The PHN Block Grant Model: Structural Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- **Free access**: No gap fee means socioeconomic disadvantage does not itself become a barrier to attendance. Given that gap fees deter lower-income Australians from Better Access (Report 011), this matters.
- **Holistic co-location**: Mental, physical, vocational, and drug/alcohol support at a single site reduces the burden on young people of navigating multiple systems.
- **Youth-specific environment**: The physical and cultural environment of headspace centres is designed to be accessible and non-stigmatising for 12–25 year olds — typically co-designed with young people.
- **Flexible staff mix**: Not restricted to registered psychologists; can include mental health nurses, social workers, Aboriginal health workers, employment consultants — enabling broader models of care.
- **No session cap barrier**: Unlike Better Access (10 sessions/year hard limit), headspace does not impose an equivalent externally mandated cutoff at point of care, though clinical protocols govern case management.

**Weaknesses:**
- **PHN discretion creates geographic variation**: PHNs have flexibility in how they allocate mental health commissioning budgets. Some PHNs have invested heavily in headspace; others have distributed funds to other commissioned services. This means headspace funding intensity varies across regions with no direct relationship to need.
- **Outcome measurement gaps**: Because there is no per-episode billing, there is no data trail equivalent to MBS claims data. Measuring clinical outcomes at scale is difficult; headspace has worked with researchers on validated tools (e.g., Kessler-5 scores) but coverage and consistency has historically been variable.
- **Clinical governance variability**: The 2017 National Mental Health Commission review found significant variation in clinical supervision, staff credentialing, and quality assurance across the network — a consequence of 164 centres operated by different lead agencies under different PHNs.
- **Rural and remote coverage gaps**: Physical centres are predominantly in metropolitan and regional cities. Many MM3–7 areas have no headspace centre; eheadspace partially compensates but cannot replicate face-to-face, multidisciplinary assessment and treatment.
- **Severity ceiling**: headspace is designed for mild-to-moderate presentation. Young people with severe mental illness typically require referral to public Child and Adolescent Mental Health Services (CAMHS) or Adult Mental Health Services (AMHS). In areas with long CAMHS waiting lists, headspace clinicians face a dilemma: continuing to see a client who needs more intensive care, or referring to a system with a multi-year wait.

### 5. The headspace–Better Access Overlap for 12–25 Year Olds

Young people aged 12–25 are served by both headspace and Better Access. In practice, there is significant competition, duplication, and confusion between the two pathways:

| Feature | headspace | Better Access (12–25) |
|---|---|---|
| Cost to client | Free | Gap fees (~$92/session in 2023) |
| GP gatekeeping | Not required for initial contact | Requires Mental Health Treatment Plan |
| Session limit | Managed by centre, no hard cap | 10 sessions/year |
| Staff mix | Multidisciplinary (varies by centre) | Psychologists, GPs, social workers, OTs |
| Scope | Mental health + physical + vocational | Mental health treatment |
| Telehealth | eheadspace online/phone | MBS telehealth items |
| Data | PHN-reported, not per-episode | MBS claims database |
| Rural access | Centre-dependent; major gap | Wherever practitioners are |

The **Productivity Commission 2020 Report on Mental Health** and multiple subsequent reviews have called for better coordination between headspace and Better Access, noting that young Australians may not know which pathway to use, GPs may refer to different pathways based on local knowledge rather than clinical need, and there is genuine overlap in the mildly-to-moderately unwell young adult population served.

There is an unresolved policy question about whether two parallel systems for the same population makes sense — or whether investment in one at the expense of the other (or a more integrated model) would produce better outcomes per dollar.

### 6. Special Populations: Indigenous Young People and Rural Access

The intersection of headspace with Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander youth mental health is significant. In communities served by ACCHOs (Report 013), youth mental health may be delivered through the ACCHO's culturally safe model rather than through headspace. Where headspace centres are present near significant First Nations communities, cultural adaptation of services has been variable.

Rural headspace coverage is a recognised gap. The government has expanded some satellite services, and eheadspace provides a degree of equity, but a young person in MM5–7 who needs face-to-face multidisciplinary support has very limited options.

### 7. Budget Commitments 2023–2026

The Albanese Government has continued headspace funding as a core mental health platform. The **2023–24 Budget** included a significant mental health investment package as part of the National Mental Health and Suicide Prevention Plan, which included headspace continuation and expansion *[needs verification: confirm the specific headspace allocation in Budget 2023-24 health measures]*. The **2025–26 Budget** is understood to have maintained headspace funding continuity, consistent with the Government's stated commitment to maintaining the 164-centre network *[needs verification]*.

The **headspace Early Psychosis** program, targeting young people with first-episode psychosis — a higher severity cohort — has also been funded within the headspace umbrella, operating from a subset of centres with specialist capacity.

## Tensions and Open Questions

**1. Is block-grant funding the right model for youth mental health at scale?** The block grant model was chosen precisely to avoid the gap-fee and per-episode structure of Better Access. But it sacrifices transparency, creates administrative overhead, and makes outcome measurement difficult. As outcomes-based commissioning becomes more standard in government contracting, the headspace model faces pressure to demonstrate cost-effectiveness it was never designed to easily quantify.

**2. PHN discretion as a funding accountability gap.** There is no public mapping of exactly how much Commonwealth mental health commissioning funding flows to headspace centre operation across all 31 PHNs. This means parliament and the public cannot readily assess whether headspace is adequately funded in different regions, whether PHNs are underinvesting in headspace relative to other services, or what the true per-centre and per-client cost is.

**3. The missing middle for young people.** Headspace is calibrated for mild-to-moderate illness. Better Access, while covering more severe presentations, has a 10-session cap that is insufficient for moderate-to-severe conditions. CAMHS serves the most acute; AMHS transitions from 18. Young people aged 16–25 with moderate-to-severe illness may fall between all three systems — not sick enough for inpatient/AMHS, too complex for headspace, past their Better Access cap. This was the "missing middle" identified in the Productivity Commission 2020 Report.

**4. The rural equity deficit is structural.** 164 physical centres in a country with 7.7 million people in regional and remote areas means large portions of the youth population cannot access in-person headspace services. eheadspace is not a substitute for the full service model for young people with complex needs.

**5. Clinical staff shortages at headspace centres.** The psychology workforce shortage documented in Report 011 (57.3% FTE undersupply in 2025) flows into headspace. If centres cannot recruit or retain qualified mental health clinicians, their clinical quality and capacity decline. Headspace's broader workforce mix (mental health nurses, social workers) provides some buffer but does not eliminate the problem.

**6. The cost-effectiveness case remains unproven.** The international evidence for early-intervention youth mental health services is broadly positive, but the specific cost-effectiveness of the headspace model relative to MBS alternatives or other international models has not been definitively demonstrated in the Australian policy context. This is partly a measurement problem: if you don't count per-episode data, you can't calculate cost per episode.
