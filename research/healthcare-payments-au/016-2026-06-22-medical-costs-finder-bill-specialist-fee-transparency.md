# The Medical Costs Finder Bill 2026: Mandatory Specialist Fee Disclosure and Australia's Unregulated Gap Fee Crisis

**Report 016 — 2026-06-22**

**Summary:** Australia's specialist fees are entirely unregulated, producing an out-of-pocket gap crisis in which the average specialist attendance gap has grown 136% since 2010 and some psychiatrists and surgeons charge $500–$1,000 upfront for a first appointment. The Morrison government's $24.2 million Medical Costs Finder website — which sought to fix this through voluntary disclosure — attracted just 7 doctors in its first three years. The Albanese government's Health Legislation Amendment (Improving Choice and Transparency for Private Health Consumers) Bill 2026 mandates publication of Medicare, hospital, and insurer billing data on the Medical Costs Finder for every eligible specialist, without requiring their consent. The AMA supports the intent but has identified a fundamental flaw: there is no mechanism to correct inaccurate published data. The bill clears the transparency problem but leaves the underlying deregulated pricing structure intact.

**Why this follows from yesterday:** Report 015 documented headspace's block-grant funding model and noted that Better Access psychology gap fees (~$92/session in 2023) are a major access barrier for young people. The specialist gap fee crisis is the same structural problem operating at greater scale — and the Medical Costs Finder Bill 2026 is the first legislative attempt to address it for non-GP specialists, making it the connective thread across almost every prior report in this series.

## Background

### The Architecture of Specialist Fees in Australia

Australia's Medicare Benefits Schedule (MBS) sets a "schedule fee" for every item — the government's benchmark for what a service is worth. For non-GP specialists (surgeons, physicians, psychiatrists, obstetricians, anaesthetists, and others), Medicare typically pays **75% of the schedule fee** for in-hospital services, and **85% for out-of-hospital** attendances, as documented in Report 001.

Critically, **specialist fees are entirely unregulated.** There is no legal upper limit on what a specialist can charge. The government's rebate is fixed; the specialist's fee is not. The gap between the two is the patient's out-of-pocket cost — their "gap fee" or "excess."

Private health insurers offer "no-gap" and "known-gap" arrangements with doctors: in a no-gap arrangement, the doctor agrees to charge only what the insurer will pay, so the patient owes nothing. These arrangements substantially reduce OOP exposure for patients with private hospital cover. But participation has been declining: in 2024–25, **87.1% of PHI hospital services were delivered at no-gap rates**, down 0.6 percentage points from the prior year and **down 2.8 percentage points over five years** — a slow but meaningful erosion (APRA, 2025). The remaining ~12.9% of PHI services involve either "known gap" charges (~10% of services, where the insurer pays a higher benefit but a capped gap remains) or full out-of-pocket exposure.

For **uninsured and out-of-hospital** attendances — including the vast majority of outpatient specialist consultations — no insurer gap arrangements apply. Patients receive only the Medicare rebate. The entire gap is paid out of pocket.

### The MBS Indexation Backstory

The gap fee crisis was significantly worsened by the 2013–2020 MBS indexation freeze documented in Report 002. For seven years, schedule fees did not rise with costs. Specialists who maintained practice viability by raising their own fees were simultaneously increasing the gap patients paid. The freeze saved the government an estimated $3.9 billion but transferred substantial cost to patients. Since indexation resumed, it has used the Wage Cost Index — still systematically lagging real cost pressures in high-skill professional services.

## Key Findings

### 1. The Scale of the Gap Fee Crisis

The average out-of-pocket gap payment for specialist attendances rose from **$49.56 in 2010–11 to $117.18 in 2023–24** — a **136% increase** over a period when CPI rose approximately 40%. In real terms, this is approximately a threefold increase in the burden relative to general price levels (AIHW data; sources cited below).

Out-of-pocket spending broadly has been rising at **6.2% per year**, more than three times the inflation rate of approximately 2%. It now accounts for approximately **17% of Australia's total annual healthcare spending** — among the highest proportions in the OECD (Commonwealth Fund, 2026 Country Profile).

In **2024–25, 8.6% of Australians — over 800,000 people — delayed or missed specialist care because of cost** (Department of Health and Aged Care, citing Medicare data). In 2024, the Commonwealth Fund reported 8.8% of Australians delayed or skipped care due to cost, up from 7% in 2023.

The **median in-hospital specialist gap fee has risen 22% since 2022** (Private Healthcare Australia, 2026). APRA data shows average out-of-pocket fees for hospital episodes have risen **65% since 2020**.

Certain specialties impose especially high gaps. According to APRA and insurance industry data:
- **Orthopaedics**: average PHI gap of **$707.78** per service
- **Psychiatry/Surgery**: some practitioners charge **$500–$1,000 upfront** for a first appointment
- **Dermatology**: average fee for a standard Item 104 consultation approximately $280, with OOP around $199
- **Obstetrics and gynaecology**: approximately $255 average, ~$174 OOP
- **ENT (ear, nose, throat)**: the Grattan Institute's 2025 report found most ENT specialists charge 2–3 times the MBS schedule fee
- **Ophthalmology**: Grattan raised specific red flags about fee levels and supply constraints

A major equity problem compounds the arithmetic: one in two patients **did not know their fee before attending an appointment**, 38% received an **unexpected bill**, and nearly one in three reported being charged **illegal booking or administrative fees** — charges with no MBS basis (Private Healthcare Australia survey data).

### 2. The Original Medical Costs Finder: $24.2 Million for 7 Doctors

The Medical Costs Finder (MCF) website was first funded under the Morrison government in 2019 with an intention to use consumer fee transparency to discipline the market. The premise was that if patients could compare specialist fees online, competitive pressure would moderate excessive charging.

The MCF was built at a cost of approximately **$24.2 million** (Department of Health; widely reported). It covered **11 non-GP specialty groups** and approximately **6,300–11,000 eligible specialists** (figures vary by source and year; the discrepancy likely reflects variation in how "eligible" is defined across different counts). Specialist participation was **entirely voluntary**.

The result was near-total failure. By end of 2022, **just 6–7 doctors** across the entire eligible cohort had voluntarily published their fees on the site. After six years and $24.2 million, the total count reached approximately **88–101 voluntary disclosures** — a participation rate of well under 2% of eligible specialists.

The core design flaw was obvious in retrospect: specialists who charge high fees have a strong financial incentive not to disclose them, while specialists who charge low fees or bulk bill have no patients needing this information. Voluntary disclosure in an environment of asymmetric information incentives produces near-zero disclosure. The "market discipline through transparency" theory requires the market to first exist.

### 3. The 2026 Bill: Mandatory Publication via Existing Data

The **Health Legislation Amendment (Improving Choice and Transparency for Private Health Consumers) Bill 2026** was introduced in the House of Representatives on **12 February 2026** by Minister for Health Mark Butler. It passed the House and was referred to the Senate Standing Committee on Community Affairs, which recommended its passage in approximately April 2026.

The bill's core mechanism shifts from voluntary disclosure to **automatic publication of data the government already holds**. Schedule 1 of the bill authorises the Department of Health, Disability and Ageing to publish on the Medical Costs Finder:

- The **name, qualifications, and specialty** of individual medical practitioners
- **Languages spoken** (relevant to culturally and linguistically diverse communities)
- **Fees charged for specific MBS items**, drawn from Medicare billing data and hospital claims data
- **Geographic location(s)** where services are provided
- The practitioner's **bulk billing rate** (extent of bulk billing)
- The practitioner's participation in **gap cover arrangements** with private health insurers

The government has committed a further **$7 million** to analyse annual Medicare, hospital, and insurer data for every common medical service and display it on the upgraded MCF.

The same bill also addresses a separate but related issue: PHI **"product phoenixing"** (previously covered in Report 008). The legislation prohibits the practice of insurers closing a product and relaunching an identical one at a higher premium, and requires Ministerial approval for premium increases on new products or for changes that reduce the value of existing products.

### 4. The AMA's Support — With a Fundamental Objection

The Australian Medical Association submitted to the Senate Committee process that it **supports the intent** of the bill — greater transparency is a legitimate policy goal and the voluntary system's failure was obvious. However, the AMA has identified what it calls a **"fundamental flaw"**: the bill as drafted contains **no mechanism for practitioners to correct inaccurate or misleading information** once it is published.

Medicare claims data is generated through billing processes that may not accurately represent the complexity of individual cases, the mix of services bundled under a single item, or legitimate variation in practice patterns. The AMA is particularly concerned about the government's plan to publish a **single annual "average fee" per practitioner** — a metric the AMA argues does not reflect meaningful transparency because clinical practice varies substantially within a practitioner's caseload. A surgeon who performs routine and highly complex procedures under the same MBS item may appear to charge a very different "average" than colleagues, without that average conveying useful information to a prospective patient.

The AMA also objected that the Department had **not consulted the medical profession** on the methodology for calculating and displaying fees before the bill was introduced, despite the clear potential for unintended consequences from displaying inaccurate or misleading data.

The AMA's principal recommendation: add a **transparent review process** allowing practitioners to contest and correct published data before it goes live, and to seek correction after publication.

### 5. Other Stakeholder Positions

**Private Healthcare Australia (PHA)** — the peak body for private health insurers — strongly backs the bill. Insurers have long wanted mandatory specialist fee disclosure because information asymmetry also harms their ability to negotiate no-gap arrangements and manage hospital benefit costs.

**Catholic Health Australia** has welcomed the reform, noting it aligns with the principle that patients have the right to informed financial consent before receiving care.

**The Coalition** warned in April 2026 that the bill was "not ready" — echoing concerns about methodology, consultation, and the absence of a correction mechanism — and indicated it would seek amendments in the Senate rather than opposing the bill outright.

**The Australian Society of Anaesthetists** engaged in the consultation process, reflecting concern that anaesthetic fee transparency is particularly complex given anaesthetic time-billing models (fees for anaesthesia typically vary with procedure duration) and the involuntary nature of anaesthetic provider choice when a patient selects a surgeon.

### 6. The Grattan Institute's Harder Line: Transparency Isn't Enough

One week before the bill's introduction, the Grattan Institute released its major 2025 report — **"Special Treatment: Improving Australians' Access to Specialist Care"** (15 June 2025) — arguing that fee transparency is necessary but insufficient. The report found:

- Specialist fees have **risen 73% in real terms since 2010** (faster than the 136% nominal increase documented above, adjusted for inflation at a different base)
- **10% of patients** pay almost **$600 per year** in specialist fees
- Waitlists for public specialist clinics extend months beyond clinically recommended maximums in some parts of Australia

Grattan's five-point national plan goes significantly further than the 2026 bill:
1. Governments should invest at least **$500 million per year** to expand public specialist clinics in areas with least access, providing **one million extra specialist services per year**
2. The federal government should **remove Medicare funding** from specialists charging excessive fees — defined as more than three times the MBS rebate for a service
3. The names of excessive chargers should be **published publicly**
4. Governments should establish a system where **GPs can obtain written specialist advice** without a patient referral, reducing unnecessary specialist visits
5. Governments should invest **$60 million** to modernise public specialist clinics and spread best practices

The Grattan report represents the intellectual case for moving beyond transparency to **price intervention** — a step the 2026 bill explicitly does not take. The bill mandates disclosure; it does not cap fees. Specialists can continue to charge whatever they like. The theory is that informed consumers, empowered with published fee data, will make different choices, putting competitive pressure on high-charging practitioners.

### 7. Why Transparency May Be Insufficient: Market Failures in Specialist Care

Healthcare economics literature identifies several structural features of specialist markets that limit the effectiveness of price transparency interventions:

**Provider choice is often constrained.** When a patient's GP refers them to a specialist, they typically accept the referral without shopping. Chronic conditions, geographic proximity, urgency, and personal trust in the GP's judgement all limit the ability to comparison-shop even with fee information available.

**Information remains asymmetric even with fee disclosure.** Knowing a specialist charges $400 for Item 104 does not tell the patient whether that specialist provides better outcomes, shorter waiting times, or more thorough consultations than the $200 specialist two suburbs away. Price comparison tools assume quality is observable and substitutable — rarely true in specialist medicine.

**Emergency and inpatient contexts eliminate choice entirely.** A patient admitted to hospital through an emergency cannot choose their treating specialist or anaesthetist. The anaesthetist's gap fee — potentially hundreds of dollars — is simply incurred.

**Geographic markets are thin.** In regional and remote Australia (documented in Reports 012–014), there may be one specialist of a given type within several hundred kilometres. Publishing that specialist's fee does not create competition if there is no competitor.

These features do not negate the value of transparency — informed consent and the capacity to budget for expected costs are legitimate goods in themselves. But they are the reason economists and health policy researchers like the Grattan Institute argue transparency must be accompanied by supply-side expansion, public specialist access, and potentially price constraints.

## Tensions and Open Questions

**1. Can a database of average fees drive meaningful behaviour change?** The MCF bill assumes that patients who can see fees online will make different choices. The international evidence on price transparency is mixed: in the US, multiple "price transparency" mandates for hospitals have had limited impact on prices or utilisation because patients rarely initiate comparison shopping for specialist care. Australia's reform will need robust evaluation.

**2. The correction mechanism gap is a real risk.** The AMA's objection that there is no data correction pathway is substantive. If a practitioner's Medicare data is miscoded — a known problem across the billing system — their published "average fee" could be incorrect. Publishing incorrect data while providing no pathway to correct it would undermine both the practitioner's reputation and the consumer's information value.

**3. Transparency vs. regulation: which tool is appropriate?** The Grattan Institute, Private Healthcare Australia, and various patient advocacy groups have noted that other comparable healthcare systems (France, Canada, Germany) directly regulate specialist fees within a schedule. Australia is an outlier in leaving specialist fees entirely to the market, then attempting to discipline the market through disclosure. The 2026 bill does not address this structural deregulation.

**4. PHI interactions: no-gap arrangements under pressure.** If the MCF publishes data showing what specialists charge outside no-gap arrangements, does this strengthen or weaken PHI's negotiating position on no-gap coverage? Some analysts suggest fee transparency could help insurers identify which specialists are charging excessive amounts and target their no-gap incentives accordingly — potentially a constructive mechanism. Others worry it could accelerate the decline in no-gap participation if specialists feel commercially exposed.

**5. EMSN interactions.** Australia's Extended Medicare Safety Net (EMSN) caps the patient's OOP contribution once a yearly threshold is reached — effectively backstopping the worst-case OOP exposure. But the EMSN also has a known perverse incentive: it reduces the cost of high-fee specialists to high-utilisation patients, insulating those specialists from fee pressure. The bill does not address EMSN design.

**6. Informed financial consent law already exists.** The Health Insurance Act already requires specialists to provide informed financial consent — patients must be told their likely out-of-pocket costs before treatment. Survey data showing 1 in 2 patients didn't know their fee before an appointment and 38% received unexpected bills suggests this legal obligation is poorly enforced. The MCF bill addresses information availability, not enforcement.
