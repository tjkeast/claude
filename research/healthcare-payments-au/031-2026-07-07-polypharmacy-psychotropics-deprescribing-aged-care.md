# PBS Prescribing in Aged Care: Polypharmacy, Psychotropics, and the Deprescribing Imperative

**Report 031 — 2026-07-07**

**Summary:** Australia's 240,000 residential aged care residents are among the heaviest per-capita PBS users in the country — averaging 9 to 11 regular medicines per person, with up to 91% experiencing polypharmacy. More than 40% are on at least one clinically inappropriate medicine. Psychotropic drugs — antipsychotics, benzodiazepines, and antidepressants — are pervasive, often prescribed off-label as a substitute for adequate dementia care, contributing to falls, hospitalisations, and preventable harm. A $333.7 million on-site pharmacist program launched in July 2024 has achieved only 12% facility uptake. Evidence shows deprescribing is both safe and cost-saving, yet structural, professional, and cultural barriers persistently prevent it from being the norm.

**Why this follows from yesterday:** Report 030 found that aged care residents face compounding out-of-pocket costs across three siloed safety nets, and noted that polypharmacy — near-universal in residential care — generates substantial PBS expenditure with no integrated ceiling. Today's report examines what is being prescribed, what harm it causes, and what the evidence says about fixing it.

---

## Background

When a person enters residential aged care in Australia, they carry with them, on average, a decade or more of prescriptions accumulated across multiple treating doctors — a GP, perhaps a cardiologist, an endocrinologist, a rheumatologist — each managing one condition without full visibility of the whole. The transition into aged care rarely triggers a comprehensive medicines review. The result is a population that is systematically over-medicated, frequently on drugs that are no longer appropriate for their age, frailty, or goals of care, and at high risk of drug-drug interactions, falls, delirium, and avoidable hospitalisation.

This is not a peripheral issue. With approximately 240,000 Australians in residential aged care at any point, and a sector whose population will grow sharply as the baby boomer cohort enters its eighties, the PBS cost, the clinical harm, and the quality-of-life consequences of unchecked polypharmacy are substantial and growing.

The Royal Commission into Aged Care Quality and Safety (2021) identified inappropriate medication use — particularly the overuse of antipsychotics as a form of chemical restraint — as a systemic quality failure. Five years on, the structural response has been slow, uptake of new programs is low, and the evidence-practice gap in aged care prescribing remains wide.

## Key Findings

### 1. The Scale of Polypharmacy in Residential Aged Care

Polypharmacy — conventionally defined as five or more regular medicines — is near-universal in Australian residential aged care facilities (RACFs). Studies estimate that between 65% and 91% of RACF residents meet this definition, with the MEDTRAC longitudinal cohort study finding 78% prescribed nine or more medicines daily (the threshold used in the National Aged Care Mandatory Quality Indicator Program).

**Average medicine load:** RACF residents take between 9 and 11 regular medications per day on average, consistently above levels in the general older population. More than two-thirds of Australians aged 75 and over are taking five or more medicines simultaneously, but in RACFs the problem is structurally amplified: residents have higher multimorbidity, see fewer specialist prescribers face-to-face, and receive less frequent medication review than community-dwelling peers.

**The Quality Indicator trend:** Since July 2021, all government-subsidised residential aged care providers have been required to collect and report quarterly data on polypharmacy (9+ medicines) and antipsychotic use. The Australian Institute of Health and Welfare (AIHW) publishes the results quarterly via the GEN Aged Care Data portal. The most recent reports (up to December 2025) show a statistically significant *decrease* in both polypharmacy and antipsychotic use over time — a positive signal, but from a very high base and declining slowly.

**The measurement problem:** Research published in 2024 in *BMC Geriatrics* found material discrepancies between *prescribed* and *administered* polypharmacy rates in RACFs, meaning that even where polypharmacy is declining on medication charts, actual administration patterns may differ. This undermines the validity of the quality indicator as a true measure of resident medication burden.

### 2. Psychotropic Medicines: Prevalence, Inappropriate Use, and Harm

Psychotropic medicines — antidepressants, antipsychotics, anxiolytics, and hypnotics (benzodiazepines and Z-drugs) — are among the most commonly prescribed and most frequently problematic drug classes in aged care.

**Admission-point prevalence (key study, 2024):** A retrospective cohort study of 23 RACFs across Australia found that within three months of admission:
- **>21%** of residents were dispensed antipsychotics
- **~30%** were dispensed benzodiazepines
- **~37%** were dispensed antidepressants
- **40%** were using at least one *potentially inappropriate* psychotropic medicine, using validated clinical criteria
- **10%** were using two or more potentially inappropriate psychotropic medicines simultaneously

The most prevalent categories of inappropriate psychotropic use were benzodiazepines and Z-drugs (27.4% of residents), followed by first- and second-generation antipsychotics (17.2%).

**Dementia and antipsychotics:** Antipsychotics are particularly problematic in the context of dementia, where they are often prescribed for behavioural and psychological symptoms (BPSD — agitation, aggression, wandering) despite evidence of increased stroke, cognitive decline, and mortality risk. The AIHW's *Dementia in Australia* report found that in 2023–24, 20% of Australians dispensed dementia-specific medicines also received antipsychotics in the same period — a rate that rises sharply inside residential care.

**The fall risk:** A 2025 Australian retrospective longitudinal cohort study of 23 RACFs (published in *BMC Geriatrics*) found a direct association between potentially inappropriate psychotropic medicines and elevated fall rates — itself a leading cause of hospital admission for RACF residents. Falls cost the healthcare system at least AUD $5,700 per hospitalisation, and older RACF residents are at high risk of fall-related injury during and after admission.

**Antipsychotic trends — some improvement:** National data shows an 11% decrease in antipsychotic dispensing to Australians aged 65 and over between 2016–17 and 2020–21 (Australian Commission on Safety and Quality in Health Care Atlas series). The reform-era data is more striking: a JAMDA 2025 study of 428 RACFs found a 34.6% relative annual reduction in antipsychotic use among residents with dementia between 2018 and 2022 — attributed in part to the Royal Commission's spotlight on chemical restraint, the introduction of mandatory quality indicators, and the Psychotropic Medicines Guidelines launched in 2020. Progress is real but still incomplete.

**Chemical restraint:** The Aged Care Quality and Safety Commission (ACQSC), the Australian Commission on Safety and Quality in Health Care (ACSQHC), and the NDIS Quality and Safeguards Commission issued a joint statement in recent years confirming concern that psychotropic medicines are being prescribed "inappropriately as a form of restrictive practice to control behaviour, off-label and for extended periods without review in older people and people with disability." Under the Aged Care Act 2024, the use of chemical restraint without a proper behavioural support plan and consent process is a regulated and reportable matter.

### 3. The National Harms and Cost Burden

**Medication-related hospital admissions:** Across Australia, 250,000 hospital admissions per year are estimated to be medication-related — approximately 2–3% of all hospital admissions — at an annual system cost of AUD $1.4 billion. Among patients aged 65 and over with medical or surgical admissions, 55% were found to be on at least one potentially inappropriate medicine, and 6% of all admissions were directly attributable to a potentially inappropriate medicine (RACGP Silver Book 5th edition).

For RACF residents, the harm is concentrated and compounding: hospitalisation exposes already-frail residents to heightened risks of delirium, functional decline, iatrogenesis, and malnutrition — each of which feeds back into increased medication need. The cost per hospitalisation for RACF residents is estimated at AUD $5,700 or more at current prices.

**The PBS cost dimension:** RACF residents are predominantly concessional cardholders paying $7.70 per script, and most exhaust the concessional PBS Safety Net threshold (36 scripts, totalling $277.20) by mid-year, after which PBS medicines are free. For the government, however, the PBS subsidy behind each script continues. A resident on 10 regular PBS medicines — a moderate figure for aged care — generates approximately 120 scripts per year at an average government subsidy cost that reflects the real price of those drugs. Australia's PBS total expenditure of $19.1 billion (2024–25) reflects, in part, the concentrated prescribing load in this population. Reducing inappropriate prescribing is simultaneously a safety, quality-of-life, and fiscal issue.

### 4. The Deprescribing Evidence Base

Deprescribing — the deliberate, supervised reduction or cessation of medicines that are no longer needed, appropriate, or safe — has a growing Australian evidence base.

**The Opti-Med trial:** The gold-standard Australian deprescribing trial — a three-arm randomised controlled trial of 303 RACF residents (2014–2019, WA and NSW) — found:
- In the blinded deprescribing group, participants had a mean reduction of **2.7 medicines** over 12 months (95% CI: 3.5–1.9)
- **Cost savings of $328.90 per participant** in the blinded group (vs $164 in open group), compared to a per-participant program cost of $239.13 — a net saving
- **No reduction in quality of life** in either deprescribing group relative to control
- Extrapolated nationally, the potential net annual system saving was estimated at $1–16 million per annum — significant, though this estimate predates the full RACF population size of today

**Pharmacist recommendation uptake:** A 2022 Australian study in *Research in Social and Administrative Pharmacy* found that 77% of deprescribing recommendations made by pharmacists following medication reviews were accepted by GPs, and 74% of accepted recommendations were successfully implemented at 12 months. The limiting factor was not patient or clinician resistance — it was the low rate at which medication reviews were being initiated in the first place.

**The 2025 scoping review:** A comprehensive scoping review published in *Australasian Journal on Ageing* in February 2025, covering 13 studies and 133,150 RACF residents, found that:
- Successful deprescribing programs were multidisciplinary (pharmacist + GP + nurse collaboration), had educational components, involved long-term follow-up, and engaged residents and families
- The most commonly targeted medicines were psychotropics (consistent with the clinical risk burden above)
- Controlled trial evidence was limited to six studies; more robust evidence is needed on mortality, hospitalisation, and falls outcomes

**Clinical decision tools:** Australia now has its own 2024 PIMs (Potentially Inappropriate Medicines) list developed specifically for the Australian context. International tools including STOPP/START criteria (Screening Tool of Older Person's Prescriptions / Screening Tool to Alert to Right Treatment), STOPPFrail (for frail patients), and STOPPCog (for cognitive impairment) are available but inconsistently used in practice.

### 5. The Structural Response — and Its Shortcomings

**Residential Medication Management Reviews (RMMR):** The RMMR program has been the primary Commonwealth mechanism for systematic medication review in RACFs since 1997. Between July 2020 and December 2024, approximately 520,000 RMMRs were completed — roughly 116,000 per year — funded through the Pharmacy Programs Administrator. Funding is also available for up to two follow-up reviews within nine months of an initial RMMR (introduced following Royal Commission recommendations).

Limitations: Not all RACF residents receive an annual RMMR. Less than half of pharmacists' recommendations in some studies are actioned by GPs. Residents and families have been largely excluded from the review process, undermining informed consent and shared decision-making.

**The Aged Care On-Site Pharmacist (ACOP) Program:** The most significant structural intervention to date is the ACOP program, funded at **AUD $333.7 million** over four years from 1 July 2024. The program provides funding for one full-time pharmacist per 250 beds (in 50-bed increments), with salary funded at a Grade 2 hospital pharmacist award rate, claimable through the Pharmacy Programs Administrator.

The intent is to shift from periodic external RMMR visits to embedded clinical pharmacy practice — with on-site pharmacists reviewing medications proactively, collaborating with GPs and nursing staff daily, supporting residents and families, and contributing to quality systems. RACFs enrolled in ACOP cannot also access RMMR or Quality Use of Medicines (QUM) programs.

**Uptake: 12% at one year.** A year into the program, fewer than 300 aged care homes had enrolled — approximately 12% of all government-subsidised RACFs. Participation was similar across major city (MM1) and large rural town (MM3) locations at ~15%, but consistently low across facility size categories. Since May 2025, Primary Health Networks have been tasked with actively supporting homes to recruit pharmacists — a recognition that the market alone would not drive uptake.

**Barriers to uptake identified by qualitative research (published 2025):**
- Pharmacist workforce shortage: not enough pharmacists with aged care experience or interest in the role
- Logistical difficulty for small and rural facilities, which may not reach the 50-bed threshold for half-day funding
- Cultural change requirements: GPs not accustomed to clinical pharmacist collaboration; nursing staff workflows not adapted
- Complexity of the credentialing requirement (APC-accredited aged care program; from 1 July 2026, mandatory for all participants)
- Financial uncertainty for small homes about whether program funding covers total pharmacist costs

**The Psychotropic Medicines Clinical Care Standard (2024):** The ACSQHC released the *Psychotropic Medicines in Cognitive Disability or Impairment Clinical Care Standard* on 9 May 2024 — a national framework containing eight quality statements and seven indicators for safe and appropriate psychotropic use. The Standard covers antidepressants, antipsychotics, and anxiolytics/hypnotics. It was developed jointly with the ACQSC and the NDIS Quality and Safeguards Commission, reflecting the shared concern across both aged care and disability settings.

The Standard promotes: informed consent before prescribing; mandatory non-pharmacological behavioural interventions as first-line for BPSD; regular review and planned de-escalation; shared decision-making with consumers and families; and documentation of rationale and duration. Implementation is not yet uniformly embedded in RACF practice; the ACQSC self-assessment tool supports, but does not mandate, facility-level review.

**Mandatory Quality Indicator reporting:** Since July 2021, all government-subsidised RACFs must report quarterly on polypharmacy (9+ medicines) and antipsychotic use. The statistically significant decreasing trend in both indicators since mandatory reporting began suggests that measurement itself is driving some improvement — a standard finding in healthcare quality improvement literature. The question is whether the rate of decline is fast enough to reach a safe and clinically appropriate baseline.

## Tensions and Open Questions

**1. Polypharmacy reduction vs. underprescribing risk.** Not all polypharmacy is inappropriate. Reducing medicine counts without clinical rationale can cause harm — untreated pain, undertreated depression, cardiovascular event risk. The risk of *underprescribing* in frail older people (omission of appropriate medicines) is less measured but real. The goal of deprescribing is optimisation, not minimisation — a clinical nuance that facility-level reporting on medicine counts does not capture.

**2. The GP gatekeeping problem.** Prescribing authority in Australian aged care rests with GPs. Pharmacists can recommend; GPs must act. The ACOP program fundamentally depends on GPs integrating pharmacist recommendations into their prescribing decisions — yet the structural incentives and communication infrastructure for this collaboration are underdeveloped. GP visits to aged care residents are funded through MBS Item GPACI (eight visits per year minimum), but there is no MBS mechanism specifically funding GP-pharmacist collaborative deprescribing meetings.

**3. 12% ACOP uptake is a program design failure signal.** A $333.7 million program achieving 12% uptake at one year is a significant policy implementation problem. The pharmacist workforce shortage is real — the broader psychology workforce data (Report 011) demonstrated how pipeline constraints become structural access limits. Aged care pharmacy is even more niche. Mandatory participation, rather than voluntary opt-in, may be needed to achieve the program's intent; the Royal Commission's final report recommended embedding pharmacists in aged care in terms that implied systematic coverage, not opt-in.

**4. Psychotropics as a proxy for inadequate dementia care.** The persistence of high psychotropic use in RACFs partly reflects the inadequacy of non-pharmacological BPSD management — dementia-specific activities, sensory stimulation, staff training in behavioural approaches, adequate staffing ratios. A medication review program that removes antipsychotics without improving the underlying care environment may lead to their reintroduction within months. The care minutes mandate (215 minutes/resident/day from 2024, 44 minutes RN) is relevant here: adequate staffing hours are a precondition for non-pharmacological interventions to be delivered. Compliance remains patchy (see Report 023).

**5. The PBS data gap.** Unlike the hospital system, where DRG-based funding creates administrative claims data on what medicines are administered in hospital, there is no systematic linkage between PBS dispensing data and residential aged care residence status. The true PBS expenditure attributable to RACF residents — and the potential fiscal saving from systematic deprescribing — has not been calculated. Services Australia holds the data in principle, but the linkage has not been performed for routine reporting purposes.

## Threads to Branch Into Next

1. **Aged Care Quality and Safety Commission (ACQSC) enforcement under the Aged Care Act 2024** — chemical restraint through psychotropics is directly a regulated matter under the Act; the ACQSC's new enforcement powers, audit and complaint-investigation processes, and reporting obligations are the legal infrastructure underpinning much of what this report described; flagged since Report 026 and now directly connected to medication management

2. **My Health Record and digital health infrastructure** — polypharmacy management depends critically on GP, pharmacist, hospital, and aged care staff all seeing the same medication list; My Health Record's role as a shared medicines record in the RACF context; upload rates; limitations on real-time prescribing visibility; the digital infrastructure for safe medicine management

3. **Private health insurance at end of life** — PHI has almost no role in residential aged care medicine coverage (the PBS prohibition); but PHI may cover hospital episodes where medication-related harms are treated; the interaction between PHI hospital benefits and medication-induced hospitalisation in aged care residents

4. **Health Workforce Australia reconstitution** — the absence of a national health workforce planning body leaves the pharmacist supply pipeline for aged care unmanaged; the ACOP program's 12% uptake is partly a workforce supply problem that HWA, if it existed, would be modelling and acting on

5. **Anticholinergic burden and the Drug Burden Index in aged care** — a more granular thread emerging from the research: the Drug Burden Index (DBI) quantifies exposure to anticholinergic and sedative medicines; a high DBI predicts falls, cognitive decline, and mortality independently of individual medicine appropriateness; Australian RACF studies find very high DBI scores; a targeted reduction in anticholinergic load may be a more clinically precise intervention than polypharmacy count reduction alone
