# Palliative Care and End-of-Life Funding for Dementia: Australia's Late-Stage Failure and the Growing Hospital Bed Block Crisis

**Report 026 · 2 July 2026**

**Summary:** Australia has a palliative care system structured almost entirely around late-stage, hospital-based intervention — the opposite of what dying Australians want and what the evidence supports. Palliative care hospitalisations have risen 46% since 2015-16, at more than double the rate of all hospitalisations. More than three in five Australians receive no specialist palliative care at all, and those who do first access it an average of just 15 days before death. For dementia — Australia's leading cause of death — this failure is particularly acute: the majority of people with dementia die in residential aged care without a formal palliative care plan, in settings underfunded for end-of-life care and overstretched by BPSD. The structural response — fragmented across three Commonwealth programs and eight state systems — is insufficient and about to become more fragile as the Comprehensive Palliative Care in Aged Care (CPCiAC) measure expires in June 2026 without confirmed continuation.

**Why this follows from yesterday:** Report 025 documented how dementia is Australia's leading cause of death and the dominant clinical condition in residential aged care, driving a hospital bed-block crisis as patients with severe BPSD cannot be placed. Palliative care is the clinical response to terminal illness in that setting — and its chronic underfunding is inseparable from the same system failure.

---

## Background

### Dementia and the end-of-life care imperative

Dementia is now Australia's leading cause of death (32,400 deaths in 2023, 9.4% of all deaths) and the second-largest cause of disease burden overall. As reported in Report 025, approximately 446,500 Australians live with dementia in 2026, with 54% of permanent residential aged care residents having a dementia diagnosis.

Dementia is terminal. It is a progressive neurodegenerative condition from which there is no recovery. The trajectory from diagnosis to death typically spans 8-12 years for Alzheimer's disease and may be shorter for vascular and Lewy body dementias. At the end of that trajectory, people with dementia are among the most complex palliative care patients: they cannot reliably articulate their own pain or distress, BPSD creates safety challenges in care environments, and the absence of a discrete "terminal phase" makes prognosis-based eligibility criteria (such as "life expectancy of three months or less") poorly suited to the dementia trajectory.

Yet palliative care in Australia has been designed primarily with cancer in mind — episodic, identifiable terminal phases, coherent specialist pathways, patient-articulated preferences. For the 54% of RACF residents with dementia, this design mismatch has real consequences.

### Who provides palliative care in Australia

Palliative care in Australia is delivered across four distinct domains, each funded differently:
1. **Hospital specialist palliative care services** — state/territory funded, via public hospital budgets
2. **Community and home-based palliative care services** — primarily state-funded PHN-commissioned block grants, with some Commonwealth-funded community nursing through Support at Home
3. **General practice primary palliative care** — Medicare MBS (though poorly adapted to ongoing palliative management)
4. **Residential aged care palliative care** — Commonwealth-funded via AN-ACC (the aged care funding classification system), with a dedicated Class 1 palliative care classification

The word "system" is misleading. These four domains operate largely without integration, with coordination between hospital, community, GP, and aged care palliative services dependent on individual relationship networks and local programme structures rather than structural design.

## Key Findings

### 1. Hospital palliative care: growing, expensive, and the wrong setting

In 2023-24, there were **107,500 palliative care-related hospitalisations** across Australia's public and private hospitals — a figure that has grown 46% since 2015-16, at an **annual rate of 4.9%**, more than double the 2.3% annual growth rate for all hospitalisations. For children, the growth rate is even sharper: approximately 10% per year.

The direct financial cost to public hospitals is substantial. In 2023-24:
- **313 of 772 public hospitals** reported admitted patient palliative care data to the Independent Health and Aged Care Pricing Authority (IHACPA)
- Total reported admitted patient palliative care cost: **$666.7 million** — representing 16% of all subacute care costs
- Total reported non-admitted (outpatient, community) palliative care cost: **$218.9 million**
- Combined hospital palliative care expenditure (reported hospitals only): approximately **$885 million**

Critically, these costs capture only reporting hospitals, and only cost directly coded to palliative care. People with dementia whose hospital admission is coded as a fall, delirium, aspiration pneumonia, or urinary tract infection — but who are functionally in the terminal phase of their dementia — are invisible in this data.

The average length of stay for palliative care hospitalisations is **nearly 11 days**, compared to 5.6 days for all hospitalisations. More than half (55%) of palliative care hospitalisations end with the patient dying in hospital.

For people with dementia in particular, this matters because hospitals are among the worst settings for end-of-life care: disorienting, noisy, prone to delirium, and structurally unable to provide the continuity, familiarity, and family-centred care that characterises high-quality dementia end-of-life management. Survey evidence consistently shows that most Australians would prefer to die at home or in a residential care setting they regard as home — yet hospital remains the most common place of death.

### 2. The specialist palliative care access gap

Australia's specialist palliative medicine physician workforce numbers just **358 nationally** — less than **one per 100,000 population**. Expert consensus places the benchmark at roughly double this level. The workforce has grown, but too slowly relative to need. Specialist palliative care nursing stands at approximately **3,900 nurses** nationally — better penetrated than medical specialists but still insufficient.

The consequence of workforce scarcity is stark access inequity:

- **More than three in five Australians** (>60%) receive **no specialist palliative care** at any stage of their life-limiting illness
- Among those who do receive specialist palliative care, the average time from first specialist contact to death is just **15 days**
- Early access to specialist palliative care — introduced at weeks to months before death rather than days — is associated with better symptom control, more preferred place of death, reduced emergency admissions, and lower acute care costs; yet the system delivers it at end-stage only

In 2024-25, approximately **15,900 people** received Medicare-subsidised palliative medicine attendances and case conferences from specialist palliative medicine physicians, comprising more than **78,000 services** nationwide. Total MBS benefits paid for these services: **$6.8 million** — up 16% from $5.9 million in 2023-24 (after five consecutive years of decline at an average annual rate of 7.0% in real terms).

These figures highlight a structural problem: MBS item design for specialist palliative medicine does not adequately remunerate the case conference, telephone liaison, and complex ongoing management activities that are central to high-quality palliative care. Palliative care delivered by GPs, community nurses, and allied health workers — who actually provide the bulk of palliative care in community and residential settings — generates limited dedicated MBS revenue. Palliative Care Australia's 2026 Budget Submission called for a new **practice payment** to better recognise primary palliative care delivered in community settings.

The PBS Palliative Care Schedule provides additional funding for palliative medications. In 2024-25, the Australian Government spent **$40.4 million** on palliative care-related medications via this schedule, at an average of **$83 per patient** — covering opioids, anti-emetics, anxiolytics, and other symptom management medicines used in end-of-life care.

### 3. Place of death: what people want vs what happens

National data on preferred vs actual place of death consistently reveals a deep mismatch. Most Australians indicate a preference to die at home or in a familiar residential setting. The actual pattern is:

- Among people **not receiving specialist palliative care**, residential aged care is the most common place of death (**43%**), followed by other community settings (31%), with inpatient care accounting for 20%
- Among people **receiving specialist palliative care**, the pattern is reversed: **67% die as admitted hospital patients** — the very population whose terminal illness is formally identified and theoretically enabling timely redirection to preferred care

This inversion suggests that specialist palliative care, in its current predominantly hospital-based delivery model, is not successfully redirecting people to community or aged care settings. Early and consistent contact from specialist palliative care — integrated with community and aged care systems — is associated with higher home-death rates internationally; Australia has not achieved this integration.

For dementia specifically, the residential aged care setting is the dominant place of death, which is appropriate in principle — but the quality of that dying is deeply variable. AIHW data shows that 46% of people in permanent residential aged care (whether or not they were in a formal palliative care classification) had a dementia diagnosis at the time of death.

### 4. Residential aged care palliative care: AN-ACC Class 1 and its limits

From 1 October 2022, the AN-ACC funding system includes a specific palliative care classification: **AN-ACC Class 1**. This class allows residents entering a facility with an anticipated life expectancy of less than three months, and an approved palliative care plan, to enter without the standard multi-day AN-ACC assessment process. The Class 1 daily funding rate is set at the national efficient price of **$295.64/day** (from 1 October 2025) — the same benchmark as other AN-ACC classes.

Key features and limitations of Class 1:

- **Eligibility**: requires a written palliative care plan and prognosis of <3 months — criteria that are difficult to apply to dementia, whose terminal phase is defined by functional decline over months or years rather than a clinically discrete final period
- **No palliative care premium**: Class 1 does not pay a higher daily rate than other classes despite the higher staffing and medication intensity required for active end-of-life care
- **Advance Care Plans (ACPs)**: AIHW data shows advance care plan documentation rates of ~60% in residential aged care overall, but formal Advance Care Directives (ACDs) are present in only 37.7% of RACF residents; quality is variable (only 73% of ACDs include all required elements)
- **Psychotropic medication over-reliance**: Without adequate palliative care resourcing, BPSD in dementia is frequently managed with psychotropic medications: more than 60% of Australian RACF residents are on regular psychotropic medications, with antipsychotics prescribed to >22% and benzodiazepines to >22%. Australia's Clinical Practice Guidelines for Appropriate Psychotropic Use (April 2023) emphasise non-pharmacological approaches; uptake in practice is constrained by workforce and time limits

### 5. The Comprehensive Palliative Care in Aged Care measure — expiring without a successor

The **Comprehensive Palliative Care in Aged Care (CPCiAC) measure** is the main Commonwealth-state partnership program for specialist palliative care in residential aged care. Launched in 2018-19 with $57.2 million in federal funding over six years, it is a matched-funding arrangement — the Commonwealth matches state and territory contributions — used to develop state-specific models and approaches.

The CPCiAC has supported:
- Integration of specialist palliative care teams into residential aged care settings
- Training and education for aged care workers in palliative care
- Models for coordinating hospital discharge to residential aged care for dying patients
- Expanded community palliative care services in some jurisdictions

The 2024-25 to 2025-26 extension of CPCiAC allocated a further **$24.9 million** (federal share) for two years. That agreement **expires on 30 June 2026** — tomorrow.

Palliative Care Australia's 2026 Budget Submission, released in December 2025, explicitly called for **securing continued Commonwealth funding for CPCiAC**, matched by states and territories. As of July 2026, no successor agreement has been publicly announced. Budget 2026-27 included no major new palliative care investment — a significant omission, given that the sector's primary Commonwealth-state coordination mechanism is lapsing.

### 6. The Support at Home End-of-Life Pathway: a step forward with critical design flaws

From **1 November 2025**, the Support at Home aged care program (which replaced Home Care Packages) includes an **End-of-Life Pathway**. This is a meaningful new entitlement:

- Eligible participants receive up to **$25,000** of in-home aged care services, available within 72 hours of approval
- Services include clinical care (nursing, allied health), personal care (bathing, dressing, continence), and practical support
- Access is available to new and existing Support at Home participants; no waiting list — an explicit policy departure from the 88,000+ people waiting for approved home care packages (March 2025 data)
- Fast-tracked approval: a doctor or nurse practitioner certifies the pathway form alongside the patient

However, Palliative Care Australia has identified two structural design flaws:

**Prognosis-based rather than needs-based access:** Eligibility requires an anticipated life expectancy of ≤3 months and an Australia-modified Karnofsky Performance Score (AKPS) of 40 or less. For dementia — a condition characterised by a slow, unpredictable decline without a discrete terminal phase — clinicians cannot reliably certify a three-month prognosis. Many people with dementia who clearly need end-of-life care intensity will not meet this threshold until they are in crisis, exactly reversing the early-intervention philosophy the pathway is meant to embody.

**The 16-week time limit:** Support for home-based end-of-life care is capped at 12-16 weeks. If a participant is still alive beyond this point, funding cannot be extended. For dementia patients whose "terminal phase" may span six to twelve months, this is a clinical mismatch. PCA's 2026 Budget Submission called for removing the 16-week limit and replacing prognosis-based eligibility with needs-based access. Neither change was made in Budget 2026-27.

## Tensions and Open Questions

**1. Dementia's definitional incompatibility with palliative care architecture.** The entire palliative care system — AN-ACC Class 1 eligibility, Support at Home End-of-Life Pathway, and MBS specialist funding — is built around identifiable prognosis of ≤3 months. Dementia doesn't work this way. The consequence is that people with advanced dementia receive inadequate palliative support for the bulk of their terminal trajectory and are only recognised as dying at the point of crisis.

**2. The CPCiAC funding cliff.** The only Commonwealth program specifically designed to integrate specialist palliative care into residential aged care expires on 30 June 2026 without announced continuation. This is a significant policy gap at exactly the moment when the Aged Care Act 2024 is imposing new rights-based obligations on providers for palliative and end-of-life care.

**3. Primary palliative care is underfunded at the GP and community nursing level.** GPs and community nurses deliver the overwhelming majority of palliative care contacts with people dying in the community and in residential aged care, yet MBS item design provides limited dedicated remuneration for the ongoing coordination, planning, and complex management work involved. The absence of a "practice payment" for primary palliative care — as PCA recommends — leaves a major structural gap.

**4. The hospital–community palliative care paradox.** The system currently invests most heavily in hospital specialist palliative care — the most expensive setting, the least preferred by patients — while community and residential care palliative supports are structurally underfunded. The $885 million hospital palliative care spend dwarfs the $6.8 million in specialist MBS services and the $40.4 million in PBS palliative medications. The ratio is not merely inefficient; it actively concentrates dying in institutional hospital settings against the wishes of most Australians.

**5. Advance care planning: coverage and quality gaps.** Formal ACDs cover only 37.7% of RACF residents. Quality is variable. Up to one-fifth of RACF residents with dementia may not have a formal dementia diagnosis recorded. Without a diagnosis, advance care planning for dementia-specific end-of-life scenarios is rarely initiated. The National Dementia Action Plan 2024-2034 flags data improvement as Action 8, but no dedicated funding was provided in Budget 2026-27.

**6. Palliative care as a hospital bed-block lever.** Palliative Care Australia has explicitly argued that the hospital bed-block crisis documented in Report 025 cannot be resolved by aged care supply alone — improved community and residential palliative care services that enable people with life-limiting illness to die without re-hospitalisation are equally necessary. The government's framing of the dementia hospital-to-aged-care problem focuses on placement transitions; the sector frames it as requiring palliative care integration at both ends.

## Threads to Branch Into Next

1. **Specialist medical training pipeline: geriatricians and palliative medicine physicians.** Australia needs to roughly double its specialist palliative medicine physician workforce. Medical college training number controls and the pipeline lag (12+ years from medical school to specialist certification) mean this requires decisions now. This thread has been flagged since Reports 016, 022, 024, and 025.

2. **Aged care quality and safety regulation under the Aged Care Act 2024 (ACQSC enforcement).** The new Act creates an enforceable right to palliative and end-of-life care for all aged care recipients. The ACQSC must now enforce strengthened palliative care standards in settings where workforce shortages, inadequate GP access, and psychotropic over-reliance are documented. How the regulator approaches this — with limited inspection capacity and 636 providers — is largely unexamined.

3. **Voluntary assisted dying (VAD) and the healthcare payment system.** All Australian states and the ACT have now enacted VAD laws (the NT is the most recent, having passed legislation in 2023, effective from 2024). VAD involves specific eligibility assessments, consultation requirements, and GP/specialist access pathways. The interaction of VAD with MBS, PBS (for the substance), aged care, and the palliative care system is an important and largely unreported dimension of end-of-life healthcare payments.

4. **Advance care planning as a healthcare payment mechanism.** Research consistently shows that completed advance care plans are associated with reduced hospital admissions, shorter hospital stays, and lower acute care costs in the final year of life. The case for investing in ACP as a healthcare cost-reduction mechanism — not just an ethical/quality-of-life measure — has a funding logic that connects directly to the MBS, hospital system, and aged care budgets.

5. **Aged care and the Medicare Safety Net interface for older Australians.** People with dementia in residential aged care face compounding out-of-pocket costs: MBS co-payments for specialist neurology and geriatric assessments, PBS co-payments for pharmacological management (memantine, donepezil, palliative medications), and aged care co-contributions. The financial toxicity of simultaneous multi-system cost-sharing for the dementia cohort is understudied and not captured in any single dataset.
