# The PHARMAC Model: New Zealand's Single-Buyer Medicine System and What Australia Can — and Cannot — Learn From It

**Report 007 — 2026-06-12**

**Summary:** New Zealand's PHARMAC is the world's most studied pharmaceutical purchasing agency — a fixed-budget, single-buyer monopsony that delivers some of the lowest medicine prices in the OECD. But the model's structural constraint is also its structural flaw: the fixed budget creates a queue, and the queue kills people. As of early 2026, Australia funds four CAR-T cell therapies and a AU$2.5 million gene therapy on its public system; New Zealand funds none of the CAR-T therapies and has not listed Zolgensma. This report examines the mechanics, trade-offs, and limits of the PHARMAC comparison — and what it actually means for Australia's trajectory.

**Why this follows from yesterday:** Report 006 examined how Australia's PBS — designed for mass-market medicines — is straining to absorb one-time gene therapies priced at AU$1–3 million per patient, improvising new institutional machinery (the HST Program) and waiting on HTA reform. A recurring question throughout the series has been: why not just copy PHARMAC? New Zealand operates a fixed-cap national pharmaceutical agency with a fraction of Australia's per-capita spending and arguably world-class negotiating leverage. This report answers that question directly.

---

## Background

Established in 1993, PHARMAC — Te Pātaka Whaioranga, the Pharmaceutical Management Agency — was created to rein in New Zealand's rapidly rising public pharmaceutical costs. It is a Crown entity operating under the New Zealand Public Health and Disability Act 2000, sitting within Vote Health. Its core function is to manage the Combined Pharmaceutical Budget (CPB): a fixed annual appropriation set by the government within which PHARMAC must fund medicines for all publicly subsidised patients in New Zealand.

This structural feature — the fixed budget — distinguishes PHARMAC fundamentally from Australia's PBS. The PBS is demand-driven: once a medicine is listed, the Commonwealth is obliged to subsidise every eligible prescription written, regardless of volume or total cost. The budget is open-ended from the payer's perspective (controlled only by co-payment design and clinical criteria). PHARMAC operates the opposite way. It decides in advance which medicines can be funded within the available appropriation, and allocates that envelope through competitive processes. Everything else is unfunded — regardless of whether it is registered, effective, and used in comparable countries.

## Key Findings

### 1. How PHARMAC's Fixed-Budget Monopsony Works

PHARMAC manages approximately **2,600 funded medicines** in community and hospital settings, serving a population of approximately 5.1 million New Zealanders. Its toolkit includes:

**Competitive Tendering:** PHARMAC runs an **Annual Tender** covering roughly 20% of the CPB by value — a multi-product competitive procurement in which suppliers bid for exclusive (sole-supply) contracts on a given active ingredient, typically for a three-year term. The winning supplier becomes the subsidised brand; patients who prefer a different brand pay the full unsubsidised price, or the difference above the reference subsidy (a "manufacturer's surcharge"). Competition within this tender can drive prices to **more than 90% below pre-tender levels** for high-volume generic medicines.

**Reference Pricing:** PHARMAC pays the same subsidy (the reference price) for medicines it deems therapeutically equivalent, regardless of which brand a patient receives. If a manufacturer prices above the reference, the patient pays the gap. This creates a powerful incentive for suppliers to price at or below the reference level.

**Biosimilar Leverage:** When a biosimilar enters the market, PHARMAC does not simply list it at a statutory discount (Australia's approach). Instead, it uses the availability of the biosimilar to renegotiate the price of the originator product simultaneously. When biosimilar infliximab (Inflectra) became available in New Zealand in 2014, PHARMAC negotiated a **30% price reduction on the originator Remicade** before listing any biosimilar, saving an estimated NZ$25 million over five years. Both products then competed under the same subsidy level, with the originator manufacturer absorbing the full discount rather than passing it to patients.

**Bundling and Risk-Sharing:** PHARMAC can bundle medicines in negotiations (e.g., offering exclusive supply of one product in exchange for favourable pricing on another). Confidential risk-sharing arrangements are used for high-cost or uncertain medicines, though the details are not publicly disclosed.

### 2. The Budget: Scale and Per-Capita Comparison

**PHARMAC Medicines Budget 2024-25:** NZD **$1.689 billion**, boosted by the NZD $604 million injection from the 2024 Budget — the **largest funding increase in PHARMAC's history**. Total gross spending (including commercial rebates) reached NZD $2.71 billion, as PHARMAC's negotiating leverage effectively doubled the headline appropriation.

In that year, PHARMAC funded **66 new medicines** — 33 for cancer and 33 for other conditions, more than in any previous year.

**Per-capita comparison:**

| System | Government pharmaceutical spend | Population | Per capita |
|---|---|---|---|
| Australia PBS | AU$19.1 billion (2024–25) | 26.5 million | ~AU$721 |
| NZ PHARMAC | NZD $1.689 billion (2024–25) | 5.1 million | ~NZD $331 |

At approximate purchasing-power parity (AUD/NZD ~1.09 as of 2025), New Zealand's government pharmaceutical spend per capita is roughly **AU$304 per person** — less than half Australia's AU$721. This gap is partly attributable to PHARMAC's pricing success and partly to the access constraints inherent in the fixed-budget model.

Noting the comparison requires care: the PBS figure covers community dispensing and does not include all hospital medicines funded through state health budgets; PHARMAC's figure covers both community and hospital (national) medicines. On a like-for-like basis, the gap narrows, but Australia's per-capita spend remains materially higher.

### 3. The Access Gap: 24 Blood Cancer Drugs, 42 Indications

The clearest evidence of PHARMAC's access constraint comes from a systematic comparison with Australia.

As of **1 January 2024**, New Zealand's Cancer Control Agency (Te Aho o Te Kahu) identified **24 blood cancer medicines** available on Australia's public system that were **not funded** in New Zealand, across **42 different treatment indications**. Of these:
- **12 showed substantial clinical benefit** (meaningful improvements in survival or quality of life)
- **2 had curative potential** in specific blood cancers

More than **50 haematologists** signed a joint letter characterising the situation as a "fundamental health policy failure," warning of eroding trust and avoidable deaths. The Lancet Oncology published a correspondence piece in late 2024 from New Zealand doctors raising alarm about cancer drug inequity, noting that New Zealand sits at or near the **bottom of the OECD** for access to modern medicines.

The 2024 Budget injection allowed PHARMAC to fund **four blood cancer medicines** for six uses from that identified gap list, reducing the active gap to **36 medicine-indication pairs as of early 2025**. Nine of these remaining gaps have substantial clinical benefit.

Budget 2025 (NZ government, May 2025) provided **no new increase** to the PHARMAC medicines budget. Blood cancer advocates described this as locking in "four more years of denied access and patient harm," with only 180 blood cancer patients estimated to have benefited from the prior year's spending — less than 1% of all New Zealanders living with blood cancer.

### 4. Gene Therapies and CAR-T: The Hardest Test

The PHARMAC model's limitations become most acute with high-cost, ultra-rare medicines — precisely the technologies examined in Report 006.

**CAR-T Cell Therapies:** As of June 2026, **no CAR-T cell therapy is publicly funded in New Zealand**. PHARMAC, Medsafe, and the Cancer Control Agency are still developing frameworks to assess CAR-T therapies for funding consideration. New Zealand patients who need Kymriah, Yescarta, Tecartus, or Carvykti face either travelling overseas (at personal cost of US$500,000–$1 million, including treatment and associated care) or going without. By contrast, Australia publicly funds all four of these therapies through the HST Program.

**Why PHARMAC cannot readily fund CAR-T:** The fixed-budget model means funding a single CAR-T infusion at ~NZD$500,000–$700,000 would consume a significant fraction of the available cancer medicines budget — displacing funding from dozens of other treatments with broader eligibility. PHARMAC's opportunity-cost framework is explicit: a funding decision is always relative, not absolute. A therapy competing for the same budget against drugs that benefit hundreds of patients annually will typically not win, even if its clinical benefit is profound for the individual receiving it.

**Zolgensma (onasemnogene abeparvovec) for SMA:** While Australia listed Zolgensma (AU$2.5 million per patient) on the PBS in 2022 with a listing expanded in August 2025, New Zealand has followed a different path. PHARMAC funded **risdiplam (Evrysdi)** — the twice-daily oral SMA treatment — in April 2023 as its second SMA therapy, expanding treatment options but stopping short of funding the gene therapy. As of mid-2026, Zolgensma remains unfunded by PHARMAC. PHARMAC's Rare Disorders Advisory Committee considered Zolgensma in 2023-24, but no funded listing has been confirmed in publicly available sources.

This divergence is structurally predictable: at NZD $3–4 million per patient (the approximate NZD equivalent of the AU$2.5 million price), funding Zolgensma for New Zealand's estimated 3–5 eligible infants per year would cost NZD $9–20 million annually — a meaningful fraction of PHARMAC's cancer budget — without displacing care from hundreds of other patients.

### 5. The Structural Mechanics: Fixed Budget vs Demand-Driven System

The core difference between PHARMAC and the PBS is not sophistication of evaluation, pricing leverage, or clinical expertise — it is the **budget architecture**.

| Feature | PHARMAC (NZ) | PBS (Australia) |
|---|---|---|
| Budget type | Fixed appropriation (CPB) | Demand-driven, open-ended |
| New listings funded by | Displacing budget from elsewhere; awaiting next appropriation | Adding to uncapped Commonwealth commitment |
| Co-payment | NZD $5 per prescription (most medicines); NZD $0 for those receiving income support | AU$25 (general, from Jan 2026); AU$7.70 (concessional) |
| Tendering model | Sole tender (exclusive supply, 3-year terms) | Price disclosure / competitive listing; no sole supply |
| Biosimilar pricing | Negotiated reduction on originator + biosimilar simultaneously | Mandatory 25% statutory reduction on biologic at biosimilar listing |
| CAR-T / gene therapy | Not funded (framework under development) | HST Program (CAR-T funded); PBS (Zolgensma funded) |
| OECD access ranking | Bottom quartile | Above-average (but below best practice) |

Australia's PBS costs more per capita but delivers broader access to listed medicines. New Zealand's PHARMAC costs less per capita but delays or denies access to many approved, effective therapies.

### 6. PHARMAC's Successes That Australia Has (Partially) Emulated

Despite its access limitations, PHARMAC has delivered genuine innovations in pharmaceutical pricing that Australia has studied and selectively borrowed:

**Biosimilar leverage at originator pricing:** Australia's Report 005 examined how Australia's 25% mandatory statutory reduction mechanism consistently underperforms against OECD peers. New Zealand's approach — using biosimilar entry to simultaneously renegotiate originator pricing — achieves deeper discounts. Australia's Sandoz White Paper (June 2025) and biosimilar policy debate implicitly acknowledge this; the PBS price disclosure mechanism partially approximates NZ's approach but without the exclusive tender leverage.

**Effective generic tendering:** PHARMAC's Annual Tender delivers price reductions well above what Australia achieves through PBS price disclosure for generics. Australia's price disclosure mechanism (capturing generic competition) typically yields discounts of 50-70% over several cycles; PHARMAC's sole-tender model often achieves equivalent reductions in a single negotiation round.

**Budget discipline as a political tool:** PHARMAC gives the NZ government a single, legible budget line for medicines. The Australian PBS's open-ended commitment makes pharmaceutical expenditure growth a recurring fiscal pressure — and politically difficult to constrain without appearing to withdraw universal access. The 2024 HTA Review noted the value of greater budget certainty, though it stopped short of recommending a PHARMAC-style fixed cap.

### 7. Why Australia Hasn't Adopted the PHARMAC Model

The political and structural reasons Australia has not adopted a PHARMAC-style fixed-cap model are significant:

**Scale and expectations:** Australia's 26 million people have built expectations of medicine access that took decades to establish. The PBS guarantees access to listed medicines for all eligible patients; a PHARMAC-style queue would be politically unacceptable at scale. The NZ experience — where doctors sign joint letters about avoidable deaths — illustrates the political cost of access rationing.

**The demand-driven commitment predates PHARMAC:** Australia's PBS dates to 1948; the open-ended commitment is embedded in the Medicare architecture. Imposing a fixed budget would require either expanding it sufficiently to maintain current access (removing the savings benefit) or reducing listings (politically toxic).

**Federal structure:** Australia's state/territory governments fund hospital medicines separately from the PBS. A PHARMAC-style national purchasing body would require Commonwealth-state agreement on a unified formulary — the same negotiation friction that creates delays in the HST Program.

**Industry investment:** The pharmaceutical industry argues (with some evidence) that fixed-budget systems like PHARMAC create disincentives to submit new medicines for listing in small markets. Manufacturers uncertain of New Zealand funding prioritise larger markets; this contributes to NZ's access lag. With Australia's larger market, the submission economics are more favourable even under the current PBS model.

## Tensions and Open Questions

**1. The equity-efficiency frontier.** PHARMAC's model is more budget-efficient than Australia's PBS. It is less equitable in terms of access to the most innovative (and expensive) medicines. Whether the trade-off is acceptable depends on values — but as the medicine gap with Australia grows, NZ policymakers increasingly acknowledge it is not acceptable to patients.

**2. The CAR-T gap is a canary.** New Zealand's inability to fund CAR-T cell therapies through PHARMAC signals something important: the fixed-budget model is structurally incompatible with the era of ultra-expensive, ultra-rare, potentially curative therapies. PHARMAC was designed to manage a world of high-volume generic medicines and biologics. The gene therapy era breaks it.

**3. Australia's 'open-ended' model is increasingly stressed.** Australia funds CAR-T and Zolgensma, but does so through improvised machinery (the HST Program and PBAC's discretionary acceptance of above-threshold ICERs) without transparent rules. At 7.3% annual PBS growth and with the gene therapy pipeline accelerating, Australia's model faces the same sustainability question — just from the opposite direction.

**4. What Australia can actually borrow from NZ.** The selective lessons are real: PHARMAC's biosimilar tendering leverage, its use of originator-negotiation-at-biosimilar-entry, and its bundling strategies all offer techniques the PBS could incorporate at the margin. The 2024 HTA Review's interest in greater price transparency and stronger price disclosure mechanisms implicitly draws on the PHARMAC experience.

**5. The political economy of "good enough."** PHARMAC is popular in New Zealand — it keeps medicine costs visible and controlled. Its access failures are concentrated in specific disease communities (blood cancer, rare diseases) that lack the political weight of the broad patient population. The access crisis is real but politically diffuse. This creates institutional inertia: the model is sustained by its cost-savings success even as its access failures accumulate.
