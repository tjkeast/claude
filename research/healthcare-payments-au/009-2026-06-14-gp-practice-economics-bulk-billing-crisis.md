# GP Practice Economics and the Bulk Billing Crisis: Why Australia Nearly Lost Its Free Doctor System — and Whether $8.5 Billion Can Fix It

**Report 009 — 2026-06-14**

**Summary:** The bulk billing rate for GP consultations crashed from 89% in 2020 to 77% in 2023-24 — a collapse that threatened to price millions of Australians out of primary care. The Albanese government's $8.5 billion Bulk Billing Practice Incentive Program (BBPIP), launched November 2025, has produced the largest quarterly rate recovery in twenty years. But behind the headline numbers lies a structural crisis in GP practice economics: MBS fees that do not cover the real cost of care, a payroll tax time-bomb that rattled the contractor model for a decade, a GP workforce pipeline that will take years to fill, and a geographic distribution problem that no payment model fully solves.

**Why this follows from yesterday:** Report 008 documented the financial toxicity paradox of private health insurance — that PHI holders face higher out-of-pocket costs than Medicare-only patients. The GP layer is where most Australians first encounter that system. Whether a GP bulk bills determines whether patients can afford to enter the healthcare pathway at all; a bulk billing crisis is therefore a gateway crisis for every downstream cost examined in this series.

---

## Background

General practice is the cornerstone of Australia's primary care system. GPs act as the first point of contact for illness, the coordinators of chronic disease management, the referral gatekeepers to specialists, and the prescribers for the vast majority of PBS medicines. The Federal Government funds GP services through the Medicare Benefits Schedule (MBS), setting a "schedule fee" for each consultation type that Medicare subsidises at 100% of the schedule fee when the service is bulk billed (the doctor accepts the Medicare rebate as full payment, with no patient contribution) or at 85% for out-of-hospital non-referred services when a gap is charged.

**Bulk billing** is the mechanism that makes GP care free at point of care for the patient. When a GP bulk bills, they assign their Medicare right directly to the Commonwealth and receive the schedule fee in full, forgoing any additional charge. For the patient — especially low-income earners, concession card holders, families with children, and people with chronic disease requiring frequent GP visits — bulk billing is the difference between accessing care and not.

For most of Medicare's history, bulk billing rates were high. In the 2020-21 financial year, an extraordinary 88.8-89% of GP non-referred attendances were bulk billed — the highest sustained rate since Medicare's founding. That was also the peak of the COVID-19 pandemic, when telehealth was mandated as bulk billed for many services and out-of-pocket gap charging was practically difficult. As the pandemic receded, so did the rate — dramatically.

## Key Findings

### 1. The Bulk Billing Collapse: 2021-2024

Between 2021-22 and 2023-24, the bulk billing rate for GP non-referred attendances fell from 88.8% to 77.3% — an 11.5 percentage point decline across just three financial years. This was the steepest sustained fall in the history of Medicare bulk billing.

The decline was not uniform. State and territory variation was stark:
- **New South Wales:** 82% bulk billing rate (2023)
- **Queensland:** ~80%
- **Tasmania:** ~69%
- **Australian Capital Territory:** 53% — the lowest of any jurisdiction, reflecting Canberra's high-income demographic and historically mixed-billing specialist culture

Rural and remote areas have consistently lower bulk billing rates than metropolitan centres. As of 2022, mean out-of-pocket costs for non-bulk-billed GP visits ranged from **$16 to $99 depending on region**, with remote patients bearing disproportionate cost burdens.

The structural cause of the collapse was straightforward: the cost of running a GP practice had grown faster than MBS fees for over a decade. From 2013 to 2020, the MBS schedule fee indexation was frozen entirely — $3.9 billion in cumulative "savings" extracted from the system, as documented in Report 002. Post-freeze indexation resumed but tracked the Wage Cost Index (WCI) rather than actual practice cost inflation, which includes rent, technology, clinical supply costs, and the wages of reception, nursing, and administrative staff.

### 2. The Item 23 Economics: What Bulk Billing Actually Pays

The workhorse of Australian general practice is **MBS Item 23** — the Level B consultation (6-20 minutes, which covers the vast majority of standard GP appointments). From 1 July 2025, Item 23 carries a schedule fee of **$43.90**, following 2.4% indexation applied from that date.

When a GP bulk bills an Item 23 consultation, they receive $43.90 per patient — the government rebate in full. Under the typical contractor model, the GP then pays the practice overhead (practice expenses: staff, rent, IT, consumables, insurance, cleaning, and practice management), retaining 70-75% of billings — roughly **$30-33 per consultation**.

The overhead problem becomes apparent when you consider what it costs to run a GP practice. A single-GP practice in metropolitan Australia typically carries:
- **Staff costs** (reception, practice nurse, practice manager): often 40-50% of revenue at smaller practices
- **Rent:** $50,000-$150,000+ per year depending on location
- **IT and clinical systems:** $15,000-$30,000 per year
- **Medical indemnity and insurance:** $10,000-$25,000+ per year
- **Pathology and allied health facilitation, consumables, cleaning:** varies

Industry advisers benchmark healthy GP clinic net profit margins at **15-25%**. Practices consistently below 15% are under financial stress. A bulk-billing-only model generating roughly $43.90 per consultation after 2.4% indexation provides minimal headroom, particularly in high-cost urban locations.

The **Australian Medical Association (AMA)** maintains a recommended fee list that departs significantly upward from the MBS schedule. The AMA does not publish its full fee schedule publicly (access requires membership or purchase), but comparable practitioner organisations have put Level B consultations (Item 23 equivalent) at **$89-$120+ per consultation** — roughly double the MBS schedule fee. This gap explains why specialists and many GPs who are not fully bulk billing routinely charge above the MBS schedule: it reflects the actual cost of providing the service.

### 3. The Payroll Tax Time-Bomb

A structural threat to GP practice economics that intensified between 2021-2025 was **state and territory payroll tax liability for contractor GPs**.

The dominant model for GP engagement in Australia is the contractor (or "service agreement") model: GPs are not employees of the practice but independent contractors who pay a service fee to the practice in exchange for consulting space, staff, equipment, and administration. Under this model, the practice does not pay payroll tax on GP fees — or so practices assumed.

From approximately 2020 onwards, state revenue authorities (beginning with Queensland and Victoria) began taking the position that under some service agreement structures, GPs were in substance "employees" for payroll tax purposes, exposing practices to retrospective assessments of significant liability. The thresholds at which payroll tax applies in 2025 vary by state (VIC $1.0M, NSW $1.2M, QLD $1.3M, SA $1.5M, ACT $2.0M, NT $2.5M, TAS $1.25M, WA $1.0M). A multi-GP practice can exceed the threshold quickly.

The response was to carve out payroll tax exemptions specifically linked to bulk billing, effectively tying the labour law issue to the payment policy debate:

- **South Australia:** Legislated payroll tax exemption for payments to doctors for bulk-billed GP services from **1 July 2024**
- **New South Wales:** From **4 September 2024**, an 80% rebate on payroll tax for bulk-billed metropolitan GP services, 70% for regional NSW
- **Victoria:** From **1 July 2025**, exemption on contractor and employee GP wages for fully bulk-billed services
- **Queensland:** Full exemption for wages paid to GPs for bulk-billed services
- **ACT:** Exemption from **1 July 2025**

These state-level exemptions simultaneously reduced the financial penalty of bulk billing and provided practice owners with an additional incentive structure for committing to full bulk billing models. The combination of state payroll tax reform and Commonwealth BBPIP changed the calculus considerably in 2025.

### 4. The 2023 Triple Incentive: A First Move That Wasn't Enough

The Albanese government's first major response to the bulk billing collapse was the **November 2023 tripling of the Bulk Billing Incentive (BBI)** for children under 16 and Commonwealth concession card holders — approximately 11 million Australians. The incentive payment was roughly tripled from its previous level.

This initiative slowed the rate of bulk billing decline but did not reverse it. The bulk billing rate stabilised in the low-to-mid 78% range through most of 2024 — well below the pre-pandemic 88-89% standard. The reason was structural: the 2023 tripled incentive applied only to a subset of patients, meaning practices still faced a complex calculation about which patients to bulk bill and which to charge a gap. Practices with mixed patient loads found the partial incentive insufficient to justify committing to full bulk billing for all patients. The incentive also did not address the practices that had already exited the bulk billing model entirely.

### 5. BBPIP (November 2025): The Policy Reboot

From **1 November 2025**, the Bulk Billing Practice Incentive Program (BBPIP) replaced the previous BBI structure with a fundamentally different design:

**What BBPIP does:**
- Pays an additional **12.5% loading** on top of **every dollar of MBS benefit** earned by practices from eligible services — not just for concession card holders or children, but for **all Medicare-eligible patients**
- The 12.5% incentive is split 50/50 between the GP and the practice
- To access BBPIP, a practice must commit to bulk billing all eligible patients for eligible services — making it a full-commitment program, not a partial incentive
- The first payments were made in January 2026 (covering November-December 2025); quarterly payments follow

**The financial effect on Item 23:**
An Item 23 consultation at $43.90 generates a total payment of $43.90 × 1.125 = **$49.39** under BBPIP (split as $21.95 to the GP and $21.95 to the practice, on top of the base $43.90 that goes to the GP as billing revenue — in practice the GP receives the $43.90 plus their share of the 12.5% loading, roughly $2.20, while the practice receives $2.20 as an additional payment and also retains its overhead share of the base billing). The effective uplift to the GP is approximately 6.25% directly, with an equivalent uplift to the practice's operational income.

**Impact:**
- As of February 2026, **3,700+ practices had registered** for BBPIP
- The national bulk billing rate for November 2025 to January 2026 reached **81.4%** — up from 77.1% for the same period a year prior
- The Health Minister described it as the **"largest quarterly jump in bulk billing in 20 years"** (outside of the COVID-19 pandemic period)
- The government projects the program will cost approximately **$2 billion per year** to taxpayers

The government's goal, as articulated in both the 2025 election campaign and the BBPIP guidelines, is to achieve **9 in 10 GP visits bulk billed by 2030** — effectively returning the bulk billing rate to near its 2020 pandemic peak and sustaining it structurally rather than via emergency telehealth mandates.

The government estimates approximately **4,800 practices** will be financially better off adopting full bulk billing under BBPIP compared to their current billing models.

### 6. The Structural Limits: What BBPIP Cannot Fix

Despite the BBPIP's significant early impact, several structural issues remain that the payment reform cannot fully resolve.

**The 23% problem.** The Department of Health's own incoming ministerial brief, prepared at the start of the second Albanese term, warned that approximately **23% of clinics are unlikely to bulk bill even when offered the BBPIP incentive**. These practices operate in premium commercial locations (inner-city areas, affluent suburbs, specialist districts), serve demographic mixes where patients can and do pay gap fees, and have business models premised on revenue per consultation well above the BBPIP-augmented MBS fee.

**The geographic access problem.** Approximately **500,000 Australians live in "GP deserts"** — communities in the bottom 5% nationally for GP services per person. People in GP deserts receive **40% fewer GP services** than the national average, regardless of billing model. The issue is not price but presence: there are simply insufficient GPs in these locations to meet demand. No payment reform delivers a GP to a town that does not have one.

**The workforce pipeline lag.** The RACGP's November 2025 National GP Workforce Insights Report warned of potential GP shortfall "nearing 9,000 full-time equivalents by 2048" if structural workforce issues are not addressed. The response has been the largest GP training cohort on record: approximately **2,100 doctors are expected to commence GP training in 2026**, with 1,800 funded AGPT training places in 2026 (up 13% on 2025) and 1,900 in 2027. The government committed more than $600 million in the 2025-26 Budget to GP workforce measures, including a $30,000 salary incentive for new GP trainees transitioning to community practice (from semester one 2026) and payments for study leave and parental leave.

However, the full AGPT training pathway takes approximately **4 years** after medical school, meaning that trainees commencing in 2026 will not be independent practitioners until 2030 at earliest. The workforce that serves 2026-2028 is already determined.

**The gender and hours dynamic.** A disproportionate share of the growing GP cohort is female, and a larger proportion of female GPs choose part-time and flexible work patterns. While this is entirely legitimate workforce behaviour, it means that raw headcount growth translates into fewer full-time equivalent consultations than historical conversion rates suggest. Overseas-trained doctors (IMGs) compensate partly, particularly in rural and underserved areas, typically working more hours — but they face their own qualification and placement barriers.

**The specialist gap relationship.** The MBS fee inadequacy problem documented in Report 002 compounds GP economics in a systems sense: GPs who cannot bulk bill their patients and charge gap fees also refer those patients to specialists who charge significantly above the MBS schedule fee. The patient who cannot afford the GP gap is often the same patient who cannot afford the specialist gap. Primary care access and specialist access are coupled, and pricing pressure on GPs shifts care towards emergency departments and urgent care centres at far greater system cost.

## Tensions and Open Questions

**1. Is 90% by 2030 achievable?** The BBPIP produced an 81.4% rate in its first three months — a genuinely significant result. However, the marginal gain from 81% to 90% requires converting the 23% of practices the department itself identifies as unlikely to bulk bill. These tend to be higher-volume, higher-earning practices in high-cost locations. The arithmetic suggests a ceiling effect well below 90% without either a further substantial incentive increase or structural measures (mandatory bulk billing for specific patient cohorts or practice types).

**2. Is BBPIP structurally sound or a blunt instrument?** The 12.5% loading applies equally to Level B Item 23 consultations ($43.90) and to Level C ($75.25) and Level D ($110.45) longer consultations. GPs who invest time in complex chronic disease management, mental health care plans, and preventive care receive proportionally the same incentive as those doing rapid short consultations. Critics argue the program rewards throughput rather than care quality. The RACGP's November 2025 guide notes that "bulk-billing rates are up — but there's more to delivering the best possible care," flagging concern that the incentive structures do not reward longitudinal, comprehensive care.

**3. The MyMedicare continuity bonus.** Separately from BBPIP, the government has pursued **MyMedicare** — a voluntary patient enrolment system linking patients to a regular GP practice and offering enhanced rebates for longer telehealth consultations with enrolled patients. As of 2026, MyMedicare participation has grown but not yet transformed continuity of care incentives at scale. The interaction between MyMedicare and BBPIP — and whether the combination shifts incentives toward longitudinal care — remains under evaluation.

**4. Payroll tax patchwork.** State-level payroll tax exemptions for bulk-billed GP services are not uniform across the country. The Northern Territory has not issued clear guidance as of mid-2026. The patchwork of state rules creates compliance complexity for practices operating across jurisdictions and means that the financial incentive to bulk bill varies meaningfully by location — adding to the geographic variation in bulk billing rates that the Commonwealth BBPIP is trying to reduce.

**5. Private billing as a quality signal.** A persistent argument in the GP economics debate is that the ability to charge above the MBS schedule fee enables GPs to invest in longer consultations, better equipment, and more comprehensive care — that "mixed billing" practices provide a different (not necessarily better, but different) care model that patients with capacity to pay may prefer. The push toward 90% bulk billing therefore involves a value trade-off: broader free access at the cost of some service differentiation. This tension has not been openly resolved in policy.

## Threads to Branch Into Next

1. **MyMedicare and continuity of care reform** — The voluntary patient enrolment system links to enhanced rebates for complex care, mental health care plans, and chronic disease management. How does MyMedicare interact with BBPIP? Is the government succeeding in shifting primary care from episodic to longitudinal models?

2. **Rare disease and orphan drug policy** — The Life Saving Drugs Program vs the PBS vs the HST Program: three overlapping funding pathways for ultra-rare conditions, each with different eligibility criteria. Deferred since Report 006 and flagged in every "threads" section since.

3. **GP mental health access and Better Access — the rebate cap debate** — The Better Access initiative (MBS items for psychology/psychiatry) was one of the fastest-growing and most politically contested areas of MBS expenditure in 2022-25. The 20-session cap, the pandemic surge, and the cuts controversy are closely connected to GP gatekeeper economics.

4. **Medical Costs Finder Bill 2026 and fee transparency reform** — First flagged in Report 002. Legislation requiring specialists to publish fees before appointment: current status, implementation timeline, and what data it will make visible for the first time.

5. **US trade pressure on PBS pricing** — The AUSFTA has brought ongoing US pharmaceutical industry pressure on Australia to open PBS pricing to bilateral negotiation. With BBPIP costing $2 billion/year and PBS expenditure at $19.1 billion, the Australian government's fiscal interest in PBS price discipline is directly in tension with US market access demands.
