# Child and Adolescent Mental Health Services (CAMHS): Australia's Invisible Crisis in Specialist Youth Mental Health

**Report 019 — 2026-06-25**

**Summary:** Australia's Child and Adolescent Mental Health Services — the state-funded specialist tier for children and teenagers with severe mental illness — are operating under chronic underfunding, near-total absence of national waiting-time standards, and a psychiatry workforce shortage that is most acute in this subspecialty. Adolescents wait an average of 100 days for mental health treatment; nearly 70% of practitioners are dissatisfied with the current system; Western Australia has implemented just 1 of 32 committed CAMHS reforms after three years; and the entire sector remains a data black hole compared with comparable countries.

**Why this follows from yesterday:** Report 018 traced how US pharmaceutical trade pressure may erode access to new medicines through market de-prioritisation rather than direct policy change. CAMHS illustrates an analogous pattern in the publicly funded care sector: not dramatic cuts, but sustained under-investment, fragmented accountability, and absent data — producing a silent access crisis concentrated among the young people least able to advocate for themselves.

---

## Background

Child and Adolescent Mental Health Services (CAMHS) occupy the highest-severity tier of Australia's youth mental health system. Where headspace (Report 015) is designed for young people with mild-to-moderate mental health difficulties, and Better Access (Report 011) funds community psychology through the MBS, CAMHS provides multidisciplinary specialist care — including psychiatry, inpatient admission, crisis response, eating disorders programs, early psychosis programs, and emergency department liaison — for children and adolescents with severe, complex, or acute mental illness.

The funding architecture separates CAMHS sharply from the Commonwealth-funded tiers:

- **State and territory governments** fund and operate CAMHS as part of their hospital and specialist mental health service systems. CAMHS runs as a network of community teams, outpatient clinics, and inpatient units within state health departments. There is no national service standard, no national waiting-time reporting, and no national dataset equivalent to Scotland's quarterly published CAMHS waiting-time statistics.
- **The Commonwealth** funds Medicare (which covers private child and adolescent psychiatry on fee-for-service through the MBS) and the NDIS (which covers developmental disability and psychosocial supports). Neither of these reaches the population that state CAMHS exists to serve: children in psychiatric crisis, with eating disorders, with severe psychotic illness or suicidality, or with complex comorbidities.
- **Primary Health Networks** commission headspace and, from 2023, Medicare Mental Health Centres (MMHCs) — but neither is designed for CAMHS-severity presentations.

The structural consequence is a system with no single accountable body, no national performance floor, and a referral endpoint — state CAMHS — that is under-resourced and largely invisible in national data.

## Key Findings

### 1. The Demand Signal is Unmistakable — and Growing

Mental disorder prevalence in 16–24 year olds rose **50%** from 26% in 2007 to 39% in 2021 (Australian National Study of Mental Health and Wellbeing, 2021). In 2021, **27% of female adolescents aged 15–17** reported serious mental illness. By 2023–24, females aged 15–19 had a self-harm hospitalisation rate of **405 per 100,000 population** — nearly four times the male rate (114 per 100,000).

Self-harm hospitalisations in 15–19 year olds increased **43% in the decade to 2019**. In New South Wales, per capita self-harm hospitalisations in 10–19 year olds **doubled** over the same period. Emergency department mental health presentations in NSW's 10–19 year old population grew at **27% per year** from 2010 to 2014. By 2025, 45% of young adults aged 18–34 were experiencing psychological distress symptoms — up from 40% in 2023.

The third Australian Child and Adolescent Survey of Mental Health and Wellbeing ("Young Minds: Our Future") commenced fieldwork in November 2025, with results expected in 2027 — Australia's first national prevalence update in more than a decade.

### 2. Waiting Times: Averaging 100 Days with No Support

A 2025 study across 375 Australian adolescents (aged 13–17) waiting for mental health treatment found a mean wait time of **99.6 days** across all provider types — 100.1 days for psychologists and **127.5 days for psychiatrists** (the CAMHS-relevant specialist). A cross-sectional practitioner survey (206 practitioners, published June 2025) found approximately **32% of services** reported wait times of **one to three months** and **21% reported wait times of three to six months**.

Critically, **85.2% of waiting adolescents** felt their waits were "too long." Most received no support from their healthcare provider while waiting and engaged in **maladaptive and risky coping behaviours** in the interim. The study found longer wait times were directly associated with **increased psychological distress** — waiting does not represent a neutral hold, it is a period of active clinical deterioration.

Unlike the UK's NHS, which publishes waiting-time data down to individual trust level, and Scotland, which publishes quarterly CAMHS waiting statistics (target: 90% starting treatment within 18 weeks), **Australia has no comparable national reporting framework** for CAMHS waiting times.

### 3. Practitioner Dissatisfaction: 70% Find the System Inadequate

A peer-reviewed study of 206 Australian practitioners in child and youth mental health services (Australian Journal of Psychology, June 2025) found that **nearly 70% were dissatisfied** with the current child and youth mental health system — 30.4% very dissatisfied and 39.2% somewhat dissatisfied. Key findings:

- Approximately **50% found it difficult to navigate** the system to identify referral pathways
- Approximately **50% of services** currently operate a waitlist
- Qualitative themes: long waitlists, **underfunded public services**, lack of specialised CYMH training in clinician education, and system fragmentation across funding streams

### 4. Inpatient Beds: A Critically Small and Uneven Resource

In 2019–20 (the most recent national data), Australia had **312 CAMHS inpatient beds** nationally — 272 acute, 40 non-acute. On a per-capita basis, this represents just **5.5 beds per 100,000 population**, compared with:
- **31.9 per 100,000** for general adult mental health
- **22.1 per 100,000** for older person mental health

State distribution is extreme. **Victoria** has approximately **60 adolescent short-term inpatient beds** for a population of 6–7 million people — a ratio that implies extraordinarily high demand concentration and pressure at point of admission. **Tasmania has no dedicated adolescent inpatient beds** and only limited access to paediatric general beds.

Nationally, CAMHS admissions show a concerning pattern: **higher cost per patient day, shorter length of stay, higher readmission rates, and lower rates of significant improvement** than adult mental health inpatient services (Brazel et al., 2023). The high 14-day readmission rate in particular suggests that CAMHS admissions are not resolving the underlying severity — patients are being discharged before stabilisation and returning in crisis.

Between 2015–16 and 2019–20, acute CAMHS expenditure grew at 4.5% per year, but non-acute (rehabilitation, step-down, extended care) expenditure fell **4.9% per year** — concentrating investment in emergency-acute at the expense of the post-acute continuum that would prevent readmission.

### 5. Coverage: Less Than 1% of Under-18 Year Olds

CAMHS coverage in Australia — the proportion of the under-18 population actually reached by state-funded specialist mental health services — is **below 1%**. This is substantially below comparable OECD countries. Given the prevalence data above (where 27–39% of young people have measurable mental health needs depending on age and gender cohort), the gap between need and service penetration is structural and severe, not merely a waiting-time problem.

### 6. Workforce: Child and Adolescent Psychiatry at the Sharp End of a System-Wide Shortage

Child and adolescent psychiatry is among the most acute shortage subspecialties in Australian medicine. The November 2025 Psychiatry Supply and Demand Compendium (Department of Health and Aged Care) confirmed:
- The psychiatry workforce meets only **56% of national demand**
- 9 in 10 (93%) psychiatrists report that workforce shortages are **negatively impacting patient care**
- The workforce faces a **20.7% projected undersupply by 2048**
- Approximately 43% of psychiatrists intend to retire in the next decade

Only **14% of psychiatrists** work in rural and remote settings, despite those regions containing approximately **29% of Australia's population** — a geographic mismatch with direct consequences for regional CAMHS access.

### 7. WA Audit: 1 of 32 CAMHS Reforms Implemented After Three Years

The most concrete recent accountability measure comes from Western Australia. In 2021, the Western Australian government commissioned a **Ministerial Taskforce** on public mental health services for infants, children, and adolescents. The taskforce made **32 recommendations** and the government committed to implementing all of them through a nine-year transformation program.

On **17 June 2026** — three years into the reform — WA Auditor General Caroline Spencer tabled a performance audit finding that the **Mental Health Commission had implemented just 1 of 32 recommendations**. Key audit findings:
- The MHC had **no credible plan or pathway** to deliver the transformation program
- The January 2023 implementation plan **failed to address 12 of the 32 recommendations** entirely
- The January 2026 updated plan still **failed to cover all recommendations**
- The MHC had not developed plans addressing **funding, workforce, and infrastructure** — described as critical by the original taskforce

While Western Australia is a single jurisdiction, the audit is emblematic: it reveals that even when government commitment exists in explicit and comprehensive form (32 accepted recommendations), the institutional mechanisms to convert commitment to delivery are absent.

### 8. Federal Budget 2026–27: Thin on CAMHS-Specific Investment

The 2026–27 Commonwealth Budget extended the **National Mental Health and Suicide Prevention Agreement** to 30 June 2027 at a cost of **$277.5 million** — broadly flat in real terms. Agreement funding flows to states/territories and supports a range of services including CAMHS, but the allocation is not disaggregated and does not constitute a structural reform of state specialist services.

Specific child mental health allocations in the budget included:
- **$3.1 million** over four years for school-based mental health support (Years 7–9)
- **$2 billion over 5 years** for "Thriving Kids" — focused on developmental delay and autism in children aged 8 and under, not the adolescent CAMHS cohort
- **$19.5 million** to extend WA's CAMHS Crisis Connect service (a WA-specific measure)

There was no dedicated Commonwealth investment to increase state CAMHS bed capacity, address psychiatry workforce shortages in child specialties, or establish national waiting-time standards.

## Tensions and Open Questions

**1. Who is accountable for CAMHS?** State governments deliver CAMHS but are funded partly through Commonwealth hospital funding agreements. The result is diffuse accountability — state health departments point to Commonwealth funding constraints; the Commonwealth points to state service delivery failures. The WA audit exemplifies this: the reform failure sits squarely with the state Mental Health Commission, but the broader underfunding is a product of the federal financing architecture.

**2. The data black hole.** Australia cannot say, at a national level, how many children are waiting for CAMHS, for how long, or what happens to them while they wait. Scotland publishes this quarterly. Without data, system failures are invisible to policymakers and absent from public debate. The upcoming "Young Minds: Our Future" prevalence survey (results expected 2027) will update the demand-side picture — but says nothing about the supply of services.

**3. The tier mismatch.** The Commonwealth has invested substantially in headspace (~$300–500M/year via PHNs) and Better Access ($1.6B+/year in MBS benefits for adult and youth psychology). Both are calibrated for mild-to-moderate presentations. CAMHS — the severe-end tier — is the responsibility of state governments that have not matched demand growth with resources. The missing middle (Report 017) exists partly because there is also a "missing top" — a state-funded specialist tier that cannot absorb the volume of young people who need it.

**4. Private market inaccessibility.** Private child and adolescent psychiatry is available under the MBS, but with waiting times exceeding 127 days and out-of-pocket costs of $500–$1,000 or more for initial appointments (Specialist Gap Fee data from Report 016), it is beyond the reach of most families. Unlike adult mental health, there is effectively no functional private market that the state system can offload demand to.

**5. The NDIS reform overhang.** The NDIS Amendment (Securing the NDIS for Future Generations) Bill 2026 — described in Report 017 — cuts participation support budgets from October 2026 and delays functional capacity assessments until January 2028. For adolescents with psychosocial disability who have NDIS plans, these cuts reduce support while their alternative — state CAMHS — has 100-day waitlists and less than 1% coverage.

## Threads to Branch Into Next

1. **Medicare Mental Health Centres (MMHCs)** — The Albanese Government's free walk-in mental health model explicitly bridges the gap between headspace and specialist CAMHS for adolescents and adults; funding confirmed in Budget 2023–24; rollout status is one of the most under-reported policy stories in mental health; directly relevant to whether the CAMHS pressure can be partially absorbed at a lower tier.

2. **HTA Reform and the 2026–27 Budget failure** — The HTA Implementation Advisory Group's reform package (faster PBS access pathways, revised discount rates, outcomes-based reimbursement) remains unfunded after the 2026–27 Budget; compounds MFN-driven launch delays (Report 018) and links back to the gene therapy and rare disease coverage gaps of Reports 003–006.

3. **Aged care and the NDIS interface** — NDIS participants must exit at age 65; psychosocial disability in older adults is largely unserved; the transition is a documented service collapse point; aged care funding (the My Aged Care system, AN-ACC funding, residential vs. home care) is the only major Report 001 thread left completely uncovered.

4. **NDIS fraud and quality failures** — The NDIS Inspector-General has identified hundreds of millions in fraudulent billing; fraud prevention is one of four pillars of the 2026 reform package; directly intersects with the psychosocial disability provider market examined in Report 017.

5. **Specialist supply and the training pipeline** — Medical college training number controls across psychiatry, paediatrics, and child psychiatry perpetuate the shortage identified in this report; Health Ministers have discussed reform but no structural change has occurred; directly relevant to the 20.7% undersupply projection.
