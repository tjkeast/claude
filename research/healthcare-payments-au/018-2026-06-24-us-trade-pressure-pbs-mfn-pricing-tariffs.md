# US Trade Pressure on Australia's PBS: MFN Drug Pricing, Pharmaceutical Tariffs, and the Question of Healthcare Sovereignty

**Report 018 — 2026-06-24**

**Summary:** The United States has used trade policy as a lever against Australia's Pharmaceutical Benefits Scheme for two decades — and in 2025–26, that pressure escalated sharply. Trump's May 2025 Most-Favoured-Nation drug pricing executive order and April 2026 pharmaceutical tariff order together pose a structural threat to Australia's PBS: not by forcing direct price increases on Australian patients, but by making Australia a less attractive market for pharmaceutical manufacturers, potentially delaying or deterring launches of new medicines listed under a scheme the US explicitly targets as an "unfair trade barrier."

**Why this follows from yesterday:** Report 017 traced how NDIS reform cuts and delayed Foundational Supports risk collapsing psychosocial disability services. The US pharmaceutical trade pressure in this report poses an analogous threat to the PBS: not demolition by decree, but erosion by economic incentive — with people needing new medicines waiting longest when market forces push companies to prioritise higher-priced countries first.

---

## Background

### The AUSFTA Pharmaceutical Annex: 2004–2025

The Australia–United States Free Trade Agreement (AUSFTA), signed in 2004 and in force from 1 January 2005, contains **Annex 2-C — Pharmaceuticals**: a bespoke chapter inserted at US pharmaceutical industry insistence to address what the industry had characterised as discriminatory PBS pricing practices. Australia's negotiators had to fight to prevent the annex from directly subjugating PBAC cost-effectiveness assessments to US-style market pricing; what emerged was a compromise that nonetheless created durable institutional mechanisms.

Key AUSFTA Annex 2-C obligations on Australia include:

- **Innovation recognition:** the PBS must give appropriate recognition to the value of innovation. This language — deliberately undefined — has provided the pharmaceutical industry a lobbying hook for two decades.
- **Transparency:** the PBAC must publish reasons for listing and rejection decisions (implemented through Public Summary Documents from July 2005) and provide industry the opportunity to comment on proposed regulations.
- **Independent review:** sponsors whose medicines are rejected by the PBAC have a right to an **independent expert review** of the PBAC's recommendation — a mechanism with no counterpart in most comparator HTA systems.
- **Review committee:** a joint Australia–US pharmaceutical review committee was established to facilitate dialogue on industry concerns.

Australian health law academics at the time were explicit: the annex introduced a US lobbying niche inside Australian health policy architecture. It stopped short of requiring price parity with the US (where brand-name drugs are typically 3–10 times their PBS price), but it established that the PBS could not be walled off from bilateral trade pressure indefinitely.

That prediction has proven accurate. From AUSFTA ratification through to 2025, the US pharmaceutical lobby — led by the Pharmaceutical Research and Manufacturers of America (PhRMA) — has filed annual submissions to the United States Trade Representative (USTR) flagging Australia's PBS as a foreign trade barrier.

## Key Findings

### 1. PhRMA's 2025–26 NTE Submissions: What the US Pharma Lobby Wants Changed

In March 2025, PhRMA filed its submission to the USTR's annual **National Trade Estimate (NTE) Report on Foreign Trade Barriers**, characterising Australia's PBS processes as damaging to US pharmaceutical companies' revenue. The 2025 submission complained specifically of:

- **"Lack of adequate patent holder notification":** Australia's generic-drug approval process requires generic manufacturers to declare they do not breach a patent or to notify the patent holder. PhRMA argued this fell short of the US standard, which triggers an automatic 30-month stay on generic approval after patent-holder notification.
- **"Difficulties in listing new medicines on the PBS":** PhRMA characterised PBAC processes — infrequent meetings (three to four per year), post-recommendation pricing negotiations, and administrative processing delays — as "contrary to Article 2(a) of Annex 2-C in the AUSFTA."
- **Access delays:** Australian patients wait an average of **33 months from global first launch** for new medicines to become available on the PBS — among the longest in the OECD.
- **Compulsory licensing:** PhRMA separately characterised Australia as a "constant target" of compulsory licensing and "other harmful practices."
- **PBS as a reference price cap:** PhRMA's broader concern is that the PBS's low negotiated prices — achieved through PBAC cost-effectiveness assessment and price disclosure — function as a ceiling that multinationals must accept to reach the Australian market, then cite as precedent in negotiations elsewhere.

The 2026 NTE submission (filed to USTR for the 2026 report cycle) continued this pattern, arguing PBS listing delays and inadequate patent notification represent structural unfair trade practices.

### 2. The May 2025 Most-Favoured-Nation Executive Order

On **12 May 2025**, President Trump signed an executive order titled **"Delivering Most-Favored-Nation Prescription Drug Pricing to American Patients."** The order's mechanism was novel in the context of pharmaceutical trade: rather than demanding that other countries raise prices, it demanded that pharmaceutical companies charge US patients no more than the lowest price charged in "comparably developed nations."

Australia's PBS is explicitly in the frame. Australia has some of the **lowest government-negotiated pharmaceutical prices among OECD countries** — a function of the PBAC's rigorous cost-effectiveness assessments, price disclosure cycles, and competitive biosimilar uptake. For drugs where Australia has a PBS-listed price, that price may constitute the MFN benchmark.

The mechanism creates a perverse incentive for pharmaceutical companies. If listing a drug on the PBS locks in an Australian price that then becomes the US MFN reference, manufacturers have a rational financial reason to **delay or avoid PBS listing** — keeping Australia off the pricing record until US pricing is settled at a higher level. This is the structural threat that Medicines Australia and academic researchers have identified: not PBS dismantlement, but pharmaceutical manufacturers voluntarily treating Australia as a de-prioritised or later-entry market.

Evidence from comparable markets supports this concern. Across European markets — where government pricing similarly establishes international reference prices — the average number of new medicine launches **fell 43%** in the 10 months following MFN policy implementation, compared with the 10 months prior.

By April 2026, the Trump administration had reached **17 MFN pricing agreements** with leading pharmaceutical manufacturers, covering approximately **86% of the branded drug market**. Under these agreements, companies commit to reducing US prices to match the lowest price in comparably developed countries. The UK, EU, and Japan had entered into parallel bilateral negotiations with the US administration.

### 3. The April 2026 Pharmaceutical Tariff Order: A 100% Stick

On **2 April 2026**, the Trump administration escalated from incentive to coercion, publishing an **executive order on pharmaceutical tariffs**. The tariff structure:

| Company status | Tariff rate |
|---------------|------------|
| MFN pricing deal + US onshoring commitment | **0%** |
| Generic pharmaceuticals (all companies) | **0%** |
| Blood plasma and biological products | **Exempt** |
| US manufacturing plan approved, no MFN deal yet | **20% for 4 years, then 100%** |
| No MFN deal, no manufacturing plan | **100%** from 29 September 2026 |

The tariffs apply to patented pharmaceutical products and pharmaceutical ingredients imported into the United States. They are explicitly designed as leverage: companies that negotiate MFN pricing agreements — accepting lower US prices based on international benchmarks — escape the tariff entirely.

### 4. Australia's Export Exposure: Largely Insulated, But Not Immune

Australia's pharmaceutical exports to the US were valued at approximately **AU$1.9 billion** in the year to December 2024, making the United States Australia's **largest pharmaceutical export market** (approximately 58.7% of all pharmaceutical exports). However, the composition of those exports matters critically:

- Approximately **87%** of Australian pharmaceutical exports to the US consist of **blood plasma products** — predominantly from **CSL**, Australia's largest biopharmaceutical company, which has major manufacturing facilities in the United States.
- Blood plasma and biologically derived therapies are **explicitly exempt** from the April 2026 tariff order.
- CSL's US manufacturing presence means it is expected to qualify for the 0% rate under MFN and onshoring provisions regardless.

The Australian government has assessed that most CSL exports are effectively insulated. The direct export tariff risk to Australia is therefore more limited than the headline 100% figure suggests.

The indirect risk is larger: **Australian patients' access to new patented medicines** from other multinational manufacturers that face the MFN pricing calculus.

### 5. The PBS Pricing Context: Low Prices Are a Feature, Not a Bug — and That's the Problem

Australia's PBS prices are, by international design, low:

- **General co-payment from 1 January 2026:** $25.00 per prescription (reduced from $31.60 following the Albanese Government's election commitment)
- **Concessional co-payment:** $7.70 (frozen to 2030 under the Same Old Story reform)
- **PBS total expenditure (2024–25):** AU$19.1 billion — $721 per person per year in government-subsidised medicines
- **PBAC implicit cost-effectiveness threshold:** approximately AU$50,000/QALY standard; up to $85,000–$220,000 for innovative oncology medicines

These prices are substantially below US prices. A study by the International Federation of Pharmaceutical Manufacturers and Associations found that for a basket of commonly used medicines, Australian patients paid roughly 20–30% of US list prices. This makes Australia exactly the kind of low-price market that the MFN policy targets when companies calculate their global reference price floor.

The PBAC's **price disclosure mechanism** — which captures real-world discounts twice per year and applies them to PBS list prices — continuously pushes prices down further. The mechanism is integral to PBS sustainability but makes Australia an increasingly uncomfortable reference point for manufacturers in a world of international reference pricing.

### 6. The Australian Government's Response: PBS "Not for Sale"

The Australian Government's position, across all senior ministers, has been consistent and unambiguous:

- **Prime Minister Albanese:** The PBS "is not for sale" and "not up for negotiation."
- **Treasurer Chalmers (August 2025):** "Our Pharmaceutical Benefits Scheme is not something we're willing to trade away or do deals on. That won't change." He described the tariff threats as "very concerning" while committing to seek US exemptions.
- **Health Minister Butler:** "This will not impact drug prices for Australian consumers. There will be no negotiation around the PBS under an Albanese government."
- **Trade Minister Farrell:** The government is "disappointed" by pharmaceutical tariffs and will continue to push for removal of "unjustified and unwarranted" tariffs.

The government has actively sought bilateral carve-outs, and DFAT has engaged on the tariff regime. The practical result: CSL's blood plasma dominance in the export profile means the direct tariff impact on Australian pharmaceutical exports is limited. The government has framed this as protection of the PBS while managing bilateral trade relations.

### 7. Budget 2026–27 and the HTA Reform Gap

Medicines Australia responded to the MFN pressure with an urgent call for **HTA reform** to make the PBS faster, fairer, and more reflective of innovation value — the argument being that if Australia cannot offer innovative manufacturers both a listed pathway and a reasonable price signal, they will delay launches or skip the market.

The **2026–27 Federal Budget**, however, did not fund the HTA Implementation Advisory Group's reform package. Medicines Australia's verdict was blunt: the budget **"ignores life-changing new and future medicines despite global challenges."** The IAG's reforms — which include revised discount rates for gene therapies, outcomes-based reimbursement, and faster access pathways — remain approved in principle but unfunded in practice.

This is the deepest tension in Australia's pharmaceutical trade position. The government's defence of PBS price levels is politically essential and economically rational for existing patients. But the failure to modernise the HTA framework — particularly for innovative oncology, gene therapy, and rare disease medicines — means manufacturers facing MFN pressure have legitimate grounds to deprioritise an already-slow-access, low-price market.

## Tensions and Open Questions

**1. The MFN paradox: lower PBS prices hurt Australian access.** The MFN policy creates an inverted incentive: countries that have successfully negotiated low drug prices become the benchmark that forces US manufacturers to cut US prices — but those same manufacturers then avoid those low-price markets to protect their global average price. Australia's PBS efficiency directly contributes to making Australian patients a lower-priority market. There is no easy resolution.

**2. Does the AUSFTA give the US legal leverage?** Australia retains sovereignty over PBS pricing under the AUSFTA. Annex 2-C's "innovation recognition" language has never been used to force a specific pricing outcome. But the annex creates procedural obligations — independent review, transparency, comment rights — that pharmaceutical industry lawyers regularly invoke. A formal AUSFTA dispute over PBS pricing has never been brought, partly because the US prefers informal pressure, and partly because the annex's language is sufficiently vague to make the outcome of dispute settlement uncertain.

**3. The launch delay chilling effect: how bad is it?** The 43% European launch decline cited by industry is a contested figure — European countries' pricing regimes were already under international reference pricing pressure before MFN, and causation is murky. But the directional effect is real. Australia already has access delays averaging 466 days (OECD comparison from Report 004). The MFN chilling effect — if manufacturers postpone Australian submissions until US pricing is locked in — could push that figure higher without any formal change to Australian law.

**4. Who negotiates for Australia?** The PBS is a Health portfolio matter; trade is DFAT; industry engagement is conducted by DISR. Multiple portfolios engage with the US on pharmaceutical issues without a single lead negotiator. The UK and EU have appointed dedicated pharmaceutical trade envoys for MFN negotiations; Australia has not.

**5. The CSL asymmetry.** CSL's effective exemption from pharmaceutical tariffs — because blood plasma is exempt and CSL has US manufacturing — means the largest Australian pharmaceutical exporter faces minimal impact. This limits political pressure on the Australian government to make PBS concessions. It also means smaller Australian pharmaceutical exporters (generics, specialist biologics) may face tariff exposure that larger industry voices are not feeling.
