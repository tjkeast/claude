# The Multi-System OOP Trap: How Australia's Three Healthcare Safety Nets Fail Older Australians Simultaneously

**Report 030 — 2026-07-06**

**Summary:** Australia runs three parallel out-of-pocket protection mechanisms — the Medicare Safety Net, the PBS Safety Net, and the aged care means-tested co-contribution framework — each covering a different slice of health expenditure, none communicating with the others. For older Australians simultaneously navigating Medicare specialist and GP services, multiple PBS medicines, and residential aged care, the compounding financial burden is substantial and almost entirely invisible in official data. A self-funded retiree with above-median assets entering residential aged care in 2026 can face annual healthcare and care co-contributions exceeding $70,000 — across three siloed systems with no integrated safety valve.

**Why this follows from yesterday:** Report 029 documented how the final year of life costs $24,000 per person on average — 14 times the per-person health cost of all other Australians — and that the MBS has no dedicated payment mechanism to fund the advance care conversations that reduce that burden. Today's report examines the broader financial architecture of late-life healthcare: how three distinct safety nets were each designed to protect against runaway costs in their respective domains, yet collectively expose older Australians to a compounding burden none of them was designed to address.

---

## Background

Australia's healthcare funding architecture was built incrementally, not coherently. Medicare (1984) created the MBS and, later, its safety nets. The PBS developed its own safety net for medicines across the same era. Aged care developed a means-tested contribution regime that has undergone its most significant overhaul since 1 November 2025. Each system has its own safety valve. None of them knows what the others are doing.

This structural siloing is benign if older Australians use only one system at a time. They do not. The population most intensively using MBS services (specialist attendances, diagnostics, pathology) is precisely the population most likely to be taking multiple PBS-listed medicines and approaching or living in residential aged care. A person with dementia, heart failure, and type 2 diabetes — a common multimorbidity cluster in residential aged care — may simultaneously hit the thresholds of all three safety nets in the same calendar year. No government mechanism tracks, limits, or even measures the combined burden.

## Key Findings

### 1. The Medicare Safety Net: What it catches — and what it doesn't

The Medicare Safety Net operates across two tiers, both exclusively covering **out-of-hospital MBS services**.

**Original Medicare Safety Net (OMSN):**
- 2026 threshold: $594.40 in annual gap costs (CPI-indexed, up from $576.00 in 2025)
- Once reached: Medicare benefit rises from 85% to 100% of the MBS Schedule Fee for out-of-hospital services for the remainder of that calendar year
- Applies universally to all Medicare card holders; families can register to pool their gap costs

**Extended Medicare Safety Net (EMSN):**
- 2026 thresholds: $861.20 for concessional individuals/families (including FTB-A recipients); $2,699.10 for all other individuals and families
- Once reached: Medicare pays 80% of further out-of-pocket costs for out-of-hospital MBS services for the rest of the year, subject to item-specific EMSN benefit caps
- EMSN caps were introduced in 2010 specifically to prevent specialists from inflating fees knowing that post-threshold patients were effectively insulated by the 80% coverage; a partial structural fix that did not stop the underlying gap fee inflation (average specialist gap rose from $49.56 in 2010–11 to $117.18 in 2023–24, a 136% real increase against 40% CPI)

**The critical exclusions for older Australians:**

1. **In-hospital services do not count toward either threshold.** All MBS services billed as part of an admitted-patient episode — which includes most specialist procedures, anaesthesia, and pathology ordered during an admission — generate out-of-pocket costs that are invisible to both the OMSN and EMSN. For older Australians averaging four hospital episodes in their final year of life, this exclusion is not a technicality.

2. **Aged care co-contributions are entirely outside the Medicare Safety Net.** Hotelling contributions, non-clinical care contributions, and accommodation costs generate no qualifying expenditure toward any Medicare Safety Net threshold, regardless of their magnitude. The two systems are not merely separate — they are structurally blind to each other.

### 2. The PBS Safety Net: Mostly functional for concession holders, but with gaps

PBS co-payments from 1 January 2026:
- **General patients:** $25.00 per script (reduced from $31.60, a 2025 election commitment)
- **Concessional patients** (Pensioner Concession Card, Health Care Card, DVA): $7.70 per script (frozen to 2030)

PBS Safety Net thresholds, 2026:
- **General:** $1,748.20 in annual PBS co-payments → once reached, scripts revert to concessional rate ($7.70) for the rest of the year
- **Concessional:** $277.20 (= 36 scripts) → once reached, PBS medicines are free for the rest of the year

For most residential aged care residents, the PBS Safety Net is relatively functional. The majority of aged care residents hold a Pensioner Concession Card or Health Care Card, pay $7.70/script, and exhaust the concessional threshold after 36 scripts — roughly mid-year for anyone taking five or more daily medicines. After that, PBS medicines are free.

The structural gaps:
- **Non-PBS medicines are entirely uncapped.** Residential aged care residents taking medicines outside the PBS schedule — including many compounded palliative medications, some antipsychotics used for BPSD when PBS criteria aren't met, and specialist prescription items — face unlimited out-of-pocket costs with no safety net protection.
- **Commonwealth Seniors Health Card (CSHC) holders** — self-funded retirees who don't qualify for the Age Pension or Pensioner Concession Card — pay the general co-payment rate ($25/script) and face the higher general safety net threshold ($1,748.20), meaning they need 70 scripts before gaining protection.
- Polypharmacy is near-universal in aged care: over 60% of residents take five or more medicines daily; high rates of potentially inappropriate prescribing compound both cost and clinical risk.

### 3. The New Aged Care Fee Architecture: Three parallel OOP components

The Aged Care Act 2024 and fee arrangements from 1 November 2025 introduced a restructured co-contribution framework for residential aged care. The total annual financial exposure for a resident comprises:

**Basic Daily Fee (BDF):**
- Mandatory for all residents; ~$65.55/day (~$23,927/year as of mid-2026)
- Set at 85% of the single Age Pension rate; not means-tested

**Hotelling Supplement/Contribution (HSC):**
- Covers "hotel-style" costs: meals, cleaning, laundry, utilities
- Asset-means-tested; maximum $22.15/day (~$8,085/year)
- **No annual or lifetime cap** — this contribution continues indefinitely throughout the stay
- HSC begins when assessable assets exceed approximately $252,000; reaches maximum when assets reach approximately $355,367

**Non-Clinical Care Contribution (NCCC):**
- Covers personal care (bathing, dressing, continence management)
- Maximum $107.32/day (~$39,172/year); **lifetime cap: $137,917.01** (or 4 years, whichever comes first, indexed biannually)
- NCCC begins when assessable assets exceed approximately $532,055; reaches maximum when assets reach approximately $1,023,455

**Accommodation costs (RAD/DAP):**
- Refundable Accommodation Deposit (RAD): interest-free lump sum (maximum $750,000 indexed to CPI from July 2025); refunded on exit; average national RAD for premium metropolitan facilities routinely exceeds $600,000
- A resident who cannot or chooses not to pay a full RAD pays a Daily Accommodation Payment instead; the two can be combined

**Worked example — a self-funded retiree with $900,000 in assessable assets, entering residential aged care in mid-2026:**

| Component | Annual cost | Cap |
|---|---|---|
| Basic Daily Fee | ~$23,927 | None |
| Hotelling Contribution (HSC) | ~$8,085 | **None** |
| Non-Clinical Care Contribution (NCCC) | ~$39,172 | Lifetime cap $137,917 |
| PBS co-payments (general rate, ~60 scripts) | ~$1,500 then drops | PBS Safety Net ($1,748) |
| MBS specialist gap fees (10–15 out-of-hospital visits) | ~$1,200–$1,800 | OMSN/EMSN (out-of-hospital only) |
| **Estimated total annual OOP (aged care + health)** | **~$73,000–$75,000** | **Partially; largest components uncapped** |

The NCCC lifetime cap provides eventual relief — once the $137,917 total is reached (approximately 3.5 years at maximum contributions), the NCCC ceases. But the Hotelling Contribution continues without ceiling, and all health system OOP costs run in parallel.

**Important structural shift from the old system:** The pre-November 2025 means-tested care fee had a lower lifetime cap ($86,185.23). The new NCCC has a higher lifetime cap ($137,917.01) and the new Hotelling Contribution has no lifetime cap at all, making the new regime potentially more expensive in total for high-asset, long-stay residents.

### 4. The Distributional Problem: Multimorbidity and Regressive Exposure

Research consistently finds that OOP health costs in Australia are regressive in practical effect: those with the most conditions — who generate the highest service use — are disproportionately concentrated among lower-income groups. A 2014 BMC Public Health analysis of older Australians found a clear positive relationship between number of chronic conditions and out-of-pocket spending, and that the cost burden falls most heavily on those with multiple conditions who are already financially constrained.

The aged care means test provides nominal progressivity — lower-asset residents pay less. But the means test is calculated against income and assets, not against total healthcare expenditure across all three systems. A pensioner aged care resident who spends an additional $6,000/year on dental care, hearing aids, and non-PBS medicines sits outside all three safety nets for those costs, yet their means assessment looks unchanged.

According to the 2024 Commonwealth Fund international health policy comparison, 8.8% of Australians delayed or missed care due to cost in 2024, up from 7% in 2023 — the highest figure recorded in Australia in the Commonwealth Fund's surveying. This figure will include older Australians delaying specialist reviews or pathology follow-up because the gap fees — uncapped and uncounted by the safety net when incurred in hospital — are prohibitive.

### 5. The Measurement Gap: Three Systems, No Joint Accounting

Perhaps the most fundamental structural deficiency is the absence of any data mechanism that combines Medicare, PBS, and aged care co-contribution records for individual Australians.

Services Australia holds Medicare Safety Net data. It holds PBS Safety Net data. It holds aged care means assessment and contribution data. These datasets are not linked at the patient level for population health reporting or policy analysis. The AIHW's Health Expenditure Australia 2023–24 reports $44.0 billion in individual out-of-pocket health spending — but this figure does not capture aged care co-contributions, which are classified as aged care rather than health expenditure under standard national accounting conventions. The result is that Australia's actual total OOP burden on older Australians is systematically undercounted.

Policy interventions are designed against individual system parameters — PBS co-payment reductions, EMSN threshold indexation, aged care lifetime cap adjustments — without visibility into the aggregate burden on the same individuals across all three systems simultaneously. No one has asked the question: what is the total annual financial impost on a high-needs 80-year-old navigating all three systems at once?

## Tensions and Open Questions

**1. The uncapped hotelling contribution is a structural vulnerability.** The old means-tested care fee had a lifetime cap. The new Hotelling Contribution has none. For a long-stay resident with high assets — including many people with dementia who may spend 5–8 years in care — the absence of a hotelling lifetime cap means total contributions can materially exceed what was possible under the pre-November 2025 regime.

**2. The clinical/personal care boundary is contested.** Dementia residents require personal care (continence, oral hygiene, repositioning) that is clinically necessary yet classified as NCCC-funded personal care under the new framework. High-asset residents pay up to $39,172/year for care that is, in clinical substance, nursing care — which is free. The classification creates both financial inequity and definitional arbitrage for providers.

**3. No mechanism links the three safety nets.** A concessional cardholder who exhausts their PBS safety net (36 scripts = $277.20), their OMSN threshold ($594.40 in gaps), and their EMSN threshold ($861.20) retains no protection from aged care co-contributions. The systems are hermetically sealed from each other. For a resident hitting all three systems simultaneously, the aggregate exposure has no integrated ceiling.

**4. Financial hardship assistance for aged care is opaque and underutilised.** Services Australia provides financial hardship assistance for aged care residents unable to meet assessed contributions after covering basic living costs. But the mechanism is procedurally complex, requires evidence of hardship relative to the means assessment, and no published data on utilisation rates appears to exist. Given the scale of the co-contribution burden demonstrated above, the gap between the need and the uptake is likely substantial.

**5. The EMSN partially insulates high-charging specialists.** Once an older patient hits the EMSN threshold, Medicare reimburses 80% of further OOP costs for out-of-hospital services. This partially insulates the specialist from patient price sensitivity — and older, multi-condition patients are the most likely to hit the EMSN threshold early in the year. EMSN benefit caps (introduced 2010) limit but do not eliminate this dynamic.
