# PBS Access Reform: Closing the 591-Day Gap — Interim Listing, Bridging Funds, and Conditional Reimbursement

**Report 004 — 2026-06-09**

**Summary:** Australia's PBS takes an average 591 days — and sometimes more than 1,000 — to fund a medicine after the TGA has already declared it safe and effective. The HTA Policy and Methods Review (September 2024) proposed a Bridging Fund and enhanced pathways as a structural fix. International models from the UK, Canada, and France show that conditional, time-limited reimbursement works — but Australia has not yet committed the funding or legislation to make it happen.

**Why this follows from yesterday:** Report 003 showed that the PBS's rigorous cost-effectiveness gating via PBAC creates an average 591-day delay between TGA approval and PBS listing, with more than 40 medicines stalled for over 1,000 days post-PBAC recommendation. This report examines what can be done about it — and compares Australia's reform proposals against working models already operating in the UK, Canada, and France.

---

## Background

When a medicine completes TGA registration, it has cleared the highest evidential bar in Australian law: it is safe, it works, and its benefits outweigh its risks. At that point, however, it is not available to most Australians. Only patients who can pay the full commercial price out of pocket — or who qualify for a manufacturer's compassionate access programme — can get it. Listing on the PBS, which makes a medicine affordable for the remaining 99%, requires a separate, subsequent, and entirely independent process.

That process — sponsored dossier to PBAC, multiple-month assessment cycle, pricing negotiation, Cabinet approval, legislative instrument — was designed for a world of small-molecule blockbusters with well-understood mechanisms, clean randomised trial data, and years of post-marketing experience. It fits poorly with the speed of modern oncology, gene therapies, and targeted biologics, where evidence matures over time and patients cannot wait two years while data accumulate.

The tension is well-established: Australia's safety and cost-effectiveness filters are genuinely world-class, but the delay between "proven safe and effective" and "available at an affordable price" is among the longest in the OECD. And unlike the MBS fee problem — which is chronic and structural — this one has a set of proven international solutions that Australia has been debating, but not yet adopting, for a decade.

## Key Findings

### 1. The Scale of the Access Gap

The **Australian Patient Access Gap Report (2024)**, which tracked every TGA-approved novel medicine from January 2021 to April 2024, found:

- **591 days**: average time from TGA approval to PBS listing for novel medicines that were eventually listed
- **>80%** of cost-effectiveness PBAC submissions required multiple attempts before a positive recommendation
- **638 days vs 488 days**: average time for cost-effectiveness vs cost-minimisation (generic/biosimilar) applications — the clinical innovation that deserves fastest access takes longest
- **>40 medicines** with a positive PBAC recommendation were still waiting for government listing more than **1,000 days** post-recommendation, caught in pricing negotiations or administrative queues

The international context is damning. The OECD average time from regulatory approval to reimbursement is approximately **384 days** across its 20 assessed members. Australia's 466-day average (the broader metric including all applications) places it well behind:

| Country | Avg. days from regulatory approval to reimbursement |
|---------|------------------------------------------------------|
| Japan | ~102 days |
| Germany | ~136 days |
| United Kingdom | ~156 days |
| Sweden | ~325 days |
| **OECD average (20 countries)** | **~384 days** |
| **Australia** | **~466 days (general); 591 days (novel cost-effectiveness submissions)** |

(Sources: Medicines Australia Access to Medicines Report; Australian Patient Access Gap Report 2024)

### 2. Why Delays Happen: The PBAC Bottleneck and the Pricing Overhang

Three structural bottlenecks drive the delay:

**A. PBAC meeting frequency.** PBAC meets only **3–6 times per year**. A failed submission may wait six months for a rehearing. In March 2025, the committee received an unprecedented backlog of more than 50 major submissions, forcing Health Minister Mark Butler to convene an **additional PBAC meeting in May 2025** — the first time in recent memory the government has had to intervene operationally to clear a queue. This was a stopgap, not a structural fix.

**B. Post-recommendation pricing negotiations.** A positive PBAC recommendation does not trigger automatic listing. It begins a further round of confidential pricing negotiations between the Department of Health and the manufacturer — negotiations that can drag on for years for high-cost medicines where the government seeks confidential rebates to bring the effective ICER below an acceptable threshold. This is the dominant driver of the 1,000-day post-recommendation delays.

**C. Back-end administrative processing.** Even after a price is agreed, listing via legislative instrument, Cabinet approval, and gazetting adds further weeks.

### 3. Australia's Existing Mechanism: The Managed Entry Scheme

Australia is not without precedent for interim or conditional reimbursement. Since **January 2011**, the PBAC has been empowered to recommend PBS listing under a **Managed Entry Scheme (MES)** — a framework that allows early coverage where:

- There is high unmet clinical need for the proposed medicine
- New clinical data would resolve current uncertainties around cost-effectiveness
- A PBAC-recommended price is justified by existing evidence, pending further data

Under MES, the most rigorous form is **Coverage with Evidence Development (CED)**: the government funds access while requiring the manufacturer to conduct a prospective registry or trial to confirm the medicine's real-world performance.

The most prominent Australian CED example was **ipilimumab (Yervoy) for metastatic melanoma**, listed in 2014 under a MES requiring the manufacturer to collect two-year overall survival data. The trial showed survival rates of 23.9%–34.2%, exceeding the 23.5% observed in the key registration trial — the CED confirmed efficacy and the listing was maintained.

However, MES adoption has been limited in practice. A review of MES arrangements found that by early 2013, **none of the 71 medicines with agreements in place** were operating an active CED scheme — all were using financial risk-sharing mechanisms instead (outcome-based rebates, volume caps). The evidence generation aspiration of CED has largely given way to commercial risk management. (Vitry, Pharmacoeconomics, 2018; ISPOR AP 2018)

### 4. The HTA Review and the Bridging Fund Proposal

The **Health Technology Assessment Policy and Methods Review** was the Albanese Government's most comprehensive examination of Australia's medicine assessment system in more than 30 years. Announced in late 2022, its **final report was published in September 2024** with 50 recommendations.

Two recommendations are directly relevant to interim access:

**Recommendation 8 — Enhanced unmet-need pathway:** Enhance or replace the existing early/facilitated resolution pathway with a more flexible mechanism for submissions of therapies with *high added therapeutic value* in areas of *unmet clinical need*. This would streamline PBAC assessment for these medicines and reduce unnecessary resubmission cycles.

**Recommendation 20 — Bridging Fund:** Establish a dedicated funding pool to provide *temporary, subsidised access* to eligible therapies **at the time of TGA registration**, running in parallel with the PBAC assessment process. Key design features proposed:

- Eligibility limited to TGA-approved medicines with **high added therapeutic value** and **high unmet clinical need**
- Sponsor must have lodged (or commit to lodging) a PBAC reimbursement application
- Access runs in parallel to PBAC assessment — patients are not waiting
- Contains **contingency arrangements** so that if reimbursement is ultimately not agreed, treated patients are not abruptly cut off (a critical patient welfare safeguard)
- Duration limited; not an open-ended entitlement

The **Bridging Funding Coalition** — a cross-sector alliance of patient advocacy organisations, oncology societies, and industry — has aligned its consensus position with Recommendation 20, and has been actively lobbying for its implementation ahead of the 2026–27 Budget cycle.

**Current status (as at June 2026):** The government established an **Implementation Advisory Group (IAG)** in November 2024 to develop an implementation roadmap. The IAG published an interim report in mid-2025 and communiques through October 2025, identifying five focus areas including "modernised assessment pathways." A final IAG report was targeted for January 2026, with a reform package submitted for **2026–27 Budget consideration**. No Bridging Fund has been funded or legislated as at the date of this report.

### 5. International Models: What Already Works

Three comparable systems offer proven models that Australia could adapt:

#### UK: NICE Cancer Drugs Fund

The **Cancer Drugs Fund (CDF)** is a managed access fund specifically for oncology medicines that NICE assesses as potentially meeting cost-effectiveness criteria but requiring further real-world evidence. How it works:

1. NICE grants **conditional approval** — a medicine enters the CDF for up to **two years**
2. During that time, **all eligible patients access the medicine via the NHS**
3. Real-world data are collected through the National Disease Registration Service
4. After two years, NICE conducts an accelerated reappraisal using the accumulated evidence
5. If confirmed cost-effective: **routine NHS commissioning** (permanent listing)
6. If not confirmed: individual funding requests only (medicine exits CDF)

Outcomes since 2016: of **39 MAA treatments** that have gone through re-appraisal, **34 (87.2%)** were recommended for routine commissioning — the conditional period overwhelmingly results in permanence. This suggests the system is identifying the right medicines for early access.

#### Canada: Time-Limited Reimbursement (TLR) and Temporary Access Process (pTAP)

Canada's Drug Agency (CDA-AMC) introduced its **Time-Limited Reimbursement (TLR)** recommendation in **November 2023** — the first formal early access pathway in Canadian HTA. It targets drugs undergoing Health Canada's Notice of Compliance with Conditions (NOC/c) process.

Key innovation: the **pan-Canadian Pharmaceutical Alliance (pCPA)** price negotiation runs **in parallel** to the HTA review, rather than sequentially after it. This parallel processing is the single biggest time-saver.

Real-world result — **epcoritamab (EPKINLY)** for advanced lymphoma:
- First TLR recommendation: 18 June 2024
- pTAP negotiation completed: 19 July 2024
- Total TLR-pTAP time: **248 days** (vs 568-day standard pathway average)
- Patient access advantage: **10.7 months faster** than the standard process
- Listed in nine provinces by 1 November 2024

Current limitation: strict eligibility criteria mean very few medicines currently qualify. Researchers advocate for expanding eligibility criteria to capture a broader range of promising therapies (Expanding the eligibility criteria, Frontiers, 2024).

#### France: Accès Précoce (AAP)

France's **Accès Précoce** scheme (formerly ATU, reformed by legislation in 2021) allows medicine access **before** or **immediately after** marketing authorisation:

- **Pre-MA access (AP1)**: before regulatory approval, for serious conditions with no satisfactory treatment alternative
- **Post-MA access (AP2)**: immediately after regulatory approval, where the medicine has presumed high added therapeutic value
- Review time: **maximum 3 months** from dossier completeness to decision
- Duration: up to **1 year**, renewable until price negotiation concludes
- Manufacturers **must make the product available within 2 months** of approval and **must reimburse the state** if the final reimbursement decision or price is less favourable than during the early access period

The cost-recovery mechanism (manufacturer rebates the difference if the final listing price is lower than the early access price) addresses a key moral hazard: manufacturers have an incentive to set the early access price at what they think the final negotiated price will be.

## Tensions and Open Questions

**1. Moral hazard and gaming:** If access is guaranteed before PBAC assessment, do manufacturers lose the incentive to submit cost-effective dossiers? France's retroactive rebate mechanism addresses this for pricing, but evidence quality is harder to enforce. The design of eligibility criteria — particularly the "high added therapeutic value" threshold — is critical.

**2. Who decides "high unmet need"?** The HTA Review recommendations rely heavily on this concept, but Australia does not have a formal, transparent process for designating conditions as having unmet clinical need. Deciding which diseases qualify for a bridging pathway (and which don't) will be politically contested. Oncology patients have the loudest advocacy organisations; rare disease patients have fewer resources.

**3. Fiscal exposure:** The UK CDF has periodically exceeded its annual budget cap, leading to access interruptions and retroactive eligibility tightening. Australia's Bridging Fund would need robust budget management — and the government has not yet provided any costing.

**4. The pricing negotiation tail:** Even if a Bridging Fund is established, the 1,000-day post-PBAC-recommendation delays are caused by *pricing negotiations*, not by the assessment process. The Bridging Fund proposal does not directly address this. Australia may also need to streamline post-recommendation pricing and listing — perhaps by strengthening time limits on negotiations or introducing automatic listing triggers.

**5. Non-oncology access:** Both the CDF and TLR are heavily skewed toward cancer medicines. Patients with rare diseases, neurological conditions, and other non-cancer serious illnesses with similarly unmet needs would benefit from equivalent pathways that do not favour oncology by design.

**6. Budget 2026–27: timing risk.** If the 2026–27 Budget does not include Bridging Fund funding, the reform window may close. Australia has reviewed this problem before — the HTA system has not been comprehensively reformed in 30 years — and reviews that don't convert to legislation are wasted work.
