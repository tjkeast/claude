# HTA Reform and Australia's Persistent Access Crisis: How a Five-Year Review Produced an Empty Budget

**Report 022 — 2026-06-28**

**Summary:** Australia's Health Technology Assessment (HTA) system — which governs how new medicines and medical technologies are evaluated for public funding — has been under formal review since 2021. The September 2024 final report produced 50 expert recommendations. An Implementation Advisory Group (IAG) delivered its roadmap to the government in January 2026. The 2026-27 Federal Budget, handed down in May 2026, contained no funded plan to implement those reforms. Australia now ranks 16th among OECD countries for medicines access, patients wait an average of 3.6 years for treatments already available overseas, and pharmaceutical companies are explicitly warning they will stop submitting PBS applications altogether.

**Why this follows from yesterday:** Report 021 documented the aged care system's structural funding gaps and its $3.7 billion Budget commitment — an area where the government did invest. HTA reform represents the inverse case: a domain where years of expert consensus, industry advocacy, and patient pressure produced near-zero Budget action, even as external pressures (US MFN pricing, reported in Report 018) compound the urgency.

---

## Background

Australia's HTA system sits at the intersection of nearly every thread this series has explored. The Pharmaceutical Benefits Advisory Committee (PBAC) and the Medical Services Advisory Committee (MSAC) are the two bodies responsible for cost-effectiveness assessment of medicines and medical technologies respectively. Without a positive PBAC recommendation, a medicine cannot be listed on the PBS; without a positive MSAC recommendation, a medical service cannot be funded via Medicare's MBS. These committees are the gatekeepers to Australia's $19.1 billion PBS and the broader MBS funding system.

The HTA system has been criticised since at least the 2021 **New Frontiers** report, which identified that Australia's PBS methods, policies, and processes were outdated and that patients were waiting too long for new, innovative treatments. For five years, the same problems have been repeatedly acknowledged and not fixed.

The **HTA Policy and Methods Review** — Australia's first comprehensive HTA review in 30 years — concluded on 4 May 2024. The Reference Committee provided its final report to the Minister for Health and Aged Care on 31 May 2024; it was publicly released in September 2024. The review produced **50 recommendations** spanning access, equity, transparency, assessment pathways, data quality, and workforce capacity.

## Key Findings

### 1. The 50 Recommendations and Five Focus Areas

The HTA Review Reference Committee organised its recommendations into five implementation focus areas:

1. **Improved access and equity** — including better pathways for rare diseases, First Nations populations, and paediatric populations
2. **Greater transparency and engagement** — clearer PBAC/MSAC processes; consumer involvement; published reasoning
3. **Modernised assessment pathways** — streamlined evaluation for breakthrough therapies; pan-tumour cancer applications; real-world evidence frameworks
4. **Better data and evidence** — strengthening post-market data collection; outcomes-based reimbursement mechanisms
5. **Building HTA workforce capacity and capability** — PBAC/MSAC secretariat resourcing; independent expertise

Three recommendations drew the most attention from industry and patient groups:

- **Recommendation 8**: An enhanced/flexible unmet-need pathway for high-value therapies facing data uncertainty
- **Recommendation 20**: A **Bridging Fund** — temporary subsidised access at TGA registration for medicines meeting criteria for high added therapeutic value or high unmet clinical need, running in parallel with PBAC assessment
- **Recommendation on discount rate**: Implicit throughout the methods recommendations — Australia's PBAC uses a **5% discount rate** for cost-effectiveness modelling, which systematically disadvantages therapies with upfront costs and long-term benefits (especially gene therapies and preventive vaccines)

### 2. The Implementation Advisory Group: Work in Progress, Then Silence

The Australian Government established the **Health Technology Assessment Review Implementation Advisory Group** (IAG), with members announced on 20 November 2024. The IAG's mandate was to develop a prioritisation roadmap and advise government on sequencing implementation.

The IAG published an **Interim Report on 3 September 2025**, detailing progress across the five focus areas, proposing pilot programs for streamlined submission processes, flagging the need for an HTA workforce study, and recommending establishment of a **First Nations Advisory Committee** to contribute to HTA decision-making.

The IAG's **final report and implementation roadmap** were due to be delivered to the government in **January 2026**. The report was delivered — but as of mid-2026, it has not been publicly released. Industry observers have noted its effective disappearance; one trade publication described the situation as the final report having been "given to the government in January 2026 but not seen since."

### 3. The 2026-27 Budget: No Funded Reform Plan

Medicines Australia's post-Budget verdict was unambiguous: the 2026-27 Federal Budget contained **"no meaningful funding commitment or progress on implementation of HTA reform."**

The Budget's headline PBS figure — **$5.9 billion over five years** for new and amended PBS listings — reflects standard-cycle new listings of approved medicines, not HTA reform investment. The structural problems that slow access and distort cost-effectiveness assessment received no dedicated funding.

The one substantive HTA-adjacent action the government did announce was a **rolling review of PBAC guidelines**, beginning with comparator selection and discount rate as the first areas. This was described as an "immediate action" — but a guidelines review process, while necessary, is not the same as funded systemic reform. The Bridging Fund (Recommendation 20) remains in "design" phase with an Expert Implementation Group working on criteria; no funding commitment was made in the Budget.

PBS investment as a proportion of GDP is **declining over the forward estimates** — a structural signal that the government is not planning increased commitment to access.

### 4. The Discount Rate: A Specific Bottleneck for Modern Medicines

The PBAC's **5% base-case discount rate** has become one of the most intensively debated technical parameters in Australian health policy. The logic of discounting is that a future health benefit is worth less than a present one (analogous to the time value of money); at 5%, long-horizon benefits are heavily penalised.

For gene therapies and other treatments that deliver upfront investment against a lifetime of avoided disease, a 5% discount rate produces structurally unfavourable cost-effectiveness ratios. This matters because, as documented in Report 006, Australia publicly funds four CAR-T therapies and two gene therapies (Zolgensma for SMA, Luxturna for inherited retinal dystrophy) — but the pipeline of similar therapies is stalling at assessment.

International comparators:
- **NICE (UK)**: 3.5%
- **Canada**: 1.5%
- **France**: 2.5%
- **New Zealand (PHARMAC)**: 3.5%

The PBAC's own advice to government, following the 2022 consultation process, was that the base-case discount rate could be reduced to **3.5%–4%** — bringing it closer to international norms — "should the Government make a broader policy decision." The government has not made that policy decision. Medicines Australia has argued for a rate as low as 1.5%, consistent with Canada.

The ATAGI interaction with PBAC processes adds a further structural bottleneck: the standard 17-week PBAC HTA cycle becomes approximately **48 weeks** when ATAGI (Australian Technical Advisory Group on Immunisation) review requirements are incorporated for vaccines. The IAG interim report proposed pilot programs to streamline these processes, but no pilots have been funded.

### 5. The Access Crisis by Numbers

The Medicines Australia **"Access Denied"** report (2026) provides the most comprehensive available benchmarking of Australia's access position:

- Australia ranks **16th among OECD countries** for access to new medicines
- Patients wait an average of **3.6 years** for access to first-in-class treatments already available in comparable countries
- Only approximately **1 in 4** (25%) new medicines available in OECD countries are funded through the PBS
- 65% of surveyed medical specialists want Australia to rank in the **top 5 OECD** for reimbursed medicines access
- The 2021 New Frontiers report raised the same access-delay concerns — **five years of acknowledged problems without structural resolution**

### 6. Industry: "Drug Makers Are Walking Away"

The access-delay problem is not merely a patient advocacy concern. Multiple pharmaceutical companies have begun citing the PBS environment as a factor in global launch sequencing decisions. The Limbic's 2026 analysis of the PBS listing crisis quoted **Eli Lilly** stating that "ongoing challenges in the reimbursement environment are a critical factor when assessing not only the viability of future PBS submissions, but whether Australia can support the full innovation pathway."

The consequence is not primarily that PBS prices are forced higher — the government has correctly resisted that. The consequence is **market de-prioritisation**: Australia falls lower in global launch queues, PBAC submissions are simply not lodged, and patients miss therapies that patients in comparable countries already receive.

This compounds the MFN pricing chilling effect documented in Report 018: US pharmaceutical companies are already motivated to avoid PBS listing (which creates a low reference price that could become the US MFN benchmark); the slow, uncertain HTA pathway provides a second independent motivation to disengage with Australia.

### 7. The Strategic Agreement Deadline

The Commonwealth's **Strategic Agreements with Medicines Australia and the GBMA** — which govern industry–government PBS relations — run to **30 June 2027**. These agreements included a commitment to HTA reform implementation (targeting July 2024, a deadline already missed). Renegotiation of the strategic agreements will begin within the next 12 months. If the HTA IAG roadmap remains unimplemented when those negotiations open, the government will have diminished leverage to maintain industry commitment to the PBS framework and limited credibility on access reform claims.

## Tensions and Open Questions

**1. The "review of reviews" problem.** Australia has now had the 2021 New Frontiers review, the 2024 Policy and Methods Review, and an Implementation Advisory Group — three rounds of structured analysis producing cumulative recommendations that remain largely unimplemented. At some point, further process fails to substitute for funded change.

**2. What is the IAG final report?** The report delivered in January 2026 has not been released publicly. This is unusual for major policy documents in a system with active stakeholder engagement. Whether the report recommends more specific or more radical reforms than the interim report — and why it has been withheld — is unknown.

**3. The discount rate and the gene therapy era.** Australia publicly funds Zolgensma and Luxturna, but the pipeline of gene and cell therapies globally is expanding rapidly. If the discount rate is not revised before the next wave of gene therapy PBAC submissions (haemophilia B, expanded CAR-T indications, RNA-based therapies), cost-effectiveness assessments will continue to produce unfavourable results for structurally sound therapies.

**4. The Bridging Fund's design problem.** Recommendation 20 is conceptually widely supported — providing access during PBAC assessment rather than adding access delays in parallel. But the design work is ongoing and no funding commitment has been made. The longer it takes to design, the more this mirrors the biosimilar uptake story from Report 005: good policy, slow execution, avoidable patient harm in the interim.

**5. PBAC/MSAC boundary.** Report 006 documented the CAR-T vs Zolgensma boundary dispute: similar products (cellular and gene therapies) assessed by different committees under different frameworks producing inconsistent outcomes. The HTA Review recommended clarifying this boundary, but no implementation has been announced.

1. **Aged care workforce pipeline** — the specific training, migration, and pay pathways for personal care workers and nurses in aged care; how Fair Work wage decisions interact with AN-ACC pricing; whether care minutes mandate can be met in rural and remote thin markets (Report 021 thread)

2. **Residential aged care market structure** — the shift from not-for-profit to for-profit provider majority; how RAD retention, accommodation supplement, and care minutes interact to shape market incentives; whether Budget 2026-27 bed construction incentives reach underserved areas

3. **Dementia and the hospital-to-aged-care boundary** — dementia is the leading driver of residential aged care demand; the $224.3M hospital-to-aged-care dementia pathway is an emerging policy focus; overlaps with hospital avoidance and sub-acute care funding

4. **Specialist training pipeline** — medical college training number controls across oncology, haematology, and psychiatry; how supply restrictions interact with HTA reform (if access to medicines improves but specialist capacity to prescribe doesn't, access remains constrained)

5. **Outcomes-based reimbursement / managed entry** — Australia has a managed entry framework (Managed Entry Scheme, since 2011) but it is rarely used; HTA reform recommendation for outcomes-based payment models; annuity payment structures for gene therapies as alternative to lump-sum PBAC assessment
