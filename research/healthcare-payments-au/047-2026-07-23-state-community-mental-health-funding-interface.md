# State-Funded Community Mental Health Services: Australia's Invisible System and the Commonwealth-State Funding Fault Line

**Report 047 — 2026-07-23**

**Summary:** While Medicare-funded mental health services (Better Access, headspace, Medicare Mental Health Centres) are visible in national data, the much larger state-funded community mental health system — Crisis Assessment and Treatment teams, Community Mental Health Teams, community mental health nursing, and psychosocial rehabilitation services — operates largely below the radar of Commonwealth health policy. This $3.5 billion-per-year sector serves nearly 500,000 Australians annually through 9.8 million service contacts, yet it is structurally underfunded in most jurisdictions, its workforce is undersupplied by nearly a fifth, and the key symptom of its failure — mounting mental health presentations to emergency departments — is worsening. The National Mental Health and Suicide Prevention Agreement that was meant to coordinate Commonwealth-state effort expired in June 2026 with the Productivity Commission declaring it "not fit for purpose," and has been extended for only one year while governments deliberate on what comes next.

**Why this follows from yesterday:** Report 046 documented how the stimulant prescribing bottleneck for ADHD forces patients through psychiatrists in states that haven't enacted GP reform — but once a patient is diagnosed, their ongoing care coordination, monitoring of comorbidities, and crisis management typically fall to state-funded community mental health services that this series has not yet examined directly. Understanding who provides community mental health care between MBS appointments is essential to understanding the full architecture of mental health access in Australia.

---

## Background

Australia's mental health system has a fundamental structural division that shapes almost everything about how care is delivered:

**The Commonwealth funds access:** Medicare Benefits Schedule (MBS) items for GP mental health treatment plans, psychology sessions (Better Access), psychiatrist consultations, headspace block grants through Primary Health Networks (PHNs), and the newly expanding Medicare Mental Health Centres (MMHCs). These are the fee-for-service and block-grant programs visible in Commonwealth data.

**The states and territories fund treatment:** public inpatient psychiatric beds, Emergency Department (ED) psychiatric liaison, Community Mental Health Teams (CMHTs), Crisis Assessment and Treatment (CAT) teams, Acute Community Intervention Services (ACIS), community mental health nursing, psychosocial rehabilitation programs, community residential care facilities, and public sector consultant psychiatry. These are the services that sit between MBS-funded GP and psychology visits and hospital admission — and they are largely invisible in Commonwealth data.

The division was formalised through a succession of National Mental Health Plans beginning in 1992 and has been partially governed through the National Mental Health and Suicide Prevention Agreement (NMHSPA) signed by all governments. In practice, it has produced a system where the Commonwealth funds what is measurable (MBS claims), states fund what is essential but hard to measure (community clinical care), and people with the most severe mental illness fall through the gap between the two.

### What State Community Mental Health Services Actually Do

State-funded **Community Mental Health Teams (CMHTs)** are multidisciplinary teams — typically comprising psychiatrists, psychiatric registrars, mental health nurses, social workers, occupational therapists, and psychologists — that provide:
- Assessment and diagnosis for people with severe and complex mental illness
- Ongoing community treatment and medication management for conditions like schizophrenia, bipolar disorder, and severe depression
- Care planning and coordination
- Carer and family support
- Mental health reviews (including medication reviews under Schedule 8 permits)
- Early intervention in deteriorating presentations

**Crisis Assessment and Treatment (CAT) teams** — known by different names in different states (e.g., ACIS in SA, CATT in Victoria) — provide after-hours and emergency community response to acute mental health crises, including home assessments, diversion from ED, and brief crisis support to prevent inpatient admission.

These are not optional supplements to the MBS system. For Australians with schizophrenia, severe bipolar disorder, treatment-resistant depression, or other serious mental illnesses, CMHT and CAT team contact is often the primary form of clinical care — far more frequent and intensive than the 10 MBS-funded psychology sessions per year that Better Access provides for milder conditions.

## Key Findings

### 1. The Financial Scale: $13.8 Billion Total; States Carry the Majority

In 2023–24, national recurrent expenditure on mental health-related services reached **$13.8 billion**, or approximately **$537 per capita** — up from $466 per capita in 2014–15. This is the total across all service types and funding sources.

The funding split by level of government:

| Funding source | Amount | Share |
|----------------|--------|-------|
| State and territory governments (incl. Commonwealth NHRA funding) | $9.0 billion | 65.3% |
| Australian Government (MBS, PBS, PHN grants, specific programs) | $4.8 billion | 34.7% |

Within state and territory expenditure on **specialised mental health services**, the two largest components are:
- **Admitted patient (inpatient) services:** $3.8 billion (41.3%)
- **Community-based ambulatory services:** $3.5 billion (38.6%)
- Residential care and non-acute hospital services make up the remainder

The community ambulatory figure — $3.5 billion — is the state-funded layer this report examines. It represents the core of what people with severe mental illness receive outside hospital.

Per-capita state and territory spending on specialised mental health services ranged significantly across jurisdictions in 2022–23:
- **NSW:** $276 per capita (lowest)
- **WA:** $387 per capita (highest)
- National average: approximately $330 per capita

NSW, Australia's most populous state, spends less per capita on specialised mental health than any other jurisdiction — a finding that underpins the most detailed documented case of community mental health system failure in the country.

### 2. Community Mental Health Service Activity: 9.8 Million Contacts, 489,000 Consumers

In 2023–24, Australia's state-funded community mental health care services delivered:
- **9.8 million service contacts** (up from 9.7 million in 2022–23)
- to nearly **489,000 consumers**
- at an average of approximately **20 contacts per consumer per year**

These contacts include face-to-face clinical appointments, group programs, home visits, telephone check-ins, and case coordination. The 489,000 consumers represent people with conditions serious enough to warrant ongoing contact with state mental health services — predominantly those with psychotic disorders, severe mood disorders, and complex presentations that exceed what the MBS-funded private sector manages.

The average cost per **community mental health treatment day** has ranged from $410 to $505 (adjusted for inflation) since 2005–06 — substantially cheaper than the average public psychiatric inpatient bed cost of **$1,665 per day** in 2023–24. This cost differential is the economic rationale for community mental health investment: a well-resourced community service system prevents more expensive inpatient episodes and ED crises.

### 3. The 1992 Deinstitutionalisation Promise — Half-Kept

Australia's current community mental health architecture was shaped by a major reform commitment made in 1992. The **National Mental Health Policy** set out an agenda of deinstitutionalisation: closing large state psychiatric institutions and replacing them with community-based care and smaller general hospital psychiatric units.

The first half of the plan was executed. By the late 1990s:
- Victoria closed all **14 state psychiatric institutions** between 1994 and 1999
- Nationally, stand-alone psychiatric institutions fell from **49% of total mental health resources** at the start of the reform period to **29% by 1998**
- Total institutional beds fell by 42%
- A larger proportion of remaining acute beds moved into general hospital settings (from 55% to 73% of acute beds in general hospital psychiatric units)

The second half — building the community infrastructure to absorb the people discharged from those institutions — was never fully funded. Savings from institutional closures were not entirely reinvested in community services, and the area-based community teams that were meant to provide mobile, extended-hours care never received the staffing levels the reform design assumed.

More than 30 years later, the consequences of this incomplete transition persist. Researchers have described Australia's mental health reform history as one of "positive rhetoric and benevolent policy intent [that] has often failed to translate into practical improvements in the experiences of mental healthcare felt by patients" (Cornell et al., 2025, *Australasian Psychiatry*).

The national academic literature confirms **relative stasis in community mental health capacity between 2017–18 and 2021–22** — a period of increasing demand, with community contacts growing while real per-capita expenditure on community services stagnated.

### 4. The NSW Case Study: The Lowest-Resourced Large State System

The most detailed public documentation of a state community mental health gap exists for NSW, through a **priority issues gap analysis** commissioned by NSW Health and released publicly in 2025.

Key findings:

- **Staffing per capita decline:** Community mental health FTE per 100,000 population fell from **54.1 to 48.9** in NSW over the past decade — the lowest rate among all states and territories
- **Funding per capita:** NSW spent the second lowest amount on community mental health per capita among all states and territories in 2020–21, approximately **$24 million below the national average** annually
- **Demand-funding mismatch:** Community mental health patient numbers in NSW increased by **21%** over the past decade, but funding for those services grew by only **13%** — a sustained real-terms cut per patient
- **Severity gap:** A projected **29% shortfall** in service capacity for people with severe and complex mental health needs — equating to an estimated **58,000 people** in NSW who cannot access the care they need
- **Psychiatrist shortage:** NSW faces a projected shortage of **74 FTE psychiatrists in 2025**, rising to **124 FTE by 2030**, with the public community mental health system most exposed

The NSW Government's 2026 Budget allocated **$30.4 million** to expand Community Mental Health Teams across targeted areas including regional NSW, providing approximately 35 additional mental health positions — a modest response to a structural deficit documented in tens of thousands of cases of unmet need.

The Black Dog Institute, RANZCP NSW Branch, and other organisations issued a joint statement in 2025 warning that community mental health gaps in NSW are "continuing to widen" despite recognition of the problem.

### 5. The Emergency Department as the System's Failure Metric

When community mental health services are unable to meet demand, the alternative access point is the hospital Emergency Department. In Australia, this has become a documented and worsening failure mode:

- Mental health-related ED presentations made up **approximately 3% of all Australian ED presentations** in 2022–23 — equating to roughly **270,000 presentations per year** based on total ED activity of approximately 9.1 million
- Mental health ED presentations grew at an average annual rate of **2.8%** between 2016–17 and 2020–21
- One longitudinal study found a **156% increase** in mental health ED presentations over nine years — far outstripping the 11.9% increase in total ED presentations over the same period
- Between 2014–15 and 2023–24, the proportion of mental health ED presentations triaged as **Urgent or higher** increased across all states and territories — a sign that presentations arriving at EDs are more acute, not less
- The NSW figure is particularly striking: **18%** of mental health ED presentations in NSW are now triaged at the highest urgency levels

EDs are costly, clinically inappropriate environments for mental health crisis management, and they divert psychiatry liaison capacity from non-crisis care. RANZCP has repeatedly described community services as "chronically underfunded" with EDs functioning as "the default entry point for people in mental health crisis" — a statement that accurately describes the structural outcome of four decades of under-investment in community mental health.

The RACGP confirmed in 2024 that mental health patients now make up **3.6% of all Australian ED presentations**, with those presentations lasting significantly longer than average ED stays due to the complexity of psychiatric assessment and the shortage of inpatient beds to transfer to.

### 6. The National Mental Health and Suicide Prevention Agreement: "Not Fit for Purpose"

The principal mechanism for Commonwealth-state coordination of mental health has been the **National Mental Health and Suicide Prevention Agreement (NMHSPA)**, signed by all governments in 2022 and running to 30 June 2026.

The Agreement established:
- Joint Commonwealth-state responsibilities for mental health workforce planning, training and accreditation
- Commitments to mental health promotion, prevention, early intervention, and social and emotional wellbeing
- Suicide prevention and stigma reduction as shared objectives
- A framework for state-Commonwealth co-investment in psychosocial supports

The Productivity Commission was commissioned to review the Agreement. Its **final report**, delivered to parties on 17 October 2025 and publicly released on **11 November 2025**, contained a damning assessment:

> *"The mental health and suicide prevention system is fragmented and out of reach for many people. The actions in the Agreement do not advance system reform. Consumers, carers and providers report services remain uncoordinated, unaffordable and difficult to navigate."*

The Commission made **24 recommendations**, with the most urgent directed at the **psychosocial supports gap** — estimating that approximately **500,000 Australians** with moderate-to-severe mental illness have no access to adequate psychosocial support (a figure directly cited in Report 017 of this series). The PC recommended that state and territory governments "immediately prioritise" addressing this gap, with funding arrangements for psychosocial supports to be included in any new national agreement.

The Commission's assessment that the Agreement was "not fit for purpose" meant that its 2022 architecture could not simply be renewed. The 2026–27 Budget response was to allocate **$206.8 million** to extend the existing NMHSPA to **30 June 2027** — a one-year bridging arrangement — while governments begin co-designing a new policy architecture.

The National Mental Health Commission welcomed the continuity but said "a clearer pathway is needed for long-term mental health reform" — diplomatic language for the view that one year of bridging without structural reform is insufficient.

### 7. Victoria's Royal Commission: The Most Ambitious Reform, With Stalling Implementation

Victoria launched the most comprehensive state-level mental health reform in Australian history following the **Royal Commission into Victoria's Mental Health System** (final report, 2021). The Commission made **65 recommendations** (plus 9 interim) calling for a wholesale restructuring of the Victorian mental health system — including a major expansion of community services, an end to the practice of people in crisis being turned away from the system, and sustained investment in rehabilitation and housing.

Funding was to be supported by a dedicated **Mental Health and Wellbeing Levy** on payroll, specifically hypothecated for mental health — a novel funding mechanism in Australian health policy.

Key components and status as of mid-2026:
- The **Community Mental Health Expansion Program** ($33.4 million) has been funded to expand community service capacity and direct care away from inpatient settings
- Victoria has established new **Mental Health and Wellbeing Local services** (the renamed area mental health teams) as the primary community access point — a recommissioning of the existing CMHT structure
- However, the RANZCP Victorian Branch noted in 2025–26 that **critical components of the Royal Commission's reform agenda — including rehabilitation, housing, trauma-informed care, and accessible community services — remain underfunded or absent**
- The RANZCP described the 2025–26 state budget as delivering "key mental health funding, but psychiatrists warn reform may be stalling"

Victoria's model — particularly its use of a dedicated hypothecated funding mechanism — represents a fundamentally different approach to mental health investment compared to other states, where mental health competes for funding within general hospital budget processes. Whether Victoria's reform achieves its community service goals will determine whether it provides a replicable template for other jurisdictions.

### 8. The 2026–27 Federal Budget Response: $7.8 Billion — But Which Services?

The 2026–27 Commonwealth Budget invested approximately **$7.8 billion** in mental health and suicide prevention, including a pre-announced **$1.1 billion election commitment** to expand free mental health services (principally the Medicare Mental Health Centres model and related initiatives announced in May 2026).

What the 2026–27 Budget did not do:
- Fund a structural reform of the Commonwealth-state mental health interface
- Respond substantively to the 24 Productivity Commission recommendations beyond the Agreement extension
- Fund the scale of psychosocial support investment the PC described as necessary for 500,000 people
- Establish a dedicated community mental health capital fund for states with demonstrable service gaps (notably NSW)

Mental Health Australia described the 2026–27 Budget as providing "important continuity" but said "a clearer pathway is needed for long-term mental health reform." The Mental Health Coordinating Council called for urgent investment ahead of the 2026–27 NSW State Budget, estimating that **more than 200,000 people in NSW** require psychosocial supports they cannot currently access.

## Tensions and Open Questions

**1. The measurement problem: what you can't see, you can't fund.** Commonwealth health data is built around Medicare claims — a transactional record of fee-for-service encounters. State community mental health services don't generate this data. AIHW's Community Mental Health Care national data collection captures service contacts, but not outcomes, not severity of presentations prevented, and not the counterfactual (what happens to people when those services aren't available). Without a shared data language, the argument that community investment prevents more expensive ED and inpatient episodes — while economically compelling in theory — is hard to make in budget processes.

**2. The Commonwealth-state blame dynamic.** When community mental health services fail, the Commonwealth points to the states (it's their responsibility), and the states point to the Commonwealth (it underfunds the sector through inadequate NHRA contributions and MBS-centric policy). The 2022 NMHSPA was designed to bridge this — and the PC's 2025 verdict that it "does not advance system reform" suggests the bridging mechanism failed. Until there is a funding formula that makes both levels of government jointly accountable for community mental health outcomes — not just inputs — the dynamic will persist.

**3. The inpatient-to-community ratio: structural inertia.** Even within state mental health budgets, admitted-patient services ($3.8B) slightly exceed community ambulatory services ($3.5B) nationally. The 1992 reform aimed to shift this balance decisively toward community — and has not succeeded in 30 years. Inpatient psychiatric beds are politically and operationally easier to fund than dispersed community teams, and states face electoral accountability for acute crises that go wrong in hospital settings in ways they don't face for slow-motion community service failures.

**4. What happens after Better Access: the severity ceiling.** The Better Access program (10 MBS psychology sessions per year) was designed for mild-to-moderate conditions. People who need more — those with psychosis, severe personality disorders, complex PTSD — are typically referred into the state CMHT system. But as documented in Report 011, the mental health workforce shortage means the referring pathway is increasingly blocked at both ends: psychology waiting lists extend beyond the 10-session cap before it's exhausted, and CMHT intake criteria have tightened as demand exceeds capacity. The result is a growing population whose needs exceed what the MBS can provide but who cannot access state services.

**5. The Victoria hypothesis: does a Royal Commission actually fix a mental health system?** Victoria's Royal Commission was the most politically significant state-level commitment to mental health reform in Australian history. If its community service expansion delivers — and RANZCP's 2025–26 warnings suggest it may not, at least on schedule — the model argues for replication. If it stalls, it provides evidence that even hypothecated funding and a royal commission mandate cannot overcome the structural inertia of mental health deinstitutionalisation's unfinished business.

## Threads to Branch Into Next

1. **Aged care provider market exits and consolidation** — the most persistently deferred thread in this series (from Report 042 onwards); StewartBrown sector financial data shows thin-market rural providers at greatest risk of exit under the new Aged Care Act 2024 funding model; directly affects the state-Commonwealth interface for older Australians with mental illness transitioning from NDIS to aged care

2. **Victoria's Mental Health Levy and hypothecated health funding** — the Victorian model of a dedicated payroll levy for mental health represents a novel approach to health finance that bypasses Commonwealth-state bargaining; its implementation progress, hypothecation adequacy, and economic effects on employer costs deserve examination as a potential template

3. **NGO/community-managed organisations in mental health: the block grant sector** — approximately 600 mental health NGOs in Australia receive state and Commonwealth funding for community services, psychosocial programs, and peer support; this sector was decimated when the NDIS was launched and states defunded non-NDIS services; its current scale, funding conditions, and role in filling the psychosocial gap is inadequately understood in public health debate

4. **Accommodation supplement equity in aged care** — a thread deferred from Report 043; the Commonwealth means-tested accommodation supplement paid to providers when residents cannot fund a RAD or DAP; its adequacy in regional markets; whether rates reflect actual building/operating costs

5. **The Fifth National Mental Health and Suicide Prevention Plan (2021–2031): implementation failure** — endorsed 2017, due for implementation by 2022, the Fifth Plan is the national framework within which the failed NMHSPA sat; examining what was in the Plan, what was achieved, and what the Productivity Commission review means for the successor framework
