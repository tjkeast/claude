# The RAD Capital Pool: Australia's $48 Billion Quasi-Financial System Inside Aged Care

**Report 037 — 2026-07-13**

**Summary:** Australia's aged care sector holds an extraordinary and globally unusual financing arrangement: providers use residents' lump-sum accommodation deposits — now totalling $48.4 billion as of June 2025 — as interest-free operating capital. The Aged Care Act 2024 introduced the first comprehensive financial and prudential oversight framework for this pool, the government confirmed a conditional phase-out by 2035, and new retention rules from November 2025 have begun converting part of this "refundable" deposit into permanent provider revenue. The sustainability tension is acute: construction of new beds requires capital that only RAD inflows can provide, yet RADs are scheduled for abolition, and the sector's EBITDA per bed is less than one-third of what is needed to attract private investment.

**Why this follows from yesterday:** Report 036 examined how private health insurance intersects with residential aged care and found it essentially irrelevant to the primary financial pressure on residents — accommodation and care costs. The dominant financial instrument shaping those costs is the Refundable Accommodation Deposit (RAD), which drives provider capital structure, pricing strategy, and construction decisions. This report examines that instrument in full.

---

## Background

### What a RAD Is and How It Works

A **Refundable Accommodation Deposit (RAD)** is a lump-sum payment made by an aged care resident to a provider at the time of entry into a residential facility. The key structural features:

- **It is refundable** — in full — when the resident permanently leaves or dies (within 14 days of departure, with interest accruing from day 15)
- **The provider holds it as operating capital** during the resident's stay; there is no requirement to hold the funds in trust or a segregated account
- **It earns no return for the resident** — the provider simply has access to interest-free capital
- **The alternative** is the Daily Accommodation Payment (DAP): a daily fee equivalent to the interest on the RAD, calculated at the Maximum Permissible Interest Rate (MPIR)

The MPIR is set quarterly by the Minister and is based on the 90-day bank bill rate plus 2.27 percentage points. For Q2 2026 (1 April – 30 June 2026), the MPIR is **7.96% per annum**. A resident facing a $750,000 RAD who chose DAP instead would pay $750,000 × 7.96% ÷ 365 = **$163.56 per day** in accommodation costs — roughly $59,700 per year — without building any equity.

Most residents with assets choose the RAD because it preserves capital that is eventually returned. Residents without sufficient assets pay the DAP (or a government-subsidised accommodation supplement for those below the means-tested threshold). Residents can also split the payment — part RAD, part DAP — with the DAP reduced proportionally.

### From "Accommodation Bond" to RAD

The RAD's precursor was the **accommodation bond**, which was entirely non-refundable. The Living Longer Living Better reforms (2014) replaced bonds with the refundable RAD structure while retaining the mechanism of providers using resident capital. The shift was welfare-improving for residents but did not alter the fundamental economics: providers still hold billions of dollars of resident capital at zero interest cost.

### The Pool: Scale and Growth

The RAD/RAC pool has grown substantially:
- **AU$33.6 billion** (30 June 2021) — ACFA data
- **AU$42.2 billion** (30 June 2024) — Financial Report on the Australian Aged Care Sector 2023-24
- **AU$48.4 billion** (30 June 2025) — Financial Report on the Australian Aged Care Sector 2024-25

This $48.4 billion pool is one of the largest quasi-financial pools in the Australian economy. For comparison, the Future Fund held approximately $230 billion at June 2025; Australia's total superannuation savings are approximately $4.1 trillion. The RAD pool represents a comparable-scale concentration of household wealth transferred to the aged care sector, held without the investment protections applied to either superannuation or bank deposits (the latter is guaranteed only to $250,000 per institution; the RAD guarantee is unlimited and government-backed).

The average full RAD received during FY25 was **$516,770** (up 4.6% from $494,106 in FY24), per the Stewart Brown Aged Care Financial Performance Survey. This reflects the price cap lift to $750,000 from January 2025.

## Key Findings

### 1. The RAD as Capital Infrastructure Financing

Residential aged care construction is expensive — **$645,000 per bed on average** in early 2026 (up from $500,000 in 2024, with premium facilities quoting up to $1 million per bed). Traditional debt financing is difficult to access at scale: lenders typically cannot hold aged care beds as collateral in the conventional sense, and providers with operating EBITDA of around $7,000 per bed per year find it difficult to service construction debt at commercial rates.

The RAD solves this problem by providing zero-interest capital from incoming residents. For a 100-bed facility where 80% of residents pay a full RAD at $516,770, the provider holds approximately **$41.3 million** of interest-free capital — roughly the cost of construction. New residents continuously replace outgoing residents, maintaining the pool as a rolling capital base.

KPMG's 2026 Aged Care Market Analysis confirms this structural relationship: "accommodation services are still running at a loss" for most providers, and the RAD capital pool is what allows many providers to remain financially viable while cross-subsidising from care income. The KPMG report notes Australian Government investment rose to **$39.2 billion in FY25** (up 9.6% year-on-year), yet demand for places continues to outpace supply.

The 2025 Mirus Australia analysis found that 36% of aged care homes increased their RAD prices above $550,000 following the cap increase to $750,000 from January 2025 (then further indexed to $758,627 from 1 July 2025). This reflects providers proactively seeking to expand their capital pool as construction costs rise.

### 2. Governance Before and After the Aged Care Act 2024

**Pre-2024 governance** was materially weaker than for comparable financial structures. Under the predecessor Aged Care Act 1997, providers were required to report RAD balances and meet basic prudential standards under the Aged Care (Prudential Supervision) Act 2015, administered by the Department of Health. The Aged Care Financing Authority (ACFA) — which has since been abolished — published annual reports on sector financial performance but had no enforcement role.

**From 1 November 2025**, the new **Financial and Prudential Standards** under the Aged Care Act 2024 came into force, administered by the ACQSC. Three new standards apply:

**The Liquidity Standard** requires providers to hold:
- **35%** of their annualised cash operating expenses in liquid assets, **plus**
- **10%** of their total refundable deposit liabilities

This is the most consequential new requirement. For a 100-bed facility holding $41.3 million in RADs, the 10% requirement alone mandates **$4.13 million** in immediately accessible liquid assets. StewartBrown's submission to the ACQSC consultation noted this creates "a significant disincentive for capital investment in aged care at a time where there are growing supply shortfalls." Providers can apply to ACQSC with an alternative liquidity policy and strategy instead of the strict ratio, providing some regulatory flexibility.

**The Solvency Standard** (Financial and Prudential Management Standard) requires providers to maintain positive net assets and demonstrate ongoing financial viability, with escalation thresholds triggering ACQSC intervention.

**The Investment Standard** restricts how RAD funds may be invested — limiting exposure to high-risk asset classes and ensuring the funds remain accessible.

**Monthly reporting**: Providers must now report RAD balances monthly as part of their subsidy claim to the Department, compared to annual and quarterly reporting under the prior regime. This substantially increases the government's real-time visibility into the pool.

### 3. The Government Guarantee: A Contingent Liability of $48.4 Billion

The **Aged Care Accommodation Payment Guarantee Scheme** is the Commonwealth's backstop. If a registered aged care provider becomes insolvent and cannot refund residents' deposits, the Australian Government guarantees full repayment including any accrued interest.

This guarantee has no cap. It covers every dollar of the $48.4 billion RAD pool. By comparison, the Australian Government's bank deposit guarantee under the Financial Claims Scheme (FCS) is capped at $250,000 per person per institution. The RAD guarantee provides stronger protection per-dollar than the bank deposit scheme — but the government has made this guarantee without the corresponding prudential supervision framework that APRA applies to deposit-taking institutions.

The Royal Commission into Aged Care Quality and Safety (2021) highlighted this gap explicitly: "Unlike banks and other deposit-taking institutions, aged care providers have not been subject to the Australian Prudential Regulation Authority's oversight regime." The new Financial and Prudential Standards represent the first step toward closing this supervision gap, but the ACQSC — unlike APRA — does not have an equivalent resolution and intervention toolkit for managing the wind-down of a large failing provider while protecting deposits.

In practice, the Guarantee has been triggered rarely, most recently during the closure of smaller operators. The regulatory regime change in November 2025 aims to reduce the probability of activation, but the contingent liability remains material. The annual government financial statements do not explicitly quantify this as a contingent liability in a way comparable to deposit insurance.

### 4. The New Retention Rule: RAD Partially Becomes Non-Refundable

The most commercially significant change from 1 November 2025 is **RAD/RAC retention**:

- Providers may retain **2% per annum** of the RAD balance for residents who entered on or after 1 November 2025
- Maximum retention period: **5 years** (maximum 10% of original deposit)
- The retained amount is **non-refundable** and does not need to be returned on exit

For a resident paying a $516,770 RAD and staying 3 years, the provider retains approximately **$31,000** (3 × 2% × $516,770). This is the provider's permanent revenue; the resident receives $485,770 on exit.

This is a significant structural shift. The RAD is no longer fully refundable — it is now partially analogous to a retirement village "deferred management fee" or "ingoing contribution" model. For residents entering at 85 with average lengths of stay of 18–24 months, the retention impact is modest. For residents entering at 75 who remain for 5+ years, the full 10% retention represents a substantial permanent cost.

For providers, the retention dramatically changes the business case. StewartBrown's FY25 survey found that aggregate "unspent funds" (i.e., funds available from RAD balances minus liabilities) per client had increased to $15,171 (from $14,517 in FY24), with sector-wide unspent funds exceeding **$4.3 billion**. The 2% retention provides an ongoing revenue stream that can be directed to facility reinvestment without requiring traditional debt service.

### 5. Provider Profitability and the Investment Gap

Despite the RAD pool growing to $48.4 billion, provider financial performance remains fragile:

- **Operating EBITDA**: $6,817 per bed per annum (FY25, Stewart Brown) — improved from $7,039 in FY24 but still far below the **$20,000–$22,000 per bed** considered necessary to attract private capital investment
- **EBITDA margin**: 4.4% in FY25 (up from 3.5% in FY24) — thin by any standard
- **Accommodation services**: running at a loss for many providers (KPMG 2026)
- **Care funding**: still cross-subsidising accommodation and capital losses

The core problem is that the AN-ACC care funding (Commonwealth) has not been priced to generate returns sufficient for greenfield construction, and the RAD capital pool — while large — does not generate a return to the provider except through the implicit interest saving on debt that would otherwise be needed. When construction costs rise faster than RAD values can keep pace with (e.g., $750,000 cap vs $645,000–$1,000,000 per bed), the RAD alone cannot finance new construction.

The result: the sector is undersupplying new beds at a dangerous rate.

- Only approximately **800 new beds** were added to the sector in 2025 (Colliers analysis), compared to an estimated **10,600 beds per year** needed to match demand growth
- Australia's aged care bed count grew by only 3% between 2020 and 2025, while the population aged 85+ grew by more than 12%
- Approximately **2,500 elderly patients** in hospitals across Australia in late 2025 were medically ready for discharge but had no aged care bed available
- Building approvals increased to 285 in 2024–25 — up 23%, but still far below what is needed
- The Budget 2026–27 allocated $1.7 billion in construction incentives targeting up to 5,000 new beds per year, and a $606.5 million capital subsidy pool

KPMG anticipates a period of market consolidation: "some smaller providers exiting the market, while medium-sized organisations and more strategic operators focus on growth."

### 6. The Phase-Out Plan: RADs to End by 2035 (Conditionally)

The Aged Care Taskforce's final report (March 2024) recommended a conditional transition away from RADs. The government's formal Response to the Taskforce – Accommodation Reform confirmed:

**The plan:**
- An **independent review** of sector financial readiness will be commissioned in **2029–30**
- If the review confirms: (a) improved financial sustainability, (b) diversified and adequate capital access, and (c) residential aged care remains affordable for consumers, the government will proceed with **phasing out RADs by 2035**
- After 2035, accommodation would shift to a **daily-fee-only (rental) model** — no lump-sum deposits

**The rationale for phase-out:**
- RADs create liquidity risk (providers cannot always refund promptly if occupancy drops sharply)
- RADs disadvantage residents who pay the full deposit vs those who pay DAP (the former subsidise the provider's capital cost; the latter pay a commercial rate)
- RADs create complex interactions with means testing, estate planning, and Centrelink assessments
- A rental model would be simpler, more transparent, and more equitable

**The risk of phase-out:**
- If RADs are abolished without alternative capital sources (bonds, real estate investment trusts, Commonwealth grants, private equity), the sector loses its primary construction finance mechanism
- The 2029–30 review's conditions are deliberately designed to require demonstrated alternative capital access before phase-out proceeds
- StewartBrown and the sector broadly have argued the conditions are unlikely to be met without a major increase in AN-ACC care pricing to improve provider profitability

### 7. Means Testing, Equity, and the Affluence Paradox

The RAD system interacts with Centrelink means testing in ways that disadvantage the asset-rich but income-poor:

- A resident who pays a full $516,770 RAD removes that asset from their assessable assets for aged care means testing (the RAD is exempt from the means-tested care fee calculation while held)
- However, the same resident's family home (if it was not exempted as the principal home of a carer or dependent) may be counted after 2 years in care
- The interaction with the pension means test is complex: the RAD is excluded from the assets test for pension purposes; this can inadvertently preserve pension eligibility for wealthy residents

Residents without sufficient liquid assets face the DAP, which at 7.96% MPIR on a $750,000 room price is $163/day — a major ongoing cost for residents receiving primarily Age Pension income. Those below means-tested thresholds receive a government accommodation supplement instead of paying the full DAP.

## Tensions and Open Questions

**1. The phase-out paradox.** The government wants to phase out RADs by 2035 but also needs providers to build 50,000+ new beds between now and then. Those beds require construction finance. Without RADs providing zero-interest capital, construction requires commercial debt — which the sector's thin margins cannot service. The 2029–30 review will need to answer how the two ambitions are reconciled.

**2. The guarantee and APRA supervision gap.** The government bears a $48.4 billion contingent liability from the Accommodation Payment Guarantee Scheme. Unlike depositor protection via APRA, aged care provider supervision (ACQSC) lacks the resolution toolkit to manage a large provider failure cleanly. The probability of triggering the guarantee is low; the consequence of failing to manage it is high.

**3. The liquidity standard vs investment tension.** The new 35% + 10% liquidity requirement places significant amounts in low-yield liquid assets — precisely when providers need capital for construction. This tension has been noted by StewartBrown (submission March 2025) and UnitingCare, which argued the draft standards were "not sufficiently calibrated to the thin margins of the sector."

**4. RAD retention and equity.** The 2% per annum retention applies only to new residents from 1 November 2025 — not to the large existing stock of RAD holders. Over the transition period, two classes of resident will coexist: those with fully refundable RADs (entered before November 2025) and those with the new retention arrangements. The equity dimension sharpens over time as the fully refundable cohort ages out.

**5. The supply shortfall and demographics.** The RAD phase-out timeline (2035) coincides with the peak of the demographic wave: Australians born during the post-WWII baby boom (born 1946–1964) will all be aged 71–89 by 2035. The residential aged care demand spike will occur precisely as the primary financing mechanism for building new beds is being dismantled. This timing is not accidental — the Taskforce assumed the construction incentive pool ($1.7 billion, Budget 2026–27) would catalyse a supply response — but the evidence base for that assumption remains thin given the 2024–25 approval figures.
