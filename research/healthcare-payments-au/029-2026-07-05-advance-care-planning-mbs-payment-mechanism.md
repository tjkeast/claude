# Advance Care Planning as a Healthcare Payment Mechanism: Australia's MBS Funding Gap, the Economic Case, and the Structural Barriers to Scaling

**Report 029 — 2026-07-05**

**Summary:** Advance care planning — the process by which Australians document their wishes for future medical care should they lose decision-making capacity — has a well-established economic case: a 2024 population-based cohort study found ACP reduced hospitalisation risk by 17% and ICU admission risk by 77% at end of life, and Australian modelling suggests a nationwide program would save hundreds of millions of dollars annually. Yet only one in three Australians (33%) have taken any steps toward ACP, and the MBS contains no dedicated item for ACP consultations. Billing for these critical conversations requires GPs to use standard time-tiered attendance items — a structurally inadequate workaround that leaves ACP both underpaid and unmeasured. With 11% of the federal health budget consumed by the final year of life, and with the Aged Care Act 2024 now legally requiring providers to support ACP conversations for all residential aged care residents, the gap between policy intent and payment reality is becoming increasingly difficult to ignore.

**Why this follows from yesterday:** Report 028 documented Australia's specialist training pipeline failures across geriatrics, palliative medicine, and psychiatry — the specialties most often involved in end-of-life care conversations. The ACP funding gap is the upstream complement to those workforce shortfalls: even where specialists are available, no payment mechanism is structured to support the systematic documentation of patient wishes across the healthcare system.

---

## Background

### What advance care planning is

Advance care planning (ACP) encompasses a range of activities: conversations with loved ones about values and care preferences; formal appointment of a substitute decision-maker (enduring power of attorney, enduring guardian, or equivalent under state/territory law); and completion of an advance care directive (ACD) documenting specific wishes about future medical treatment, including refusal of life-sustaining treatment.

ACP is distinct from a "not-for-resuscitation" (NFR) or "do not resuscitate" (DNR) order, which is a clinician-authored medical order. An ACD is a patient-authored legal document. Confusion between these two instruments — and between ACP and general care planning documents like GP Management Plans — is a persistent source of both clinical uncertainty and billing ambiguity.

The key legal instrument varies by jurisdiction. All Australian states and territories except New South Wales have statutory advance care directive legislation. NSW continues to rely on common law, which creates cross-border recognition difficulties. Witnessing requirements, scope of authority, substitute decision-maker hierarchies, and document names differ across each jurisdiction — an enduring source of confusion for both patients and clinicians.

### The payment architecture problem

There is no dedicated Medicare Benefits Schedule (MBS) item for advance care planning consultations. This is the central structural deficiency. A GP who conducts a one-hour ACP consultation — a conversation that may encompass diagnosis explanation, values clarification, substitute decision-maker identification, and directive documentation — bills it identically to any other attendance under a time-tiered general attendance item.

The currently available items are:
- **Standard GP attendance items (Items 23, 36, 44)**: Level B ($43.90 rebate, ≥6 min), Level C ($79.70, ≥20 min), Level D ($125.10, ≥40 min) as at July 2025 post-indexation
- **New Level E attendance items (from 1 November 2023)**: Items for consultations ≥60 minutes, introduced specifically to allow adequate remuneration for complex conversations including ACP and palliative care. These are the closest the MBS has to an ACP-specific item, but they are not coded, counted, or tracked as ACP
- **75+ Health Assessment (Items 703, 705, 707)**: Annual comprehensive assessment for patients aged ≥75; ACP discussion can be incorporated but is not mandated and is not separately recorded in MBS claims data
- **GP Chronic Condition Management Plans (Items 965, 967 from 1 July 2025)**: Care planning framework that can embed ACP for patients with chronic conditions
- **General Practice in Aged Care Incentive (GPACI, from 1 August 2024)**: $300/patient/year GP incentive for registered aged care residents; eligible care planning services can include ACP discussions, but ACP is not a standalone billable component

The consequence of this architecture is twofold: ACP is structurally underpaid relative to its clinical complexity and time cost, and ACP activity is invisible in MBS administrative data — making it impossible to track uptake, target interventions, or evaluate programs systematically at a national level.

## Key Findings

### 1. Uptake is critically low and inequitably distributed

The 2025 National ACP Prevalence Study (commissioned by Advance Care Planning Australia, surveying 3,390 Australian adults in February–March 2025) established the first nationally representative benchmark:

- **33%** of Australians have taken any step toward advance care planning
- Awareness exceeds action: most Australians understand ACP is important but have not acted

These figures align with older clinical data. A multicentre audit of health and residential aged care services found advance care directive prevalence of:
- **37.7%** in residential aged care facilities (RACFs)
- **11.1%** in hospital settings
- **5.5%** in general practice

Even in the setting where ACP uptake is highest (residential aged care), more than six in ten residents have no documented directive — despite the Aged Care Act 2024 now legally requiring providers to support ACP conversations for all residents from 1 November 2025.

Uptake is consistently lower among people from culturally and linguistically diverse backgrounds, people with lower health literacy, people in rural and remote areas, and people from lower socioeconomic groups — the populations with least ability to navigate the end-of-life care system without documented preferences.

### 2. The last year of life is the most expensive — and most improvable — phase of healthcare

The AIHW's analysis of last-year-of-life health service use (covering 2010–16, published 2021) established the scale of end-of-life spending:

- Average annual health service cost in the final year of life: **$24,000/person** — **14 times** the average for all other Australians ($1,700/person)
- **11%** of the federal health budget is consumed by the 1% of Australians who die each year
- At least **25%** of all hospital beds are occupied by patients in their final year
- Patients average **four hospital episodes and 33 days in hospital** in the last 12 months of life
- Hospital expenditure in the final year is **30 times** higher than in other years

The AIHW palliative care hospitalisation data for 2023–24 shows these trends continuing:
- **107,500** palliative care-related hospital admissions
- **55%** of palliative care hospitalisations ended in in-hospital death
- Growth rate of palliative care hospitalisations: **4.9%/year** — more than double the rate for all hospitalisations (2.3%/year)
- Median time from palliative care referral to death: **6 days** — indicating most patients are referred only in the terminal phase, after the opportunity for ACP to prevent unwanted interventions has passed

This late-referral pattern is a direct consequence of the absence of structured, funded ACP in primary care: without documented preferences on file, clinicians default to intervention.

### 3. The economic case for ACP is strong — and unimplemented

A 2024 population-based retrospective cohort study (the most recent Australian evidence) found that patients who had ACP documents uploaded to their My Health Record at least six months before death, compared with matched controls, had:
- **Fewer hospitalisations** (adjusted odds ratio 0.83, 95% CI 0.74–0.92) — a 17% reduction
- **Fewer ICU admissions** (aOR 0.23, 95% CI 0.10–0.48) — a 77% reduction
- **Fewer ED presentations**
- **Lower rates of in-hospital death** — suggesting more patients died in their preferred setting

A 2017 BMC Health Services Research economic model of a nationwide ACP program in Australia found the intervention to be cost-effective or cost-saving compared to usual care, with cost savings generated from reduced hospitalisation and less-intensive end-of-life care. The key conditionality: savings hold if ACP completion exceeds 50% and adherence to documented wishes exceeds 75%. Both are achievable with structured programs but are not achieved under current ad hoc billing arrangements.

An end-of-life case conference study found that implementing structured case conferences (a model involving ACP elements) reduced annualised per-patient healthcare costs from **AUD$90,060 to AUD$11,841** — a mean saving of **$41,023/patient**.

The investment side of the equation: an ACP consultation costs, under the MBS, between $79.70 (Level C) and $125.10 (Level D) in rebate. A comprehensive Level E consultation (60+ minutes) would cost somewhat more. Even at a conservative program cost of $200-$400/person when including nurse facilitation, documentation support, and My Health Record upload, the return on investment against $41,000 in average end-of-life cost reduction is unambiguous. The Productivity Commission reached this conclusion in 2017. The recommendation was never implemented.

### 4. The Aged Care Act 2024 creates a new legal obligation without new payment support

From 1 November 2025, the Aged Care Act 2024 requires aged care providers — across residential, home, and transition care settings — to actively support ACP conversations and guide older people through the process of documenting care preferences. This is a rights-based obligation, not merely a best-practice recommendation.

The Act's Statement of Rights guarantees aged care recipients the right to plan ahead, to have their wishes respected, and to have substitute decision-makers recognised. Providers who fail to support these rights are exposed to regulatory action by the Aged Care Quality and Safety Commission (ACQSC).

Yet the payment system has not moved to match this obligation. Residential aged care providers who are legally required to facilitate ACP conversations for their residents — potentially hundreds of conversations per year across a facility — have no ACP-specific MBS pathway to fund GP involvement in those conversations. The GPACI incentive ($300/patient/year) is designed to incentivise regular GP engagement with aged care residents, and ACP conversations are among the eligible activities, but the incentive is structured around visit frequency and care plan completion rather than ACP documentation specifically.

### 5. State/territory legislative fragmentation creates portability failures

Even where ACP documents are completed, they may not be honoured when needed. Each Australian state and territory has different legislation governing advance care directives:
- Different document names (AHD in Queensland, ACD in South Australia, ADRT in Victoria, AHD in the NT, etc.)
- Different witnessing requirements (number of witnesses, professional requirements, capacity attestation)
- Different scope of authority (some allow specific treatment directions; others allow only values statements)
- NSW: no statutory ACD legislation; relies on common law and health agency policies

Cross-border recognition is not guaranteed. A person who completes an ACD in Victoria and is admitted to a Queensland hospital in a medical emergency may find their document unrecognised under Queensland's substitute decision-making framework.

The National Framework for Advance Care Planning Documents (first published 2011, Department of Health) attempts to address this by providing nationally consistent templates. My Health Record enables digital storage and clinician access. But neither framework resolves the underlying legislative fragmentation, and digital upload uptake remains low — ACP documents in My Health Record are not automatically visible to emergency clinicians unless specifically searched for.

## Tensions and Open Questions

**1. The Productivity Commission recommendation that won't be implemented.** In 2017, the PC recommended that the Australian Government introduce a specific MBS item for practice nurses to facilitate ACP, and that GPs be required to introduce ACP as part of the 75+ health assessment. Neither recommendation was implemented. The structural case for a dedicated ACP MBS item is at least as strong in 2026 as it was in 2017, but it has never been funded. Without a dedicated item, ACP remains invisible in claims data, unmeasurable at national level, and inadequately compensated.

**2. The measurement gap.** Because ACP is billed under generic attendance items, it is impossible to determine from Medicare data how many ACP consultations occur, where, by whom, and for which patient populations. This invisibility makes it impossible to target interventions where uptake is lowest, or to evaluate the impact of programs like Advance Care Planning Australia. A dedicated billing item is partly a payment mechanism and partly a data-collection mechanism.

**3. The Aged Care Act obligation without payment support.** Aged care providers are now legally required to support ACP but there is no corresponding payment reform to fund the GP time needed to complete ACP for the 240,000+ residents of residential aged care. The GPACI provides a contribution, but ACP facilitation is one of many activities covered by the incentive and is not separately resourced.

**4. The digital portability gap.** Australia's ACP document landscape is fragmented — state-specific legislation, paper-based completion, voluntary My Health Record upload, and no mandate for emergency departments to check ACP records before initiating treatment. The 2024 Journal of Bioethical Inquiry analysis found that wet-ink signature and witnessing requirements in most jurisdictions prevent digitally-native ACP, creating a paper-document ecosystem in an increasingly electronic health record world.

**5. Palliative care referral is too late.** The median 6-day gap between palliative care referral and death means that, for most Australians, specialist palliative care engagement is a terminal episode rather than a planned care pathway. ACP, properly funded and conducted early in the trajectory of serious illness, is the upstream intervention that could shift this pattern — but the payment system provides no mechanism to fund it at scale.
