# Private Health Insurance and the Medicine Access Gap: What PHI Covers, What It Doesn't, and Why It Matters

**Report 008 — 2026-06-13**

**Summary:** Australia's private health insurance system covers 12.6 million people for hospital treatment and pays out $26.7 billion in annual benefits — but it is legally prohibited from subsidising any PBS-listed medicine. For the vast majority of Australians, PHI contributes almost nothing to medicine costs. The system is structurally designed around procedures and hospital stays, not pharmaceuticals. This report examines the architecture of PHI medicine coverage, where the gaps are, who bears the costs, and why the sector's structural design creates a paradox: having private health insurance is consistently associated with *higher* out-of-pocket health spending, not lower.

**Why this follows from yesterday:** Report 007 found that New Zealand's PHARMAC cannot publicly fund CAR-T cell therapies or Zolgensma due to its fixed-budget architecture, raising the question of whether private health insurance bridges the access gap in either country. This report answers that question for Australia — and the answer reveals a fundamental structural misalignment between what Australians believe PHI does and what it actually does for medicine costs.

---

## Background

Private health insurance in Australia operates within a heavily regulated community-rated system: all insurers must accept all applicants regardless of health status, cannot discriminate on the basis of genetic information, and must charge the same premium for the same product to all members of the same age (before government rebate adjustments). This distinguishes the Australian PHI market from, for example, the US private insurance market.

The Australian Government maintains PHI participation through three interacting incentives designed to shift demand from the public to the private system:

**1. The Medicare Levy Surcharge (MLS).** Individuals with taxable income above approximately $93,000 (2025-26 threshold; $186,000 for families) who do not hold adequate private hospital cover pay an additional 1.0%–1.5% tax levy on top of the standard 2% Medicare Levy. For a $150,000 income-earner without PHI, this surcharge is $1,500 per year — a strong incentive to hold at least basic hospital cover.

**2. The Lifetime Health Cover (LHC) Loading.** Individuals who do not take out private hospital cover before age 31 pay a 2% loading on their premium for every year they delay, up to a maximum of 70% — applied for 10 consecutive years after first taking out cover. A 45-year-old taking out hospital cover for the first time pays 28% above the base premium.

**3. The PHI Government Rebate.** The Commonwealth subsidises private health premiums through an income-tested rebate. In 2025-26, the base-tier rebate is 24.288% for those under 65, rising to 27.8% for those aged 70 and over. The rebate phases out above higher income thresholds and reaches zero at the top income tier.

These incentives have produced a market in which, as of December 2025, **12.6 million Australians hold private hospital cover** and **15.3 million hold extras cover** — roughly 45–47% of the population for hospital cover and approximately 57% for extras. The industry is concentrated: Medibank (including its AHM brand) holds 27.1% market share; Bupa holds 25.4%; HCF (not-for-profit) holds 12.6%; NIB and HBF round out the top five, which together account for approximately 79% of all premium income.

## Key Findings

### 1. The PBS Prohibition: PHI Cannot Cover Government-Listed Medicines

The most important — and least understood — structural feature of PHI medicine coverage in Australia is its legal prohibition. Under Commonwealth health insurance legislation, **private health insurers are prohibited from paying a benefit towards any medicine listed on the PBS.** This prohibition applies regardless of the cost of the medicine, the patient's clinical circumstances, or the level of PHI coverage held.

The rationale is coherent in policy terms: if PHI could top up PBS co-payments, it would effectively subsidise manufacturers by supporting higher consumer demand at the PBS subsidy price, eroding the bargaining leverage that the PBS co-payment is intended to create. It would also create vertical subsidy stacking — public PBS subsidy plus private PHI benefit — generating equity concerns and budget pressure simultaneously.

In practice, this means:

- A Gold-tier PHI holder whose doctor prescribes adalimumab (Humira) for rheumatoid arthritis receives **zero benefit** from their insurer toward the PBS co-payment.
- A patient paying the general PBS co-payment of $25.00 (as of January 2026) for a $600 medicine receives no PHI contribution to that $25.
- The same applies to all listed medicines, from a $7.70 generic antibiotic to a $2.5 million gene therapy (Zolgensma) — if it is PBS-listed, PHI cannot contribute.

### 2. What Extras Cover Pharmacy Does Provide: Non-PBS Medicines

PHI extras cover can include a "pharmacy" or "non-PBS pharmaceuticals" benefit — but only for medicines that are **not listed on the PBS** and that meet specific eligibility criteria:

- The medicine must be a Schedule 4 (prescription-only) or Schedule 8 (controlled drug) item
- It must be prescribed by a registered Australian medical practitioner
- It must be dispensed by a registered pharmacist
- It must be listed on the Australian Register of Therapeutic Goods (ARTG)

Typical extras cover pharmacy benefits are modest: annual limits of **$200–$500 per person per year**, often with a per-script cap (e.g., $30 per script or 80% of cost). Some funds impose waiting periods of two months before the pharmacy benefit activates.

This benefit exists primarily to cover medicines that are TGA-approved but either awaiting PBS consideration or commercially available but below the PBS cost-effectiveness threshold. Examples include:

- **Non-PBS weight-loss medicines:** Semaglutide (Ozempic/Wegovy) for weight loss (not type 2 diabetes) is available on a private prescription in Australia but is not PBS-subsidised for the obesity indication. Monthly cost is approximately $300–400 as a private script. Some extras policies will reimburse a portion of this cost up to the annual benefit limit.
- **Non-PBS travel vaccines:** Some extras policies cover vaccines not on the National Immunisation Program.
- **Non-PBS cosmetic or specialist medicines:** Certain dermatological or specialist medicines with ARTG listing but no PBS listing.

The practical ceiling — $200–$500 per year — means extras pharmacy coverage is materially useful only for patients who need occasional, low-cost, non-PBS medicines. For anyone requiring ongoing high-cost therapies outside PBS listing, extras cover provides negligible relief.

### 3. Private Hospital Medicines: The Section 100 Pathway

For medicines administered in a private hospital, the picture is more complex and more favourable than the extras cover situation suggests.

**Highly Specialised Drugs (HSDs) under PBS Section 100** are medicines for the treatment of chronic conditions that — because of their clinical complexity — are restricted to supply through hospitals with appropriate specialist facilities. These include major biologics used in oncology, rheumatology, gastroenterology, and neurology (e.g., multiple sclerosis treatments, biologics for Crohn's disease, cancer immunotherapies that are PBS-listed).

Critically, **HSD prescriptions written in private hospitals remain on the PBS** — meaning the Commonwealth subsidises them at the same rate as public hospital prescriptions, subject to the same authority requirements, the same co-payment structure, and the same PBS safety net. Private patients in private hospitals can access PBS-listed HSDs at the concessional or general PBS co-payment, provided the prescribing specialist holds appropriate authority.

Remuneration for private hospital HSD dispensing does differ slightly from the public hospital rate (a mark-up schedule applies), but the patient-facing cost structure is the same: the PBS co-payment, not the full commercial price.

This means that, for example, a private patient receiving PBS-listed biologic immunotherapy (e.g., a checkpoint inhibitor for lung cancer that is PBS-listed for a specific indication) in a private oncology hospital pays **only the PBS general co-payment** — currently $25 per script. Their PHI plays no role in medicine costs, but PBS access is preserved.

### 4. CAR-T Cell Therapies and Gene Therapies in Private Settings

The situation changes fundamentally for CAR-T cell therapies and gene therapies.

**Publicly funded CAR-T therapies** (Kymriah, Yescarta, Tecartus, Carvykti) are funded through the **Highly Specialised Therapies (HST) Program** — a separate mechanism to the PBS, administered jointly by the Commonwealth and state/territory governments under the National Health Reform Agreement Addendum. The HST pathway is specifically designed for hospital-only treatments delivered in accredited public hospitals with specialist infrastructure.

**Private hospitals are not currently participants in the HST Program** for CAR-T therapies. This creates a structural fork: patients who receive CAR-T treatment in an approved public hospital receive it free of charge (as a publicly funded HST). A patient treated at a private hospital — even one with equivalent oncological capability — would face the full commercial cost of the CAR-T product, which ranges from approximately **AU$450,000 to AU$700,000** per infusion.

**Does PHI cover CAR-T in private hospitals?** In principle, PHI hospital cover applies to the *hospital admission* component — the room, theatre fees, nursing, and associated services — not the drug product itself, which is typically the majority of the cost. The drug costs would not be covered by PHI without specific arrangements, and no major Australian insurer has a publicly available product covering CAR-T drug costs in a private hospital.

The result is that CAR-T access in Australia remains effectively a two-tier system: **public hospital = funded; private hospital = not funded**. Patients who insist on being treated as private patients, or who attend a private-only cancer centre, fall outside the HST funding umbrella.

**Zolgensma** (AU$2.5 million per patient for spinal muscular atrophy) is PBS-listed — so the PBS prohibition on PHI coverage means that PHI cannot contribute to the patient co-payment. However, as a PBS-listed product with full Commonwealth subsidy, eligible patients (children with SMA meeting the clinical criteria) receive it at the standard PBS co-payment — effectively free at point of care for concessional holders.

### 5. The Financial Toxicity Paradox

Perhaps the most counterintuitive finding in recent research on Australian PHI and medicine costs is that **holding private health insurance is associated with higher out-of-pocket health expenditure**, not lower.

A 2024 Medical Journal of Australia study (Goldsbury et al.) found that NSW participants with a recent cancer diagnosis were **more likely to report out-of-pocket expenses exceeding $1,000** if they held private health insurance compared to those with Medicare only. The odds of financial burden were consistently higher for PHI holders across multiple analyses.

This paradox has a structural explanation:

- PHI holders are more likely to use the private system (by design of the LHC and MLS incentives).
- In the private system, **specialists routinely charge above the MBS schedule fee** — by 20% to 200% or more depending on specialty.
- PHI covers the mandatory "gap top-up" (the 25% above the Medicare 75%), but if the specialist charges well above the MBS fee, the remainder falls entirely on the patient (unless a specific no-gap or known-gap arrangement exists).
- PHI does not cover the medicine co-payment for PBS medicines.
- PHI does not cover out-of-hospital specialist consultations — Medicare pays 85% of schedule, and if the specialist bills above the schedule, the patient pays the gap without PHI contribution.

For cancer patients specifically, research shows that almost **50% spend more than $5,000** in out-of-pocket costs over the course of treatment; approximately **25% spend more than $10,000**. Out-of-pocket costs for prostate cancer patients average AU$11,077 — predominantly driven by specialist fees and adjuvant treatments, not PBS medicine co-payments.

### 6. The Structural Value Problem: PHI as a Tax Product

A sustained critique of Australian PHI is that it has, over time, evolved from a genuine mechanism for financing private healthcare into primarily a **tax-minimisation vehicle**: millions of Australians hold PHI to avoid the Medicare Levy Surcharge rather than because they value the health coverage.

Evidence of this structural tension includes:

- **Gold-tier policy decline.** As of June 2025, there were **743,000 fewer Australians in gold-tier hospital cover** compared to the pre-pandemic March 2020 quarter — representing 360,000 fewer policies. Gold-tier policies declined even as overall participation held steady, as members downgraded to bronze or basic tiers that fulfil MLS obligations at lower premiums.
- **Premium increases outpacing wages.** The 2026 premium round approved a **4.41% average increase from April 2026** (the largest increase since 2018), with gold-tier products rising an average of **13.3%** — well above both inflation and wage growth. PHI premiums have consistently exceeded CPI over the past decade.
- **Benefits ratio collapse and recovery.** The proportion of hospital premium revenue returned to members as benefits — the hospital benefits ratio — fell from approximately 90% pre-pandemic to **83% in 2022-23**, recovering to 85.9% by September 2025. The government projects a recovery to 87% for 2026, but this remains below pre-pandemic levels, indicating insurers captured pandemic "savings" (from deferred elective surgery) without returning them as premium relief.
- **Product phoenixing.** The practice of insurers closing an existing product and relaunching an effectively identical product at a higher price — circumventing government limits on premium increases within existing products — prompted the Butler Government to legislate against the practice in 2025.

### 7. PHI Total Benefits and Market Scale (2024-25)

Despite the pharmaceutical prohibition and the value questions, PHI remains a major financing mechanism for hospital services. In the 12 months to 30 September 2025, private health insurers paid out more than **$26.7 billion in total benefits**:

- **Hospital treatment benefits: $20 billion** (up 6%, or AU$1.2 billion year-on-year)
- **General treatment (extras) benefits: $6.7 billion** (up 5%, or AU$341 million)

Industry premium revenue for the December 2025 quarter alone was **$8.6 billion**, up from $8.2 billion in December 2024. The five largest insurers — Medibank, Bupa, HCF, NIB, and HBF — capture approximately 79% of this revenue.

The Prostheses List reform, phased in from July 2022 through July 2024, is projected to deliver **$900 million in total savings** to the PHI system through reduced medical device prices, with these savings anticipated to moderate future premium growth.

## Tensions and Open Questions

**1. The access illusion.** Many Australians hold PHI with the belief that it provides meaningful medicine coverage. It does not — and is legally prohibited from doing so for the vast majority of medicines. The disconnect between consumer expectations and actual coverage is significant and underexplored in public debate.

**2. PHI and non-PBS access: a thin but real safety valve.** For medicines that are TGA-approved but not PBS-listed — including novel therapies awaiting PBAC consideration, off-label uses not funded under PBS, and medicines that fail PBS cost-effectiveness assessment — extras cover pharmacy provides a marginal and capped benefit. For patients who need these medicines and can afford them, this is real value. For lower-income patients in the same situation, neither PHI nor the PBS helps.

**3. The CAR-T private hospital gap is a policy design failure.** The HST Program's restriction to public hospital delivery means patients who would otherwise access accredited private cancer hospitals are excluded from publicly funded CAR-T therapy. This is an accident of the program's architecture, not a deliberate access constraint — but the practical effect is the same. As private oncology hospitals expand capability, this gap will become increasingly untenable.

**4. GLP-1 and emerging non-PBS private prescription market.** The rapid growth of GLP-1 receptor agonists (semaglutide, tirzepatide) for weight management outside the PBS has created a new frontier: a high-volume, high-cost private prescription market not supported by either PBS subsidy or PHI coverage. PBAC has been examining equitable access to GLP-1 therapies for obesity, and any future PBS listing would extend access to concessional holders — but until then, the $300-400/month private prescription is inaccessible to low-income Australians regardless of PHI status.

**5. PHI's role in the reform agenda.** As the HTA Implementation Advisory Group works toward the 2026-27 Budget reform package, there is no current proposal to alter PHI's relationship to the PBS. However, the question of whether PHI might play a role in conditional reimbursement arrangements (e.g., sharing financial risk with government on novel high-cost therapies) is a live policy design question in several comparable jurisdictions (Germany, Switzerland). Australia has not moved in this direction.

**6. The not-for-profit vs for-profit divide.** Not-for-profit funds (HCF, HBF, Teachers Health, Police Health) consistently return higher proportions of premium revenue as benefits compared to their listed peers (Medibank, NIB). The community-rating requirement theoretically removes one lever of competition (risk selection), but the structural incentives for profit extraction in a captive-demand market remain. The PHI reform debate continues to centre on this tension.

## Threads to Branch Into Next

1. **Rare disease and orphan drug policy** — Australia's Life Saving Drugs Program vs the PBS vs the HST Program: three overlapping funding pathways for ultra-rare conditions, each with different eligibility criteria, different cost-sharing arrangements, and different patient outcomes. The gene therapy era is exposing gaps in all three.

2. **HTA Implementation Advisory Group 2025-26 reforms** — A thread deferred from Reports 006 and 007. What specific HTA reforms has Australia locked in for the 2026-27 Budget? Has the PBAC discount rate been revised for curative therapies? Are annuity payment models for gene therapies now in place?

3. **GP practice economics and the bulk billing crisis** — Deferred since Report 002. How do the structural funding pressures on GPs — in a system where PBS medicines are free at point of care but specialist referrals expose patients to open-ended gap costs — shape prescribing patterns, referral pathways, and where patients actually access treatment?

4. **US trade pressure on pharmaceutical pricing** — Australia's PBS, like New Zealand's PHARMAC, has been characterised by US trade negotiators as an "unfair trade barrier." The political economy of bilateral pharmaceutical pricing in the context of the AUSFTA and any future renegotiation.

5. **Private health insurance reform proposals** — With PHI participation in gold-tier declining, premiums rising, and benefits ratios recovering slowly, what structural reform proposals are on the table? The Productivity Commission, private health advocates, and academics have proposed divergent approaches ranging from subsidy reform to mandatory minimum benefits ratios.
