# Long-Term Care Insurance for Australia: The Funding Question the Government Won't Answer

**Report 042 — 2026-07-18**

**Summary:** Australia's aged care system is heading toward a fiscal wall — government spending projected to more than double as a share of GDP by 2062–63, driven by a tripling of the 85+ population — yet the country has emphatically rejected the structural funding reforms that comparable nations implemented decades ago. The Royal Commission recommended an aged care levy; the Aged Care Taskforce rejected it; the Albanese government ruled out any new tax. Private long-term care insurance does not exist in Australia. What does exist is a Support at Home regime (from November 2025) that shifts more cost onto individuals through means-tested co-payments — a quietly escalating privatisation of risk, without the population-wide insurance architecture that Germany, Japan, and South Korea built to manage it. New CEPAR actuarial research offers a blueprint for private LTCI products, but market failure dynamics make them unlikely to develop without government mandate or subsidy. The question of how Australia funds long-term care in the 2030s and 2040s remains formally unresolved.

**Why this follows from yesterday:** Report 041 documented that nurse practitioners in residential aged care face a 36% MBS revenue disadvantage compared with GP practices — a structural gap that reflects a deeper systemic failure: Australia has never built a dedicated insurance or funding architecture for long-term care, leaving patients, providers, NPs, and governments improvising around the edges of a system designed for episodic acute care, not decades of functional dependency.

---

## Background

### The Scale of the Problem

Australia's aged care system is currently government-funded to the tune of **$36.4 billion in 2023–24**, with 59% directed to residential care and the remainder to community and home-based care. The 2023 Intergenerational Report (IGR), the most authoritative long-term fiscal projection, projects that Australian Government spending on aged care will rise from **1.1% of GDP in 2022–23 to approximately 2.5% of GDP in 2062–63** — representing a more-than-doubling in fiscal weight over forty years. Aged care is expected to account for around **40% of the projected growth in government spending** over that period.

The driver is demographic arithmetic. The number of Australians aged **85 years and over is projected to triple** by 2062–63, reaching more than **3.5 million people**. As of early 2026, more than **22% of Australians are aged 65 or over**, up from 16% in 2020 — an acceleration that has caught policymakers short. Life expectancy at birth is projected to rise from 81.3 (men) and 85.2 (women) in 2022–23 to **87.0 (men) and 89.5 (women) by 2062–63**, lengthening the average period of potential care dependency.

The 2026–27 Budget allocated approximately **$45 billion** in total to the aged care system, with $3.7 billion in new measures including $1 billion to make personal care services under Support at Home free for all recipients. This is not a system nearing the end of its budget pressures — it is a system entering their steepest phase.

### What Long-Term Care Insurance Would Mean

Long-term care insurance (LTCI) — whether social or private — is a mechanism to pre-fund the probability of future dependency, spreading the risk across a population (or cohort) rather than leaving it as an unfunded contingency on both individuals and the public budget. The distinction between social and private LTCI matters:

- **Social (compulsory) LTCI**: Government-mandated contributions from workers and/or employers, typically levied as a percentage of income, with defined benefit entitlements. Examples: Germany (Pflegeversicherung, 1995), Japan (Kaigo Hoken, 2000), South Korea (2008), the Netherlands (AWBZ/WLZ). Contributors pay in across their working lives and draw on the fund when care is needed.
- **Private LTCI**: Voluntary commercial insurance purchased by individuals, covering some or all of expected aged care costs in exchange for regular premiums. Not widely available in most countries, including Australia.

Currently, Australia has neither. The government funds aged care through general taxation, individuals contribute means-tested co-payments, and the gap is increasingly borne by individuals drawing on retirement savings, family wealth, or — in the worst cases — going without.

## Key Findings

### 1. The Fiscal Position: Why the Status Quo Is Unsustainable

Australia's current hybrid funding model — predominantly government taxation, topped up by means-tested individual contributions and accommodation payments — was designed for a smaller, shorter-lived elderly population. The structural problem is that:

- **The ratio of workers to retirees is declining**: from 4.5 workers per retiree in 2022–23 to approximately 2.7 by 2062–63 (IGR 2023), meaning the taxation base that funds aged care grows more slowly than the aged population
- **The political economy of cost-sharing reform is toxic**: both main parties have repeatedly refused to include the family home in the means test (despite it being the largest asset of most aged care residents) and the government explicitly ruled out an aged care levy in 2024
- **Unbudgeted individual exposure is large and growing**: residential aged care costs Australians between $50,000 and $150,000 annually in means-tested fees plus accommodation (with Refundable Accommodation Deposits averaging approximately **$573,000** as of mid-2025), yet there is no insurance mechanism to manage this risk

The 2025 Support at Home framework (operational from 1 November 2025) introduced more structured means-testing for home care services, with self-funded retirees now contributing **50% of independence support costs and 80% of everyday living costs**, while full pensioners pay **5% and 17.5%** respectively. This is a significant shift in individual cost burden — but it is co-payment restructuring, not insurance architecture.

### 2. The Levy Debate: Recommended, Rejected, and Buried

The Royal Commission into Aged Care Quality and Safety (Final Report, March 2021) addressed the funding question directly. Both Commissioners — despite differing on some recommendations — supported an **aged-care specific levy** as the mechanism to fund the sector's needs. Commissioner Briggs recommended a **1.0% aged care and disability levy** on all taxable income; Commissioner Pagone recommended a Medicare-style income-based levy calibrated to aged care demand.

ACOSS has persistently backed a similar approach, proposing a Medicare-style health and disability services levy that scales slowly as the population ages. A **1% increase in the Medicare Levy** (currently 2% of taxable income for most Australians) could raise approximately **$8 billion per year** at current tax scales — enough to materially close the funding gap without structural reform of co-payments.

The **Aged Care Taskforce Final Report** (March 2024) — a government-convened body with sector, consumer, and expert membership — explicitly **rejected the levy approach**. The Taskforce's reasoning:
- An aged care levy would exacerbate **intergenerational inequity** by placing a permanent tax burden on working-age Australians to fund the care of a generation that has benefited disproportionately from property wealth appreciation and historical tax concessions
- The proper mechanism is **means-tested individual contributions** calibrated to ability to pay, not universal levies

Following the Taskforce report, the **Albanese government confirmed it would not impose any new tax or levy to fund aged care** and would not change the means-testing treatment of the family home. The government adopted 17 of the Taskforce's 23 recommendations, with modelling suggesting total system revenue could increase by approximately **$2.3 billion for residential care and $965 million for home care** in the first year if all recommendations were implemented.

The net effect of the reform package — per government estimates — was a **$930 million spend over four years and a $12.6 billion save over eleven years** through higher co-payments and more efficient service design. This is not a funding transformation; it is a cost-shifting ratchet.

### 3. Private LTCI: Why It Doesn't Exist in Australia

Private long-term care insurance is **not commercially available in Australia**. No insurer has brought a product to market. This is not an oversight — it reflects structural market failure dynamics that have prevented private LTCI markets from developing in most countries (the US partial exception proves the rule: the American private LTCI market has been contracting since the mid-2000s due to chronic under-pricing and mass insurer exits):

**Adverse selection**: People who know they are at elevated care risk (family history of dementia, existing chronic conditions) are more likely to seek LTCI. Insurers pricing for an average risk pool face a pool that skews high-risk, driving up premiums and reducing take-up, in a spiral that collapses the market.

**Longevity and morbidity uncertainty**: LTCI requires insuring against care needs that may occur 30–40 years after purchase, over a period where disability trajectories, care cost inflation, and the services landscape are deeply uncertain. Unlike life insurance (which has actuarially stable mortality tables), disability and long-term care transitions are harder to model and have higher parameter uncertainty.

**Insurer capital costs**: The Solvency II–equivalent capital requirements for products with long-dated contingent liabilities are prohibitive. CEPAR research found that **capital requirements significantly increase standalone LTCI premiums**, making them unaffordable for most potential purchasers.

**The public system problem**: Australia's publicly funded aged care system functions as an implicit insurance backstop. Rational individuals — knowing that the government will fund the majority of their care needs if they become poor enough — have limited incentive to purchase private LTCI unless they have significant assets to protect. PHI in Australia faces a version of this problem (the Medicare fallback), but at least PHI delivers tangible near-term benefits (shorter waiting lists, private rooms). Private LTCI delivers a contingent future benefit that most buyers will never need, making it psychologically difficult to sell.

**The Medigap problem**: Even for high-asset individuals for whom the public system's means-testing would result in significant co-payments, the means test itself reduces LTCI's insurance value. If your assets are spent down on care, you exit the means-testing band and become fully funded by government — so the insured risk becomes your estate, not your care. LTCI is primarily inheritance protection for affluent families, not genuine universal care risk coverage.

### 4. CEPAR Actuarial Research: What Private LTCI Would Look Like

CEPAR (Centre for Population Ageing Research, UNSW) published the most comprehensive Australian actuarial analysis of private LTCI to date, first as a working paper in 2024 (Working Paper 2024/14) and subsequently in the *Journal of Risk and Insurance* in 2026.

Key findings from the CEPAR modelling:

- The research used a **multistate Markov model** of functional disability and illness transitions in Australia, incorporating transitions among healthy, chronically ill, and disabled states using longitudinal Australian data
- Product designs examined include: **standalone LTCI** (covering care costs above means-tested thresholds) and **life care annuities (LCA)** — hybrid products combining a life annuity with LTC benefit triggers
- **Premium differentiation is essential**: premiums are found to be **12–23% higher for females than for males**, reflecting women's longer life expectancy and higher probability of extended dependency periods. A gender-neutral pricing requirement (as applies in some jurisdictions) would make LTCI financially unviable for insurers or require cross-subsidy
- **Capital requirements are the key constraint**: under Solvency II–style capital rules, the additional capital insurers must hold for standalone LTCI products substantially increases effective premiums. Life care annuity structures are relatively less capital-intensive and may be more commercially viable
- **The income replacement framing matters**: the most demand-efficient products cover the actual means-tested out-of-pocket gap (roughly the gap between what government funds and what the individual owes) rather than attempting to cover gross care costs. This limits the insured liability but also limits the product's appeal to those with significant assets to protect
- The research concludes that **developing a robust private LTCI market requires carefully designed models tailored to Australia's institutional architecture** — specifically, the interaction with means-testing thresholds, the age pension, and superannuation — and that government policy facilitation (tax treatment, regulatory design, possibly mandatory product offerings) is necessary for market development

The CEPAR research is significant because it establishes the actuarial foundations that would be needed for any commercial product launch, but its findings also underscore why no insurer has moved: the economics are marginal, the capital demands are large, and the market is shallow.

### 5. International Models: What Australia Is Not Doing

Three social LTCI systems offer direct comparison points:

**Germany (Pflegeversicherung, since 1995–96)**: Germany introduced mandatory social long-term care insurance alongside its statutory health insurance system. The **2026 contribution rate is 3.6% of gross income** for employees with children (4.2% for childless employees aged 23+), split between employer (1.7%) and employee (1.9% with children, 2.5% childless). The maximum income subject to contributions is **€69,750/year** in 2026 (€5,812.50/month), capping maximum contributions at approximately **€1,226–1,261/month**. Private supplementary care insurance (PPV) is simultaneously available and rising sharply in cost (+16% in 2026 for private plan holders). Germany's system provides cash benefits or in-kind care based on five care-need levels. Crucially, benefits are **not means-tested** — all contributors are entitled to the defined benefit regardless of income or assets, making it a genuine social insurance mechanism rather than a welfare backstop.

**Japan (Kaigo Hoken, since 2000)**: Japan's LTCI system is mandatory from age 40 and covers approximately **7 million people aged 65+ certified as needing care or support** — around one in five of the entire 65+ population. Funding is **evenly split between insurance premiums (50%) and general tax revenue (50%)**. The average base monthly premium for those 65+ is approximately **¥6,000 (around A$60)**, varying by municipality and income level, set on three-year cycles. Users receive care within certified care-need levels (7 categories), paying **10–30% co-payments** for a comprehensive menu including home-visit care, day services, equipment rental, and residential facility care. Japan's system provides services rather than cash (unlike Germany's cash-or-care option), reflecting concerns about informal family care economics.

**South Korea (2008)**: South Korea introduced universal LTCI in 2008 — proactively, when the elderly population was only about 10% of the total, rather than reactively. Run by the National Health Insurance Service (single public insurer), the scheme converted a means-tested welfare programme into a social insurance entitlement, with demonstrable reductions in hospital utilisation by older people (inpatient hospitalisation rates fell 2.7%, length of stay fell 15.6%, inpatient costs fell 9.5% for covered populations in the first decade). South Korea is notable for having acted early, before the fiscal pressure became acute — the opposite of Australia's trajectory.

**The common thread**: all three social LTCI models introduced mandatory contributions across the working-age population during a window of relative fiscal capacity, locked in a pre-funding model before dependency ratios deteriorated, and separated care insurance from health insurance — avoiding the co-mingling of acute and chronic care costs that distorts Australia's MBS/PBS system.

### 6. The Superannuation Angle: An Untested Funding Mechanism

One distinctively Australian possibility — not formally on the table but discussed in academic and policy circles — is using the **superannuation system** as a vehicle for pre-funding aged care. Australia's superannuation pool exceeded **$4.2 trillion in 2025** and will continue to grow. Several policy ideas circulate:

- **Designated care savings component**: a portion of mandatory super contributions could be earmarked for future long-term care costs, creating individual care savings accounts that supplement public funding
- **Lump-sum drawdown at care transition**: super funds could be required (or incentivised) to facilitate structured drawdown of accumulated savings specifically for aged care costs, with a government top-up for those with insufficient balances
- **Pooled longevity insurance embedded in super**: life care annuity–style products embedded within superannuation retirement-phase products, providing care benefit triggers without requiring a separate insurance purchase

The Grattan Institute has noted that **super tax concessions** (estimated at $52 billion/year in forgone revenue) are an available pool from which aged care could be funded without a new levy — effectively redirecting existing subsidies rather than creating new ones. However, the political economy of super tax reform is comparable in toxicity to the family home means-test question: both touch the accumulated wealth of the same voter cohort.

## Tensions and Open Questions

**1. The timing paradox.** Social LTCI works by collecting premiums from people who are unlikely to need care soon (the young) to fund those who need it now (the old). Australia's window for a low-cost transition — when the dependency ratio was favourable — has largely closed. Introducing a mandatory levy now places the burden on working-age Australians who are already squeezing their own cost of living; the political constituency for it is weak. South Korea's lesson is act early; Australia's history is act late.

**2. The means-test-as-stealth-insurance problem.** The government's preferred approach — tighter means testing under Support at Home — creates a de facto graduated care insurance for the affluent (they pay more if they can) while leaving the poor fully covered by government. This is a progressive outcome in isolation, but it provides no pre-funding mechanism and no risk pooling. Middle-wealth Australians — those with a home and some super but not large liquid assets — face the greatest exposure: too wealthy for full government subsidy, too asset-poor to weather extended residential care costs.

**3. The family home is the elephant in the room.** The average Australian residential aged care RAD is approximately $573,000 — almost exactly the median Australian house price. Most residents could fund their care from home equity, but the family home is politically sacrosanct: it is largely excluded from the assets means test (capped at $214,884 as a counted asset, while a partner is in residence it is fully exempt). Treating the family home as insurance capital — through reverse mortgages, home equity release, or deferred RAD payment mechanisms — remains politically unacceptable despite being financially rational.

**4. The private LTCI feasibility gap.** CEPAR's actuarial research demonstrates that private LTCI products could be designed for Australia, but the capital cost burden, gender pricing problem, public system interaction, and absence of mandatory purchase requirements make voluntary market development highly unlikely. Targeted government interventions (tax deductions for LTCI premiums, mandated product standards, possible pooled risk facility) could help — but the government has not signalled interest in activating a private market.

**5. The NP employment model is LTCI-sensitive.** Report 041 noted that nurse practitioners in aged care face the BBPIP exclusion gap. A social LTCI model would potentially fund NP-led aged care facilities differently from the current MBS-centric model — shifting payment from fee-for-service Medicare items to capitated care packages funded from a dedicated insurance pool. NPs who are structurally disadvantaged in the current Medicare architecture might fare better in an insurance-funded model that prices care bundles rather than individual consultations.
