# High-Cost Medicines and PBS Sustainability: Gene Therapies, CAR-T Cells, and the Coming Million-Dollar-Medicine Era

**Report 006 — 2026-06-11**

**Summary:** Australia's PBS was designed to fund mass-market medicines for chronic disease — not one-time curative gene therapies priced at AU$2.5 million per patient. The system has responded with a new assessment architecture (the Highly Specialised Therapies Program via MSAC) that sits alongside the PBS, but this framework is ad hoc, jointly funded with cash-strapped state governments, and structurally unprepared for the coming wave of cell and gene therapies. The 2024 HTA Review made 50 recommendations to modernise assessment methods, but implementation is slow and the pipeline of $1 million+ therapies is accelerating faster than policy reform.

**Why this follows from yesterday:** Report 005 examined how Australia's PBS uses biosimilar competition to contain the cost of biologic medicines — a mechanism that works in the aggregate (AU$562 million in savings) but underperforms against international peers. That analysis raised the question of what happens to PBS sustainability when the next generation of medicines (gene therapies, CAR-T cells) is priced not at the $10,000–$50,000 per year of biologics, but at $1–3 million as a single one-time treatment. The answer is that the PBS, as currently structured, largely cannot handle them — and Australia has been improvising.

---

## Background

The Pharmaceutical Benefits Scheme is, at its core, a high-volume cost-sharing programme. Its power comes from pooling demand across 26 million Australians, using PBAC's cost-effectiveness gating to drive competitive pricing, and applying price disclosure to ratchet costs downward over time. In 2024–25, the PBS dispensed 331 million prescriptions at a total government cost of approximately AU$19.1 billion — an average government subsidy of roughly AU$58 per prescription. The median PBS-listed medicine costs the government hundreds of dollars per patient per year; the expensive biologics reviewed in prior reports cost tens of thousands per patient per year.

Gene therapies and cell therapies break this model entirely. They are:

- **One-time treatments**: a single infusion or injection rather than a lifetime of prescriptions
- **Potentially curative**: designed to correct an underlying genetic defect permanently, yielding benefits measured across a patient's entire lifetime
- **Ultra-expensive**: priced globally at AU$1 million to AU$3.5 million per patient
- **Ultra-rare**: eligible populations measured in dozens to hundreds of Australians, not thousands or millions
- **Data-limited**: clinical trials are small; long-term efficacy evidence is inherently unavailable at the time of listing

None of these characteristics align with the PBS's standard evaluation tools: cost-per-QALY modelling over a defined time horizon, price disclosure-driven competition, or formulary-level pricing. The result is that every gene therapy listing in Australia has required the government to build new institutional infrastructure on the fly.

## Key Findings

### 1. The PBS's Growth Trajectory: A System Under Pressure

Before examining the emerging therapies specifically, the baseline context matters: the PBS is already growing at a rate that exceeds healthcare inflation.

- **Total PBS expenditure 2024–25:** AU$19.1 billion (from Report 003)
- **Year-on-year growth:** approximately 7.3% in 2024–25 (from AU$17.7 billion in 2023–24)
- **Oncology's share:** antineoplastic and immunomodulating agents — a category that includes both conventional chemotherapy and the newer biologics/checkpoint inhibitors — absorb approximately 18.6% of the pharmaceutical market by therapeutic class, and an average of AU$259 per person per year in PBS government expenditure
- **PBS total projected expenditure:** at 7% annual growth, PBS spending would reach AU$38 billion by 2035 if trajectory continues unchanged

The biosimilar savings examined in Report 005 (AU$562 million over a decade from three molecules) are meaningful in isolation but modest relative to the scale of the growth trajectory. The PBS needs both supply-side savings mechanisms (biosimilars) and fiscally coherent frameworks for the new high-cost wave.

### 2. Two Assessment Pathways: PBS vs MSAC's Highly Specialised Therapies Program

Australia has, in practice, developed a two-track system for high-cost medicines:

**Track 1: The PBS (PBAC pathway)**
The standard route for medicines dispensed in community pharmacy. PBAC assesses cost-effectiveness; the Minister lists on the PBS; patients access through pharmacies at the standard co-payment. Funded entirely by the Commonwealth.

**Track 2: The Highly Specialised Therapies (HST) Program (MSAC pathway)**
Established under the National Health Reform Agreement (NHRA), this pathway applies to treatments costing **more than AU$200,000 per patient** and delivered only at **selected public hospitals**. MSAC (Medical Services Advisory Committee) assesses clinical evidence and cost-effectiveness. Funding is **jointly shared between the Commonwealth and state/territory governments**, typically on a 50/50 basis, administered through NHRA addenda. These therapies are not dispensed through community pharmacy; they are provided within hospital settings that can manage complex administration, monitoring, and follow-up.

The HST framework was the vehicle through which Australia first funded CAR-T therapies and the gene therapy Luxturna. It represents a pragmatic workaround for the PBS's inability to handle ultra-expensive, hospital-only treatments — but it introduces its own complications: funding negotiations require agreement from all state and territory governments, hospital capacity is limited to designated centres, and the framework lacks the systematic price disclosure and competition mechanisms of the PBS.

### 3. CAR-T Cell Therapies: The First Wave

Chimeric antigen receptor T-cell (CAR-T) therapies are personalised cancer immunotherapies in which a patient's own T-cells are extracted, genetically engineered to target cancer cells, and reinfused. They represent a paradigm shift in haematological oncology — for some patients with relapsed/refractory blood cancers, they offer remission where no other treatment exists.

Australia currently publicly funds **four CAR-T therapies** through the HST Program:

| Therapy | Indication | Sponsor | Funding Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Kymriah** (tisagenlecleucel) | Relapsed/refractory B-cell ALL (paediatric/young adult); diffuse large B-cell lymphoma | Novartis | Publicly funded via HST |
| **Yescarta** (axicabtagene ciloleucel) | Relapsed/refractory large B-cell lymphoma; follicular lymphoma | Gilead | Publicly funded via HST |
| **Tecartus** (brexucabtagene autoleucel) | Relapsed/refractory mantle cell lymphoma | Gilead | Publicly funded via HST (as of ~2024) |
| **Carvykti** (ciltacabtagene autoleucel) | Relapsed/refractory multiple myeloma (≥4 prior lines of therapy) | Johnson & Johnson | MSAC positive recommendation April 2024; funding confirmed, available from May 2026 |

(Source: Victoria's HST Program; Myeloma Australia; MSAC assessments)

The cost of CAR-T therapies is not publicly disclosed in Australia's MSAC public summary documents (prices are redacted), but US and European list prices are indicative: Kymriah is priced at approximately US$475,000 for paediatric ALL; Yescarta at US$373,000; Carvykti at approximately US$465,000. Australian prices are negotiated confidentially and are likely lower following government negotiations, but remain in the AU$300,000–$600,000 per patient range.

Each CAR-T treatment is a bespoke, single-use product manufactured from the patient's own cells — there is no biosimilar pathway, no price disclosure mechanism, and no generic competition conceivable in the near term.

### 4. Gene Therapies: Zolgensma and the $2.5 Million Milestone

The most significant recent development in Australian high-cost medicine policy was the **PBS listing of Zolgensma (onasemnogene abeparvovec)** for spinal muscular atrophy (SMA).

Key facts:
- **Disease:** SMA type 1, caused by biallelic mutations in the SMN1 gene; without treatment, most affected infants die or require permanent ventilation before age 2
- **Treatment:** A single intravenous infusion delivering a functional copy of the SMN1 gene via an adeno-associated virus (AAV9) vector
- **Global price:** AU$2.5 million per patient (US$2.59 million per FDA label in 2025)
- **PBS listing:** Zolgensma is listed on the PBS — making it Australia's **first gene therapy listed on the PBS proper** (rather than through the MSAC/HST pathway). The listing was expanded in August 2025 to cover pre-symptomatic infants up to nine months of age with ≥3 copies of the SMN2 gene
- **Eligible patients:** Approximately **15 infants per year** will be eligible under the current listing
- **Financial impact:** At AU$2.5 million per patient and 15 patients/year, the annual PBS cost is approximately **AU$37.5 million** before any confidential risk-sharing arrangements

The Zolgensma PBS listing raises an immediate question: at the standard PBAC cost-effectiveness threshold of roughly AU$50,000 per QALY (where recommendations are most likely), how was a AU$2.5 million therapy judged acceptable? The answer is that it almost certainly was not cost-effective at the standard threshold — but PBAC has discretion to recommend listings where clinical need is severe and the therapy is life-saving for an ultra-rare condition. A 2022 published cost-effectiveness analysis of gene therapies for SMA Type 1 in Australia found that, even with optimistic assumptions about durability of effect, Zolgensma's ICER exceeded AU$100,000 per QALY. The government's acceptance reflects a de facto willingness to pay more for curative therapies in ultra-rare, life-threatening paediatric diseases — but without articulating this as explicit policy.

### 5. Luxturna: The First MSAC-Funded Gene Therapy

**Luxturna (voretigene neparvovec)** was Australia's first publicly funded gene therapy, listed via the MSAC/HST pathway in **March 2022**.

- **Disease:** Inherited retinal dystrophy (IRD) caused by biallelic RPE65 gene mutations, causing progressive blindness from childhood
- **US price:** Approximately US$850,000 (AU$1.18 million) for treatment of both eyes
- **Eligible population:** Approximately **100–125 Australians** total; roughly 14 treated as of October 2024
- **Treatment sites:** Two designated centres — Sydney Eye Hospital and Royal Victorian Eye and Ear Hospital
- **Funding:** Commonwealth/state joint under NHRA Addendum; Australian government price is confidential

The small eligible population means Luxturna's total PBS/HST expenditure is minimal in absolute terms — but it set the precedent that Australia would fund transformative gene therapies even at prices far beyond the standard PBS threshold. The limited treatment sites also reflect a key structural constraint: gene therapies are not dispensed at a pharmacy; they require highly specialised hospital infrastructure, cold-chain logistics, and post-treatment monitoring that only a handful of Australian hospitals can provide.

### 6. Hemgenix: The Next Challenge

**Hemgenix (etranacogene dezaparvovec)** for haemophilia B received **TGA provisional approval on 9 July 2024** and was submitted to MSAC for public funding assessment.

- **Disease:** Haemophilia B (congenital factor IX deficiency), a rare bleeding disorder requiring lifelong clotting factor replacement therapy
- **Treatment:** Single-dose AAV5-based gene therapy that enables patients to produce their own factor IX, potentially eliminating the need for lifelong replacement therapy
- **Global US price:** Approximately US$3.5 million — the most expensive medicine in the world at time of US approval
- **MSAC assessment:** August 2024 MSAC meeting reviewed the public summary; proposed Australian price is redacted in public documents
- **Patient population:** Approximately 300–400 Australians with haemophilia B could potentially benefit, though the eligible subset (those meeting clinical criteria, without inhibitors) is smaller

Hemgenix illustrates the escalation trajectory: if Zolgensma at AU$2.5 million stretched the framework, Hemgenix at the equivalent of AU$5.5 million per patient tests it further. The fact that haemophilia B is treated with lifelong factor replacement therapy — a treatment with well-established, ongoing cost — makes the cost-effectiveness comparison cleaner than for SMA: the gene therapy can be compared against a lifetime of factor IX infusions. Australian modelling submitted to MSAC reportedly used this offset to argue for cost-effectiveness, but the price remains redacted and no funding decision has been publicly confirmed.

### 7. The QALY Valuation Problem: Why Standard Tools Fail

The core methodological challenge is that Australia's standard HTA framework was designed for maintenance therapies — treatments taken indefinitely to manage a condition — not for one-time curative interventions.

**The discount rate problem:** PBAC's standard economic evaluation applies a 5% annual discount rate to both costs and health benefits (QALYs). For a chronic maintenance therapy, this is relatively neutral — costs and benefits stream over time at the same rate. But for a gene therapy that delivers most of its benefit in the first years of life (for a child with SMA) or over a patient's lifetime, the 5% discount rate drastically reduces the present value of future benefits. A therapy that keeps a child alive and healthy for 60 years has most of those QALYs discounted into near-irrelevance under a 5% rate. Medicines Australia submitted to PBAC in 2022 that the discount rate for health outcomes (not costs) should be reduced to 1.5% for curative therapies, in line with international best practice.

**The clinical uncertainty problem:** Gene therapies are approved based on relatively small trials with short follow-up periods. A therapy priced on the assumption of lifelong efficacy may not in practice deliver 30+ years of benefit. Durability data beyond 5 years barely exists for most approved gene therapies. This uncertainty makes PBAC's standard requirement for a robust long-term model inherently difficult to satisfy.

**The small-n problem:** With eligible populations of 15 to 400 patients, the PBS's standard competitive pricing mechanisms (mandatory price disclosure, biosimilar entry) are irrelevant. There is, and will be, only one Zolgensma. The manufacturer faces no competitive pressure and sets price based on a willingness-to-pay calculation — typically anchored to the cost of the alternative lifetime treatment that the gene therapy displaces.

### 8. The HTA Review's Response

The September 2024 **HTA Policy and Methods Review** — the first comprehensive review of Australia's health technology assessment system in nearly 30 years — specifically addressed cell and gene therapies as a distinct challenge requiring new approaches. Its 50 recommendations, delivered after two years of review (October 2022 – May 2024), include:

- **New evaluation pathways** for advanced therapies that "do not fit neatly" into the standard PBAC/MSAC frameworks
- **Recognition that the discount rate** for curative therapies with long-term benefit should be revisited — aligning with international practice in the UK (NICE uses 1.5% for specific circumstances)
- **Proposals for outcomes-based reimbursement** — tying payment to demonstrated real-world outcomes rather than paying the full treatment cost upfront
- **Annuity-based payment models** — spreading the cost of a one-time gene therapy over years, so the budget impact more closely mirrors the timing of clinical benefit and reduces the peak-year budget shock
- **Streamlining the MSAC/PBAC boundary** for jointly funded technologies — reducing the administrative friction of Commonwealth-state cost-sharing negotiations

The Implementation Advisory Group (IAG), established November 2024, is working through these recommendations with an interim report due September 2025 and a reform package targeting the 2026–27 Budget.

**Medicines Australia** has publicly criticised the 2025–26 Budget for failing to commit resources to HTA implementation, arguing it "ignores life-changing new and future medicines despite global challenges." The industry peak body's position is that delayed funding decisions cost patients access and deter pharmaceutical companies from submitting to Australia first — accelerating the access gap identified in Report 004 (591 days from TGA approval to PBS listing).

### 9. International Comparisons: How Other Systems Cope

Australia is not alone in struggling with gene therapy pricing. Every comparable payer system has improvised.

**United Kingdom (NICE/NHS):** The UK's Cancer Drugs Fund and the Innovative Medicines Fund both function as conditional reimbursement vehicles for high-cost therapies pending longer-term data collection. NICE introduced a **Highly Specialised Technologies** pathway specifically for ultra-rare diseases, with an explicit willingness-to-pay threshold of up to £300,000 (AU$600,000) per QALY for ultra-rare conditions — approximately six times the standard threshold. For truly curative therapies (like Strimvelis for ADA-SCID), NICE has approved at even higher ICERs where clinical benefit is profound and no alternative exists.

**United States:** The US has no national HTA framework. Gene therapies are priced on a negotiated basis between manufacturers and insurers/pharmacy benefit managers. The Institute for Clinical and Economic Review (ICER) publishes recommended cost-effectiveness prices; Zolgensma's ICER-recommended price was US$2.1 million, close to the actual US$2.59 million price. Medicaid coverage is mandatory but reimbursement approaches vary by state.

**Germany:** The AMNOG framework provides free pricing in the first year after approval, then triggers a cost-effectiveness assessment. Gene therapies have been listed under this system with outcomes-based contracts in several cases.

The common theme across systems: explicit or implicit higher thresholds for ultra-rare, curative conditions; outcomes-based payment mechanisms; and managed entry with real-world data collection requirements. Australia's trajectory with the HTA Review is broadly aligned with international direction — but implementation lag means patients and manufacturers face uncertainty.

## Tensions and Open Questions

**1. The explicit threshold question.** Australia has no published willingness-to-pay threshold. This creates a paradox: manufacturers cannot price predictably, PBAC cannot be held accountable for its decisions, and patients cannot know in advance whether a therapy will be listed. The HTA Review implicitly endorsed moving toward greater transparency, but the government has not committed to an explicit tier for gene therapies or ultra-rare conditions as NICE has in the UK.

**2. State-Commonwealth cost-sharing friction.** The HST Program's joint funding model means that every new high-cost therapy listing requires not just a Commonwealth decision but negotiation with eight state and territory governments. States with smaller budgets (Tasmania, NT) are less able to absorb their share of a AU$5 million per patient gene therapy. This creates inequity in access: a therapy may be 'listed' nationally but practically unavailable in jurisdictions that cannot fund their share or establish a designated treatment site.

**3. Outcomes-based payment: who monitors, and who recoups?** If Australia moves to outcomes-based contracts for gene therapies — paying for durable clinical benefit rather than one-time infusion — who tracks long-term outcomes? Patients may change GPs, move states, or be lost to follow-up over a 10-year outcomes horizon. The haemophilia community (which has the Australian Bleeding Disorders Registry) is better positioned than most; other conditions lack comparable infrastructure.

**4. The pipeline is accelerating.** As of 2024, the TGA has registered 22 biosimilars in a single year and is processing an expanding pipeline of gene and cell therapies. Globally, there are more than 1,000 cell and gene therapy clinical trials underway. Australia currently has Luxturna, Zolgensma, and the CAR-T therapies publicly funded — but Hemgenix (haemophilia B), Adstiladrin (bladder cancer), Roctavian (haemophilia A), and others are either approved or in the TGA/MSAC pipeline. Each one requires a new funding negotiation, a new institutional infrastructure build, and a new political decision about whether AU$3–5 million per patient is acceptable.

**5. PBS co-payment architecture is irrelevant.** For gene therapies, the entire patient co-payment system — designed to share costs between government and individuals — is structurally irrelevant. No patient can be expected to pay AU$100,000 or AU$500,000 in co-payments. These therapies are 100% government-funded if they are funded at all; patients who miss the listing criteria are simply excluded from access. This is a harder equity question than the co-payment debate: it is access vs. no access, not subsidised vs. full-price.

## Threads to Branch Into Next

1. **The PHARMAC comparison** — New Zealand's single-buyer tendering model for medicines: how does it handle high-cost medicines and ultra-rare diseases? Has PHARMAC listed any gene therapies? What are the access trade-offs compared with Australia's approach?
2. **Private health insurance and high-cost medicines** — For Australians who don't qualify for HST public funding (or who want treatment in a private hospital), does PHI cover gene therapies or CAR-T? What are the coverage exclusions, and what does this mean for health equity?
3. **The HTA Implementation Advisory Group's 2025–26 reform agenda** — What specific reforms are on the table for the 2026–27 Budget? Has the discount rate changed? Have annuity payment models been adopted?
4. **Rare disease and orphan drug policy** — Australia does not have a formal 'orphan drug' pathway distinct from the PBS and MSAC; the Life Saving Drugs Program fills some of this space but is not designed for the gene therapy era. How does Australia compare with the EU and US on rare disease access policy?
5. **MSAC vs PBAC: the boundary dispute** — Cell therapies are assessed by MSAC (a services committee), not PBAC (a medicines committee), because CAR-T is technically a 'service' not a 'medicine.' Zolgensma is on the PBS (assessed by PBAC). This distinction creates inconsistent evaluation frameworks for functionally similar products. How does the HTA Review propose to resolve this?
