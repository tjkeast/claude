# MyMedicare and the Continuity of Care Imperative: Australia's Attempt to Shift Primary Care from Episodic to Longitudinal

**Report 010 — 2026-06-15**

**Summary:** MyMedicare — Australia's voluntary patient registration system launched in October 2023 — is the centrepiece of a $6.1 billion Strengthening Medicare reform package designed to end the country's transactional, drop-in model of general practice and build longitudinal care relationships instead. Two and a half years in, only ~10% of Medicare card holders have enrolled, 1 in 3 Australians have even heard of it, and the government's most important evaluation question — does enrolment improve health outcomes? — remains almost entirely unanswered. Yet MyMedicare has quietly become the mandatory infrastructure for two of the government's largest primary care payment reforms: the Bulk Billing Practice Incentive Program (BBPIP) and the new GP Chronic Condition Management Plan (GPCCMP) framework. What started as a voluntary opt-in is becoming the architecture of Australian primary care.

**Why this follows from yesterday:** Report 009 documented how the BBPIP is lifting bulk billing rates by paying practices a 12.5% loading to commit to universal free GP access — but the RACGP noted the incentive doesn't yet reward longitudinal, comprehensive care. MyMedicare is the government's answer to that gap: the layer sitting above the bulk-billing floor that is meant to transform free-at-point-of-care consultations into genuinely coordinated, patient-centred care.

---

## Background

### The Problem MyMedicare Is Trying to Solve

Australian general practice has operated, for most of its Medicare history, on a transactional model: a patient attends whichever GP is available, receives a consultation funded by an MBS item, and leaves. There is no formal registration of the patient to a practice or a GP. There is no funding mechanism that pays a GP more for knowing a patient's history, coordinating their care across multiple conditions, or preventing an avoidable hospitalisation. The payment is entirely episodic — it attaches to the visit, not the relationship.

This model produces predictable problems. Patients with complex chronic conditions cycle through multiple GPs without any single clinician having a comprehensive picture. Medication records are fragmented. Care plans are duplicated. Referrals are made without awareness of what other specialists the patient is already seeing. Preventive and coordinated interventions fall through the gaps between the billable visit and the next billable visit.

The Strengthening Medicare Taskforce, convened in 2022 and reporting in December of that year, identified continuity of care as the highest-priority reform for Australian primary care. Its central recommendation: introduce voluntary patient registration to formalise the GP-patient relationship and create a platform for population-based care funding alongside the existing fee-for-service system.

### The Strengthening Medicare Reform Package

The Albanese government's 2023-24 Budget committed **$6.1 billion over five years** to Strengthening Medicare — described at the time as the largest single investment in Medicare in its four-decade history. The package has three pillars:

1. **Fee uplift:** $1.5 billion in the biggest MBS rebate indexation increase in 30 years (June 2023), plus the November 2023 tripling of bulk billing incentives for children and concession card holders.
2. **Workforce:** Expanded GP training; rural incentives; aged care GP supports.
3. **Care model reform:** MyMedicare as the enabling infrastructure for new care funding models — starting with the GP Aged Care Incentive, the BBPIP, and the chronic conditions management overhaul.

MyMedicare is not itself the reform; it is the registration substrate that makes the new care model reforms work.

## Key Findings

### 1. How MyMedicare Works: The Architecture

MyMedicare is a **tri-level voluntary registration system**:

- **Practice registration:** A general practice registers with MyMedicare through the Organisation Register. Registration requires the practice to be accredited under RACGP or ACRRM standards (or hold an exemption). Practices registered from 1 November 2025 onward received an accreditation exemption to enable rapid BBPIP take-up.
- **Provider registration:** Individual GPs and other eligible providers (nurse practitioners, Aboriginal health workers) register as providers within the practice.
- **Patient registration:** Patients with a valid Medicare or DVA card register with a specific practice — and optionally nominate a preferred GP within that practice — through the MyGov / Medicare online account, or in-practice using a Services Australia digital tool.

Patients can only enrol at a practice that has itself registered for MyMedicare. The voluntary nature applies in both directions: practices can choose not to register (though financial incentives are now strong), and enrolled patients can change their registration to a different practice at any time.

General practices began registering from **1 July 2023**. Patient enrolment opened **1 October 2023**.

### 2. Enrolment Numbers: The Slow Uptake Problem

As of June 2025, MyMedicare had achieved:

- **~3 million patients enrolled** — approximately **10% of Australia's ~30 million Medicare card holders**
- **6,654 practices registered** — out of an estimated 7,500-8,000 GP practices nationally

The gap between practice registration (near-universal among accredited practices, especially post-BBPIP) and patient registration (~10%) is the defining feature of MyMedicare's first two and a half years.

Research published in 2025 found that **only 1 in 3 Medicare card-holding Australians has even heard of MyMedicare**. Among those aware of it, approximately half had registered — implying that awareness is the primary barrier, not reluctance once informed. The 2025 National Consumer Sentiment Survey found that the proportion of patients aware of MyMedicare had not grown between the 2024 and 2025 surveys — suggesting the awareness gap is structural, not merely a lag.

The UNSW International Centre for Future Health Systems (April 2025) identified three principal barriers:
1. **Patient awareness** — most patients have never been told MyMedicare exists
2. **Registration complexity** — enrolling via MyGov requires digital literacy and a functioning MyGov account; a non-trivial barrier for elderly, low-income, and CALD patients
3. **No immediate patient benefit signal** — patients who enrol don't notice any difference in their next GP visit; the benefits (longer telehealth, priority booking, coordinated chronic care) are diffuse and hard to attribute to the registration

### 3. What MyMedicare Unlocks: The MBS Incentive Stack

The true importance of MyMedicare is not its enrolment rate but the growing list of MBS items and programs that require it as a gateway. From the launch in 2023 through to 2026, the following have been layered onto the MyMedicare platform:

**a) Enhanced telehealth consultations (from November 2023)**
Enrolled patients gain access to longer MBS-funded telephone and video consultations with their registered practice. Concession card holders and children under 16 who are enrolled receive these at triple bulk-billed rates. From **1 November 2025**, enrolled patients are also exempt from the 12-month face-to-face requirement that otherwise governs telehealth eligibility — a significant convenience benefit for patients who move, travel, or prefer remote access.

**b) GP Chronic Condition Management Plans — Item 965 (from 1 July 2025)**
This is the most structurally significant change to date. From 1 July 2025, the long-standing GP Management Plan (GPMP, Item 721) and Team Care Arrangement (TCA, Item 723) — which had governed chronic disease management for over 20 years — were replaced by the **GP Chronic Condition Management Plan (GPCCMP)**, a single integrated instrument:

| New item | Description | Rebate | Replaces |
|----------|-------------|--------|----------|
| **965** | Preparation of a GPCCMP (face-to-face or telehealth) | **$156.55** | Items 721, 723 and telehealth equivalents (92024, 92025, 92055, 92056) |
| **967** | Review of a GPCCMP | **$156.55** | Items 233, 732, 92028, 92059 |

The critical change: **patients registered in MyMedicare can only access GPCCMP items at their registered practice**. A patient enrolled at Practice A who temporarily attends Practice B cannot have a GPCCMP prepared at Practice B. This is the first hard linkage between MyMedicare enrolment and MBS access — the point at which "voluntary" registration begins to carry a material consequence.

Patients who had existing GPMP/TCA plans before 1 July 2025 have a two-year transition period (until 1 July 2027) before the new rules apply to them.

**c) GP in Aged Care Incentive — GPACI (from August 2024)**
The GPACI introduced a new income stream for GPs and practices providing systematic care to residents of Residential Aged Care Homes (RACHs), funded as a response to the Royal Commission into Aged Care Quality and Safety:

- **$300 per RACH patient per year** to the responsible GP provider
- **$130 per RACH patient per year** to the registered practice
- Requires: two eligible care planning services per year and eight regular visits per year (minimum two per quarter, in separate calendar months)
- Paid quarterly; requires both practice and patient to be registered in MyMedicare

As of June 2025, **1,744 GPACI-registered practices** were actively using the incentive. The program represented the first systematic movement away from fee-for-service payment toward a capitation-adjacent model — a fixed annual payment per patient rather than a per-visit fee.

**d) BBPIP — MyMedicare as prerequisite (from November 2025)**
As documented in Report 009, the BBPIP (12.5% loading on all MBS billings for fully-bulk-billing practices) requires practices to be registered in MyMedicare. The 3,700+ practices that registered for BBPIP by February 2026 simultaneously became MyMedicare-registered, expanding the potential patient enrolment pool significantly. MyMedicare registration is now a de facto requirement for practices seeking the most important GP payment incentive in a generation.

### 4. The Frequent Hospital Users Incentive: The Promise That Didn't Happen

One of the most compelling original rationales for MyMedicare was the **Frequent Hospital Users incentive** — a program designed to use the patient registration platform to identify patients who were repeatedly presenting to emergency departments and acute hospitals, and fund GPs to provide "wraparound" primary care that would reduce those presentations.

The concept was well-evidenced internationally: patients in the top 5% of hospital users account for a disproportionate share of healthcare expenditure, and many of these presentations are preventable with well-coordinated primary care. The Federal Government allocated **$16 million over four years** to develop the system infrastructure for this program.

By late 2024, the Frequent Hospital Users incentive was officially discontinued before it launched — one of the two Strengthening Medicare initiatives that did not progress past the planning phase. The reasons were not publicly detailed. The cancellation was notable because it removed the most clinically and economically powerful justification for mandatory longitudinal patient registration.

### 5. International Evidence: What Patient Enrolment Actually Does

A scoping review published in the *Medical Journal of Australia* in 2025 (Bates et al., 2025) examined 24 peer-reviewed studies from 12 high-income countries (UK, Canada, Ireland, New Zealand, Denmark, France, Germany, Israel, Italy, Netherlands, Norway, Sweden and Switzerland) covering patient enrolment models in primary care between 2014 and 2024.

Key findings relevant to Australia's MyMedicare:

- **Continuity improvement:** Enrolment generally facilitates better care planning, practice efficiency, and longitudinal data quality — but the effect on clinical outcomes (hospitalisations, chronic disease control, mortality) is heterogeneous and weakly evidenced in most jurisdictions.
- **Access risk:** In systems where enrolment is voluntary and capacity is limited, enrolment can function as a **barrier to access** — patients who cannot find a registered practice with capacity are locked out of incentivised care pathways.
- **Compulsory vs voluntary:** Systems with automatic or mandatory enrolment (Denmark, Netherlands, UK) achieve near-universal registration and allow population-level data analysis. Voluntary systems achieve lower penetration and introduce selection bias (healthier, more health-literate patients self-enrol, potentially inflating apparent health outcomes).
- **Design lesson for Australia:** The effectiveness of MyMedicare will depend heavily on ensuring there is a registered practice with capacity for every patient who wants to enrol — directly intersecting with the GP desert problem identified in Report 009.

### 6. The Evaluation Gap: Reform Without Evidence

A review covering 2023 to June 2025 found that of 47 Strengthening Medicare reform measures, only **1 had been fully evaluated**. Fifty-seven percent had completed delivery; 38% were still being implemented. The evaluation gap is substantial: billions of dollars are being committed to a model transformation without prospective evidence that the specific Australian design will deliver the promised continuity benefits.

The Prospective Evaluation of Patient Registration (PEPR) Study was announced in 2026 (Frontiers in Health Services, protocol published 2026) to provide the formal evaluation framework. MyMedicare registration status will be flagged in linked Lumos administrative data from 2026, enabling comparison of health service use, continuity, and outcomes between enrolled and non-enrolled patients. Results are likely several years away.

## Tensions and Open Questions

**1. Voluntary in name only?** The combination of BBPIP (requiring MyMedicare practice registration) and GPCCMP items (requiring patient registration to access at a given practice) is progressively eroding the "voluntary" character of the scheme — at least at the practice level. Practices that do not register cannot access BBPIP; patients whose practices are not registered cannot access the incentivised chronic care pathway. The government's architecture is creating de facto mandates through incentive design rather than legislation.

**2. The 10% problem and the fragmentation risk.** With only 10% of patients enrolled as at June 2025, the system is bifurcating: enrolled patients can access enhanced telehealth, streamlined chronic care plans, and potentially better-coordinated care; non-enrolled patients (especially those in GP deserts, with low digital literacy, or from CALD backgrounds) remain on the old episodic model. If enrolment rates stagnate, MyMedicare risks becoming a benefit for the already-advantaged.

**3. Lock-in vs. patient mobility.** The requirement for MyMedicare-enrolled patients to access GPCCMP items only at their registered practice creates care continuity at the cost of geographic flexibility. Patients who move, travel seasonally, or attend multiple practices for specialist access may find their chronic care management disrupted if the mechanism is not designed with sufficient flexibility for exceptions.

**4. GPACI proof of concept — or early signal?** The 1,744 GPACI-registered practices represent roughly 20-25% of the GP practices with aged care patients — significant uptake for a relatively new program. Whether the GPACI model (fixed annual payment per patient, attendance requirements) translates to better outcomes for aged care residents — reduced hospitalisations, better end-of-life planning, fewer avoidable presentations — is the key question that the evaluation framework must answer.

**5. The capitation question.** MyMedicare's architecture is the infrastructure for a potential future shift from pure fee-for-service to **blended payment models** — a mix of capitation (per-patient funding) and fee-for-service (per-consultation billing). The GPACI is a prototype of this. The RACGP has historically been ambivalent about capitation, worried it might provide perverse incentives to under-serve complex patients. The government has been careful to describe MyMedicare as complementary to, not replacing, fee-for-service. But the trajectory — GPACI, CCM linkage, BBPIP — suggests the architecture is being built incrementally toward a blended model whether or not it is publicly described as such.

## Threads to Branch Into Next

1. **Better Access and mental health care: the 20-session cap and its consequences** — The Better Access initiative (MBS items 2710, 2712, 2721, 2723 for psychologists; referred by GP) is the most politically and clinically contested area of MBS reform in recent years. The pandemic saw sessions per patient surge; the 2022 budget cut the cap from 20 to 10 sessions; the 2023 budget partially reversed this. The GP as mental health gatekeeper, the out-of-pocket cost crisis for psychology, and the interaction with MyMedicare care plans are ripe for analysis.

2. **Medical Costs Finder Bill 2026 and specialist fee transparency** — First flagged in Report 002, now overdue for dedicated analysis. The legislation requiring specialists to publish fees before the appointment: where is it in the legislative process? What data will it expose? How does it interact with MBS indexation gaps and the specialist out-of-pocket crisis?

3. **MyMedicare and Aboriginal Community Controlled Health Organisations (ACCHOs)** — The Strengthening Medicare Taskforce specifically recommended investment in ACCHOs as a priority. How have ACCHOs engaged with MyMedicare? The enrolment model intersects with Community Controlled care in complex ways — ACCHOs already provide longitudinal population-based care funded through differently structured mechanisms.

4. **Rural and remote healthcare payments** — GP deserts, the Modified Monash Model, rural incentive payments, and the limits of payment reform where no GPs exist. How do rural communities experience the combination of BBPIP and MyMedicare in practice?

5. **US trade pressure on PBS pricing and AUSFTA** — Deferred from Report 007 and flagged repeatedly. The US pharmaceutical industry has long targeted both the PBS and PHARMAC as "unfair trade barriers" in bilateral negotiations. With the BBPIP adding $2 billion/year to Commonwealth health expenditure and PBS at $19.1 billion, the fiscal pressure to manage pricing is intensifying exactly as trade negotiations heat up.
