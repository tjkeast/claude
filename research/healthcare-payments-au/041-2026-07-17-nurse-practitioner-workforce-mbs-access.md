# Nurse Practitioner Workforce and MBS Access: Australia's Established Prescribing Model Navigating Reform and Competition

**Report 041 — 2026-07-17**

**Summary:** Australia's approximately 10,000 endorsed nurse practitioners have spent two decades fighting for the MBS rebates, PBS prescribing rights, and professional autonomy that comparable clinicians in the UK and Canada already take for granted. Three landmark reforms — a 30% MBS rebate increase from July 2024, removal of the mandatory collaborative arrangement requirement from November 2024, and a rolling expansion of PBS medicines NPs can prescribe — have materially improved the NP payment model. But the biggest story of 2026 is a parallel development: new legislation passed in July 2026 creates a "designated registered nurse prescriber" category allowing ordinary registered nurses (not NPs) PBS prescribing access for the first time from October 2026, opening a workforce architecture question — is Australia building a coherent advanced practice nursing tier, or layering competing prescribing credentials that will confuse employers, patients, and the payment system?

**Why this follows from yesterday:** Report 040 documented Australia's pharmacist scope-of-practice expansion and the June 2026 safety coalition — co-signed by the Australian College of Nurse Practitioners (ACNP) — that challenged pharmacists' ability to prescribe without equivalent training. The NP model is the established comparator: a decade-and-a-half older, with a higher training standard, and with the structural MBS/PBS plumbing that the pharmacist prescribing agenda is still trying to access.

## Background

### What a Nurse Practitioner Is — and Isn't

In Australian health law, a nurse practitioner (NP) is a specific registered nurse who holds **endorsement for scheduled medicines** from the Nursing and Midwifery Board of Australia (NMBA). This endorsement is distinct from general registration as a registered nurse (RN) or enrolled nurse (EN). To obtain endorsement, an applicant must:

1. Be currently registered as an RN with no conditions relevant to the endorsement
2. Have completed at least **5,000 hours of advanced clinical practice** within the prior six years (equivalent to approximately three years full-time post-registration experience in an advanced practice role)
3. Hold a successful **NMBA-approved Master's degree** in nurse practitioner or advanced practice nursing from one of the 13 universities nationally offering these programmes (or equivalent units of study)

The total educational timeline from commencement of a three-year bachelor's nursing degree to NP endorsement is approximately **seven to eight years**. This is materially longer than the graduate certificate pathway for pharmacist prescribing endorsed by state governments in 2025–26 — a difference the ACNP has explicitly foregrounded in its opposition to pharmacist scope expansion.

### The Workforce Numbers

The NMBA quarterly registrant data has tracked steady growth in NP endorsements. The Commonwealth's **Nurse Practitioner Workforce Plan** (published May 2023) identified approximately **9,800 endorsed NPs** at the time of publication. Growth has continued since: based on NMBA quarterly data trends, Australia had approximately **10,000–10,500 endorsed nurse practitioners** as of mid-2025, against a national registered nursing and midwifery workforce of approximately 450,000. NPs therefore remain a small, highly specialised workforce — roughly 2.2–2.3% of all registered nursing practitioners.

The **Nursing Supply and Demand Study 2023–2035** (Department of Health) projects an NP-specific workforce gap of **296 FTE by 2035** — remarkably modest compared to the broader nursing sector (projected undersupply of 70,707 FTE by 2035, of which 17,551 are in aged care and 21,765 in primary care). The modest NP forecast gap reflects both demand growth and the assumption that reforms to training access and MBS viability will increase supply.

NP employment is concentrated in:
- **Aged care and chronic disease management**: aged care NPs provide complex care reviews, prescribing for polypharmacy, palliative care, and wound management
- **Primary healthcare and general practice**: about a third of PHC NPs work in private general practice or community health settings; in rural and remote areas they frequently substitute for absent GPs
- **Emergency and specialist settings**: hospital NPs in ED, oncology, and mental health settings, where they can prescribe and manage defined patient cohorts without routine physician involvement
- **Mental health**: psychiatric-mental health NPs hold prescribing authority for psychotropic medicines; high-need area given psychiatry workforce shortage

## Key Findings

### 1. The 30% MBS Rebate Increase — the First in Two Decades of Real Significance

From **1 July 2024**, Medicare fees for NP attendance and telehealth items were increased by **30%** — the largest single uplift since NP MBS access was established in 2010. The increase was a Budget 2024–25 measure, funded at an estimated $135.1 million over four years, and was explicitly linked to the government's Nurse Practitioner Workforce Plan commitment to reduce the gap between NP and GP rebate levels.

Current MBS schedule fees and benefits for NP professional attendance items (as of the November 2025 MBS Quick Reference Guide, reflecting the 85% Medicare rebate):

| MBS Item | Duration | Rebate (85%) | Approximate Schedule Fee |
|---|---|---|---|
| 82200 | Obvious problem, limited exam | $12.40 | ~$14.59 |
| 82205 | 6–20 minutes | $27.05 | ~$31.82 |
| 82210 | ≥20 minutes | $51.25 | ~$60.29 |
| 82215 | ≥40 minutes | $75.60 | ~$88.94 |
| 82216 (from March 2025) | ≥60 minutes | (new item) | — |

Equivalent GP items provide significantly higher rebates for the same time investment. GP Item 23 (6–20 minutes) carries a schedule fee of **$43.90** from 1 July 2025, generating an 85% rebate of **$37.32**. An NP billing Item 82205 for the same 6–20 minute consultation earns a rebate of $27.05 — **27% lower** than the GP equivalent, despite the NP requiring a Master's degree plus years of advanced practice to gain prescribing authority. This structural fee gap is the central financial tension in NP practice viability.

From **1 March 2025**, two new long-attendance MBS items were created for NPs — including Item 82216 for consultations of 60 minutes or more, with equivalent telehealth items — reflecting that complex NP care (multimorbid elderly patients, chronic disease review, psychiatric assessment) routinely involves longer consultations than the existing item structure captured.

Telehealth equivalents exist across all attendance items, using items 91178–91193. From **1 November 2025**, NP telehealth items were amended to require an **established clinical relationship** — at least one face-to-face consultation with the NP or another practitioner at the same practice within the preceding 12 months. This mirrors equivalent changes to GP telehealth and is intended to prevent fully virtual NP practices operating without a prior face-to-face relationship.

### 2. The Collaborative Arrangement Removal — the Most Significant Reform in Two Decades

For fourteen years after NP Medicare access was established in 2010, the ability to bill MBS items and prescribe PBS medicines depended on a **formal, documented Collaborative Arrangement** — a written agreement between the NP and a named medical practitioner confirming clinical oversight. Without an active Collaborative Arrangement, the NP had no MBS or PBS access. In practice, this required NPs to secure the ongoing co-operation of a willing medical practitioner — who might charge for the arrangement, change employment, become unavailable, or simply decline — before the NP could see patients independently.

From **1 November 2024**, the **Health Legislation Amendment (Removal of Requirement for Collaborative Arrangement) Bill 2024** took effect, abolishing the mandatory Collaborative Arrangement for nurse practitioners and endorsed midwives. This followed fifteen years of ACNP and ANMF advocacy, and was described by nursing bodies as "the most transformative structural change to NP practice since 2010."

Key points about the reform:
- **NPs can now directly access MBS and PBS without a co-signed medical arrangement**. Prescribing authority is governed by the NMBA's standards for practice and each state/territory's drugs and poisons legislation — not by the availability of a willing GP co-signatory
- **Clinical consultation, referral, and transfer of care are expected to continue** in the ordinary course of good clinical practice; the reform removes a legal requirement, not a clinical expectation
- **All PBS medicines listed for NP prescribing** became immediately accessible without a Collaborative Arrangement from 1 November 2024
- **The reform applies to endorsed midwives** as well as NPs, and combined oral contraceptives were subsequently added as a PBS prescribing category for endorsed midwives from **1 June 2025**

The AMA expressed concern that removal of the Collaborative Arrangement could lead to NPs practising without adequate medical oversight in complex cases. The ACNP's position is that the old model was never a meaningful clinical safeguard — it was a bureaucratic barrier that forced NPs to subordinate their employment and revenue to the availability of individual GPs.

### 3. PBS Prescribing Expansion — 70+ Medicines Added in 2025

Simultaneously with the Collaborative Arrangement removal, the PBAC undertook a systematic review of PBS medicines to expand NP prescribing access to conditions within their scope of practice. Prior to this review, NPs had PBS prescribing access for a relatively narrow formulary — largely primary care and community medicines — with large gaps in specialist areas.

At its **March 2025 PBAC meeting**, approximately 70 PBS medicines identified by stakeholders as lacking NP prescribing access were considered. The PBAC recommended most be amended to include NPs as authorised prescribers — typically permitting prescribing in accordance with existing PBS restrictions, sometimes under continuing-therapy-only or shared-care conditions. These recommendations were implemented on **1 October 2025**.

Earlier expansions included:
- **November 2024 PBAC**: sacubitril/valsartan (heart failure), fingolimod/ofatumumab/siponimod (multiple sclerosis), inclisiran (hypercholesterolaemia) — implemented 1 March 2025
- **July 2025 PBAC**: oncology and haematology medicines on the General Schedule and Section 100 Highly Specialised Drugs; NPs can continue existing therapy for most identified oncology/haematology medicines, and prescribe iron chelating medicines in shared-care arrangements. Implemented 1 February 2026.

The effect is cumulative: NP prescribers in aged care, oncology, and chronic disease settings now have access to a substantially wider PBS formulary — reducing the previous paradox where a prescribing-qualified NP managing a patient's warfarin, metformin, and antihypertensives had to refer to a GP for the same patient's rituximab prescription.

### 4. The New Designated Registered Nurse Prescriber — A Separate, Lower-Tier Pathway

The most significant recent development — and one that complicates the NP workforce architecture — is the creation of a new prescribing category for **ordinary registered nurses**, distinct from and at a lower training threshold than NP endorsement.

The sequence of events:
- **December 2024**: All state and federal Health Ministers approved a new national registration standard for "Designated Registered Nurse Prescribers"
- **30 September 2025**: The NMBA's registration standard for the **Endorsement for Scheduled Medicines — Designated Registered Nurse Prescriber** commenced
- **July 2026**: The **Health Legislation Amendment (Prescribing of Pharmaceutical Benefits) Bill 2025** passed Parliament, amending the National Health Act 1953 to allow authorised nurses to prescribe PBS medicines — for the first time in Australia's history applying PBS prescribing access to a category of practitioners who are not nurse practitioners
- **1 October 2026**: PBS administrative system changes ready to support the new prescribing pathway

**Requirements for Designated RN Prescriber endorsement:**
- Current general registration as an RN with no conditions relevant to the endorsement
- **Three years full-time post-registration clinical experience** (5,000 hours within the prior six years) in a relevant clinical context
- Completion of an **NMBA-approved prescribing course** (graduate certificate level, currently the primary training vehicle)
- A **six-month clinical mentorship** with an authorised health practitioner post-endorsement
- Prescribing only under a **specific prescribing agreement** with an authorised health practitioner (a structured but not mandatory arrangement — different from the old Collaborative Arrangement, which required a named GP co-signatory)
- Prescribing from a **limited formulary** of approved medicines in accordance with clinical practice guidelines and the RN's individual scope of practice

**Key distinction from NPs:** Designated RN prescribers are not nurse practitioners. They prescribe from a restricted formulary (not the full NP-accessible PBS schedule), under a specific prescribing agreement, and with ongoing mentorship requirements. The ACNP has been explicit: "RN Prescribers are not Nurse Practitioners. The RN Prescriber role should be limited in scope of medicines that can be prescribed using an approved medicines formulary and supporting clinical practice guidelines." The ACNP's concern is role-clarity: if employers can hire an RN prescriber at an RN salary grade to do work previously requiring an NP, the incentive to invest in the seven-to-eight year NP pathway diminishes.

The new PBS prescribing law also amends the Health Insurance Act 1973 to subject nurse prescribers to the **Professional Services Review** — the peer-review mechanism for PBS and MBS integrity — applying equivalent accountability to that which applies to medical practitioners.

### 5. Structural Underutilisation — and the BBPIP Gap

Despite the 2024–25 reforms, NPs continue to face structural underutilisation in the Australian healthcare system. The **2024 APNA (Australian Primary Health Care Nurses Association) Workforce Survey** found:

- **31%** of all primary healthcare nurses were unable to work to their full scope of practice most of the time
- **34%** of nurses in residential and community aged care settings were not utilised most of the time
- Almost half of nurses had asked to take on more complex clinical duties, but only **21%** of those requests were approved
- For the first time in 2024, **35%** of PHC nurses said they would likely choose self-employment if government funding for self-employed PHC nursing were available

The underutilisation finding reflects a structural gap in the MBS architecture: the MBS continues to be principally designed for GP-led care. Two specific gaps remain:

**The BBPIP Gap:** The Bulk Billing Practice Incentive Program (BBPIP), introduced 1 November 2025 with $7.9 billion in investment and a 12.5% incentive loading on all MBS benefits, applies to **general practices** that bulk bill all patients. NP-led practices are not GP general practices and are therefore not eligible for BBPIP registration. This means:
- A GP bulk billing Item 23 earns $37.32 in rebate + 12.5% BBPIP loading = approximately $41.98 in total MBS revenue per consultation
- An NP bulk billing Item 82205 earns $27.05 with no BBPIP equivalent — a structural disadvantage of approximately 36%

**The Home Medicines Review architecture:** Under MBS Item 900, when an NP conducts a medication review, the MBS reimbursement structure still routes through the GP coordination model — the GP is reimbursed for coordinating the review, while the NP's clinical work is funded through 8CPA Community Pharmacy Programs or practice employment. This architecture has not been updated to reflect NP autonomous practice.

### 6. NPs and the Pharmacist Prescribing Debate

The ACNP's co-signature of the June 2026 safety coalition against expanded pharmacist prescribing is not merely a professional turf defence. It reflects a substantive policy argument about prescribing system architecture:

1. **Training hierarchy**: NPs complete a Master's degree (1.5–2 years) plus 5,000 hours of advanced clinical experience before prescribing independently. Pharmacist prescribers complete a Graduate Certificate in Prescribing. The ACNP argues that these training levels are not equivalent and should not produce equivalent prescribing authority
2. **Prescriber/dispenser separation**: Pharmacist prescribing from within a dispensing pharmacy creates a commercial conflict that NP prescribing does not — NPs typically prescribe without also dispensing the medicines
3. **My Health Record integration**: NPs interact with My Health Record and the Active Script List in their clinical workflow, partially mitigating the information asymmetry concern; the question is whether pharmacist prescribers will do so consistently
4. **Scope creep risk**: Pharmacist prescribing began with UTIs and contraception (low-acuity, high-volume) but the pharmacy bodies' long-term vision — as evidenced by the Guild's $1 billion savings modelling — encompasses chronic disease management, where NPs are the established provider model

## Tensions and Open Questions

**1. The RN prescriber salary arbitrage.** If employers can achieve "prescribing" from an RN at RN wages (below NP wages), the business model case for employing NPs at higher salaries in practice-based settings could erode. The NMBA and ACNP's intent is that RN prescribing is limited to a narrow formulary in defined clinical settings — but labour market pressures may push toward substitution.

**2. BBPIP exclusion is the NP payment system's biggest unresolved gap.** A 36% structural MBS revenue disadvantage for bulk-billing NP practices versus GP practices is not a minor anomaly — it materially undermines the policy goal of using NPs to extend bulk billing coverage in GP-scarce areas. The government's stated goal is 90% bulk billing by 2030, with NPs explicitly identified in the NP Workforce Plan as one pathway. The BBPIP gap works against that goal.

**3. Rural NP deployment is limited by prescribing formulary gaps.** In very remote communities (MMM6–7), NPs frequently operate as the sole healthcare provider. Their ability to manage the full range of conditions presenting in those communities depends on their PBS formulary. The 2025 expansions help, but NPs in remote settings can still be blocked from prescribing medicines that are within their clinical competency but outside the specified PBS listing authority for NPs.

**4. The PBS prescribing formulary for RN prescribers is yet to be formally defined.** The NMBA standard says "limited formulary" and "approved medicines formulary with supporting clinical practice guidelines." What medicines will actually be in that formulary, and who sets it, is not publicly specified as of July 2026. The ACNP's position is that this formulary must be tightly bounded — for example, urinary tract infection antibiotics and wound management medicines in community nursing settings. If the formulary expands through successive government decisions, the boundary with NP prescribing becomes blurred.

**5. The missing NP bulk-billing incentive is an equity failure.** In lower-income urban areas with GP shortages — including many outer-suburban and regional communities — NP-led practices are an access option. Without BBPIP, these practices cannot compete financially with GP practices for patients who expect bulk billing. The result is that the populations most likely to be seen by NPs instead of GPs — those in GP-scarce areas — are also most likely to face gap fees in an NP practice, compounding rather than alleviating access disadvantage.
