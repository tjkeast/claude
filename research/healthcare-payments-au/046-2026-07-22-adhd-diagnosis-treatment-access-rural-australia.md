# ADHD Diagnosis and Treatment Access in Rural Australia: The Psychiatry Bottleneck, Stimulant Prescribing Controls, and 600,000 Patients in an Underfunded System

**Report 046 — 2026-07-22**

**Summary:** Australia now has nearly 600,000 people receiving ADHD medication on the PBS — a figure that has doubled in four years — but the architecture of stimulant prescribing has until recently made psychiatrist involvement mandatory in most states, directly amplifying the rural psychiatry shortage documented in Report 045. Assessments cost up to $2,500 privately, wait lists run 12–18 months, and a commercial telehealth sector has emerged to fill the gap, raising serious quality concerns. A wave of state-level GP prescribing reforms in 2025–2026 is restructuring who can initiate stimulant treatment, with profound implications for rural access — but the reforms are uneven, and the Commonwealth's response to a 2023 Senate inquiry that made 15 recommendations remains thin.

**Why this follows from yesterday:** Report 045 documented that only 1.8% of Australia's psychiatry FTE workforce serves rural and remote regions, with a national median wait of 258 days for a first psychiatrist appointment. ADHD is the clearest practical consequence of that bottleneck: stimulant medications are Schedule 8 controlled drugs whose prescribing has been legally restricted to psychiatrists and paediatricians in most states, making rural Australians doubly disadvantaged — they need a service that barely exists in their community to access a medicine dispensed to 2% of the national population.

---

## Background

Attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD) is a neurodevelopmental condition characterised by inattention, hyperactivity, and impulsivity. Once considered primarily a childhood diagnosis, it is now recognised as persisting into adulthood in the majority of affected individuals. The diagnostic and treatment landscape in Australia has been reshaped by three converging forces: expanding adult recognition, PBS listing changes that made stimulant medicines available to adults with retrospective diagnoses, and a social media–driven rise in self-referral to private assessment services.

Stimulant medications — methylphenidate (Ritalin, Concerta), dexamfetamine (dexamphetamine), and lisdexamfetamine (Vyvanse) — are the first-line pharmacological treatments for ADHD in Australia. They are listed on the PBS, making them affordable at the point of dispensing once access to the prescribing system is obtained. The problem is that access to that prescribing system has been tightly controlled by state and territory drug law rather than Commonwealth health policy, creating a patchwork of rules across eight jurisdictions and an asymmetry that has fallen hardest on adults, on rural residents, and on people without the financial resources to navigate private specialist care.

## Key Findings

### 1. The Scale: 600,000 Patients, a System Under Pressure

AIHW data published in 2024–25 documents the scale of Australia's ADHD treatment population:

- **2023–24:** Nearly **600,000 Australians** received PBS-subsidised ADHD medications, covering **4.6 million prescriptions** (an average of 8 prescriptions per patient per year)
- **Population rate:** 22 patients per 1,000 Australians in 2023–24, **double** the 11 per 1,000 recorded in 2020–21, and **11-fold** the rate of 2 per 1,000 in 2004–05
- **Adult proportion:** By 2022–23, adults over 18 represented **44.6%** of all PBS-subsidised ADHD patients — a dramatic shift from a decade ago when ADHD treatment was understood as primarily a paediatric issue
- **Female growth:** For the first time in 2022–23, lisdexamfetamine overtook methylphenidate as the most-dispensed ADHD medicine for females, driven by rising adult diagnosis rates in women who were missed during childhood

A separate UNSW/NDARC bulletin (August 2025) tracking stimulant use in the general population found **2.36% of Australian adults** were taking ADHD medicines as of 2025, up from just 0.35% in 2016–17 — a nearly seven-fold increase in under a decade.

### 2. The Economic Weight: $20.42 Billion Per Year

A Deloitte Access Economics report (commissioned by AADPA, the Australasian ADHD Professionals Association) estimated ADHD's total economic burden on Australia at:

- **$20.42 billion per year** in total costs
  - Financial costs: **$12.83 billion** (81% of which is productivity loss from reduced workforce participation, absenteeism, and presenteeism)
  - Wellbeing losses: **$7.59 billion**
- **At least 800,000 Australians** are estimated to have ADHD: 281,200 children and adolescents; 533,300 adults
- Health system costs represent only ~6% of the total economic burden — suggesting that investment in diagnosis and treatment generates large returns through productivity and wellbeing improvements, even before accounting for reduced comorbidities (depression, anxiety, substance use disorder)

These figures predate the 2021 PBS listing expansion for adult lisdexamfetamine and the subsequent near-doubling of the treatment population; the true current economic burden is likely substantially higher.

### 3. The Prescribing Architecture: How State Law Created the Bottleneck

ADHD stimulants are **Schedule 8 (S8) controlled drugs** under state and territory Poisons legislation. Unlike Schedule 4 (prescription-only) medicines where any authorised prescriber can generally write a script, S8 psychostimulants have historically required prescribers to hold a specific **prescribing authority** or **permit** from the relevant state Chief Health Officer or equivalent authority.

In practice, this meant that in most states until 2025:
- **Only psychiatrists** could initiate stimulant treatment for adults with ADHD
- **Only paediatricians** could initiate for children (with some variation)
- **GPs could only continue** existing prescriptions in some states — and only with the original specialist's written authority

This rule was not a clinical necessity but an administrative consequence of drug scheduling law. Its effect was to make access to the most common ADHD medication contingent on seeing a specialist in one of the most undersupplied categories of the health workforce (psychiatry — see Report 045). For rural Australians, this created a direct compounding disadvantage.

### 4. The State-by-State Reform Wave: 2025–2026

Facing mounting pressure from patient advocacy groups, a damning 2023 Senate inquiry, and evidence of rural access failure, states and territories have moved — unevenly — to liberalise stimulant prescribing in 2025–26:

**Queensland (1 December 2025):** The most radical reform. Queensland is the **first state in Australia** to allow specialist GPs — those holding FRACGP or FACRRM fellowship — to **initiate, modify, and continue** stimulant prescriptions for adults with ADHD, within existing dose limits. Critically, Queensland did so **without requiring additional mandatory ADHD-specific training**. This is particularly significant for rural Queensland: FACRRM-qualified rural generalists, who often provide the only specialist-level medical care in remote communities, can now also initiate ADHD treatment.

**New South Wales (September 2025 Stage 1 / mid-2026 Stage 2):** NSW adopted a staged approach:
- *Stage 1 (active from September 2025):* GPs who complete an endorsed training program can **continue** stimulant prescriptions initiated by a psychiatrist or paediatrician, where the patient is on a stable dose with a handover letter. By January 2026, **749 GPs had completed Stage 1 training**
- *Stage 2 (expected mid-to-late 2026):* Trained GPs will be able to **diagnose and initiate** stimulant prescriptions independently — matching Queensland's reform, but with a mandatory training requirement

**South Australia (February 2026):** A new GP ADHD training program allows trained GPs to assess ADHD in patients aged 10 and older and prescribe stimulants; the first cohort of approximately **65 GPs** was expected to complete training by end of 2026.

**Western Australia:** Committed **$1.3 million** to train **65 specialist GPs** in ADHD diagnosis and prescribing, using an online module followed by a mandatory 6-month co-management period with specialist mentorship. The first WA GPs began operating independently from early 2026.

**ACT (February 2026):** Removed the requirement for Chief Health Officer approval before psychiatrists, paediatricians, and neurologists can prescribe within defined dosage ranges — reducing administrative burden, but not yet extending prescribing to GPs.

**Victoria, Tasmania, Northern Territory:** As of mid-2026, these jurisdictions had not enacted equivalent GP-initiation reforms. Victoria's permit system remains in place. This is a critical gap: rural Victorian and Northern Territory patients continue to depend on psychiatrist access for adult ADHD initiation.

The asymmetry between states creates a postcode lottery within a postcode lottery. A rural Queenslander with a FACRRM GP can now access treatment without a psychiatrist; a rural Victorian with the identical presentation and the same GP qualification cannot.

### 5. The Cost Barrier: $800–$2,500 for a Diagnosis

Even where a psychiatrist is theoretically available, the private-fee structure of ADHD assessment creates a financial barrier that sits entirely outside the PBS co-payment system:

- **Initial psychiatrist consultation (private):** $500–$800
- **MBS rebate (Item 291, consultant psychiatrist initial consultation >45 min):** ~$455.60
- **Out-of-pocket for initial psychiatrist visit:** $266–$566
- **Full ADHD assessment** (psychiatrist plus psychologist/neuropsychological testing): **$800–$2,500+**
- A University of Wollongong analysis (2026) described the typical patient experience as "a 12-month wait and a $1,400 bill"
- The **2023 Senate Community Affairs References Committee inquiry** found wait lists of **12 to 18 months or more** as the norm at many specialist clinics

Once in the system, PBS co-payments make medications affordable ($25 general, $7.70 concessional from January 2026), but the assessment entry cost is borne entirely privately with no bulk-billing equivalent.

### 6. The Telehealth ADHD Clinic Phenomenon: Access with Risks

In response to the psychiatrist access shortage, a commercial telehealth ADHD sector has expanded rapidly. Dedicated telehealth platforms marketing specifically for ADHD assessment commonly advertise:

- **Wait times of 1–4 weeks** (compared to 12–18 months for in-person specialists)
- **Nationwide availability** — particularly marketed to rural patients
- Assessment fees typically **at or above** in-person private specialist rates ($500–$1,000+ for an initial appointment)

This model surfaced serious concerns in the 2023 Senate inquiry. Key testimony included:

- The **Australian College of Mental Health Nurses** raised concerns that the telehealth ADHD clinic model, while improving geographic access, has also increased the risk of "inadequate assessment and misdiagnosis" through abbreviated assessments
- The **Canberra and Queanbeyan ADHD Support Group** described clinics offering "exceedingly high fees delivering a quick turnaround" with concerns of "price gouging"
- A **2025 Medical Journal of Australia** article (*"Adult ADHD in Australia: how its current commercial model for diagnosis and treatment is encouraging misdiagnosis"*, Bradlow et al.) argued the private-sector ADHD assessment model creates perverse incentives toward over-diagnosis: clinics that operate primarily on volume and patient satisfaction have less structural incentive to challenge self-referred diagnoses

The telehealth models do not remove the prescribing barrier in states that still require psychiatrist initiation: a telehealth psychiatrist is still a psychiatrist, and the consultation cost remains high. For rural patients in non-reforming states, telehealth closes the travel barrier but not the cost or access-quality barriers.

### 7. The Senate Inquiry: 15 Recommendations, 1 Accepted

The Senate Community Affairs References Committee inquiry into ADHD (referred March 2023, reported November 2023) was the most comprehensive national examination of Australia's ADHD system. It received **701 submissions** and held **3 public hearings**. Its **15 recommendations** addressed:

- Development of a **National ADHD Framework** with clear accountability and an action plan
- Improved MBS and PBS coverage for ADHD assessment
- Nationally consistent prescribing rules
- Better school and workplace support
- Improved services in prisons and child protection settings
- Research funding

The Australian Government's response (released more than 13 months after the report) accepted **1 of 15 recommendations in full** — support for uniform prescribing rules across jurisdictions. The remaining 14 recommendations were noted, deferred to other processes, or declined.

AADPA declared the response "signals a policy of maintaining the status quo rather than holistic reform." ADHD Australia described it as a "missed opportunity." The Commonwealth has not funded a National ADHD Framework, has not commissioned dedicated PBS or MBS reviews for ADHD, and has not committed to professional training programs equivalent to those launched by individual states.

### 8. The PBS Prescribing Landscape: Who Can Access What

Once a patient obtains a diagnosis and an initiating prescriber, the PBS listing landscape is relatively comprehensive:

| Medicine | PBS listing for adults | Cost (general co-pay) |
|----------|----------------------|----------------------|
| Methylphenidate (immediate-release) | Yes | $25.00 |
| Methylphenidate (extended-release, e.g. Concerta) | Yes | $25.00 |
| Dexamfetamine (dexamphetamine) | Yes | $25.00 |
| Lisdexamfetamine (Vyvanse) | Yes (adults, from February 2021) | $25.00 |

The **February 2021 PBS listing expansion** for lisdexamfetamine — extending it to adults with ADHD persisting from childhood, even when diagnosed after age 18 — was a significant access reform that partly explains the subsequent prescription surge. Previously, adults who were diagnosed as adults (without a documented childhood history) could not access lisdexamfetamine on PBS.

Without PBS subsidy, monthly stimulant costs range from **$80–$350+** depending on medication type and dose — a significant barrier for patients in the assessment pipeline who haven't yet secured a diagnosis.

### 9. The Rural Compound: FIFO Psychiatry Plus Prescribing Restrictions

The interaction between FIFO psychiatry (Report 045) and stimulant prescribing restrictions creates a particularly harmful compound effect in rural communities where Victoria, Tasmania, and the NT have not yet enacted reforms:

A FIFO psychiatrist visiting a remote community **monthly or quarterly** cannot reliably manage the ongoing medication adjustments that ADHD treatment typically requires — especially in the titration phase (the first 3–6 months of treatment, during which dose is progressively adjusted). Clinical guidelines recommend regular follow-up during titration that is incompatible with quarterly visits or inconsistent telehealth bulk-billing availability.

The consequence is a treatment pipeline where:
1. Patients may wait 12+ months for any psychiatry appointment
2. The initiating psychiatrist may be geographically inaccessible for follow-up
3. GP continuation of prescriptions (where legally available) depends on a handover letter that may not arrive, or may be from a FIFO psychiatrist who visited once and has limited longitudinal knowledge of the patient
4. Rural pharmacists may be reluctant to dispense without clarity on prescribing authority documentation

This is not a hypothetical cascade; it describes the documented experience of ADHD patients in thin rural markets across several non-reforming jurisdictions.

## Tensions and Open Questions

**1. The Queensland no-training-required reform: access vs. quality?** Queensland's decision to allow FRACGP/FACRRM GPs to initiate adult ADHD prescriptions without additional mandatory training was criticised by RANZCP and others as prioritising access over clinical rigour. Psychiatry colleges argue that ADHD is frequently comorbid with anxiety, mood disorders, and substance use — conditions that require skilled differential diagnosis. Rural GP proponents counter that FACRRM GPs are trained generalists with broad mental health competency, and that mandatory extra training creates barriers that will primarily be felt by rural patients. This tension — between gatekeeping for quality and gatekeeping for access — is unresolved.

**2. The commercial telehealth model: who regulates diagnostic adequacy?** Telehealth ADHD clinics operate within AHPRA registration requirements and MBS rules, but there is no specific regulatory framework governing the adequacy of an ADHD assessment conducted via video. The Senate inquiry identified this gap but the government did not act on the recommendation for assessment standards. As the commercial model grows, the risk of systematic over-diagnosis — encouraged by clinic incentive structures — is a serious healthcare quality concern.

**3. The gender dimension: late-diagnosed women.** The shift in lisdexamfetamine use — now the most-dispensed ADHD medicine for adult women — reflects a recognition that ADHD in females was historically under-identified due to different symptom presentation (inattention rather than hyperactivity, internalised rather than externalised behaviour). Many adult women being diagnosed today experienced significant educational and occupational disadvantage due to undiagnosed ADHD during childhood. The current system's high cost and long wait times disproportionately burden women who are already likely to have lower wealth and more career disruption as a consequence of their undiagnosed condition.

**4. Interstate prescription recognition.** All Australian states and territories now accept interstate prescriptions for S8 stimulants — a modest but real improvement. This means a patient diagnosed by a Queensland or NSW practitioner (both now allowing GP initiation or telehealth) can theoretically fill their prescription in Victoria or the NT. But the initiating prescriber relationship remains in the originating state, creating friction for follow-up and medication changes.

**5. The PBS hasn't been reviewed.** The Senate inquiry recommended a review of MBS and PBS arrangements for ADHD. The government declined this recommendation. Current MBS billing for ADHD assessment uses generic psychiatry and psychology items — there is no dedicated item for ADHD assessment, no bulk-billing incentive equivalent to the BBPIP for GP mental health work, and no rebate for the neuropsychological testing component that many clinicians consider a necessary part of a rigorous adult ADHD assessment.

## Threads to Branch Into Next

1. **State-funded mental health services and the Commonwealth-state interface** — community mental health teams (CMHTs), crisis assessment and treatment (CAT) teams, and community mental health nursing are state-funded and invisible in MBS data; how state mental health funding interacts with the gaps left by Better Access, headspace, and the NDIS; a critical dimension of rural mental health access that this series has not directly addressed

2. **The NDIS and neurodevelopmental disability** — ADHD is a neurodevelopmental condition and NDIS can fund supports for people with ADHD when functional impairment is sufficient; the NDIS eligibility threshold for ADHD, the overlap with autism (ASD frequently comorbid), and the question of whether the "missing middle" documented for psychosocial disability also applies to neurodevelopmental conditions in adolescents

3. **Aged care provider market exits and consolidation** — a long-deferred thread; provider financial viability is being restructured by the Aged Care Act 2024, RAD retention changes, and Support at Home subsidy adequacy; StewartBrown sector data shows providers in thin rural markets most at risk of exit

4. **Accommodation supplement equity in aged care** — the Commonwealth means-tested accommodation supplement paid to providers when residents cannot fund a RAD or DAP; its adequacy across regional markets; whether supplement rates reflect actual regional building/operating costs

5. **The Disability Royal Commission response and NDIS amendment interaction** — the Disability Royal Commission's final report (September 2023) made 222 recommendations affecting both NDIS and mainstream services; the NDIS Amendment (Securing the NDIS) Bill 2026 amends the scheme in ways that intersect with Royal Commission recommendations about inclusion, access, and alternative support pathways
