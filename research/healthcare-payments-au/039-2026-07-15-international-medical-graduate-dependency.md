# International Medical Graduate Dependency: How Australia Built a Healthcare System That Cannot Function Without Foreign Doctors

**Report 039 — 2026-07-15**

**Summary:** Australia relies on international medical graduates (IMGs) for 32% of its total medical workforce — nearly double the OECD average of 18.9% — and that dependency deepens sharply in general practice (43%), rural medicine (53%), intensive care (49%), and emergency medicine (44%). A 30-year-old regulatory instrument — the Section 19AB ten-year moratorium — channels IMGs into workforce shortage areas while tying their Medicare access to geographic compliance, creating a system where Australia both depends on foreign doctors and structurally constrains them. The AMA's 2025 consultation draft calls for progressive dismantlement of the moratorium; the Government's March 2025 Five-Year IMG Recruitment Scheme offers partial relief; but neither addresses the deeper question: what happens when the global competition for doctors intensifies and Australia's recruitment pool shrinks?

**Why this follows from yesterday:** Report 038 documented the ePrescribing ecosystem and the growing role of digital infrastructure in connecting and supporting the health workforce. That report's "threads to branch into next" listed international medical graduate dependency as the most persistently deferred major thread in this series — deferred across Reports 034 through 038 — because it is foundational to understanding why the health system cannot solve its workforce problems through domestic training alone.

---

## Background

### A System Built on Migration

Australia's modern medical workforce has been shaped by international recruitment since the 1970s, when shortages in rural and remote areas first drove active recruitment from the United Kingdom and Ireland. The pattern accelerated through the 1980s and 1990s as domestic medical school graduation rates failed to keep pace with population growth, geographic expansion, and rising demand. By the early 2000s, international medical graduates had become structurally embedded — not as a temporary supplement but as a permanent load-bearing component of the workforce.

The scale of this dependency is now documented with precision. According to the Australian National Centre for Health Workforce Studies (NCHWS) 2024 data insight:

- IMGs constitute **32.2%** of Australia's total medical workforce
- The OECD average for foreign-trained doctors is **18.9%**
- Australia ranks **sixth** among 38 OECD countries for the proportion of foreign-trained medical practitioners
- Australia ranks **21st lowest** for domestically trained doctors per capita — meaning it both imports doctors at an above-average rate and trains them at a below-average rate

This combination — high import reliance, low domestic production — creates a structural vulnerability that no plausible domestic training expansion can quickly remedy.

## Key Findings

### 1. The Depth of IMG Dependency by Specialty

The aggregate 32% figure understates the concentration in specific sectors.

**General Practice:** IMGs account for approximately **41–43% of all GPs** in Australia. In rural and remote areas, the proportion rises to **53% of the rural medical workforce**. Australia's 2,400+ FTE GP shortage — projected to exceed 5,500 by 2033 and potentially 17,000 by 2040 — would be catastrophic without continued IMG recruitment.

**Intensive Care Medicine:** The highest IMG concentration of any major specialty, at **49.2%** (2024 NCHWS data). ICUs in regional hospitals are particularly IMG-dependent, often with no viable domestic replacement pipeline in their catchment area.

**Emergency Medicine:** **43.9%** of emergency physicians are IMGs. Emergency departments serve as the system's last resort; their staffing risks are correspondingly high.

**Specialists overall:** More than **30% of Australia's specialists** received their primary medical training overseas. In some regional and rural hospital systems, this proportion exceeds 60%.

The data is consistent across sources: Australia's healthcare system — including those services considered most critical — could not function at current service levels if IMG recruitment were to be interrupted or significantly reduced.

### 2. Section 19AB — The Thirty-Year Moratorium

**Section 19AB of the Health Insurance Act 1973** is the central regulatory instrument governing IMG distribution. Introduced in 1996, it creates a condition for IMGs (and Foreign Graduates of Accredited Medical Schools, FGAMS) accessing Medicare provider numbers: they must work in areas with workforce shortages for up to ten years from the date of their first Australian medical registration.

- **For GPs:** must work in a **Distribution Priority Area (DPA)** — a catchment identified by the Department of Health, Disability and Ageing as having inadequate GP access relative to population need (assessed using socioeconomic factors, age and gender distribution, and existing service supply)
- **For non-GP specialists:** must work in a **District of Workforce Shortage (DWS)** — an area identified as having insufficient specialist supply relative to need

An IMG who practises outside a DPA or DWS without an exemption cannot access Medicare — meaning their patients pay in full out-of-pocket and the IMG earns no rebate-linked income. This creates a de facto geographic constraint that has been remarkably effective at channelling doctors to underserved areas.

The moratorium was introduced for three purposes: limit IMG entry to general practice, reduce Medicare expenditure, and encourage salaried roles in the public health system. The AMA's September 2025 consultation draft notes, candidly, that **"these objectives are arguably no longer relevant"** — Australia faces a severe GP shortage rather than a surplus, and the fiscal concern has been overtaken by access imperatives.

**Section 19AB exemptions** exist for a range of circumstances including:
- Working in a public hospital (hospitals are exempt; the restriction applies to private/community practice)
- Training in a recognised fellowship program
- Spousal/family circumstances that prevent DPA practice
- Working in an Area of Need location declared by a state health department

The practical effect is that exemptions are frequently sought and frequently granted, making the moratorium as much a bureaucratic burden as a genuine geographic constraint.

### 3. The Five-Year IMG Recruitment Scheme — A Partial Reform

On **12 March 2025**, the Department of Health, Disability and Ageing launched the **5-Year International Medical Graduates Recruitment Scheme**, superseding the previous Five-Year Overseas Trained Doctors Scheme.

The scheme allows eligible IMGs to reduce the ten-year moratorium to **as few as three years** by working in rural and remote locations (MMM categories) under a structured placement:

| Placement Category | Location | Moratorium Reduction |
|---|---|---|
| Category A | MMM6 or MMM7 (Remote/Very Remote) | Reduce to 3 years |
| Category B | MMM4 or MMM5 (Outer Regional/Remote) | Reduce to 4 years |
| Category C | MMM3 (Inner Regional) | Reduce to 5 years |

The scheme is administered by **Rural Workforce Agencies** (RWAs) in each state and territory and requires IMGs to:
- Be subject to Section 19AB restrictions
- Obtain fellowship (or be enrolled in an accredited training program)
- Obtain permanent residency during the scheme period
- Work in an eligible DPA or DWS

By **May 2025**, 127 international specialists had registered through a new government specialist registration pathway (a related but distinct scheme). Enrollment data for the broader 5-Year Scheme was not publicly reported by mid-2026, suggesting the scheme is early in its implementation.

**The scheme's limitations are significant.** It does not address the barriers that prevent IMGs from reaching the rural positions in the first place: employer-tied visa sponsorship, concentrated examination bottlenecks, professional isolation, and inadequate support structures. The RACGP's National Workforce Strategy 2025–30 explicitly cautions against treating IMG recruitment as a "crutch" — a structural dependency that defers the harder work of building domestic training capacity and retention infrastructure.

### 4. The Registration Labyrinth

An IMG seeking to practice in Australia must navigate a multi-stage pathway that is significantly more complex — and expensive — than equivalent processes in the UK, Canada, or the United States.

**Pathway 1: Competent Authority Pathway** — for doctors with qualifications and practice experience from designated "competent authority" countries (UK, Ireland, USA, Canada, New Zealand). These doctors can receive general registration without sitting AMC examinations. This is the fastest and least expensive pathway.

**Pathway 2: Standard Pathway** — for all other IMGs, including the large cohorts from India, Pakistan, South Africa, Sri Lanka, and the Philippines. Requirements:
1. AMC CAT MCQ Examination (multiple-choice; computer-adaptive)
2. AMC Clinical Examination (OSCE format; in-person) **or** AMC-accredited Workplace-Based Assessment program
3. A period of supervised practice (internship equivalent)
4. AHPRA registration

**Cost of the Standard Pathway:** The AMC reduced the Clinical Examination fee from $3,991 to **$3,000** (in-person) from 1 July 2025. Total pathway costs — including AMC fees, AHPRA registration, visa costs, and supervised practice requirements — typically run to **$8,000–$12,000 AUD**, before living expenses during the potentially lengthy assessment process.

**Pathway 3: Specialist Pathway / Expedited Specialist Pathway** — for IMGs with specialist qualifications; assessed by relevant specialist medical colleges. Waiting periods and assessment costs vary widely by college and specialty.

**The bottleneck:** AMC examination sittings are limited in number and geography. Candidates in major source countries wait months for available examination slots. Once in Australia, they face further delays in supervised practice placement — particularly outside the major cities — and cannot bill Medicare during that period. The UQ analysis published in November 2025 documented hundreds of qualified international physicians already resident in Australia who remained unable to practise due to unresolved administrative stages in the registration pipeline.

The AMC introduced a **free MCQ preparation app** in 2025 (210 practice questions) and announced **Part 1 exam format changes from 2026**, suggesting awareness of candidate experience issues, if not yet a fundamental acceleration of the pipeline.

### 5. The Visa Dependency Problem — Employer Leverage and Exploitation Risk

A distinctive feature of Australia's IMG system is that most IMGs arrive on **employer-sponsored visas** (Subclass 482 or 494) tied to a specific employer or location. The AMA's 2025 consultation draft identified this as a structural governance failure.

Under the current model:
- The IMG's visa status is contingent on the employment relationship
- If the employment ends (including if the IMG attempts to leave an exploitative practice), the visa may be cancelled and residency status lost
- IMGs are explicitly dependent on their employer's cooperation for visa continuation
- The 19AB DPA requirement means IMGs often cannot simply find another employer — they must find one in the same DPA

The AMA's recommendation: **decouple visa sponsorship from individual employers**, allowing IMGs to hold occupation-level sponsorship tied to a Regional Health Authority or rural workforce agency rather than a single practice. This would restore bargaining power and reduce the risk of exploitation in isolated rural practices — a risk documented in AMA member surveys.

### 6. Wellbeing: The Hidden Cost

A 2025 cross-sectional survey of IMGs in Australia — the first to specifically measure mental health outcomes at scale — found:
- **15.7%** of IMG respondents recorded a high likelihood of serious psychological distress
- **46.4%** recorded some level of burnout
- Discrimination in the preceding five years was significantly associated with higher distress and burnout

A 2026 multi-staged qualitative study (BMC Medical Education) examining IMGs moving into general practice careers in rural Australia identified a consistent set of integrated challenges:
- **Loss of status and professional identity** — doctors trained at high-status institutions abroad (specialist surgeons, hospital consultants) finding themselves in junior or supervised roles
- **Professional isolation** — solo or near-solo rural practice without specialist backup, peer review, or clinical governance support
- **Social isolation** — spouses unable to work due to visa conditions; children struggling with school transitions; communities that are welcoming but not culturally proximate
- **Restricted medical practice** — difficulty performing procedures within the scope registered abroad, due to credentialing differences and facility limitations
- **Financial insecurity** — during examination waiting periods and supervised practice, no Medicare access; savings depleted

These wellbeing deficits are not merely humanitarian concerns — they directly affect retention. An IMG who burns out or is driven from rural practice by isolation returns the area to shortage, negating the investment in their recruitment.

### 7. The OECD and Ethical Dimension: Who Are We Recruiting From?

Australia's top source countries for IMGs — **UK, India, New Zealand, South Africa, and Sri Lanka** (accounting for over 50% of IMG registrations as of 2021) — represent a mixed ethical picture.

- **UK and New Zealand**: high-income countries with their own workforce pressures; mutual migration flows are broadly balanced
- **India and Sri Lanka**: lower-middle-income countries with significant domestic health workforce shortages and high unmet need; recruitment raises genuine global equity concerns
- **South Africa**: faces a severe domestic health crisis, particularly in rural and peri-urban areas; losing doctors to Australia represents a real health system cost to South African communities

The **WHO Global Code of Practice on the International Recruitment of Health Personnel** (adopted 2010) provides voluntary guidance on ethical recruitment and maintains a list of countries from which active recruitment is discouraged. Australia is a signatory but the Code is non-binding, and its practical effect on Australian recruitment patterns has been minimal.

A **2024 The Conversation analysis** raised the question directly: with Australia planning to hire more overseas doctors, is it ethical to recruit from countries with their own doctor shortages? The honest answer embedded in policy is: Australia recruits where it can, subject to loose voluntary constraints, because the domestic alternative would require an investment in training and retention that the political system has not yet committed to making.

The **OECD International Migration Outlook 2025** documented growing global competition for health workers, with OECD countries collectively expanding recruitment from a narrowing pool of source nations. Australia is simultaneously a major recruiter and increasingly vulnerable as destination countries multiply (Germany, Canada, the UK, the Gulf states) and source countries develop retention programs of their own.

## Tensions and Open Questions

**1. The moratorium as deterrent vs enabler.** The 10-year moratorium pushes IMGs into DPAs, but the associated professional isolation, visa dependency, and inadequate support causes early attrition. Australia may be using IMGs as rural doctors for 3-5 years before losing them to burnout or migration onward, yielding a worse return than a better-supported shorter placement would provide.

**2. Domestic training vs IMG recruitment.** The RACGP's 2025–30 Workforce Strategy identifies GP training expansion as the primary lever, with IMG recruitment as a "bridge, not a crutch." But the current domestic pipeline — approximately 1,600 GP training places per year, against a projected 17,000 FTE shortfall by 2040 — cannot close the gap in any realistic scenario without continued high IMG intake for at least a decade.

**3. The source country equity dilemma.** Australia cannot simultaneously commit to global health equity and recruit aggressively from health-worker-scarce countries. The WHO Code provides cover but not resolution. Bilateral agreements with source countries (training support, return pathways, remittance acknowledgement) have been proposed but not systematically implemented.

**4. Employer-tied visas and exploitation.** The structural dependency of IMGs on employers in isolated rural locations creates power asymmetries that are difficult to monitor and document. AHPRA complaint data suggests IMGs are underrepresented in formal complaints against employers — consistent with visa-tied reluctance to escalate.

**5. Post-2026 global competition.** Germany's nurse recognition reform (2022), Canada's expansion of its IMG physician pathways, and UK post-Brexit workforce strategies are increasing competition from OECD countries for the same pool of English-speaking, Western-trained graduates. Australia's AMC pathway, at $8,000–$12,000 and up to 24+ months to complete, is not the most competitive offer on the global market.
