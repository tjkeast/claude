# How Healthcare Payments Work in Australia — Medicare, the MBS, Bulk Billing, and Out-of-Pocket Costs

**Report 001 — 2026-06-08**

**Summary:** Australia's universal healthcare system is built on Medicare, which subsidises services listed on the Medicare Benefits Schedule (MBS). Patients pay nothing when bulk-billed, or pay a gap between the provider's fee and the Medicare rebate. After a sustained decline, bulk billing rates are recovering sharply following landmark November 2025 policy changes, but out-of-pocket costs continue to rise in real terms.

---

## Background

Australia's publicly funded health system rests on three pillars: Medicare (covering medical services and diagnostics), the Pharmaceutical Benefits Scheme (PBS, covering medicines), and state/territory-run public hospitals. Medicare is legislated under the *Health Insurance Act 1973* and administered by Services Australia. It is funded primarily through a combination of general taxation and the Medicare Levy (currently 2% of taxable income for most earners, with a Low-Income Threshold below which the levy is not payable).

In 2023–24, Australia spent **$270.5 billion on health — equivalent to 10.1% of GDP**, up from the decade before but with real growth slowing to 1.1% (well below the decade average of 3.2%). The Australian Government funded $106.2 billion (39.3% of total), state and territory governments funded $82.0 billion (30.3%), and non-government sources — primarily private health insurers and individuals — funded the remaining ~30%. (AIHW, October 2025)

## Key Findings

### 1. The Medicare Benefits Schedule (MBS)

The MBS is the government's published list of over 6,000 medical services for which Medicare rebates are payable. For each item, the schedule sets a **Schedule Fee** — the government's benchmark of what is "reasonable" for that service — and a corresponding **Medicare Benefit** (the actual rebate paid).

The rebate structure differs by setting:

| Setting | Medicare Benefit | Patient liable for |
|---|---|---|
| Out-of-hospital (GP, specialist, diagnostics) | **85%** of Schedule Fee | 15% gap (plus any amount above Schedule Fee) |
| In-hospital, public patient in private hospital | **75%** of Schedule Fee | 25% gap (private health insurance can cover this) |
| Bulk-billed (any setting) | **100% of Schedule Fee** (accepted by provider) | Nothing |

The Schedule Fee is not a price cap — providers can charge any amount, and often charge above it, leaving patients to pay the full excess above the Medicare rebate. This "out-of-schedule" charging is the primary driver of growing gap payments.

**Example:** For MBS Item 23 (standard GP consultation), the Schedule Fee is $42.85 (as of mid-2025). A GP who bulk bills accepts $42.85 as full payment. A GP who charges $80 sees the patient pay $80 − ($42.85 × 85%) = $80 − $36.42 = **$43.58 out of pocket**.

### 2. Bulk Billing: The No-Gap Option

Bulk billing occurs when a provider accepts the Medicare benefit as full payment and does not charge the patient anything. The provider uses an **assignment of benefit** — a signed agreement transferring the patient's right to the Medicare rebate directly to the provider. From **1 July 2026**, new digital assignment-of-benefit processes will apply to all bulk-billed services.

**Bulk billing rates — key data points (AIHW):**

- 1984 (Medicare's first year): **51%** of GP services bulk-billed
- 2020 (40-year high): **89%**
- 2023 (recent trough): **77%**
- October 2024: **78%**
- November 2025–January 2026: **81.4%** (up from 77.1% in the same period one year earlier — the largest quarterly increase since early COVID)

The November 2025 jump follows two interlocking policy changes:

**a) Expanded Bulk Billing Incentive Items (from 1 November 2025):** Previously, GPs could only claim MBS bulk billing incentive items for concession-card holders and children under 16. The Albanese Government expanded eligibility to **all Medicare-eligible patients**. These incentive items effectively top up the MBS rebate when a GP bulk bills, making it commercially viable to avoid charging a gap.

**b) Bulk Billing Practice Incentive Program (BBPIP, from 1 November 2025):** Practices that bulk bill **all** eligible Medicare patients for **all** eligible services receive an additional **12.5% payment on every dollar of MBS benefit earned**, split equally between the practice (6.25%) and the treating GP (6.25%). Payments commenced in early 2026.

**Geographic variation remains sharp.** In 2023, annual bulk billing rates ranged from 82% in New South Wales to 53% in the ACT and 69% in Tasmania. Remote and outer-regional areas often have lower rates due to workforce shortages and the additional costs of maintaining practices in those areas.

### 3. Out-of-Pocket Costs: The Growing Gap

When providers charge above the Schedule Fee and do not bulk bill, patients face **gap payments** — the difference between what Medicare pays and what the provider charges. These costs have been rising significantly:

- Average **known gap** (disclosed upfront): rose from ~$99 to ~**$135** over the past five years
- Average **unknown gap** (not disclosed before treatment): rose from ~$418 to ~**$685** over the same period (Mandala Partners analysis)
- Without structural reform, Australians could be paying **$10 billion per year in out-of-pocket healthcare costs by 2030** (up ~$1.6 billion on current levels)
- Health CPI rose **3.2%** in the 12 months to February 2026; medical and hospital services rose **4.4%**, the primary driver (ABS)

A 2025 survey found that **46% of Australians** named rising GP out-of-pocket costs as the most pressing health system challenge for government to address, and **49%** reported visiting their GP less often specifically because of gap payments.

Gap payments are particularly acute for specialist services. Gap fees for specialist appointments can range from tens to several hundred dollars per visit, with no equivalent to the bulk billing safety net for procedural specialists.

### 4. Safety Net Mechanisms

Australia operates two Medicare Safety Nets to protect high-cost patients:

**Original Medicare Safety Net (OMSN):** Once cumulative Medicare gap payments exceed **$576 per calendar year** (2025 threshold), Medicare reimburses **100% of the Schedule Fee** (instead of 85%) for subsequent out-of-hospital services for the rest of that year.

**Extended Medicare Safety Net (EMSN):** Provides a higher rebate once out-of-pocket costs exceed a higher threshold (varying by concession status), covering a percentage of costs above the Schedule Fee — subject to item-specific benefit caps.

**Greatest Permissible Gap (GPG):** Adjusted annually for inflation, the GPG was **$105** from 1 November 2025. If a provider charges within $105 of the MBS Schedule Fee, this is considered a "known-gap" arrangement under private health insurance rules.

### 5. Private Health Insurance: The Parallel System

Private health insurance (PHI) sits alongside Medicare rather than replacing it. As of June 2025 (APRA data):

- **45.4%** of Australians hold **hospital treatment cover** (up from 45.0% at June 2024)
- **55.1%** hold some form of **general treatment (extras) cover** (dental, optical, physiotherapy, etc.)
- **68%** of hospital policies now contain exclusions for certain procedures — narrowing real coverage even as headline participation holds steady

PHI primarily covers:
1. The 25% Medicare gap for in-hospital services (the "gap" between 75% benefit and Schedule Fee)
2. Accommodation and theatre fees in private hospitals
3. Extras/ancillary services (dental, optical, allied health) not covered by Medicare

A significant incentive structure governs PHI uptake:
- **Medicare Levy Surcharge (MLS):** Taxpayers without eligible hospital cover who earn above $93,000 (singles) pay an additional 1–1.5% levy on income
- **Private Health Insurance Rebate:** The Australian Government rebates a percentage of premiums (income-tested; e.g., ~24.6% for those under 65 earning below $93,000 in 2024–25)
- **Lifetime Health Cover (LHC) loading:** People who do not take out hospital cover by age 31 pay a 2% loading per year of delay, up to 70%

## Tensions and Open Questions

1. **Bulk billing vs. practice viability:** Even with the November 2025 incentives, many GPs argue that MBS Schedule Fees have not kept pace with the true cost of running a practice. The AMA's fee list (the "AMA Rates") typically sits 40–60% above MBS Schedule Fees. The sustainability of universal bulk billing as a policy goal depends on regular, adequate indexation.

2. **Geographic inequity:** Rural and remote Australians face simultaneously lower bulk billing rates, greater travel distances, and higher overall out-of-pocket costs — yet also have worse health outcomes. Closing this gap requires more than incentive payments.

3. **Specialist access:** The bulk billing push has focused on GPs. Specialist and procedural services remain largely outside bulk billing, and gaps for these services are the fastest-growing component of household health expenditure.

4. **Private health insurance value erosion:** With 68% of hospital policies containing exclusions and premiums consistently outpacing wage growth, questions mount about whether PHI provides genuine value or primarily functions as a tax-avoidance mechanism.

5. **Sustainability of $270.5b:** With Australia's health spending at 10.1% of GDP and non-government costs growing at 3.7%, the question of who bears future cost growth — government, insurers, or individuals — remains unresolved.
