# Superannuation and Aged Care Pre-Funding: Australia's $4.4 Trillion Missed Opportunity

**Report 043 — 2026-07-19**

**Summary:** Australia sits on a $4.4 trillion superannuation pool — the fourth-largest pension pool in the world — yet barely any of it is structured to meet the care costs most retirees will face. A 2022 Retirement Income Covenant legally requires super trustees to help members manage longevity risk, but APRA's own pulse checks reveal widespread non-compliance. Treasury issued non-binding best practice principles in February 2026. APRA reduced capital requirements for longevity products from 1 July 2026. Division 296 begins taxing high-balance super accounts from the same date — but its revenue is not earmarked for aged care. The government's preferred home equity tool, the Home Equity Access Scheme, reaches fewer than 19,000 Australians. The result: Australia's two largest pools of retirement wealth — superannuation and housing equity, together worth roughly $7.4 trillion — are structurally disconnected from the aged care system they will increasingly be expected to fund.

**Why this follows from yesterday:** Report 042 found that Australia has no long-term care insurance architecture and that the government's preferred response — tighter means-testing under Support at Home — is cost-shifting rather than risk-pooling. The same report identified superannuation as the most plausible vehicle for aged care pre-funding; today's report examines whether the super system is being designed to fulfil that role.

---

## Background

Australia's superannuation guarantee has operated since 1992. The mandatory employer contribution rate reached **12% of ordinary-time earnings on 1 July 2025** — the final step in the legislated increase schedule — making Australia one of the highest-rate mandatory defined-contribution systems in the world. APRA reported total superannuation assets of **$4.5 trillion as at December 2025**, falling slightly to **$4.4 trillion at March 2026** after market movements (of which $3.1 trillion is held in APRA-regulated funds). Annual contributions reached **$226.1 billion in the year to March 2026**, up 11.3% year-on-year. Annual benefit payments were **$143.5 billion** in the same period, of which $79.7 billion were lump sums and $63.8 billion pension payments.

By any measure, the super system is large enough to be a material source of aged care funding. The question is whether it is designed to reach that purpose.

## Key Findings

### 1. The Retirement Income Covenant: Legal Obligation, Patchy Compliance

The **Retirement Income Covenant** came into force on **1 July 2022**, requiring every APRA-regulated superannuation trustee to formulate and implement a **retirement income strategy** for members in and approaching retirement. Trustees must seek to maximise expected retirement income, manage longevity, sequencing, market, and inflation risk, and provide flexible access to funds.

APRA's **2025 Pulse Check on Retirement Income Covenant Implementation** found a widening gap between leaders and laggards. While some trustees had genuinely redesigned their retirement offerings, many remained focused on minimal compliance — writing strategy documents without substantively changing how they assist members at the point of retirement. A key deficit: few funds have developed products that deal with the specific late-retirement risk of needing capital for residential aged care at a point when account-based pensions may be substantially depleted.

Only **3.5% of assets** held in superannuation pension accounts are in annuities or lifetime income products; **84% remain in account-based pensions** subject to legislated minimum drawdown rates. The minimum drawdown forces asset depletion but at a rate calculated for income needs, not aged care capital needs. For a 75-year-old, the minimum drawdown rate is 6% per annum; by the time they need a Refundable Accommodation Deposit (average approximately $573,000 in 2025), the account-based pension may be significantly depleted.

### 2. Treasury's February 2026 Best Practice Principles: A Step Forward, Non-Binding

On **23 February 2026**, Treasury issued *Guidance on Best Practice Principles for Superannuation Retirement Income Solutions* — the most concrete government signal yet about what trustees should be doing. The principles are **non-binding** but represent a benchmark against which APRA scrutiny is now calibrated.

Key expectations under the principles:
- Trustees should offer members access to a **lifetime income product** that is not the Age Pension, alongside account-based pensions and lump sums
- Drawdown pathways should be designed to convert balances into income more efficiently than minimum drawdown rates, while managing longevity risk
- Products should consider the joint life expectancy of couples (a reform from previous individual-only frameworks)
- Capital for aged care costs should be accessible late in retirement — with the principles explicitly noting that **account-based pensions are likely to be depleted by the time residential aged care is needed**, and that a longevity product such as a Deferred Lifetime Annuity (DLA) may be supporting income at that stage

The explicit acknowledgment of aged care capital needs in a Treasury guidance document is new. It does not mandate a product, but it signals that the regulatory expectation is shifting.

### 3. APRA's July 2026 Capital Reforms: Unlocking the Annuity Market

The structural obstacle to life insurers and super funds offering affordable lifetime annuities has long been the capital cost. APRA **finalised changes to the capital treatment of longevity products** in April 2026, effective **1 July 2026**. The centrepiece is the **Advanced Illiquidity Premium (AILP)** — an optional factor life insurers and trustees can apply to capital calculations for longevity products, recognising that the long-dated, illiquid nature of these liabilities allows investment in higher-yielding illiquid assets, reducing the effective capital cost.

Eligible products include term-certain annuities, lifetime income products, and retirement income stream products meeting specified criteria. Life companies planning to adopt AILP from 1 July 2026 must engage their APRA supervisor in advance.

The market impact is not yet visible — these changes are brand new — but the direction is clear: APRA is trying to make longevity products cheaper to issue and thus more competitive with account-based pensions. **Challenger**, the dominant Australian annuity provider, backed the changes publicly, and analyst estimates suggest the AILP could reduce capital costs sufficiently to allow lower annuity pricing. Whether trustees actually bring products to market at scale, and whether members choose them, remains to be seen.

### 4. The $326 Billion Stranded Balance Problem

A specific manifestation of the retirement-phase design failure: as at **31 December 2025**, more than **1.5 million Australians aged 65 and over** hold what industry analysts call "stranded balances" in APRA-regulated funds — assets accumulated in super but sitting in accounts not connected to any retirement income product or strategy. Aggregate value: approximately **$326 billion**, averaging over **$210,000 per person**.

These funds are neither generating structured retirement income nor explicitly reserved for aged care. They are largely in account-based pensions drawing down at minimum rates, or retained in accumulation accounts that trigger no drawdown obligation at all until death. For individuals in this cohort, the absence of a structured product means a large capital reserve is theoretically available for aged care — but there is no mechanism to ensure it is preserved for that purpose rather than gifted to heirs, drawn down early, or depleted on non-care expenses.

### 5. Division 296: New Revenue, No Aged Care Earmark

**Division 296** of the Income Tax Assessment Act 1997, enacted through the *Treasury Laws Amendment (Better Targeted Superannuation Concessions) Bill 2025*, takes effect from **1 July 2026**. It imposes an **additional 15% tax** on superannuation earnings attributable to balances between **$3 million and $10 million**, and an additional **25% tax** on earnings attributable to balances exceeding $10 million. The measure affects approximately **90,000 Australians** — less than 0.5% of those with super accounts — and is projected to raise significant additional revenue.

Revenue from Division 296 flows into consolidated revenue. There is no earmark for aged care, despite a straightforward policy logic: the cohort with >$3 million in super is overwhelmingly one that has benefited most from the tax-preferred accumulation of the system, and they are also the cohort most likely to face significant aged care costs above means-tested thresholds. Directing a portion of Division 296 revenue toward an aged care reserve or LTCI risk pool would have been a fiscally consistent choice; it was not made.

### 6. Housing Equity: The $3 Trillion Parallel Pool

Report 042 flagged the family home as the single largest untapped aged care funding mechanism, but the government's own instrument for accessing it — the **Home Equity Access Scheme (HEAS)** — reaches only **18,691 participants** as of December 2025, with an average loan balance of approximately $35,700. The HEAS offers a government-backed reverse mortgage at **3.95% per annum** (unchanged since January 2022), well below commercial reverse mortgage rates of 8.75–8.79% in April 2026.

Despite the rate advantage, the HEAS is chronically underused. Behavioural factors (debt aversion, inheritance preservation preferences), low awareness (UNSW CEPAR research describes it as "little-known"), and structural design limitations (non-property assets must be assessed; lump-sum access is capped) all reduce take-up. Total Australian housing equity available for release is estimated at approximately $3 trillion (of which $600 billion is potentially accessible through structured equity release). The commercial reverse mortgage market reached $3.98 billion as at early 2025, up approximately 90% in 12 months — driven partly by the need to fund RADs as residential aged care demand rises.

The HEAS and commercial reverse mortgage market together could, in principle, act as a bridge from the two-to-three-year period when care costs begin to the point at which the estate is wound up. But without policy reform to the family home means-test exemption or active promotion of equity release alongside MyAgedCare planning, take-up will remain marginal relative to the scale of need.

## Tensions and Open Questions

**1. Gender equity in superannuation is a care funding equity gap.** Women retire with an average superannuation balance approximately **24% lower than men** — a product of part-time work, career gaps for caregiving, and lower lifetime wages. Women also have longer life expectancy and higher probability of entering residential aged care. The result: those most likely to need care capital have the least of it in super. No current policy addresses this as an aged care financing problem specifically (super gender gap policies target contribution equity, not care-cost coverage).

**2. The timing paradox has two phases.** Current retirees — who began super contributions late in the system's history — often have insufficient balances to materially self-fund residential care. The structural pre-funding argument applies most powerfully to workers who are 30–50 today, for whom a well-designed system could accumulate genuine aged care reserves. The 2026 policy settings affect the current cohort first, when the funding problem is most acute and the least amenable to super-based solutions.

**3. Account-based pension depletion leaves a gap that super cannot see.** If a member exhausts their account-based pension before entering residential aged care, super has done everything the law requires — and yet the person enters aged care as a full-pensioner paying government-funded rates. There is no instrument that detects this gap prospectively and arranges care financing accordingly.

**4. Longevity products versus care products are not the same.** Lifetime annuities address income longevity risk — the risk of outliving savings. Long-term care risk is distinct: it is the risk of incurring very large, time-limited care costs late in life. A deferred lifetime annuity that kicks in at age 85 is better than nothing, but it still provides income rather than capital — and RADs require capital, not income. A genuine aged care savings product would need to be structured differently from standard annuities.
