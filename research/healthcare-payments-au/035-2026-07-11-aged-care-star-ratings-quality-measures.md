# Aged Care Star Ratings and the Quality Measures Problem: Australia's Accountability System Under Scrutiny

**Report 035 — 2026-07-11**

**Summary:** Australia's residential aged care Star Ratings system, launched in December 2022, aims to give consumers the information to choose quality care and to drive providers to improve. Three and a half years in, an independent KPMG evaluation found only 7% of residents were aware of the system when entering care; the Commonwealth Ombudsman declared ratings "not sufficiently meaningful" to inform decisions; facilities with documented standard failures were still scoring 3–4 stars; and the self-reported data underpinning the Quality Measures sub-rating has attracted persistent credibility concerns. Major structural changes — a redesigned Staffing rating from October 2025 and a redesigned Compliance rating from November 2025 — are meant to fix the most glaring gaps, but both will take years to fully materialise. Underneath all of this sits an unresolved methodological tension: the workforce shortages documented in this series make it impossible to cleanly separate poor management from inadequate staffing as the cause of bad outcomes.

**Why this follows from yesterday:** Report 034 documented the structural collapse of national health workforce planning following HWA's 2014 abolition, and the compounding workforce shortfalls that resulted. One of the direct consequences is that quality accountability tools — like the Star Ratings system — have a fundamental confounder built into their environment: when providers cannot fill roles, quality indicators deteriorate regardless of how well management performs. Understanding what the Star Ratings system actually measures — and what it cannot — is essential context for any accountability mechanism operating in that environment.

---

## Background

### The System Architecture

Australia's residential aged care Star Ratings system launched in December 2022, replacing a simpler compliance-focused accreditation display. It assigns each of Australia's ~2,580 government-subsidised residential aged care homes an Overall Star Rating from 1 to 5, displayed publicly on the My Aged Care website. The rating is constructed from four sub-ratings, each weighted in the overall calculation:

| Sub-rating | Weight (from May 2026) | What it measures |
|---|---|---|
| Residents' Experience | 33% | Resident satisfaction via independent face-to-face survey |
| Compliance | 22% | Regulatory decisions and (from Nov 2025) graded assessment against Quality Standards |
| Staffing | 22% | Care minutes delivery — total care and registered nurse care |
| Quality Measures | 22% | Clinical quality indicator performance across 5–7 clinical domains |

The system was designed with two explicit objectives: to help older Australians make more informed choices about their care, and to drive improvement by making provider performance publicly comparable.

### The Quality Indicator Program

The Quality Measures sub-rating draws on data from the **National Aged Care Mandatory Quality Indicator (QI) Program**, which has required all government-subsidised residential aged care homes to submit quarterly data since July 2019. As of the QI Program Manual 4.0 (effective April 2025), there are now **14 mandatory quality indicators**, expanded progressively from the original five:

The original five clinical measures that feed into the Quality Measures star sub-rating are:
1. **Pressure injuries** — proportion of residents with one or more pressure injuries
2. **Physical restrictive practices** — proportion experiencing restraint (physical or environmental)
3. **Unplanned weight loss** — significant or consecutive weight loss without clinical rationale
4. **Falls and major injury** — falls in total and falls resulting in hospitalisation-level injury
5. **Medication management** — two sub-measures: polypharmacy (9+ medicines) and antipsychotic use

The April 2025 expansion added: activities of daily living (ADL) decline, incontinence care, hospitalisation rates, workforce turnover, consumer experience, quality of life, enrolled nursing utilisation, allied health access, and lifestyle officer provision. These additional indicators are published quarterly but their incorporation into the star rating sub-calculation is phased — the Quality Measures sub-rating currently captures the core five clinical domains.

For the October–December 2025 quarter, data was received from **2,575 homes** — representing 99.7% of all subsidised facilities — spanning 18 consecutive quarters of trend data since July 2021.

## Key Findings

### 1. What the Clinical Data Shows

The AIHW's quarterly QI reporting shows a genuine pattern of improvement across several key clinical indicators since the program began:

**Statistically significant improvements (over 18 quarters to December 2025):**
- Proportion of residents experiencing one or more pressure injuries: declining
- Significant and consecutive unplanned weight loss: declining
- Falls resulting in major injury: declining
- Polypharmacy (9+ medicines): declining
- Antipsychotic medication use: declining
- Physical restraint use: declining (though measurement of restrictive practices overall has not significantly changed — physical restraint exclusions may account for the discrepancy)
- Workforce turnover rate: declining

**No statistically significant change:**
- Overall fall rates (not just major injury): unchanged
- Restrictive practices as a whole (when secure-area-only isolation is included): flat
- ADL score decline: flat

**Worsening:**
- Emergency department presentations and hospital admissions: **increasing** over the trend period

The antipsychotic use and polypharmacy trends are directly connected to the policy environment described in Report 031 — the aged care-specific PBS deprescribing programs and the 2019 reforms to chemical restraint regulation appear to be having measurable impact on medication rates. The restraint picture is more mixed: physical restraint has declined but restrictive practice rates overall have not improved significantly, suggesting substitution toward environmental restraints (locked units, exit-securing) which may be harder to capture and audit.

### 2. The Credibility Problem: The KPMG Evaluation

The Department of Health and Aged Care commissioned **KPMG** to conduct an independent evaluation of the Star Ratings system, completed in late 2024 and released publicly on **10 January 2025**. The findings were damaging:

- **Only 7% of surveyed aged care residents** were aware of the Star Ratings system at the time they entered care. This is the single most important failure: the system's primary objective — informing consumer choice — is structurally undermined if consumers do not know it exists.
- Among those who were aware, **scepticism about accuracy and credibility was widespread**, with distrust driven by perceptions that data is insufficiently verified.
- Opportunities to improve public awareness, data accessibility, comprehensiveness, and confidence in the compliance and staffing sub-rating methodologies were specifically identified.
- The report recommended incorporating more detailed metrics for residents from culturally and linguistically diverse backgrounds, people with dementia, and other vulnerable groups — populations whose ability to participate meaningfully in a face-to-face survey is limited.

The timing of the evaluation is notable. The system was nearly two years old when the evaluation concluded it was substantially unknown to its primary intended beneficiaries.

### 3. The Ombudsman's Intervention

On **31 October 2024** — before the KPMG evaluation was publicly released — the Commonwealth Ombudsman issued a **public statement** to a Senate inquiry declaring that the Star Ratings were "not sufficiently meaningful to help people make informed decisions about their aged care," and may in fact mislead or misinform people.

Specific concerns raised:
- **Compliance failings not reflected in ratings**: Under the pre-November 2025 system, a compliance rating would only change when the ACQSC issued a formal regulatory notice — meaning a home could be found non-compliant with the Quality Standards for an extended period without its star rating reflecting that. This created a structural lag in which consumers were receiving a signal that diverged from regulatory reality.
- **Data verification opacity**: The Ombudsman noted that while the Department indicated data checks existed, the nature and rigour of those checks were not transparent — leaving unresolved questions about what quality controls applied to self-reported QI data.
- **System complexity**: The My Aged Care website design was found to be overly complex and difficult to navigate.

The Ombudsman's intervention was unprecedented in the short history of the system, and triggered the accelerated public consultation and redesign process that led to the October 2025 and November 2025 changes.

### 4. The Self-Reported Data Problem

The core tension in the Quality Measures sub-rating is that the QI Program data feeding it is **self-reported by providers**. Homes submit their own data on falls, weight loss, pressure injuries, and medication use to the government each quarter. External audit of QI data remains limited:

- ACQSC assessors can cross-check data during on-site assessments, but assessments are periodic and assessment teams are not specifically resourced for systematic QI data validation
- The Department publishes national QI reports, but does not publish a framework for how data anomalies are investigated or what enforcement applies for misreporting
- Research has identified that the ability to account for variation in resident complexity (case-mix) at the service level is limited — the risk adjustment applied to pressure injuries, falls, and weight loss helps but does not fully account for the difference between a home that provides mainly post-acute care (high clinical acuity) and one providing steady-state dementia care

National Seniors Australia's 2025 advocacy submission and the Inspector-General of Aged Care (IGAC) both identified data verifiability as an ongoing gap. The KPMG evaluation (82% of survey respondents agreed providing more information about environmental restraint would be beneficial) suggests consumer appetite for greater transparency exceeds what the current system provides.

**The gaming concern**: Academic research on Australia's QI data found that the year-on-year correlation for Residents' Experience scores is approximately 10% — meaning a home's score one year explains only a small fraction of the variation in its score the next year. This level of noise makes it difficult to use the score as a reliable signal of stable underlying quality, and creates ambiguity about whether short-term score movements reflect real care changes or measurement variation.

### 5. The For-Profit Performance Gap

A peer-reviewed study published in 2024 (covering the April–June 2023 reporting period) found significant variation in Star Rating outcomes by facility type:

- **Government-operated facilities** had the highest odds of achieving 4 or 5-star ratings
- **Not-for-profit facilities** performed better than for-profit facilities
- **For-profit facilities** had the lowest odds of achieving high star ratings
- **Smaller facilities** (60 residents or fewer) had higher odds of high ratings than large facilities
- **Rural and remote facilities** faced structural disadvantages in achieving high rating levels

This ownership-type gradient mirrors the compliance compliance divergence documented in Report 032 — for-profit providers were more likely to receive non-compliance notices under ACQSC enforcement action. The Star Ratings data provides quantitative confirmation at scale of what qualitative evidence has long suggested. For-profit providers spend, on average, $832 less per resident per year on care than not-for-profit providers (per KPMG Aged Care Market Analysis), and this spending differential appears to translate into measurable quality differences.

In the April–June 2023 reporting period: **53.7% of 2,476 facilities received 4 or 5 stars; 44.1% received 3 stars; only 2.1% received 1 or 2 stars.** Critics have argued this distribution is too compressed — the majority concentrated at 3 stars — to meaningfully differentiate quality for consumers.

### 6. The 2025 Structural Reforms

Two significant redesigns are working through the system:

**Staffing sub-rating (from 1 October 2025):**
From October 2025, homes must meet **both** of their mandatory care minutes targets — total care minutes (average 215 minutes per resident per day, with some variation by resident acuity) and registered nurse care minutes (44 minutes) — to achieve a Staffing rating of 3 stars or more. Previously, a home could receive an acceptable (3-star) Staffing rating without meeting both targets, reflecting workforce constraint tolerances that allowed providers to benefit from a rating they did not operationally earn.

Context: only **41% of residential services** met both care minutes targets in Q4 of financial year 2024. While improvement has been significant — the number with the largest gaps dropped by ~82% between Q2 2023-24 and Q3 2024-25 — a substantial minority of homes continue to fall short. The care minutes changes to the Staffing rating will first appear in published Star Ratings from the **May 2026 quarterly update**, meaning their sector-wide impact is only now beginning to surface.

**Compliance sub-rating (from 1 November 2025):**
The redesigned Compliance rating incorporates **graded assessment against the seven strengthened Aged Care Quality Standards** — which replaced the previous eight-standard framework when the Aged Care Act 2024 took effect. Rather than the binary compliant/non-compliant signal of the old system, the new methodology provides a gradient. However, the transition is phased over up to three years as each home cycles through its registration renewal assessment under the new Standards — meaning the full coverage won't materialise until 2027-28.

## Tensions and Open Questions

**1. Information tool versus accountability mechanism.** The Star Ratings system was designed primarily to inform consumer choice, but consumer awareness is 7%. If consumers are not using the system to make choices, its primary mechanism of action — competitive pressure driving quality improvement — does not operate. The secondary mechanism (regulatory attention triggered by low ratings) has more traction, but that is an enforcement function the ACQSC already has without the ratings system.

**2. Self-reported data versus verified outcomes.** The quality indicators that form the backbone of the Quality Measures sub-rating are collected from providers rather than independently measured. The system's credibility depends on the reliability of that data, but the verification architecture is not publicly documented and the complaint that data "lacks thorough verification" was the leading credibility concern in both the KPMG evaluation and the Ombudsman's public statement.

**3. Workforce shortage as unmeasured confounder.** Only 41% of services met both care minutes targets through most of FY24. A home with chronic registered nurse shortages will produce systematically worse quality indicator outcomes — more falls, more pressure injuries, more unplanned weight loss — not because management is poor but because there are not enough hands. The Star Ratings system does not fully separate provider effort from structural market failure in workforce supply. The addition of workforce indicators to the QI Program in April 2025 is a step toward making this visible, but the relationship between staffing adequacy and clinical quality outcomes remains analytically entangled.

**4. Case-mix and acuity adjustment.** The risk adjustment applied to pressure injury, falls, and weight loss indicators helps but does not fully account for the acuity of residents different homes serve. A provider specialising in high-acuity post-acute residents will face structurally higher adverse event rates than one serving lower-acuity residential care populations. Until case-mix adjustment is robust and transparent, comparison across providers is methodologically limited.

**5. Three-year transition to new compliance ratings.** The strengthened Quality Standards framework and its graded compliance assessment are being phased in over three years. For most of 2026 and 2027, the published compliance sub-rating will reflect a hybrid of old and new assessment methodologies — potentially making comparisons across providers even harder. Homes not yet assessed under the new Standards retain their prior compliance rating.

**6. The dementia and CALD gap.** The Residents' Experience Survey reaches 20% of residents via face-to-face interview conducted by independent surveyors. For residents with advanced dementia — who represent a large and growing share of the residential aged care population — participation in a structured verbal survey is practically impossible. Similarly, residents from culturally and linguistically diverse backgrounds face language and cultural barriers to meaningful participation. The 33% weight assigned to Residents' Experience in the Overall Star Rating is thus based on a sample that systematically under-represents the most vulnerable residents.

## Threads to Branch Into Next

1. **Private health insurance at end of life and in residential aged care** — PHI covers hospitalisation but not the pharmaceutical or ongoing clinical care costs inside residential aged care facilities; what PHI actually contributes in the final years of life; the ~40% of RACF entrants who hold hospital cover and the financial interface between PHI and aged care co-contributions; direct link from Reports 026, 030

2. **Aged care financial governance and the RAD capital pool** — the Refundable Accommodation Deposit pool (~$33.6 billion in 2021, likely higher since) is the most globally unusual feature of Australian aged care finance; Aged Care Act 2024 mandatory disclosure obligations; the sustainability risk from providers dependent on RAD capital inflows as occupancy tightens; ACQSC financial compliance auditing; the for-profit market structure as financial risk amplifier

3. **Active Script List and the ePrescribing ecosystem** — the digital medication management infrastructure that interacts with the polypharmacy and antipsychotic quality indicators: ASL, Schedule 8 controlled-drugs monitoring systems (state DORA/SONDA databases), ePrescribing legislation, and the December 2026 eNRMC deadline in aged care settings

4. **International medical graduate dependency and structural vulnerability** — IMGs fill 42% of projected Australian medical workforce growth to 2048; Section 19AB rural bonding creates geographic distribution side effects; global competition for health workers is intensifying as aging populations in Europe and North America increase demand; whether Australia's IMG reliance is a managed strategy or an unmanaged structural risk

5. **Clinical guideline adherence in aged care: the CareTrack Aged findings** — a landmark study assessing whether residents of Australian aged care facilities received care recommended by clinical guidelines found significant gaps between evidence-based practice and delivered care; connects to the quality indicators narrative but from the opposite angle — measuring what should happen versus what does
