# Renal Disease and Dialysis Funding in Australia: The $8.3 Billion Crisis, Remote Access, and the Transplant Equity Gap

**Report 014 — 2026-06-20**

**Summary:** End-stage kidney disease costs Australia an estimated $8.3 billion annually and disproportionately falls on Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Australians — who face ESKD rates up to 15 times higher than non-Indigenous Australians, a transplant waitlisting rate four times lower, and a chronic structural mismatch between where dialysis is funded and where the highest-need patients live.

**Why this follows from yesterday:** Report 013 documented the $4.4 billion annual funding gap in Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander health, noting renal dialysis as a paradigm case of remote-area funding complexity and "one of the most expensive per-patient Commonwealth health commitments outside the PBS." This report examines that claim in detail — unpacking how dialysis is funded, where it falls short, and what reforms are underway.

---

## Background

### Chronic Kidney Disease in Australia

Chronic kidney disease (CKD) is defined as abnormal kidney function or structure present for more than three months. It exists on a spectrum from Stage 1 (mildly reduced kidney function, often asymptomatic) through to Stage 5 — end-stage kidney disease (ESKD) — where kidneys fail entirely and survival requires renal replacement therapy: either dialysis or a kidney transplant.

CKD affects an estimated **1.7 million Australians**, with the majority undiagnosed at earlier stages. The disease causes progressive decline and, at ESKD, binds patients to a schedule of dialysis treatment three times per week, typically lasting 3–5 hours per session, for the rest of their lives or until transplant.

The condition overlaps heavily with the chronic disease burden documented in earlier reports. The primary drivers of ESKD in Australia are type 2 diabetes and hypertension — conditions whose prevalence in Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander communities is multiples of the national average.

## Key Findings

### 1. The Total Cost: $8.3 Billion Annually

A 2024 study published in *BMC Health Services Research* — the most comprehensive national cost analysis to date — estimated the total cost of CKD to Australia at **$8.3 billion for 2019 (in 2023 AUD values)**.

Costs rise steeply with disease stage:
- Stage 1: $3,367 per patient per year
- Stage 3b: $6,572 per patient per year
- Stage 5 (non-dialysis management): $62,558 per patient per year
- Stage 5 on dialysis: **~$85,000 per patient per year**

The AIHW's earlier top-down estimate placed CKD healthcare costs at **$1.9 billion in 2020–21** (1.2% of total health expenditure), but this figure is widely regarded as an undercount because it excludes many indirect and non-allocated hospital costs. The $8.3 billion figure, which uses a bottom-up methodology incorporating hospital episodes, medications, and community care, is considered the more accurate current benchmark.

The dominant cost driver at Stage 5 is **facility-based haemodialysis**. Of the approximately **15,518 adult and paediatric patients** on dialysis in Australia:
- **76% receive in-centre haemodialysis** — three sessions per week at a hospital or satellite dialysis centre
- **24% use home dialysis** (peritoneal dialysis or home haemodialysis)

At $85,000 per patient per year, dialysis for the current cohort costs the Australian health system roughly **$1.32 billion annually** — a figure that will grow as ESKD prevalence increases.

### 2. The Indigenous Disparity: Up to 15 Times Higher Rates

The kidney disease burden is dramatically skewed toward Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Australians, with a gradient that intensifies with geographic remoteness.

**National data (AIHW, ANZDATA 2018–2022):**
- The age-standardised ESKD notification rate for First Nations Australians was **605 per 1,000,000 population** — many times higher than the non-Indigenous rate
- As at December 2021, there were **2,568 First Nations people** receiving kidney replacement therapy; **85% (2,170 patients) were on dialysis**, while only **15% (398) had received a transplant**
- Among non-Indigenous Australians, approximately **49% of patients with kidney failure had received a transplant** — more than three times the Indigenous rate

**Northern Territory data (most acute):**
Research using NT population data found that after age-adjustment:
- The ESKD **prevalence** among NT Aboriginal people was **17.97 per 1,000 population** versus **1.07 per 1,000** for non-Aboriginal NT residents
- The ESKD **incidence** was **5.26 per 1,000** versus **0.36 per 1,000** — a 15-fold gap
- Overall, ESKD and renal replacement therapy rates for First Nations Australians nationally are estimated at **6- and 8-fold** those of non-Indigenous Australians after age adjustment

**Remoteness gradient:**
Hospitalisation rates for dialysis as a principal diagnosis increased from 137 per 1,000 hospitalisations in major cities to **681 per 1,000 in remote and very remote areas** — a five-fold increase. This is not simply because remote populations are sicker (though they are): it also reflects that remote patients lack local dialysis infrastructure and therefore accumulate multiple-night hospital admissions for transport-dependent treatment.

### 3. The Funding Architecture: A State–Commonwealth Divide

Dialysis funding in Australia is split across several mechanisms, with structural consequences for equity and access.

**In-centre haemodialysis (the majority of treatment):**
In-centre dialysis is delivered by **public hospitals** and funded primarily through **state and territory governments** via the National Health Reform Agreement (NHRA). It is classified as a hospital service, not a Medicare service — meaning the Commonwealth's MBS contributes minimally to its direct cost. States fund the majority of in-centre dialysis costs from their hospital budgets.

This creates an equity problem: the highest-burden populations (remote Aboriginal Australians) live in jurisdictions — particularly the Northern Territory — with the smallest state/territory fiscal capacity and the least hospital infrastructure.

**Medicare MBS items for remote dialysis:**
A critical change came in **November 2018**, when two MBS items were created specifically for very remote (Modified Monash Model 7) dialysis:
- **MBS Item 13104**: management of haemodialysis in a very remote area by a medical practitioner
- **MBS Item 13105**: management of haemodialysis in a very remote area **by a registered nurse, Aboriginal Health Worker, or Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Health Practitioner** on behalf of a medical practitioner, where the patient's care is managed by a nephrologist (reviewed every 3–6 months)

Item 13105, attracting a rebate of approximately **$590 per session**, was a transformative policy change for the Purple House (see below). Before it existed, remote community dialysis by non-doctors generated no Medicare revenue — meaning the entire cost fell on grant funding.

**IAHP remote dialysis funding:**
The Indigenous Australians' Health Programme (IAHP) funds renal dialysis services in remote communities as one of its specific program streams, separate from the Comprehensive Primary Health Care grants that fund ACCHOs generally. This includes funding for satellite dialysis units and workforce in communities where the patient mix is overwhelmingly First Nations.

**PBS medicines:**
The immunosuppressant medications required post-transplant, and the suite of medicines managing CKD comorbidities (antihypertensives, phosphate binders, erythropoiesis-stimulating agents), are listed on the PBS. Access is broadly available via the Section 100 Highly Specialised Drugs (HSD) mechanism for post-transplant medications, and through standard PBS pharmacy dispensing for outpatient CKD management medicines.

### 4. The Purple House Model: Community-Controlled Dialysis

The most significant community-controlled response to the remote dialysis crisis is the **Purple House** (formally: Western Desert Nganampa Walytja Palyantjaku Tjutaku Aboriginal Corporation, meaning "working together to make families well").

**Origins:**
In the late 1990s, Pintupi Luritja people from remote Western Desert communities were forced to leave Country and relocate — often permanently — to Alice Springs or other urban centres to access dialysis. In 2000, community members organised an auction of works by Papunya Tula Artists that raised over $1 million, seeding what became the Purple House. This origin story — a community buying its own healthcare — is unusual in Australian health policy.

**Current operations:**
- Purple House delivers dialysis services across **20 remote communities** in the NT, WA, and SA
- It operates a **Purple Truck**: a mobile dialysis unit that drives circuits of remote communities, enabling patients to receive dialysis on Country for one to two week periods
- Services include haemodialysis, peritoneal dialysis support, aged care, primary health, and social support
- Purple House is entirely **Indigenous-owned and governed**, with an all-Yanangu Board of Directors elected by its membership

**Cost and funding:**
Before MBS Item 13105, each Purple House remote session cost approximately **$500 per treatment** — almost entirely grant-funded. The 2018 introduction of MBS Item 13105 (~$590/session) meant that "every dialysis on the Purple Truck" now attracts a Medicare rebate, substantially changing the organisation's financial viability.

Purple House is the only provider of fully remote community dialysis of this type in Australia, illustrating both the innovation possible within the community-controlled model and the dependence of that innovation on a single organisation.

### 5. The Government's Better Renal Services Program: $73.2 Million for 30 Units

A major recent Commonwealth commitment is the **Better Renal Services for First Nations Peoples** program — $73.2 million over four years to deliver **up to 30 satellite dialysis units** in remote First Nations communities, including workforce accommodation.

The program is overseen by the **Better Renal Services Steering Committee**, which advises the Health Minister on site selection (using shared decision-making with communities to identify highest-need locations) and on strategic issues including workforce, accommodation, and service sustainability.

**Funding rounds:**
- **Round 1**: Six units — Coober Pedy and Yalata (SA); Balgo (WA); Atitjere (Harts Range), Borroloola, and Ti-Tree (NT). Construction of the first unit (Coober Pedy) was completed in late 2024.
- **Round 2**: $10.35 million — Badu Island (QLD, via Wakaid Tribal Council); Tom Price (WA, Pilbara Aboriginal Health Alliance); Wiluna (WA, Ngangganawili Aboriginal Health Services)
- **Round 3**: $18.58 million — Alekarenge (Ali Curung), Galiwin'ku, Maningrida, and Yuendumu (NT); Jigalong and Warburton (WA); Normanton (QLD)

Three rounds account for approximately 16 sites; remaining sites will be selected in subsequent rounds. The program represents the largest single Commonwealth investment specifically targeting the geographic access gap for remote dialysis patients.

**Why "dialysis on Country" matters clinically:**
Research consistently documents that forced relocation for dialysis causes profound harm: social isolation, separation from family and culture, secondary depression, reduced treatment adherence, and premature death from causes unrelated to the primary kidney disease. A qualitative evaluation of the SA Mobile Dialysis Bus found that access to dialysis on Country produced measurable improvements in social and emotional wellbeing, treatment engagement, and quality of life. The IMPAKT study (a qualitative study of ESKD experience for First Nations Australians) documented that geographic dislocation may in itself accelerate decline. The Better Renal Services investment is, in part, a recognition that the indirect wellbeing costs of centralised dialysis — borne by patients and communities — represent a health cost not captured in the $85,000/patient/year figure.

### 6. The Transplant Access Gap: A Structural Equity Failure

Kidney transplantation is the superior renal replacement therapy by almost every measure: better quality of life, lower mortality, lower long-term cost (transplant eliminates the $85,000/year dialysis cost after initial surgical costs), and freedom from the three-sessions-per-week schedule.

Yet the transplant access gap for First Nations Australians is severe:

- As at December 2020, just **2% of First Nations Australians with kidney failure were waitlisted for transplant** (47 of 2,046 patients) — compared to **8% of non-Indigenous patients** (904 of 11,214)
- Among First Nations patients, only **15% had received a transplant**; among non-Indigenous patients, **49% had**
- Geographic isolation is the strongest predictor of non-waitlisting: patients in remote areas are significantly less likely to be referred, evaluated, and listed — a disparity that widens with increasing remoteness

The barriers are well-documented and multi-factorial: remoteness from transplant evaluation centres (most are in major city teaching hospitals); higher rates of comorbidities (obesity, infection, cardiovascular disease) that are evaluated as contraindications; cultural and health literacy barriers in navigating evaluation systems; lower rates of investigation and referral by clinicians; and documented instances of systemic cultural bias. Additionally, cultural connections to Country may mean that some patients make an informed choice to prioritise remaining on Country over pursuing urban transplant pathways — but research indicates that many non-waitlisted patients report they were not given adequate opportunity to understand or pursue transplantation.

**The NIKTT response:**
The **National Indigenous Kidney Transplantation Taskforce (NIKTT)** was established in 2019 with $2.3 million in Commonwealth seed funding following a report from an Expert Panel of the Transplantation Society of Australia and New Zealand (TSANZ), which identified 36 specific recommendations to improve access and outcomes. The NIKTT has focused on:
- Establishing Indigenous Reference Groups at transplant units across the country
- Developing a national data dashboard to track equity across the transplantation pathway
- Trialling multidisciplinary service models to improve pre-transplant evaluation of Indigenous patients
- Developing culturally tailored patient education resources
- Convening national Gatherings to drive cross-jurisdictional coordination

A second national Gathering was held at SAHMRI in Adelaide in **February 2025**, producing recommendations on data collection, culturally safe evaluation pathways, and governance of ongoing equity work. Commonwealth bridging funding supported the NIKTT Secretariat through 2024; longer-term funding arrangements beyond the 2025 Gathering remained under development.

### 7. Home Dialysis: The Unrealised Potential

A major policy lever for reducing the cost and improving the equity of dialysis is the shift toward **home dialysis** — either peritoneal dialysis (PD, a different mechanism using the abdominal membrane) or home haemodialysis (HHD, a machine in the home).

Home dialysis advantages:
- Lower cost than facility-based treatment (dialysis at home avoids high overhead of hospital-based centres)
- Greater freedom and flexibility for patients
- More frequent, gentler dialysis schedules associated with better outcomes (nocturnal HHD)
- Eliminates the need for 3x-weekly transport to a centre

Australia's national home dialysis rate is **24%** — higher than some comparable countries but well below the government's aspiration. Among remote Indigenous patients, home dialysis rates are substantially lower due to structural barriers: inadequate housing (overcrowded, lacking clean water and electricity), insufficient community health infrastructure to support PD peritonitis management, and workforce gaps in training patients.

The MJA published a landmark commentary in 2025 — "Dialysis care in Australia: a call for reform and innovation" — that called for systematic reform of dialysis financing to incentivise home modalities, including tying hospital funding for dialysis to modality mix targets and investing in the supportive infrastructure (housing, community nursing) that would enable home dialysis in remote communities.

## Tensions and Open Questions

**1. The state–Commonwealth funding split penalises high-need jurisdictions.** Funding in-centre dialysis through state hospital budgets means the NT — with the highest Indigenous ESKD burden and the smallest budget — bears costs that are disproportionate to its fiscal capacity. The NHRA has historically not included specific provisions that adjust for this. The Commonwealth's Better Renal Services program partially compensates, but the structural inequity in the hospital funding baseline remains.

**2. Is $73.2 million enough?** Thirty dialysis units over four years sounds substantial, but there are over 2,500 First Nations people on dialysis — and the expansion of units to communities does not by itself solve the workforce question. Each unit requires trained nurses or Aboriginal Health Practitioners, nephrologist oversight via telehealth, consumables, and machine maintenance. Several of the targeted communities face simultaneous health workforce shortages across every clinical category.

**3. The home dialysis infrastructure gap.** Enabling home peritoneal dialysis in communities where houses are overcrowded, damp, and lack adequate water/electricity is not primarily a health policy problem — it is a housing policy problem. Yet the health system is expected to provide dialysis without the housing reform that would enable the most cost-effective modality.

**4. The transplant pathway requires urban engagement.** The most effective long-term solution for ESKD — transplantation — requires evaluation and surgery at metropolitan teaching hospitals. Making this pathway more accessible to remote Indigenous Australians requires investment in supported travel and accommodation, culturally safe pre-transplant care in urban settings, and post-transplant follow-up that can be managed closer to home. None of this is straightforwardly a Medicare or hospital funding question; it requires cross-sectoral coordination across health, housing, and community services.

**5. Diabetes prevention remains the highest-value intervention.** Almost all of the downstream cost and suffering in this report traces back to type 2 diabetes and hypertension — themselves largely preventable conditions with strong social determinants. A dollar invested in First Nations primary care, healthy food access, safe housing, and clean water is likely worth many dollars in prevented dialysis. The renal funding discussion cannot be fully separated from the prevention funding discussion examined in Report 013.

## Threads to Branch Into Next

1. **Headspace and youth mental health block grants** — deferred six times; youth mental health infrastructure funded through PHN grants rather than MBS; urgently needs its own report.

2. **Medical Costs Finder Bill 2026** — specialist fee transparency legislation, deferred six times since first flagged in Report 002. Gap fees for specialist consultations are directly relevant to nephrology access (patients needing specialist CKD management face OOP costs).

3. **NDIS and psychosocial disability in regional and remote areas** — the "missing middle" in mental health support; thin NDIS provider markets amplified in communities already carrying dialysis transport burdens.

4. **US trade pressure on PBS pricing** — deferred from Reports 007–013; specifically relevant here because pharmaceuticals for post-transplant immunosuppression and CKD management represent a large PBS subcategory, and any US trade deal concessions on PBS pricing would affect affordability of these medicines.

5. **The Medical Costs Finder and specialist transparency reform** — nephrology is one of the specialties where significant gap fees can deter early referral; combined with primary care access barriers for Indigenous patients, specialist OOP costs may delay ESKD diagnosis and referral to transplant services.
