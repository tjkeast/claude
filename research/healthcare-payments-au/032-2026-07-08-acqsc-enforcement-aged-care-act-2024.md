# ACQSC Enforcement under the Aged Care Act 2024: Australia's New Rights-Based Regulatory Model and Its Enforcement Gap

**Report 032 — 2026-07-08**

**Summary:** The Aged Care Act 2024 commenced on 1 November 2025, handing the Aged Care Quality and Safety Commission (ACQSC) the strongest enforcement toolkit in the sector's history — civil penalties up to 4,800 penalty units, banning orders now applicable to providers as well as workers, mandatory registration, and risk-tiered supervision. Approximately 392 worker banning orders have been issued since January 2023. Yet the Inspector-General of Aged Care's September 2025 progress report concluded that sector transformation has "not yet been achieved," with reforms characterised as "disparate and disconnected" rather than systemic. The enforcement of chemical restraint — the direct regulatory link from Report 031 on polypharmacy — remains on a transition timeline extended to December 2026, and residential aged care compliance against the strengthened Quality Standards averaged only 83% in 2024–25. The new Act is the most significant legal achievement; converting it into genuine resident safety requires enforcement credibility that has not yet been demonstrated.

**Why this follows from yesterday:** Report 031 found that psychotropic medicines are pervasively used as chemical restraint in Australian residential aged care (RACFs) and that the Aged Care Act 2024 explicitly classifies this as a regulated restrictive practice — but the enforcement architecture underpinning that classification had not yet been examined. Today's report examines the ACQSC's regulatory toolkit, what has changed since 1 November 2025, and where the accountability gap between law and practice persists.

---

## Background

The Royal Commission into Aged Care Quality and Safety (2018–2021) delivered a scathing verdict on the state of Australia's aged care regulatory system. Its final report — 148 recommendations — found that the existing framework was "not protecting the people it was meant to serve." The Commission described a system marked by chronic understaffing, widespread neglect, inadequate clinical governance, and a regulator (then called the Aged Care Quality and Safety Commission, established in 2018 to replace the earlier Australian Aged Care Quality Agency) that lacked the legal tools and resources to act decisively on what it found.

The five years since that report have produced the most significant legislative and regulatory overhaul in the sector's history. The new Aged Care Act 2024 — passed 25 November 2024, in force 1 November 2025 — replaces the Aged Care Act 1997. The new Act is rights-based and person-centred in its architecture. But whether its vastly expanded enforcement provisions will translate into genuine improvement in the care of Australia's 240,000 residential aged care residents remains the central question for regulators, providers, advocacy groups, and the people who depend on the system.

## Key Findings

### 1. The Old Regulatory Model: Why It Failed

Before the Aged Care Act 2024, the ACQSC operated under a primarily compliance-and-accreditation model. Providers were assessed against eight Quality Standards (from 2019) in a largely pass/fail system where accreditation — typically a three-year grant — was the primary regulatory lever. The system had several critical weaknesses:

- **Enforcement lag:** The ACQSC could identify non-compliance but escalation to serious sanctions required lengthy administrative processes that left residents at risk during extended remediation periods.
- **Accreditation as the only big stick:** The threat of losing accreditation (and therefore government funding) was disproportionate for many compliance failures — providers knew that government was unlikely to pull accreditation from a large for-profit operator, given the disruption to residents and the market.
- **Incident reporting gaps:** The Serious Incident Response Scheme (SIRS) was introduced in March 2021 for residential aged care but home care providers were not covered until November 2025.
- **Individual worker accountability gap:** Banning orders for aged care workers were introduced in December 2022 under the Aged Care Amendment (Strengthening Quality and Safety) Act 2021, but the tool was new and the register was only beginning to accumulate data.
- **No civil penalties for care failures:** The old Act had no civil penalty framework allowing courts to impose financial sanctions on providers for care standard breaches.

### 2. The Aged Care Act 2024: What Changed on 1 November 2025

The new Act is comprehensive in scope. Key changes effective from 1 November 2025 include:

**Seven Strengthened Quality Standards (replacing eight)**

The previous eight Quality Standards were reorganised and strengthened into seven, covering: Standard 1 (The Individual — rights, dignity, identity); Standard 2 (The Organisation — governance, risk, workforce); Standard 3 (The Care and Services — assessment, planning, delivery); Standard 4 (The Environment); Standard 5 (Clinical Care — safety, evidence-based practice); Standard 6 (Food and Nutrition); Standard 7 (The Residential Community).

The restructuring places individual rights at the apex (Standard 1) rather than as an embedded consideration. Chemical restraint falls primarily under Standard 2 (restrictive practices governance) and Standard 5 (clinical care), with documentation, consent, and review obligations across both.

**Universal Registration System**

All providers must now hold formal registration with the ACQSC. Registration is assigned to one of six categories by service type and risk level:
- Categories 1–3: lower-complexity home and community services
- Category 4: home care packages and NDIS-adjacent services
- Category 5: specialised services (dementia-specific units, palliative care)
- **Category 6: Residential aged care** (the highest-scrutiny category)

Registration is for three years but can be varied, suspended, or revoked at ACQSC discretion. From 1 November 2025, all existing government-funded providers were "deemed registered" in their relevant categories — no new applications required. Registration renewal will, over time, require conformance audits against the strengthened Quality Standards.

**Risk-Tiered Supervision Model**

Rather than uniform treatment of all providers, the ACQSC now allocates each provider a **supervision status** corresponding to one of four tiers:
1. **Risk surveillance** — standard monitoring, periodic assessment contacts, primarily data-driven
2. **Targeted supervision** — elevated monitoring, more frequent assessment contacts, specific risk areas under scrutiny
3. **Active supervision** — active regulatory engagement, compliance notices possible, enhanced oversight
4. **Heightened supervision** — intensive regulatory engagement, escalated enforcement risk, may include conditions on registration

The tier determines the frequency and intensity of ACQSC contact and the speed of escalation to formal enforcement action. This replaces the old "accreditation period" framework with a dynamic, risk-responsive model — closer to the model used by the Australian Prudential Regulation Authority (APRA) for financial institutions.

**Expanded Civil Penalty Framework**

The new Act introduces civil penalties that can be pursued through the Federal Court for serious or persistent breaches:
- **Up to 4,800 penalty units** for the most serious breaches (approximately AUD $1.66 million at the current $345/penalty unit value) for organisations
- **Up to 240 penalty units** (approximately AUD $82,800) for individual workers in egregious cases
- **Infringement notices** (administrative fines not requiring court action): up to 500 penalty units for specified breaches, including victimising a whistleblower

These penalties represent a qualitative shift. A $1.66 million civil penalty is financially significant for most aged care providers; under the old Act, no equivalent sanction existed.

**Banning Orders: Extended to Providers**

Worker banning orders — available since December 2022 — were extended from 1 November 2025 to include **provider organisations** as well as individuals. The ACQSC can now ban a provider from offering specified categories of aged care services entirely, in addition to banning individual workers. This closes the gap under the old framework where a provider's organisation could continue to operate while individual workers were removed.

**Expanded SIRS Scope**

The Serious Incident Response Scheme, previously restricted to residential aged care, was extended on 1 November 2025 to all Support at Home (SaH) providers — the home care system that replaced Home Care Packages from the same date. SIRS now covers approximately 250,000 home care recipients in addition to residential aged care residents.

**Whistleblower Protections**

The new Act introduces explicit protections for aged care workers who report concerns about care quality or provider compliance. Victimising a whistleblower is a specific civil penalty offence (up to 500 penalty units, approximately AUD $172,500).

### 3. Enforcement Statistics: What the Data Shows

**Worker Banning Orders (most concrete enforcement metric available)**

Approximately **392 banning orders** have been issued against aged care workers since the banning order regime commenced in January 2023, as at February 2026 (based on the publicly maintained ACQSC banning order register). The geographic distribution reflects workforce patterns and investigation capacity:
- New South Wales: 145 (37%)
- Queensland: 94 (24%)
- Victoria: 66 (17%)
- South Australia: 44 (11%)
- Western Australia: 27 (7%)
- Tasmania: 10 (3%)
- ACT: 4 (1%)
- Northern Territory: 2 (<1%)

Grounds for banning orders include: unsuitability to work in aged care; breach of the Aged Care Code of Conduct; posing a safety risk to residents; conviction for specified offences. The public register allows prospective employers to verify worker eligibility before engagement.

**Complaints (2024–25)**

The ACQSC received approximately **10,658 complaints** in 2024–25:
- Residential care: 6,302 complaints
- Home Care Packages: 3,848 complaints
- Commonwealth Home Support Programme: 508 complaints

The top complaint issues in residential aged care over 2023–25 were consistently **medication administration and management** and, from Q2 2024–25, **falls prevention and post-fall management** (rising to become co-equal with medication issues). Both of these are directly linked to the polypharmacy findings of Report 031.

**Serious Incident Response Scheme (SIRS)**

SIRS notifications in residential care — which had been rising since Q1 2022–23 — showed their first quarterly decrease in Q4 2024–25 (April–June 2025). The ACQSC noted this may reflect improved reporting compliance (driving numbers up before genuine improvement shows as decline) rather than a true reduction in incidents. In home care, neglect accounted for 60% of the 1,529 reported incidents in the same period.

**Compliance Rates Against Quality Standards**

Residential services' compliance with the Quality Standards ranged **81–87% across quarters in 2024–25, averaging approximately 83%**. This means roughly 17% of assessment contacts found at least one area of non-compliance. Home services have been consistently 15–20 percentage points lower than residential care — approximately 63–68% compliance — a persistent and concerning gap.

**Star Ratings and Public Accountability**

Since 2022, every residential aged care provider has a published Star Rating across four dimensions:
- Compliance with Quality Standards: **40% weighting**
- Residents' experience surveys: **33% weighting**
- Staffing levels (care minutes): **15% weighting**
- Quality measures (falls, pressure injuries, polypharmacy, antipsychotic use): **12% weighting**

Non-compliance findings are recorded on providers' public profiles on My Aged Care, giving consumers and families access to enforcement history.

### 4. Chemical Restraint Under the New Act: Enforcement with a Transition Delay

The direct link between Report 031 and today's report is the regulation of chemical restraint — psychotropic medicines used to control behaviour (particularly dementia-related BPSD) rather than for clinical therapeutic purpose. Under the Aged Care Act 2024 and the Aged Care Rules 2025, chemical restraint is classified as a **restrictive practice** subject to:

- A requirement for formal assessment by a medical practitioner or nurse practitioner confirming the resident poses a risk of harm to themselves or others before any chemical restraint is initiated
- Mandatory **consent processes** — informed consent from the resident or their authorised representative
- Documentation of the rationale, expected duration, review dates, and non-pharmacological alternatives trialled
- A behavioural support plan as a precondition for ongoing use
- **SIRS reporting** if chemical restraint is used in a way that was unauthorised (i.e., without proper consent or assessment — classified as a Priority 1 reportable incident requiring notification to ACQSC within 24 hours)
- ACQSC powers to suspend or cancel registration and issue banning orders for systematic misuse

However, the full implementation of the restrictive practices framework under the new Act is on a **transition timeline extended to 1 December 2026** — 13 months after the Act's commencement. This reflects the practical complexity of transitioning approximately 2,400 residential providers to new consent, assessment, and documentation requirements. During the transition period, providers must comply with the old restrictive practices rules while preparing for the new framework.

This delay is clinically significant. It means that for the 20%+ of residents currently being dispensed antipsychotics — some proportion of whom are receiving chemical restraint without proper consent or adequate BPSD assessment — the new, stronger enforcement framework is not yet fully operational.

### 5. The Inspector-General's Verdict: Transformation Not Achieved

The Inspector-General of Aged Care (IGAC), an independent statutory officer established in 2022 to oversee the implementation of the Royal Commission's 148 recommendations, released a **2025 Progress Report in September 2025**. The verdict was sobering:

> *"The aged care system has not been transformed. Australians cannot be confident that the system will deliver the high-quality care and human rights envisaged by the new Aged Care Act."*

The Inspector-General found:
- Reform efforts have consisted of "disparate and sometimes disconnected changes targeting specific issues rather than fundamental planks for systemic change"
- No evidence that any "policy logic or theory of change approach was developed" to link individual reforms into a coherent transformation program
- The passage of the Aged Care Act 2024 itself was identified as the most significant achievement — but questions remain about how human rights under the Act will be **enforced in practice**
- Specific concerns remain about funding inconsistencies affecting Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander aged care services and gaps between NDIS psychosocial supports and aged care
- Co-payment changes under Support at Home risk "pushing people prematurely into residential care"

The Inspector-General stopped short of saying the reforms have failed, but made clear that achieving the Royal Commission's transformative intent requires a coherent implementation strategy that remains absent.

### 6. The ACQSC's Regulatory Priorities for 2025–26

The ACQSC published a **Regulatory Strategy 2025–26** (November 2025) identifying its priorities under the new Act. Key themes:

- **Care minutes compliance**: enforcing the 215 minutes/resident/day mandate (44 minutes RN) now linked to AN-ACC funding from April 2026
- **Restrictive practices**: monitoring chemical restraint rates via quality indicators and complaint data; the first full year of mandatory SIRS reporting from home care services
- **Clinical governance**: Standard 2 (organisational governance) and Standard 5 (clinical care) are priority audit dimensions given the evidence that systematic governance failures, not individual worker misconduct alone, underlie the worst care outcomes
- **Worker screening**: ensuring all aged care workers have satisfactory background checks under the new worker registration requirements
- **Financial and prudential compliance**: 54 Home Care Package providers and 97 residential providers were audited for financial and prudential compliance in 2024–25, with high compliance rates found

## Tensions and Open Questions

**1. Civil penalties: still largely untested.** The civil penalty framework came into force on 1 November 2025. As of mid-2026, there is no published evidence that the ACQSC has pursued civil penalty proceedings through the Federal Court. The threat of civil penalties is new and may be deterring the worst behaviour, but it is also possible that the ACQSC is conserving its litigation resources, still building cases, or pursuing compliance through lower-level enforcement tools. The credibility of the civil penalty regime will depend on it actually being used.

**2. The gap between complaints and enforcement action.** The ACQSC received 6,302 residential aged care complaints in 2024–25. Medication management was the most common issue over three consecutive years. Yet the connection between complaints received and enforcement actions taken remains opaque in published data. The ACQSC does not routinely publish the ratio of complaints to compliance notices, sanctions, or banning orders — making it difficult to assess whether complaints are generating enforcement or being resolved through lower-level engagement.

**3. For-profit versus not-for-profit compliance divergence.** Report 024 identified that for-profit residential aged care providers have historically shown lower compliance against quality standards and higher rates of regulatory intervention. The new regulatory model's tiered supervision should, in theory, direct more intense scrutiny toward high-risk providers. Whether this plays out in practice — or whether industry concentration among large for-profit chains creates political and commercial pressures on enforcement — is a live question.

**4. Chemical restraint transition gap.** The 13-month transition period to December 2026 for full restrictive practices compliance creates a period during which approximately 40,000+ aged care residents potentially receiving chemical restraint without current-era consent and documentation requirements face a regulatory gap. The ACQSC is monitoring this via SIRS reporting and quality indicators, but its enforcement powers under the full restrictive practices framework will not be fully operational until late 2026.

**5. The 83% compliance average masks a 17% gap.** Approximately one in six assessment contacts finds at least one Quality Standard breach. Given that assessment contacts are themselves risk-stratified (meaning the worst providers are more likely to be assessed), the real rate of non-compliance in the sector at any given time is likely higher. The graded audit model — which produces detailed outcome-based results rather than a simple pass/fail — should over time reveal where the persistent gaps are; but it requires the ACQSC to publish its analysis transparently.

**6. The Inspector-General's theory-of-change critique.** Perhaps the most profound tension is the Inspector-General's finding that the reform program lacks a coherent theory of change. The aged care system is changing on multiple fronts simultaneously — new Act, new Quality Standards, new funding model (AN-ACC, Support at Home), care minutes mandate, residential bed construction incentives, workforce strategy — but these are being implemented through different Commonwealth agencies, with different timelines, without a clear account of how they collectively produce the outcomes the Royal Commission envisioned. This is not merely an academic critique: it means there is no mechanism to identify when reforms are interacting perversely, to correct course, or to know when transformation has actually been achieved.

## Threads to Branch Into Next

1. **My Health Record and digital health infrastructure for aged care** — the enforcement of medication management and chemical restraint obligations depends on having accurate, up-to-date medication records accessible to GPs, pharmacists, nurses, and ACQSC auditors; My Health Record's shared medicines record function in the RACF context; upload rates; the Aged Care Rules 2025 data-sharing obligations; digital governance as a foundation for safe care (flagged in Reports 029, 030, and 031)

2. **Health Workforce Australia reconstitution and the workforce governance gap** — the ACQSC's ability to enforce care minutes compliance and clinical governance standards depends on there being enough workers; the absence of a national health workforce planning body since HWA was abolished in 2014 means the pipeline for RNs, pharmacists, and allied health in aged care is not centrally modelled or managed; proposals for reconstitution

3. **Aged care quality measures and the Star Ratings system in depth** — the five quality measures published in Star Ratings (falls resulting in hospitalisation, pressure injuries, unplanned weight loss, restraint use, medication-related incidents) are the public-facing layer of the enforcement system; how they are collected, their limitations, and whether they are driving genuine quality improvement or strategic behaviour

4. **PHI coverage at end of life in aged care** — private health insurance has almost no role in residential aged care (PBS prohibition on PHI covering government-subsidised medicines; PHI hospital benefits cease on permanent residential care entry); but PHI remains relevant for hospital episodes during which medication harms are treated; the financial interface between PHI and aged care for the 40% of residents who hold hospital cover on entry

5. **Aged care financial governance and the RAD capital pool** — the new Aged Care Act 2024 introduced mandatory financial and prudential reporting, including disclosure of Refundable Accommodation Deposit (RAD) balances and investment practices; the ACQSC audited 97 residential providers for financial compliance in 2024–25; the sustainability risk of RAD-dependent providers is a quality and enforcement issue, not just a financial one
