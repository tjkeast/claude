# Home Equity Release for Aged Care Financing: Australia's $3 Trillion Housing Pool and the Scheme Nobody Uses

**Report 044 — 2026-07-20**

**Summary:** Australians aged 60 and over collectively hold approximately $3 trillion in home equity — yet less than 1% of it flows into retirement or aged care financing. The federal government's own reverse mortgage vehicle, the Home Equity Access Scheme (HEAS), reaches fewer than 19,000 people at terms cheaper than any commercial product. The commercial reverse mortgage market is growing at 12.4% per year and now holds around $5.5 billion in outstanding loans across 40,000 households. Despite this, the architecture for deploying housing wealth toward aged care fees remains fragmented, structurally misaligned, and chronically underused. A new RAD retention rule from November 2025, a lump-sum cap that cannot fund even a partial deposit, and a means test that rewards staying in the family home all compound the problem. Australia is storing its largest private wealth pool at the precise moment of greatest public cost — and has built no effective bridge between the two.

**Why this follows from yesterday:** Report 043 found that Australia's $4.4 trillion superannuation pool is structurally disconnected from aged care financing, and that the Home Equity Access Scheme — sitting over a $3 trillion housing wealth pool — is chronically underused. Today's report examines why, and what it would take to fix it.

---

## Background

The family home is the single largest financial asset held by most older Australians. The Australian Bureau of Statistics Household Income and Wealth survey puts average owner-occupier housing net worth for those aged 65–74 at approximately $800,000–$900,000, and for those aged 75 and over at slightly less due to some drawdown. Aggregate housing equity held by Australians aged 60 and over is estimated at **$3 trillion**, of which around **$600 billion is potentially accessible** via equity release products based on borrower age, loan-to-value ratios, and property location (Deloitte Australian Reverse Mortgage Survey 2026).

By contrast, the total outstanding balance of all reverse mortgage products — government and commercial combined — was **$5.5 billion as at 30 June 2025**: just 1.8% of the technically accessible pool, and less than 0.2% of the total equity held. Australia's housing wealth is the largest dormant aged care funding mechanism in existence. It is dormant by design as much as by choice.

Residential aged care in Australia has two main accommodation payment mechanisms:
- **Refundable Accommodation Deposit (RAD):** A lump-sum, interest-free bond repayable on death or discharge (minus retention). National median RAD approximately $575,000 in 2026; Sydney and Melbourne metro rooms regularly exceed $1 million.
- **Daily Accommodation Payment (DAP):** A daily rental equivalent, calculated at a government-set interest rate — currently **5.7% per annum** on the published maximum accommodation price. For a room with a maximum price of $575,000, the DAP would be approximately $89.50 per day ($32,000/year).

Residents may pay a combination of RAD and DAP. A resident who cannot afford the full RAD can pay a partial deposit and a proportional DAP. From **1 November 2025**, under the new Aged Care Act framework, providers retain **2% per annum** of RADs for new entrants — meaning the RAD is no longer fully refundable for residents entering care after that date. Over a two-year stay, a $575,000 RAD would generate $23,000 in provider retention. This change, while modest in isolation, shifts the calculus between paying a RAD (capital deployed, partially eroded) and funding via DAP (income required, no capital commitment).

## Key Findings

### 1. The Home Equity Access Scheme: Structurally Attractive, Functionally Marginalised

The **Home Equity Access Scheme (HEAS)** — renamed from the Pension Loans Scheme in 2022 — is the Australian Government's own reverse mortgage product. Key 2026 parameters:

| Feature | HEAS 2026 |
|---------|-----------|
| Eligibility age | 67 (Age Pension age) |
| Interest rate | **3.95% per annum** (compounding fortnightly; unchanged since January 2022) |
| Maximum regular payment | 150% of maximum Age Pension (singles: ~$1,723.50/fortnight; couples: ~$2,598.30/fortnight combined) |
| Maximum lump sum (12 months) | 50% of annual maximum Age Pension (~$13,832 for singles; ~$20,852 per person for couples) |
| Lump sum events per year | 2 |
| Security | Real property in Australia |
| Negative equity protection | Statutory no-negative-equity guarantee (since 2022) |
| Administration | Services Australia (Centrelink) |
| Participants (Dec 2025) | **18,691** |

The HEAS interest rate of **3.95%** is dramatically below the commercial market. Commercial reverse mortgage rates in Australia were approximately **8.75–8.79% per annum** in April 2026 (Heartland Bank, the market leader). A borrower choosing HEAS over a commercial product saves over 4.8 percentage points annually — on a $300,000 loan, that is approximately **$14,400/year** in avoided interest.

Despite this rate advantage, HEAS participation remains confined to fewer than 19,000 Australians — a rate of penetration well under 0.5% of eligible homeowning retirees.

### 2. The Lump Sum Cap: Why HEAS Cannot Fund a RAD

The most structurally significant limitation of HEAS for aged care financing is the **lump sum payment cap**. The maximum lump sum accessible in a 12-month period is approximately $13,832 for a single person and $20,852 per member of a couple. Any lump sum taken reduces the fortnightly payment entitlement for the following 12 months on a proportional basis.

The national median RAD is approximately **$575,000**. The HEAS lump sum cap covers roughly **2.4% of the median RAD** for a single applicant. Even aggregated over several years of pre-entry drawdown, the lump sum provision cannot realistically fund a RAD.

HEAS is therefore structurally useful for:
- Supplementing income while waiting for a Support at Home package
- Funding the Daily Accommodation Payment (daily rental equivalent) rather than the lump-sum RAD
- Topping up a partial RAD when combined with other assets
- Meeting ongoing aged care service co-contributions under Support at Home

But it cannot bridge the RAD capital gap that typically arrives when a person enters residential aged care — the moment of peak financial need. The commercial reverse mortgage market fills this gap, but at more than double the HEAS interest rate.

### 3. The Commercial Reverse Mortgage Market: Growing But Narrow

Australia's commercial reverse mortgage sector has grown at a **compound annual growth rate of 12.4% between 2021 and 2026** (IBISWorld). Market revenue reached **$461.8 million in 2026**, though this is projected to fall slightly (0.3%) as interest rate competition increases.

**Heartland Bank Australia** dominates with a mortgage portfolio that grew from **$1.98 billion (June 2025) to $2.17 billion (December 2025)**, an increase of $188 million in six months. Household Capital is the second major commercial provider. The Deloitte 2026 Australian Reverse Mortgage Survey found that across private and government products combined, approximately 40,000 Australian households hold a reverse mortgage — still a fraction of the eligible market.

Deloitte found that among current reverse mortgage borrowers:
- **~45% use proceeds for home modifications** to age in place safely
- **~10% use proceeds for medical and healthcare costs**, including mobility aids
- Aged care funding was identified as a significant but underdisclosed use, with many borrowers accessing equity at the point of residential care entry to fund a RAD or partial RAD

The statutory **no-negative-equity guarantee**, legislated in 2012 for commercial products and formalised in HEAS in 2022, means borrowers can never owe more than their property's value at sale. This removes the primary downside risk that deterred earlier generations from reverse mortgages — though behavioural and bequest factors remain powerful inhibitors.

### 4. The Means Test Problem: The System Rewards Staying in the Family Home

The means test for residential aged care contains a fundamental structural tension for equity release.

When a resident retains their family home after entering care, up to a **capped amount of $214,884 (as at 20 March 2026)** is included in their aged care means assessment, regardless of the home's actual market value. A home worth $1.5 million in Mosman is assessed identically — at $214,884 — as a home worth $600,000 in regional Victoria (if not occupied by a protected person).

When a resident **sells the home** to fund a RAD or pay care costs, the net sale proceeds become fully assessable assets. For an asset-rich individual with a $900,000 home, selling triggers dramatically higher means-tested care fees — potentially the full maximum means-tested fee of approximately **$35,910 per year** under the pre-November 2025 framework, or the equivalent under the new Act.

This creates a perverse incentive architecture: **holding the family home in aged care is the fiscally rational strategy for most residents**, even if the home lies empty and generates no income. The exemption was designed to protect spouses and carers who continue to occupy the home — a legitimate purpose — but it simultaneously insulates the largest asset pool from contributing to care costs.

The **family home Age Pension exemption** runs for two years after moving into aged care (extended indefinitely if a protected person remains). After two years with no protected occupant, the home is assessed at its net market value for Age Pension purposes, creating pressure to sell — but the aged care means test cap of $214,884 continues regardless.

A reverse mortgage does not resolve this tension cleanly. Borrowing against a retained home under HEAS generates income or lump sums, which reduce the Age Pension entitlement dollar-for-dollar above the income-free threshold. Using HEAS to fund a RAD creates a further complication: the RAD paid is exempt from the Age Pension assets test (it is treated as an extension of the home), but in the aged care means assessment it is treated as an assessable asset — potentially increasing means-tested care fees payable.

### 5. Support at Home and the New Co-Contribution Architecture

From **1 November 2025**, the Support at Home program replaced the Home Care Package and Commonwealth Home Support Programme systems. Under Support at Home:
- **Clinical services**: Fully Commonwealth-funded; zero co-contribution
- **Independence services**: Co-contribution between 5% (full pensioner) and 50% (self-funded retiree)
- **Everyday living services**: Co-contribution between 17.5% (full pensioner) and 80% (self-funded retiree)

For a self-funded retiree accessing the highest Support at Home tier (Level 8: ~$78,106/year), the maximum everyday living co-contribution could approach **$62,500/year** — a figure that significantly exceeds annual income for many asset-rich, cash-poor retirees. The HEAS regular payment (maximum ~$44,800/year for singles at 150% of pension) could plausibly bridge this gap, but only for pensioners or part-pensioners (self-funded retirees with income above pension thresholds are not eligible for the HEAS regular payment at its maximum).

Support at Home packages have **minimum and maximum co-contribution caps** set annually by the Independent Health and Aged Care Pricing Authority (IHACPA). There is a lifetime cap on non-clinical contributions — important protection against open-ended financial exposure. However, the system does not currently automate any linkage between HEAS, equity release, or superannuation drawdowns and the co-contribution assessment or payment mechanism.

### 6. CEPAR Research: The Case for Systemic Integration

The UNSW Centre of Excellence in Population Ageing Research (CEPAR) has produced the most comprehensive academic evaluation of HEAS for aged care financing. Key findings from the 2024–25 working paper series:

- A stochastic lifecycle model incorporating house price, financial, aged care, and longevity risks finds that HEAS use **materially improves welfare outcomes** for asset-rich households who would otherwise exhaust liquid savings before death
- The welfare gain is largest for households with moderate financial assets ($200,000–$500,000) and substantial home equity ($600,000+) — precisely the profile of many people entering residential aged care
- CEPAR researchers proposed a **central home equity coordination unit** that would automate the linkage between equity release products and aged care fee payment: running real-time eligibility checks, registering property charges, and routing drawdowns directly to home care or residential care providers
- Barriers identified by CEPAR: debt aversion; inheritance preservation preferences; low awareness of HEAS specifically (described as "little-known and underused"); complexity of interaction with Age Pension and aged care means tests; structural lump-sum caps under HEAS; and the asymmetry that selling the home triggers a means-test disadvantage while retaining it does not

The 2020 **Retirement Income Review** reached similar conclusions — identifying HEAS as an effective but chronically underutilised option, and recommending that older Australians should draw down housing equity more efficiently to supplement retirement income and reduce the budgetary burden on the Age Pension and aged care systems.

### 7. The New RAD Retention Rule: Changing the Calculus

The **2% per annum RAD retention** introduced from 1 November 2025 for all new residents adds a new variable to the rent-versus-buy analysis in aged care accommodation. Before November 2025, a RAD was fully refundable on exit — pure capital storage. After November 2025, a $575,000 RAD generates approximately $11,500/year in provider retention: still a cheaper accommodation cost than the full DAP rate, but no longer free.

The financial comparison for a resident with sufficient assets:

| Option | Annual cost on $575K room | Capital at risk |
|--------|--------------------------|-----------------|
| Full DAP | ~$32,750/year (at 5.7%) | Zero |
| Full RAD (post Nov 2025) | ~$11,500/year retention | $575K |
| HEAS funding DAP | DAP + 3.95% on loan | Home equity only |
| Commercial reverse mortgage | DAP + ~8.77% on loan | Home equity only |

For an estate-conscious family, the RAD retention change may reduce the relative attractiveness of lump-sum accommodation payment and increase interest in alternatives that preserve capital, including equity-release-funded DAP arrangements.

## Tensions and Open Questions

**1. The lump sum cap structurally excludes HEAS from RAD financing.** The cap of ~$13,832 per year for a single applicant is not a design accident — it was deliberately set to supplement income, not provide capital. Raising it to allow a meaningful RAD deposit (say, $200,000–$300,000 accessible in a 12-month period) would require significant policy change and expose the Commonwealth to much larger capital risk on the book of HEAS loans. But without such a change, HEAS plays no role in the largest one-off aged care cost.

**2. The means test creates a perverse structural incentive.** Keeping the family home in residential aged care is advantageous under both the Age Pension and aged care means tests for most asset profiles. Selling to fund care accelerates means-tested cost exposure. This is not irrational behaviour — it is a rational response to system design. Reforming the family home means-test treatment is the highest-leverage policy intervention for mobilising housing equity toward care costs, and also the most politically toxic.

**3. HEAS awareness is a failure of government.** At 18,691 participants, HEAS operates at approximately one-tenth of the scale the 2020 Retirement Income Review suggested it could reach with adequate promotion. The scheme costs the Commonwealth nothing on net (loans are recovered from the estate) and reduces reliance on the Age Pension. The failure to promote it is unexplained by any obvious public interest rationale.

**4. The commercial reverse mortgage market grows, but at punitive rates.** The 12.4% CAGR in commercial reverse mortgage volume reflects genuine demand. At 8.75–8.79% compound interest, however, a 10-year loan on a $400,000 advance would accrue to approximately $900,000 — consuming the equity that might otherwise fund ongoing care or estate transfers. The HEAS rate advantage (3.95%) is enormous, but structural design limits mean commercial products carry the load.

**5. No system connects equity release to care planning.** MyAgedCare has no integration with HEAS or commercial reverse mortgage products. Financial advisers navigating the interaction between reverse mortgages, means tests, and aged care fees face extraordinary complexity — one reason that fewer than 1 in 4 people entering aged care has sought financial advice about accommodation costs. CEPAR's proposed home equity coordination unit remains a research recommendation without a policy home.
