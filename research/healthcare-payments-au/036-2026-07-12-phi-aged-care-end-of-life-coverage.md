# Private Health Insurance at End of Life and in Residential Aged Care: What PHI Covers, What It Doesn't, and Why the Gap Matters

**Report 036 — 2026-07-12**

**Summary:** Private health insurance does not cover the costs of residential aged care — accommodation, personal care, or the daily nursing provided within a facility. It does cover acute hospital admissions for aged care residents who need specialist procedures or emergency treatment. In practice, PHI's value proposition for Australians in their final years of life is narrow and shrinking: the 2026-27 Budget's decision to cut the seniors PHI rebate from April 2027 will increase premiums for 2.6 million older Australians, while a parallel research agenda from CEPAR/UNSW is asking whether long-term care insurance products could be designed to cover what PHI currently leaves uncovered.

**Why this follows from yesterday:** Report 035 examined how the Star Ratings system attempts to measure and communicate quality in residential aged care, and noted that quality indicators ultimately reflect resource inputs — staffing levels, medication management — that depend on how aged care is funded. This report examines the third major financial actor in that environment: private health insurance, which roughly 40% of aged care residents carry into residential care, and which shapes how they access acute services but provides no subsidy for the aged care costs that now constitute the primary financial pressure in their lives.

---

## Background

### The Structural Exclusion

The foundational rule is simple and absolute: **private health insurance does not cover residential aged care**. The Private Health Insurance Act 2007, and the product rules that flow from it, restrict what private health insurers may offer. "Hospital treatment" under the legislation means care provided in a hospital; residential aged care facilities are not hospitals. Extras cover (dental, optical, physiotherapy) has annual limits of $200–500 that are not designed for and would not meaningfully offset the cost of aged care.

Residential aged care costs, under the Aged Care Act 2024 and the Support at Home program (from 1 November 2025), are funded through a combination of:
- **Commonwealth basic subsidy** (via the AN-ACC funding model) — paid directly to providers
- **Hotelling supplement** — for everyday living costs; increased to $22.15 per resident per day from 20 September 2025
- **Resident means-tested co-contributions** — for accommodation and everyday living
- **Lifetime co-contribution cap** — $135,318.69 under Support at Home from 1 November 2025

PHI contributes nothing to any of these components. The financial architecture of residential aged care is entirely separate from the PHI system.

### What PHI Is Actually Used For in Aged Care

Aged care residents with private hospital cover use it — when they use it at all — for acute hospital admissions. This occurs when a resident requires treatment beyond what the aged care facility can provide: elective surgery (cataract removal, hip replacement, cardiac procedures), emergency admissions, specialist consultations in a private hospital, or planned diagnostic procedures.

APRA data shows that the age group generating the highest hospital treatment benefits from PHI is **75–79 years**, with average hospital benefits per person of $1,539.94 in the year to September 2025 (up from $1,490.91 the prior year). In 2023–24, **48.8% of all private hospital admissions** involved people aged 65 and over — demonstrating that, even though most aged care costs are beyond PHI's scope, older Australians remain heavy users of private hospital services.

The estimated 78,000 permanent residential aged care residents who hold private hospital cover do so primarily to retain access to private hospitals for these acute episodes. The decision calculus is: will the cost of premiums (roughly $295/month on average for a combined hospital and extras policy for an over-60) be offset by the expected value of private hospital access? For many, the answer is increasingly uncertain.

## Key Findings

### 1. PHI and Palliative Care: Partial Coverage, Patchwork Geography

The clearest intersection between PHI and end-of-life care is **inpatient palliative care in private hospitals**. When a dying patient is admitted to a private hospital — or to a private hospice — PHI is the principal funding source for 76% of those palliative care-related hospitalisations (lower than the 81% rate for all private hospital admissions, suggesting some uninsured patients access private palliative services through other means).

In 2023–24, **palliative care-related hospitalisations across all hospital settings** increased 46% compared to 2015–16 (AIHW, May 2026). The total hospital cost of admitted palliative care reported by 313 of 772 public hospitals alone was **$666.7 million** in 2023–24. Non-admitted (outpatient) palliative care expenditure reached **$218.9 million**, having almost doubled since 2019–20 as home-based and community models expanded.

The 2026 AIHW data release also documents PBS expenditure on palliative care-related medications: **$40.4 million in 2024–25** (average $83/patient), with pain relief prescriptions accounting for 86% of that ($34.5 million). Palliative medications grew at an average annual rate of 11% between 2016–17 and 2024–25, reflecting both population ageing and expanding formulary access.

**What PHI does NOT cover in palliative care:**
- Palliative care delivered *inside* a residential aged care facility (this is funded through the AN-ACC subsidy and state-funded community nursing programs)
- Medications dispensed under the PBS (regardless of setting — PHI is legislatively prohibited from covering PBS medicines)
- Home-based palliative nursing delivered through government programs
- The AN-ACC Class 1 palliative care supplement received by the aged care facility

**What PHI *can* cover in palliative care (with significant variation by fund and location):**
- Inpatient episodes in a private hospital or private hospice
- Some home-based palliative care through specific PHI fund programs (Bupa's Palliative Care Choices Program being the leading example)

Bupa's Palliative Care Choices Program — available in selected postcodes in Adelaide, Brisbane, Melbourne, Sydney, and Toowoomba — provides specialist palliative nurses, doctors, and allied health professionals for home visits at no additional cost to eligible members with hospital policies including unrestricted palliative care cover. This represents an expanding but geographically restricted model. There is no systematic PHI-funded in-home palliative care program available outside major metropolitan areas.

### 2. Palliative Care Inside Residential Aged Care: The AN-ACC Architecture

For residents who die inside a residential aged care facility — which is the modal end-of-life location for aged care residents — the funding mechanism is entirely the Commonwealth AN-ACC system, not PHI.

**AN-ACC Class 1** is the funding classification for residents who enter permanent residential care specifically to receive planned palliative care. It provides the facility with an adjusted daily base subsidy above the standard rate to reflect the intensive nursing and care resources required. Providers must submit a palliative care status form — including medical assessment by an independent practitioner — within 14 days of the resident's permanent entry to obtain the classification.

The AN-ACC price increased from $282.44 to **$295.64 per resident per day** on 1 October 2025. The Comprehensive Palliative Care in Aged Care program runs as a separate Commonwealth-state initiative: the 2024–25 to 2025–26 program provides specific funding to improve palliative and end-of-life care in aged care settings through specialist palliative care consultancy services embedded into facilities.

**PHI plays no role in any of this funding architecture.** An aged care resident classified as AN-ACC Class 1 generates no benefit payment from their insurer. The facility receives its subsidy from the Commonwealth. The resident pays their means-tested co-contribution. PHI is irrelevant to the economics of dying in aged care.

### 3. The Support at Home End-of-Life Pathway

For older Australians who are *not yet* in residential care and wish to die at home, the **Support at Home End-of-Life Pathway** (operational from November 2025) provides up to **$25,000 over 12 weeks** (extendable to 16 weeks) for urgent in-home services — nursing, personal care, household help — for those with a prognosis of three months or less, certified by a doctor or nurse practitioner.

This is a means-tested-free pathway: the $25,000 is available regardless of income or assets. Services under the End-of-Life Pathway are designed to complement, not substitute, state-funded palliative care clinical services (symptom management, specialist palliative medicine consultations). Eligibility requires being 65 years or older (50+ for Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander people) and not being in residential aged care.

Palliative Care Australia has welcomed the pathway but called for further action, noting that it does not cover palliative care clinical services themselves, that integration with state services varies significantly, and that rural and remote access remains problematic.

PHI has no role in this pathway either. A private hospital policy confers no benefit on a person dying at home under the Support at Home End-of-Life Pathway.

### 4. The Budget 2026-27 Decision: Cutting the Seniors PHI Rebate

The single most consequential policy decision in the PHI-aged care intersection for 2026 is the **2026-27 Budget announcement** to reduce the age-based PHI rebate tiers for seniors, effective **1 April 2027**.

The change:
- Australians aged **65–69** with income under $101,000 (single) / $202,000 (family): rebate reduced from **28% → 24%**
- Australians aged **70 and over** in the same income brackets: rebate reduced from **32% → 24%**
- The higher rebate for over-65s is being abolished; the rebate structure will be based entirely on income, not age

**Affected population:** approximately **2.6 million older Australians** holding PHI

**Financial impact on individuals:**
- Average cost increase: $226–$255 per year per person
- Couples, particularly over 70s: potential increase of **$1,000–$1,600 per year**
- Average over-60 combined hospital and extras policy: ~$295/month (~$3,540/year); rebate cut will increase this

**Government savings:** $3 billion over 4 years. The government has explicitly stated these savings will be directed to aged care funding — a policy logic that effectively transfers the cost of aged care from the tax system (via the rebate subsidy) to individuals (via higher premiums).

The sector response has been near-unanimous:
- **Private Healthcare Australia** (industry body): "older Australians among the biggest Budget losers"
- **Members Health** (not-for-profit fund peak body): "budget rebate changes risk pushing 3 million older Australians out of private cover"
- **National Seniors Australia**: provided a rebate estimator showing the out-of-pocket impact; called for full impact modelling before Senate passage
- **The Australian Medical Association** expressed concern about the knock-on effect on private hospital capacity if the over-65 PHI pool contracts significantly

For aged care residents specifically, the calculus is acute: if the premium increase becomes significant enough, many will rationally drop PHI entirely. Since PHI already provides no benefit for their residential care costs, and their hospital admission risk (though real) is partly managed through their aged care facility's nursing staff, the value threshold for keeping PHI drops. The risk is a structural exodus of elderly Australians from the private hospital system, concentrating older, sicker patients in the public system precisely as it faces workforce and capacity pressures.

### 5. Is Keeping PHI Worthwhile for Aged Care Residents? The Paradox

The practical financial question for aged care residents and their families is whether maintaining PHI is economically rational. The analysis is complicated:

**Arguments for maintaining PHI:**
- Hospital access for elective surgery: cataracts, hip/knee replacements, cardiac procedures — all of which have long public waitlists and, if delayed, cause materially worse outcomes (increased falls, fractures, loss of mobility, mortality)
- Emergency admission to a private hospital with choice of specialist
- Access to private rooms in public hospitals (if permitted by fund and hospital agreement)
- **Lifetime Health Cover (LHC) loading penalty**: if a person drops PHI and then re-takes it up, they face a 2%/year loading for each year over 30 without cover (up to 70% for 10 years) — though for someone in their 80s in residential care, this loading concern is largely irrelevant as they are unlikely to need to re-enter PHI

**Arguments for dropping PHI:**
- PHI provides zero benefit for residential aged care costs — the primary financial burden
- The facility's nursing staff manage most clinical needs in situ
- Hospitalisation becomes less likely (not more) as frailty increases and advance care plans steer against acute hospital transfer
- Premium costs (~$295/month) are a real cash outflow that competes with aged care co-contributions
- The premium cut from 2027 makes this calculus even less favourable

The clinical literature confirms the paradox from a different angle: delayed cataract surgery, joint replacement, and cardiac procedures in the frail elderly lead to materially worse outcomes — which makes the case for maintaining hospital access genuinely strong. But the access gap between PHI and the aged care costs it doesn't cover creates a situation where many residents are paying for insurance they largely cannot benefit from.

### 6. The Long-Term Care Insurance Gap

The fundamental structural gap is the absence of any **private long-term care insurance (LTCI)** market in Australia. Unlike Germany (mandatory social LTCI since 1995), Japan (public LTCI since 2000), the United States (voluntary private LTCI, though market is shrinking), or France (hybrid supplementary LTCI), Australia has never developed either a social insurance contribution system or a viable private LTCI market to pool the risk of needing aged care.

A 2024/2026 CEPAR working paper (Park, Sherris et al., published in the *Journal of Risk and Insurance*) models the design and pricing of private LTCI for Australia. Key findings:
- A product covering aged care out-of-pocket co-payments is actuarially feasible
- Solvency capital requirements (SCR) under Solvency II-equivalent rules are significant for stand-alone LTCI products, adding substantially to premiums
- Life Care Annuity (LCA) designs — combining an annuity with aged care cost coverage — produce lower capital requirements and more competitive pricing
- The age pension means-testing rules create complex interactions with private LTCI benefit design

The Royal Commission into Aged Care (2021) explicitly recommended exploring an actuarially based contributory social insurance scheme for aged care — an aged care levy model, analogous to the Medicare Levy, that would pool the population-wide financial risk of needing high-cost care. No such scheme has been implemented; the Government has instead chosen the annual-budget model of financing aged care through Commonwealth subsidies.

The absence of LTCI means that the financial risk of needing residential aged care — which costs $85,000–$200,000+ per year for high-acuity residents — falls almost entirely on individuals (through means-tested co-contributions) and the Commonwealth (through AN-ACC subsidies). PHI neither pools that risk nor mitigates it.

## Tensions and Open Questions

**1. The rebate cut paradox.** The 2026-27 Budget simultaneously cuts PHI subsidies for over-65s to fund aged care, while doing nothing to extend PHI coverage to aged care costs. The policy logic requires elderly Australians to pay more for insurance that doesn't cover the thing they most need it for.

**2. The advance care planning–PHI interaction.** As advance care planning (ACP) rates improve (Report 029), more residents will have documented preferences to avoid acute hospital transfer. A successful ACP program — which reduces emergency hospitalisation — also reduces the value of PHI for aged care residents. Good policy in one domain undercuts the rationale for the other.

**3. The LCA product gap.** CEPAR's modelling suggests life care annuity products could efficiently pool aged care costs at retirement, but no product has been brought to market. The insurance industry has no commercial incentive to pioneer products that compete with PHI for premium income while also requiring large regulatory capital holdings.

**4. Geographic inequity in PHI palliative care.** Bupa's Palliative Care Choices Program is available in five cities. Regional and rural PHI holders — already documented to face worse end-of-life outcomes (Reports 012, 026) — cannot access in-home PHI-funded palliative care. This reflects a pattern visible throughout this series: PHI innovations are metropolitan.

**5. The private hospital capacity risk.** If the rebate cut from April 2027 causes even a 5–10% contraction in the elderly PHI pool, private hospitals will face reduced occupancy in surgical categories heavily weighted toward older patients (elective orthopaedic, cardiac, ophthalmology). This would either accelerate private hospital closures or increase pressure on public hospitals — undoing some of the aged care funding gain.
