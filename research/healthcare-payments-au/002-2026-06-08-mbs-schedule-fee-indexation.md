# MBS Schedule Fee Indexation: How Australia Prices Medical Services — and Why Fees Keep Falling Behind

**Report 002 — 2026-06-08**

**Summary:** The Medicare Benefits Schedule fee is not set by market forces or independent cost assessment — it is a government-administered price that has been deliberately suppressed for much of the past three decades, saving $3.9 billion through a seven-year freeze (2013–2020) but leaving Schedule Fees well below the actual cost of providing care. Annual indexation, theoretically pegged to a wage cost index, has averaged just 1.1% per year since 1995, versus CPI of 2.4% and average wage growth of 3.5%. The resulting fee adequacy crisis now underlies Australia's bulk billing sustainability problem, the explosion in specialist out-of-pocket costs, and a wave of transparency legislation targeting fees that have become almost impossible for patients to predict.

**Why this follows from yesterday:** Report 001 identified a core tension — that MBS Schedule Fees "have not kept pace with the true cost of running a practice," with the AMA's recommended fees sitting 40–60% above MBS levels. Today's report examines why that gap exists, how the Schedule Fee is set, and what is being done about it.

---

## Background

Every Medicare rebate paid in Australia flows from a single published number: the **Schedule Fee** for a given MBS item. The rebate is a fixed percentage of that fee (85% for out-of-hospital GP and specialist services, 75% for in-hospital). If the Schedule Fee stagnates, everything downstream stagnates with it: Medicare rebates fall in real terms, the gap between what providers need to charge and what patients receive grows, and out-of-pocket costs rise. Understanding how Schedule Fees are set and updated is therefore foundational to understanding the Australian healthcare payments system.

The Schedule Fee is not derived from a cost-of-production calculation. It does not track the true cost of running a medical practice — wages for staff, rent, medical indemnity insurance, or equipment depreciation. Instead, it is a **government-administered price**, increased annually through a formula set within Treasury and the Department of Finance, subject to the political constraint of the annual federal budget.

## Key Findings

### 1. The Indexation Mechanism: The Wage Cost Index

MBS Schedule Fees are adjusted annually on **1 July** using the Department of Finance's **Wage Cost Index (WCI)** — a weighted blend of:
- The ABS **Wage Price Index** (WPI), less a government-assumed long-run **productivity offset**
- The **Consumer Price Index** (CPI), to reflect non-labour cost movements

The WCI is designed to reflect the cost of delivering government-funded services, with an inbuilt assumption that efficiency gains will offset some cost growth over time. Different WCI variants apply to different program types; GP consultation items (including Item 23) have historically been indexed to WCI-5.

In the **2023–24 Budget**, the Albanese Government updated the formula, switching the wage cost component from the older Safety Net Adjustment (SNA) to current WPI growth (through-year to the previous December). This change produced higher indexation payments and has been seen as partially addressing the legacy of the fee freeze. The rates have been:

| Financial Year | Indexation Applied from 1 July |
|---|---|
| 1995–2022 (27-year average) | **1.1%** per annum |
| 2022–23 | 1.6% |
| 2023–24 | ~3.5% (post-formula update) |
| 2024–25 | **3.5%** |
| 2025–26 | **2.4%** |

For context, CPI averaged 2.42% per year and Average Weekly Earnings averaged 3.53% per year over the same 27-year period. On these figures, the MBS Schedule Fee has fallen, in real terms, by roughly 28–40% since 1995 compared to what it would need to be to track actual cost growth. (AMA analysis of Medicare indexation freeze)

### 2. The Seven-Year Freeze (2013–2020): Saving $3.9 Billion at Doctors' Expense

The indexation shortfall was dramatically worsened by a deliberate policy decision. In 2013, the Gillard Labor Government introduced a **freeze on MBS indexation** as a temporary budget savings measure, projected to save $664 million. The Turnbull and Morrison Coalition Governments then repeatedly extended the freeze, which ultimately remained largely in place until July 2020 — seven years rather than the intended one or two.

**Financial impact:**
- Total savings from prevented indexations: approximately **$3.9 billion** (AMA analysis)
- A fee that should have been indexed to, say, $50 by 2020 based on prior indexation history instead remained at $38–39 — an effective real-terms reduction of 20–25%
- The freeze applied to almost all MBS items; lifting was phased:
  - July 2017: GP bulk billing incentive items lifted first
  - July 2018: Standard GP consultations and specialist consultations
  - July 2019: Medical procedures
  - July 2020: Diagnostic imaging services

The freeze was deeply contested. The RACGP and AMA argued — and independent researchers subsequently confirmed — that it accelerated the decline in bulk billing, made it harder for practices to remain financially viable, and provided the economic rationale for specialists to widen the gap between their charges and MBS Schedule Fees. The Australian Economic Review (2026) noted that "the indexation failure (2014–2019) was a fundamental failure that led to the schedule fee falling far behind the true cost of practice, providing a legitimate excuse for specialists to charge higher gaps."

### 3. Current Schedule Fees: Specific Items

After the 2.4% indexation applied on 1 July 2025, the Schedule Fees for key items are:

| Item | Description | Schedule Fee | Medicare Benefit (85%) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **23** | GP attendance, < 20 minutes | ~$42.85 | ~$36.42 |
| **36** | GP attendance, 20–40 minutes (Level B) | ~$82.90 | ~$70.47 |
| **44** | GP attendance, > 40 minutes (Level C) | ~$121.85 | ~$103.57 |
| **104** | Specialist initial consultation (rooms) | $101.30 | $86.15 (out of hospital) / $76.00 (in hospital) |

*Sources: MBS Online, MBS July 2025 News. Note: Item 23/36/44 figures are approximate, derived from pre-indexation bases plus confirmed 2.4% increase.*

The Extended Medicare Safety Net (EMSN) benefit cap for Item 104 (specialist initial consultation) is $303.90 — meaning even with safety net support, there is a structural ceiling on patient protection.

### 4. The AMA Fees List: The Shadow Price Signal

In parallel with the government's MBS Schedule, the Australian Medical Association publishes its own **AMA Fees List** — updated annually on **1 November** and indexed to movements in CPI and WPI. This list is not binding; the AMA explicitly notes that "each practice remains responsible for determining its own fees." But it functions as the de facto industry reference point for gap fees.

The AMA has consistently argued that the reason doctors charge above MBS Schedule Fees is simple: **the Schedule Fee does not cover the cost of providing the service.** In a 2022 comparison (the most recent with publicised data), the AMA recommended approximately $86 for an Item 23 (standard GP consultation), against an MBS Schedule Fee of around $39–40 at the time — a ratio of roughly 2.1:1. By 2025, with the MBS fee having risen to approximately $42.85 and AMA fees having tracked CPI/WPI more closely, the ratio has narrowed but remains substantial (estimated at 1.8–2.0x for GP items, and potentially wider for specialist items).

The long-run arithmetic is stark: if MBS fees average 1.1% indexation annually and AMA fees average CPI (2.4%) and WPI (3.5%), the gap compounds every year. Each percentage point of below-CPI indexation represents a permanent structural widening.

### 5. The Specialist Fee Problem: An Even Wider Gap

For GP services, bulk billing incentive payments (introduced November 2025) have largely bridged the gap between MBS rebates and practice viability for those practices that choose to fully bulk bill. But for **specialist and procedural services**, no equivalent mechanism exists. The consequences are stark:

- The average out-of-pocket gap payment for specialist consultations rose from **$49.56 in 2010–11** to **$117.18 in 2023–24** — an increase of 136%, far above inflation
- Mean fees for an initial specialist consultation (Item 104) are now **34% higher than inflation would predict** since the freeze (Australian Economic Review, 2026)
- Average gap payments for **private hospital specialist treatment** rose **7.7% in 2025**, from $437.61 to $471.29 (APRA data)
- A 2025 survey found **55% of Australians had received a higher specialist bill than expected** in the prior three years
- Almost **1 in 3 people delayed or cancelled specialist care** specifically due to cost

Australia stands almost alone among OECD nations with universal healthcare systems in having **no effective mechanism to regulate private specialist fees** above the MBS Schedule Fee. Specialists can and routinely do charge multiples of the MBS Schedule Fee, with no regulatory ceiling and — until recently — almost no way for patients to know in advance what they would be charged.

### 6. The Medical Costs Finder: Transparency as the Reform Tool

Faced with the political difficulty of raising Schedule Fees significantly (each 1 percentage point increase in MBS indexation costs approximately $500 million per annum), successive governments have attempted transparency as an alternative lever.

The **Morrison Government** spent **$24 million** building the **Medical Costs Finder** website — a consumer-facing tool where patients could look up specialist fees. The model was voluntary: specialists could choose to display their fees. By the end of 2022, only **6 doctors** out of approximately 6,300 eligible specialists had listed fees. By end of 2025, the number had grown to approximately **88 individual doctors** — still a near-total failure.

In early 2026, the Albanese Government introduced the **Health Legislation Amendment (Improving Choice and Transparency for Private Health Consumers) Bill 2026** — which would:
- Allow the government to **automatically populate** the Medical Costs Finder with Medicare, hospital, and insurer billing data it already collects via claims processing
- End the voluntary model and replace it with **automatic mandatory display** of individual specialist fees
- Enable patients to compare costs before booking an appointment, rather than learning the fee retrospectively

Private Healthcare Australia (the health fund peak body) has backed the legislation, noting that lack of prior fee disclosure drives both out-of-pocket costs and "unknown gap" payments — bills that blindside patients post-procedure. The AMA's response has been more cautious, noting that fee transparency does not address the underlying Schedule Fee adequacy problem.

### 7. The Political Economy of Fee-Setting

The core political economy problem is straightforward: MBS Schedule Fee increases are a direct budget expenditure line. Each 1% increase in indexation costs approximately $500 million per year, and the government bears roughly 40% of all health expenditure. Higher fees encourage more services to be rendered at or near the Schedule Fee, further increasing government outlays. The incentive for governments — of both parties — is to keep Schedule Fees low and use targeted incentive programs (like the BBPIP and bulk billing incentives) to manage access to care for specific populations.

The fee freeze was bipartisan in effect: introduced by Labor, maintained and extended by the Coalition. The current government has invested heavily in bulk billing incentives ($8.4 billion over five years) as a substitute for structural fee reform. The RACGP's **2025 Health of the Nation** report found that **82% of GP practice owners** cite financial and business challenges as their top concern — and explicitly link this to the multi-decade failure of MBS indexation to track real practice costs.

## Tensions and Open Questions

1. **Adequacy vs. budget constraint:** The WCI mechanism is designed to approximate cost growth, not to cover actual practice costs. Even at its best, it has lagged CPI and AWE. Unless the government either raises Schedule Fees substantially above WCI-implied increases, or funds a different cost-structure review, the real value of MBS fees will continue to erode.

2. **GP vs. specialist differentiation:** The bulk billing incentive approach has been applied exclusively to GP services, leaving specialist fees entirely unregulated. The 136% rise in specialist out-of-pocket costs since 2010–11 reflects this asymmetry.

3. **Transparency vs. price regulation:** Publishing specialist fees on the Medical Costs Finder is a necessary first step, but transparency alone does not set prices. Whether it changes specialist charging behaviour — or whether patients can act on fee information in the real world of referrals and urgent care needs — remains contested.

4. **Fee-for-service vs. blended payments:** The MBS Review Taskforce (2015–2020) recommended moving from pure fee-for-service to blended payment models (combining block payments with activity-based elements). This would decouple practice viability from individual Schedule Fees, but has not been implemented at scale. Without this structural shift, every discussion of GP practice viability returns to the Schedule Fee.

5. **The compounding shortfall:** A schedule fee that has fallen ~28–40% in real terms since 1995 cannot be corrected with 2–3% annual indexation increases. The gap between what MBS fees cover and what care actually costs will continue to widen unless there is either a one-off recalibration or a sustained above-CPI correction period.
