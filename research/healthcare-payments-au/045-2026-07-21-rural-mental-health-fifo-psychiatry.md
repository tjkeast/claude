# Rural Mental Health and FIFO Psychiatry: Australia's 7 Million People Without a Local Psychiatrist

**Report 045 — 2026-07-21**

**Summary:** Australia has 3,812 full-time equivalent psychiatrists — but 85% of them work in metropolitan areas, leaving only 1.8% to serve rural and remote regions where 29% of the population lives. The dominant model for filling this gap is fly-in fly-out (FIFO) and drive-in drive-out (DIDO) psychiatry, supplemented by telehealth and block-grant funded services from the Royal Flying Doctor Service (RFDS). Neither approach adequately substitutes for a resident workforce: average wait times for a first psychiatry appointment exceed 258 days nationally and are longer again outside capital cities. Suicide rates in very remote Australia (20.5–24.6 per 100,000) are more than double those in major cities (9.9–10.9). The projected workforce shortfall is deepening — from roughly 600 FTE undersupply in 2024 to an estimated 6,100 FTE by 2048. The 2026-27 Budget left the crisis largely unaddressed.

**Why this follows from yesterday:** Report 044 traced how Australia's $3 trillion housing wealth pool fails to connect to aged care financing — a design failure of structural disconnection. Today's report examines a different structural disconnection: a mental health workforce overwhelmingly concentrated in capital cities serving a country where one in three Australians lives outside those cities, and where the health consequences of that gap are most severe.

---

## Background

Rural and remote Australia carries a disproportionate burden of mental ill-health. The drivers are well-documented: geographic isolation, economic disadvantage, drought and climate stress, occupational injury, limited social support networks, and reduced access to all forms of healthcare. Yet the mental health workforce has historically been, and remains, one of the most geographically maldistributed components of Australia's healthcare system.

Psychiatrists sit at the apex of the mental health service hierarchy. They are the only clinicians who can prescribe and monitor complex psychotropic medication, admit patients involuntarily under mental health legislation, manage severe and enduring conditions such as schizophrenia and bipolar disorder, and provide medico-legal assessments. Their geographic concentration therefore has consequences that ripple throughout the entire stepped-care system.

Australia's 31 Primary Health Networks (PHNs) are the primary commissioning bodies for mental health services outside the MBS, especially in thin rural markets where the MBS alone cannot attract and sustain private practitioners. PHNs commission FIFO psychiatry, telehealth services, and block-grant-funded programs including Headspace and the Royal Flying Doctor Service as the primary tools for bridging the gap between metropolitan workforce supply and rural demand.

## Key Findings

### 1. The Workforce Data: A 10:1 Metropolitan Advantage

The Department of Health, Disability and Ageing's *Psychiatry Supply and Demand Compendium* (released June 2025, updated November 2025) provides the most current national data:

- **Total psychiatry workforce (2023):** 4,272 psychiatrists; **3,812.6 FTE** (average 35.7 hours per week)
- **Geographic distribution:** 85.0% of FTE psychiatrists are in metropolitan areas; **1.8% are in rural and remote areas**
- **Growth rate:** The workforce grew at 2.8% CAGR from 2019 to 2023 (3,417.8 → 3,812.6 FTE), but geographic maldistribution has not materially improved
- **International medical graduates:** 1,002 FTE — about 26% of the total psychiatry workforce — are IMGs (see Report 039 on IMG dependency across the healthcare system)

A 2024 peer-reviewed study in the *Australian Journal of Rural Health* — "The changing psychiatry workforce in Australia: Still lacking in rural and remote regions" — confirmed that despite overall workforce growth, the rural-remote psychiatrist rate per 100,000 population has not kept pace with population growth. The national rate is approximately 15.2 psychiatrists per 100,000 (up from 12.6 in 2000), but outer regional New South Wales and South Australia have the lowest per-capita rates in the country.

A 2025 commentary by Looi, Allison, Bastiampillai and Kisely, mapping the 2022-23 AIHW mental health workforce data, found that **only 14% of Australian psychiatrists work rurally, while 29% of the population — approximately 7 million people — live in regional, rural, and remote areas**. Outer regional NSW and South Australia had the greatest absolute shortfalls. The NT and Queensland recorded slightly higher per-100,000 rates in outer regional and remote areas than the national average, but from very low baselines.

### 2. The Projected Shortfall: A Worsening Structural Problem

The Compendium projects the following undersupply trajectory:

| Year | Projected FTE Undersupply |
|------|--------------------------|
| 2024 | ~600 FTE |
| 2028 | ~1,900 FTE |
| 2048 | ~6,100 FTE |

The 2048 projection represents a **20.7% projected undersupply** relative to modelled demand. Academic commentary on this report — published in the *Australian and New Zealand Journal of Psychiatry* in 2025 — cautions that the model may understate the rural-specific gap because it uses aggregate FTE projections that do not capture the within-group geographic maldistribution: a 20.7% national undersupply masks a far more severe localised shortfall in outer regional and remote areas.

### 3. The Consequence: 258 Days to See a Psychiatrist

A 2024 University of Melbourne study analysed MBS claims data from 2011 to 2022 to estimate wait times from GP referral to first psychiatric specialist appointment. Key findings:

- **National median wait: at least 258 days** (approximately 8.5 months) from GP referral to first psychiatrist consultation
- Wait times increased **steadily across the decade**, with a further sharp increase after 2020
- Rural and remote residents face longer waits than city residents — the study found lower per-capita rural psychiatrist supply was **associated with more telepsychiatry consultations**, suggesting telehealth is used to compensate, not as a preference
- In 2023-24, at least **4 in 10 people** who sought a psychiatrist referral faced barriers to access
- Six private hospitals providing psychiatric care **closed their doors in 2024**, further compressing inpatient capacity

The suite of problems is compounded for rural patients: even if a telehealth appointment becomes available, the MyMedicare registration requirement introduced from November 2025 requires patients to have an established clinical relationship before telehealth mental health consultations are subsidised — a rule that creates additional friction in rural areas with higher GP turnover.

### 4. The FIFO and DIDO Model: How Rural Psychiatry Actually Functions

In the absence of a resident psychiatrist, rural Australia's primary service model is visiting specialist outreach. Two forms operate in parallel:

**Fly-In Fly-Out (FIFO) psychiatry:** A psychiatrist based in a metropolitan or regional hub flies to a rural or remote location on a scheduled basis — typically monthly or quarterly — to conduct face-to-face consultations, case reviews, crisis assessments, and liaison with local health staff. Services are contracted through PHNs, state health departments, or the RFDS.

**Drive-In Drive-Out (DIDO) psychiatry:** Used where air travel is unnecessary or prohibitively expensive; a psychiatrist drives from a regional centre to outlying communities.

The FIFO/DIDO model has two narrow advantages: it maintains some face-to-face interaction for complex patients, and it allows the psychiatrist to physically inspect the environment in which patients live. Academic evidence (summarised in a widely-cited 2015 *Rural and Remote Health* paper, Humphreys et al.) identifies the model's significant limitations:

- **No continuity:** A monthly or quarterly visiting schedule is incompatible with management of acute deterioration, medication titration for complex conditions, or crisis response
- **No peer support or supervision:** FIFO psychiatrists in remote areas typically work without local specialist peers, increasing clinical risk and contributing to burnout
- **Efficiency losses:** Travel time reduces productive clinical hours; the proportion of a working week consumed by transit can approach 30-40% in very remote sites
- **The "one-clinician-knows-everything" problem:** Local GPs and nurses defer complex mental health decisions to the visiting psychiatrist, rather than building local capability — creating dependency rather than capacity
- **Burnout:** In a 2025 RANZCP survey of rural psychiatrists, 74% reported experiencing multiple symptoms of burnout and 33% were considering leaving the profession within five years — attrition risk that is catastrophically concentrated in the thin rural markets that can least replace them

### 5. The RFDS: Scale Without Permanence

The Royal Flying Doctor Service is Australia's most significant provider of remote mental health services outside the MBS. In 2023-24, the RFDS delivered:

- **2,600 mental health clinic visits** to rural and remote communities
- **40,000 remote telehealth consultations** (mental health included)
- Emergency psychiatric retrievals (air transfer to acute care) for severe presentations

The Commonwealth has committed **$1 billion to the RFDS over 10 years (2024-25 to 2031-32)**, with an additional **$74.8 million** announced to expand primary care, mental health, and dental services to more remote communities. NSW added a further **$15 million** in the 2025-26 state budget.

RFDS mental health services typically involve mental health nurses, allied health workers, and — on the visiting specialist model — psychiatrists and psychologists who fly into remote clinics on a scheduled basis. The RFDS model is explicitly designed as a **supplement to**, not a replacement for, resident mental health services: the RFDS's own literature describes its role as providing care where no other option exists. In very remote areas of the NT, WA, and Queensland, the RFDS may be the only mental health service a community ever sees.

### 6. MBS Telehealth for Psychiatry: The Funding Mechanism

The MBS telehealth psychiatry framework expanded substantially during and after the COVID-19 pandemic. Current key items include:

- **Item 296** (face-to-face): Consultant psychiatrist initial consultation, >45 minutes; the foundational attendance item
- **Item 92437** (video telehealth equivalent to Item 296): Initial consultation by video for new patients or patients not seen in the preceding 24 months
- **Item 294**: A **50% fee loading** applied to bulk-billed telehealth psychiatry consultations — designed to incentivise bulk billing for video consultations where patients would otherwise face a gap fee
- **Items 91827–91831** (video) and **91837–91839** (phone): Time-tiered telehealth attendances for established patients (seen within the preceding 24 months)

From **1 November 2024**, new inpatient telehealth psychiatry MBS items were added for an initial two-year period, allowing psychiatrists to conduct hospital admission assessments and inpatient reviews via video — aimed at reducing delays caused by hospital-based psychiatry shortages in rural hospitals.

From **1 November 2025**, the MyMedicare-linked established clinical relationship requirement was extended to mental health telehealth services. A patient in a remote community who has never accessed telehealth mental health care, or whose registered practice has changed, now faces additional administrative barriers to access.

### 7. Telehealth as a Band-Aid: The Out-of-Pocket Problem

A 2025 administrative data linkage study by Woon, Maguire, Reay, Bastiampillai, Looi and Liu — analysing **13.5 million telepsychiatry consultations** from Medicare data — produced the most comprehensive picture of telepsychiatry's actual financial outcomes:

- **Average bulk-billing rate: 50.9%** — meaning nearly half of all telepsychiatry consultations involve an out-of-pocket (OOP) gap fee
- Bulk-billing rates **decreased over time** as the telehealth system expanded
- Fee gaps **increased across all consultation modes** (video, phone, and face-to-face)
- Face-to-face consultations were less likely to be bulk-billed and incurred **higher gap fees** than video or phone
- Patients in rural and remote areas were likely to bear **greater OOP costs**, including for video consultations, due to lower competition and reduced access to bulk-billing psychiatrists

This matters because the argument frequently made for telehealth — that it reduces OOP costs for rural patients by eliminating travel — is not supported by the evidence. Rural patients using telepsychiatry still pay gap fees at almost the same rate as urban patients, while incurring additional costs if they need to travel to obtain adequate broadband connectivity for video consultations.

The Item 294 fee loading for bulk-billed telehealth is intended to address this, but evidence suggests it has not reversed the declining bulk-billing trend.

### 8. The Burnout and Attrition Crisis

The RANZCP's 2025 survey of rural psychiatrists — conducted in the context of the Psychiatry Supply and Demand Compendium release — found:

- **94%** of rural and remote psychiatrists said workforce shortages were **negatively impacting patient care**
- **74%** reported multiple symptoms of burnout
- **33%** were considering leaving the profession in the next five years
- Burnout was attributed primarily to caseload pressure (covering large geographic areas with inadequate support), isolation, limited peer supervision, and the emotional burden of managing severe presentations without adequate backup

If one-third of the existing rural psychiatric workforce were to leave the profession in the next five years, the already-critical rural psychiatrist supply would become a public health emergency. The compounding effect on the projected 2028 and 2048 shortfall figures would be severe.

### 9. Policy Response: The Rural Psychiatry Roadmap and Budget Failure

The RANZCP published the **Rural Psychiatry Roadmap 2021–31** as the profession's 10-year plan for equitable service access. Key elements:

- Development of a dedicated **Rural Psychiatry Training Pathway (RPTP) to Fellowship** — creating a structured career path for psychiatrists who choose to train and practise rurally, rather than treating rural psychiatry as a temporary posting
- Expansion of the **Psychiatry Workforce Program (PWP)** — a four-year funded program (2021-22 to 2025-26) providing salary contributions for training posts in rural and regional locations, including a rural loading of **$18,047–$30,938 per annum** per post
- The **Integrated Rural Training Pipeline (IRTP)** — posts combining rural psychiatric training with experience in allied settings
- The **$74.8 million RFDS commitment** as a complementary investment

In its 2026-27 Federal Pre-Budget Submission, RANZCP asked the government to invest an additional **$24.85 million** to expand the PWP by 45 trainee and supervisor posts, prioritising rural and regional locations. In response to the 2026-27 Budget, RANZCP declared that **"crucial funding for rural and regional mental health workforce [was] missing"** — the fifth consecutive budget year in which RANZCP made the same assessment.

The PWP extension under the new tranche includes **10 additional MMHC (Medicare Mental Health Centres) training posts**, recognising the service model examined in Report 020. But this is insufficient to materially alter the geographic distribution trajectory.

### 10. The Stepped-Care Gap: What Happens Between the GP and the Psychiatrist

In metropolitan areas, the Better Access initiative (see Report 011) provides subsidised psychology sessions that absorb much of the moderate-severity demand that would otherwise pressure psychiatry. In rural areas, the psychology workforce is also thin: psychologists and mental health nurses have higher per-100,000 rates in rural areas than psychiatrists, but the absolute numbers remain insufficient, and the Better Access gap fee (a typical gap of $80–$150 per session after the MBS rebate) prices out many rural patients.

The PHN-funded stepped care model — where low-to-moderate need is addressed through low-intensity services and psychology, with psychiatry reserved for complex cases — requires a depth of local clinical capacity that simply does not exist across much of rural Australia. From **1 July 2026**, PHN guidance formalised a new commissioning framework for low-intensity services and stepped care, but the guidance explicitly acknowledges the need for "flexibility in implementation for certain rural and remote areas" — a bureaucratic acknowledgement that the model cannot be fully implemented where the workforce does not exist.

## Tensions and Open Questions

**1. FIFO psychiatry produces access without care.** A quarterly visiting psychiatrist can provide an initial assessment and write a care plan, but cannot manage acute deterioration, emergency presentations, or the nuanced titration of complex medication regimens over time. Yet for many remote communities, the choice is between FIFO care or no care. The fundamental ethical question — whether periodic access to specialist care meets the standard of equitable access — remains unanswered by policy.

**2. Telehealth expanded access but not equity.** The 13.5-million-consultation dataset confirms that telepsychiatry reached more rural patients. But it did not reduce gap fees, and the declining bulk-billing trend means the financial barrier to rural mental health care has increased alongside service availability. The 50% telehealth fee loading (Item 294) is insufficient to reverse this.

**3. The MyMedicare clinical relationship requirement creates rural access friction.** Rural patients with high GP turnover, or who travel to regional centres for mental health care, or who access FIFO services through the RFDS, may struggle to satisfy the established clinical relationship requirement now attached to telehealth mental health items. This requirement was designed for urban continuity-of-care purposes; its rural application creates unintended barriers.

**4. The training pipeline is too slow.** Producing a psychiatrist from medical school takes 12-14 years (MBBS + intern year + prevocational + 5-year RANZCP training program). The Compendium projects a 6,100 FTE shortfall by 2048. Even if the government funded the full RANZCP ask of 45 additional posts immediately, the pipeline would take more than a decade to fill — and only if those trainees then practise rurally, which requires sustained financial and structural incentives to achieve.

**5. The 33% attrition risk is the immediate crisis.** Pipeline solutions address future supply. The immediate crisis is retention: a third of the existing rural psychiatric workforce is considering departure. Burnout prevention, peer support infrastructure, and rural incentive payments are the short-run levers. None received dedicated Budget 2026-27 funding.

## Threads to Branch Into Next

1. **Aged care provider market exits and consolidation** — provider financial viability is being reshaped by the new Aged Care Act 2024, RAD retention changes, and subsidy adequacy under the Support at Home program; KPMG/StewartBrown sector data confirms providers in thin rural markets are most at risk

2. **Accommodation supplement equity in aged care** — the government means-tested accommodation supplement paid to providers when residents cannot fund a RAD or DAP; adequacy across regions; rural pricing dynamics

3. **Psychiatric nurse practitioners in rural mental health** — how far NPs can substitute for psychiatrists in rural settings; MBS items; PBS prescribing authority for mental health NPs; current numbers and geographic distribution

4. **State-funded mental health services and the Commonwealth-state interface** — community mental health teams, CAT teams, and community mental health nursing are state-funded and sit outside the MBS; how state funding allocation shapes rural mental health access in ways invisible to Medicare data

5. **ADHD diagnosis and treatment access in rural Australia** — ADHD stimulant prescribing requires psychiatrist assessment and ongoing review in most jurisdictions; the psychiatry shortage creates a specific bottleneck for the fastest-growing diagnostic category in Australian mental health; a proxy measure of the rural psychiatry shortage's practical consequences
