# Specialist Medical Training Pipeline: How Australia's Colleges Control Specialist Numbers, the Structural Bottleneck, and Why Shortages Persist Across Psychiatry, Geriatrics, Palliative Medicine, and Oncology

**Report 028 — 2026-07-04**

**Summary:** Australia is producing more medical graduates than ever — medical school commencements rose 9.86% from 2018 to 2023 — but specialist trainee intake grew at only 4.42% to 6.78% in the same period. The gap between the two pipeline stages is widening: by 2048 the prevocational hospital doctor pool is projected to nearly double from 15,900 to 38,000 FTE, while specialist shortfalls deepen across every state except Western Australia. The structural explanation is not a shortage of talented junior doctors but a governance failure: thirteen specialist medical colleges independently control training capacity with no statutory obligation to align numbers to community health need, and the national planning body that might provide independent oversight — Health Workforce Australia — was abolished in 2014. The consequences thread through nearly every report in this series, from the psychiatry workforce deficit underpinning the CAMHS crisis (Report 019) and Better Access session cap (Report 011), to the geriatrician shortage amplifying the hospital-to-aged-care boundary problem (Report 025), to the palliative medicine physician gap running underneath the end-of-life funding failures documented in Reports 026 and 027.

**Why this follows from yesterday:** Report 027 documented the VAD payment and access crisis, and explicitly named the specialist training pipeline as the most persistently deferred thread in this series — opened in Report 016 on specialist gap fees, never followed. This report closes that thread by examining the structural architecture that determines how many specialists Australia can train, who controls that capacity, and whether the system is capable of self-correction.

## Background

### How Australia trains specialist doctors

Australia's pathway from medical graduate to specialist physician is long and largely controlled by the profession itself.

A medical graduate completes **two prevocational years** (PGY1 and PGY2) as a hospital intern and resident. From 2025, these are governed by the new **National Framework for Prevocational Medical Training (NFPMT)**, a nationally standardised curriculum replacing the previous state-by-state frameworks. Prevocational positions are funded and allocated by state and territory governments through public teaching hospitals.

After prevocational training, a junior doctor must be selected into a **specialist college training program**. This selection is the critical bottleneck. Training places are accredited and allocated by the relevant specialist medical college — one of thirteen colleges that have formal funding agreements with the Australian Government Department of Health, Disability and Ageing. Entry is competitive; the number of training positions available in any given year is a decision made by each college.

Once admitted to training, the pathway takes **three to six years** depending on specialty, followed by college examination and fellowship requirements. The total elapsed time from first-year medical student to fully qualified specialist is commonly **10 to 15 years**.

### The governance gap since Health Workforce Australia

From 2009 to 2014, **Health Workforce Australia** (HWA) was the national body responsible for collecting workforce data, modelling supply and demand, and advising on training capacity across all health professions including specialist medicine. HWA was abolished by the Abbott Government in 2014.

Since HWA's abolition, no comparable independent national body has existed. Workforce policy is coordinated through Health Ministers' meetings and managed within the Commonwealth Department of Health and Aged Care's Health Workforce Division — but without an independent standing body with statutory planning authority. A 2025 review found that the current meeting of health ministers is "not an effective way to govern health workers," and called for re-establishing a body analogous to HWA.

The governance gap matters because the thirteen specialist colleges are, in legal and financial terms, private self-regulating bodies. They set their own membership fees, eligibility criteria, training curricula, and — critically — the number of positions available in each year. There is no statutory mechanism requiring colleges to align training capacity with projected community need.

## Key Findings

### 1. The Structural Bottleneck: Medical Schools Growing, Training Capacity Not Keeping Pace

Australia's medical school expansion since the early 2000s was a policy response to projected doctor shortages. It has succeeded in producing more graduates — but the downstream infrastructure (specialist training positions) has not expanded proportionally.

From 2018 to 2023:
- **Medical school commencements** rose **9.86%**
- **Specialist medical trainee numbers** rose only **4.42% to 6.78%**

The consequence is a growing pool of prevocational doctors unable to progress to specialist training. The projected oversupply of prevocational hospital doctors is striking:

- **2025**: estimated ~15,900 FTE prevocational doctors nationally
- **2048**: projected ~38,000 FTE — an increase of approximately 139%

This population of junior doctors circulating in prevocational positions, unable to secure specialist training places, creates measurable harms: prolonged training periods (junior doctors spending years in holding patterns waiting for training acceptance), increased job insecurity, and a retention risk as doctors who cannot progress consider migration or career change.

The Australian Medical Student Association's 2025 policy document on medical schools identifies this as a structural failure: specialty training positions are "decided by the specialty college, and they are not guided by evidence of community need."

### 2. Specialist Shortfalls by State and Specialty: The Pipeline Is Deficient

Australia's Department of Health has published workforce modelling projecting specialist supply against demand. The picture is alarming for most states:

| State | Projected specialist shortfall by 2033 |
|---|---|
| Victoria | -10.1% (−1,761 FTE) |
| Queensland | -7% |
| New South Wales | -6.1% (already operating at -3.2% in 2025) |
| South Australia | Shortfall projected |
| Western Australia | Only state projected to avoid negative supply through 2048 |

By 2048, Victoria, Queensland and South Australia are projected to face **registrar shortfalls of 30–40%**.

These are aggregate projections. The supply picture for individual specialties is considerably worse.

**Psychiatry:** The most extensively documented shortage. Australia has approximately **2,277 active psychiatry trainees** nationally (end 2024), training under RANZCP. The Department of Health's November 2025 Psychiatry Supply and Demand Compendium estimates **20.7% undersupply of psychiatrists by 2048**. The Northern Territory faces the most acute shortage at **83.8%**. In 2025, 93% of surveyed psychiatrists said workforce shortages negatively affect patient care; more than 30% are considering leaving the profession within five years. Intake into RANZCP training actually fell in the first half of 2025, with an overall national decrease of 24 trainees compared to the prior period.

**Geriatric Medicine:** With baby boomers turning 80 from 2026 and approximately 400,000 Australians with dementia (projected to rise to 1.1 million by 2058), the demand for geriatricians is structurally growing. Specific national workforce shortage data are less readily available than for psychiatry — partly because geriatric medicine is a smaller specialty with less political advocacy profile — but training is competitive, the career pathway is long (5+ years of advanced training within the RACP structure), and rural geriatric medicine positions are difficult to fill. The RACP has called for urgent action on specialist workforce issues but has published no specific shortage quantification for geriatrics in 2025-26.

**Palliative Medicine:** The AIHW's October 2024 palliative care workforce report confirms the employed workforce of palliative medicine physicians remains "well below a level sufficient to deliver quality palliative care to meet the growing needs of the ageing population." The specialty has a relatively small training base; training requires 3 years of full-time equivalent specialist supervision. The specialist palliative care workforce is concentrated in major cities and large tertiary hospitals; rural palliative medicine positions are extraordinarily difficult to fill. Report 026 documented that Australia has approximately **0.25 specialist palliative medicine physicians per 1,000 people who die each year** — among the lowest rates in comparable OECD countries.

**Medical Oncology and Haematology:** These specialties intersect directly with the HTA access crisis documented in Report 022 — Australia's slow PBS listing pathway compounds access problems if, even when a medicine is finally listed, there are insufficient specialist oncologists or haematologists to prescribe it. An MJA InSight+ analysis titled "More Doctors, Fewer Specialists: Australia's Medical Training Pipeline in Disrepair" cited oncology workforce at "breaking point." The Australian Medical Oncologist Workforce Study (referenced in MJA) documented shortfalls persistent since at least 2012. The RACP's haematology and medical oncology training programs are accredited but have insufficient positions relative to projected cancer incidence growth.

### 3. The International Medical Graduate Safety Valve — and Its Limits

Australia's chronic specialist shortfalls are partially masked by **International Medical Graduates (IMGs)**. The DoH workforce projections estimate that IMGs will account for **42% of total medical workforce growth** between 2024 and 2048 — including **more than half of all registrar growth**.

This reliance creates specific structural vulnerabilities:

- **DPA/Section 19AB restrictions** (from Report 012): IMGs on Medicare-restricted provider numbers must work in Distribution Priority Areas for up to 10 years before gaining unrestricted access. This means IMGs are disproportionately directed to rural and underserviced areas, where they are critical to access — but where recognition of overseas specialist qualifications is also most contested and where professional isolation drives attrition.

- **Skills in Demand visa changes (April 2026)**: The new visa structure for skilled migrants (replacing TSS/482) introduced the Essential Skills stream for aged care and disability workers but retains barriers for specialist-level medical IMGs at the Core and Specialist stream tiers. Specialists certified overseas face pathway challenges under the Standard Specialist Assessment Pathway and AMC assessments that are slower and less straightforward than GP IMG pathways.

- **Quality and continuity risks**: Workforce strategies dependent on IMGs for specialist growth are structurally fragile if source countries tighten export of their own specialists — a real risk as the UK, Canada, and Europe face parallel shortfalls.

### 4. The Specialist Training Program: Modest, Extended, Under Redesign

The Commonwealth's primary lever for expanding specialist training capacity outside traditional metropolitan teaching hospitals is the **Specialist Training Program (STP)**. Administered through the Department of Health, Disability and Ageing, the STP funds:

- A salary contribution of **$110,619 per FTE per year** (2025; GST exclusive) for specialist registrars training in regional, rural, remote, or private settings
- An additional **rural loading of up to $30,000 per FTE per year** for positions in regional, rural, and remote areas

The STP was designed to broaden the geographic reach of specialist training — on the theory that trainees who train in rural settings are more likely to practise there as consultants. It has created hundreds of additional training positions in non-metropolitan facilities over its operational life.

However:
- Current STP grant agreements were extended for a further **12 months from March 2026 to February 2027** while the Department works with colleges and stakeholders on an **STP redesign**
- The Reserve List process (for new organisations to become STP posts) was formally paused in 2025 and will not run in 2026 — no new expansion is possible while redesign is pending
- The STP is a supplement to the state-funded specialist training system (states fund over 90% of all specialist positions), not a replacement for it

In contrast, the Government committed **over $1 billion** for GP training over five years from January 2026 ($751M to RACGP and $331M to ACRRM) — a signal that the policy priority on workforce has been focused on general practice, not specialist medicine.

### 5. The Training-Payment Nexus: How Training Controls Sustain Specialist Fee Power

The structural link between controlled specialist training supply and specialist gap fees (documented in Report 016) is direct and well-established in health economics.

When specialist training numbers are constrained below community need:
- **Supply-side pricing power increases** — with fewer specialists, average waiting times extend and individual practitioners face excess demand
- **Fee competition is dampened** — patients cannot easily find alternative providers at lower fees when supply is thin
- **Geographic concentration intensifies** — specialists cluster in high-fee urban markets; rural areas have thin supply where market competition does not apply at all

Report 016 documented that specialist gap fees rose 136% from 2010-11 to 2023-24, compared to 40% CPI growth. The causal factors are multiple, but constrained specialist training supply — particularly in high-demand specialties like dermatology, ophthalmology, ENT, and psychiatry — is among the structural contributors. The Grattan Institute's 2025 "Special Treatment" report proposed de-listing Medicare benefits for specialists charging more than 3x the MBS rebate and using $500M/year to fund 1 million additional public specialist consultations — in part as a corrective to insufficient specialist supply for public patients.

### 6. The HTA-Training Link: Approved Medicines Without Prescribers

Report 022 documented Australia's HTA access failure — only 25% of new OECD medicines are listed on the PBS, and patients wait an average of 3.6 years for first-in-class treatments. A less-noted dimension of this access problem is the **training pipeline interaction**: even when innovative medicines are eventually approved and PBS-listed, access requires a specialist with the training and authorisation to prescribe them.

- **CAR-T cell therapies** (reported in Report 006): all four publicly funded, but administered only in specific HST Program centres by trained haematologists and oncologists. If haematology trainee numbers do not grow proportionally to CAR-T listing expansion, access remains constrained even for funded treatments.
- **Gene therapies** (Zolgensma, Luxturna): require specific prescriber training and centre accreditation; the number of eligible prescribers is tiny.
- **Complex psychiatric medications**: new antipsychotic and mood disorder treatments increasingly require specialist supervision to initiate; psychiatrist shortfall constrains access.
- **Geriatric medicine**: dementia medicines (cholinesterase inhibitors, the emerging amyloid-targeting therapies), delirium management, and polypharmacy review are competencies requiring geriatric medicine training that is chronically undersupplied.

## Tensions and Open Questions

**1. Who should set training numbers?** The current arrangement gives specialist colleges autonomy over training capacity in their specialty — a design that creates an inherent tension between the college's interests (managing quality, sustaining member income, controlling professional standards) and the public interest (maximising access to specialist care). No independent statutory body currently arbitrates these interests with enforceable powers. The 2025 review calling for a reconstituted national health workforce body has been agreed in principle but not acted on.

**2. The prevocational cliff.** Australia is producing a large and growing cohort of junior doctors who cannot access specialist training pathways. By 2048, this group is projected at 38,000 FTE — nearly five times the specialist registrar pool. What happens to these doctors? Some migrate overseas; some remain in unaccredited generalist positions; some leave medicine entirely. None of these outcomes serve the public interest, and all represent waste of the training investment made in medical school education.

**3. The rural-specialist paradox.** The STP is designed to place trainees in rural settings to improve rural specialist supply. But the STP is simultaneously paused for redesign, and rural specialist positions are most difficult to fill because: training quality is perceived as lower; urban life is preferred by trainees; rural practice has professional isolation risks; and rural specialist fees (where bulk billing rates are high) may not generate the income to sustain specialist practice. Incentives may be necessary, but the quantum is presently insufficient.

**4. The HTA-training compound access failure.** Australia invests in HTA reform to improve PBS access but does not simultaneously invest in the specialist workforce required to prescribe the medicines. This produces a structural access constraint at the point of care even when funding is resolved — a form of access failure that is harder to see than a missing PBS listing but equally real for patients.

**5. The $1B GP vs negligible specialist training investment gap.** The government has invested over $1 billion in GP training (2026-2030) while the Specialist Training Program is paused for redesign with no equivalent investment commitment. The policy priority is clear — but specialist shortfalls are as documented and in some clinical domains (psychiatry, geriatrics, palliative medicine) arguably more acute than GP shortfalls in their specific disease burden.

## Threads to Branch Into Next

1. **Advance care planning as a healthcare payment mechanism** — MBS items for ACP consultations (Items 90089/90090); published evidence that ACP reduces hospitalisation in the final year of life; the economic case for MBS-subsidised ACP as a preventive health investment linking palliative care, aged care, and end-of-life policy; opened in Report 027

2. **Aged Care Quality and Safety Commission enforcement under the Aged Care Act 2024** — new powers to enforce rights to palliative care, VAD access, and advance care planning in 636 residential facilities; complaint-based vs proactive inspection; resourcing and capacity questions; opened from multiple prior reports

3. **Medicare Safety Net and the financial toxicity of late-life multi-system OOP** — people with life-limiting conditions simultaneously face MBS specialist gaps, PBS co-payments, and aged care co-contributions; the cumulative financial burden has not been systematically studied; flagged since Report 021

4. **Private health insurance coverage in end-of-life and specialist care** — PHI pays hospital costs and some specialist fees, but what it actually purchases at end of life (community palliative care, coordination costs, specialist-initiated MBS services) is poorly mapped; PHI premiums at highest rate since 2018 (4.41% average April 2026)

5. **Health Workforce Australia reconstitution** — calls to re-establish an independent national body for health workforce planning; what it would need to do differently from its 2009-2014 predecessor to enforce training alignment with community need; governance model options
