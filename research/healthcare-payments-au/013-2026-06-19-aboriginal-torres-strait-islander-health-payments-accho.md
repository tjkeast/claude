# Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Health Payments: ACCHOs, the IAHP, and the $4.4 Billion Funding Gap

**Report 013 — 2026-06-19**

**Summary:** Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Australians face a life expectancy gap of 8–9 years, a disease burden 2.3 times higher than non-Indigenous Australians, and a structural funding shortfall of $4.4 billion annually — yet the community-controlled health model that best serves First Nations communities remains chronically underfunded relative to need, and the majority of Closing the Gap health targets are not on track to be met by 2031.

**Why this follows from yesterday:** Report 012 documented an $8.35 billion rural health funding gap and showed that very remote communities face the worst health outcomes in Australia. The worst outcomes within that already-disadvantaged population concentrate specifically in Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander communities — making their distinct health funding architecture the natural next thread to examine.

---

## Background

### The Aboriginal Community Controlled Health Sector

The **Aboriginal Community Controlled Health Organisation (ACCHO)** model is the defining feature of First Nations health service delivery in Australia. Unlike mainstream general practice, ACCHOs are governed and operated by the communities they serve: each organisation is controlled by its local Aboriginal community through an elected Board. They provide comprehensive primary health care that integrates clinical services with social and emotional wellbeing support, cultural care, and connections to other community services.

The peak body, **NACCHO** (National Aboriginal Community Controlled Health Organisation), represents 148 member ACCHOs across every state and territory. As of 2024–25:

- The NACCHO-affiliated network operates across **550+ service locations**
- It delivers **over 3.6 million episodes of care annually** to approximately **410,000 patients**
- ACCHOs collectively employ around **7,000 people**, of whom **approximately 3,500 (50%) are Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Australians** — making the sector one of the largest First Nations employers in the country
- Services span general practice, nursing, dental, allied health, social and emotional wellbeing, antenatal care, child health, aged care, and chronic disease management

Beyond NACCHO-affiliated ACCHOs, Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Australians also access mainstream general practice, public hospitals, and a small number of Aboriginal-specific government-operated health services. The full picture is heterogeneous, but the ACCHO sector is the locus of culturally safe, community-controlled care.

### Why ACCHOs Matter for Health Payments

The ACCHO model is not just a service delivery structure — it is a distinct funding architecture. Unlike mainstream GPs, who derive revenue almost exclusively from Medicare (the MBS and PBS), ACCHOs operate under a **mixed funding model** that blends:

1. **Commonwealth block/activity-based grants** through the Indigenous Australians' Health Programme (IAHP)
2. **Medicare Benefits Schedule (MBS) revenue** — primarily from bulk-billed services
3. **State and territory government contributions** for specific services
4. **Targeted program grants** (e.g., mental health, maternal health, substance use)

The structure creates both flexibility and fragility. ACCHOs can integrate services that do not attract MBS rebates (e.g., social and emotional wellbeing workers, community health workers, cultural programs) because of their grant funding — but their total revenue is contingent on Commonwealth grant renewals, MBS utilisation rates, and a patchwork of time-limited programs that do not always continue.

## Key Findings

### 1. The $4.4 Billion Annual Funding Gap

The most comprehensive analysis of First Nations health expenditure adequacy — an **Equity Economics report commissioned by NACCHO** — found that total government and private health expenditure on Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Australians falls short of need by an estimated **$4.4 billion per year**.

The methodology adjusted for need rather than just population: because First Nations people have a **disease burden 2.3 times higher** than non-Indigenous Australians (AIHW data), equity of expenditure requires more spending per person, not simply equal per-capita expenditure.

Key figures from the analysis:
- The estimated gap is **$5,042 in health expenditure per Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander person** annually (total governments + private sector)
- **Commonwealth Government expenditure** alone is short by an estimated **$2.6 billion per year**
- Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Australians continue to live lives **8–9 years shorter** than other Australians
- **Aboriginal children are 55 times more likely to die of rheumatic heart disease** than non-Aboriginal children

In absolute terms, actual per-capita government health expenditure on First Nations people has been rising — reaching approximately **$12,146 per person per year in 2022–23** (up 4.3% per annum in real terms from $9,447 in 2016–17) — but expenditure growth has not kept pace with the scale of need. NACCHO's assessment is that successive federal budgets have delivered incremental increases that are insufficient relative to the structural underfunding embedded in existing funding formulae.

### 2. The Indigenous Australians' Health Programme (IAHP)

The federal government's primary funding vehicle for First Nations health is the **Indigenous Australians' Health Programme (IAHP)**, administered by the Department of Health, Disability and Ageing with contribution from the National Indigenous Australians Agency (NIAA).

A major structural change took effect on **1 July 2024**, when new IAHP Comprehensive Primary Health Care (CPHC) funding agreements commenced under a reformed **activity-based funding distribution model**. This replaced the previous approach with a more systematised allocation method:

- **Up to $2.697 billion (GST exclusive, indexed)** is available over four years from 2024–25 for ACCHOs delivering comprehensive primary health care
- Each ACCHO's funding allocation is determined **50% by adjusted client numbers** and **50% by adjusted episodes of care** — reported annually through the Online Services Report (OSR)
- The 2024–25 funding round provided each ACCHO with its 2023–24 amount plus a **one-off 5% increase**

The IAHP also funds a range of other programs including renal dialysis services in remote areas, maternal and child health programs (including Birthing on Country), and substance use services.

The **Australian National Audit Office (ANAO)** conducted a performance audit of Primary Healthcare Grants under the IAHP, with findings that have informed the ongoing reform of the funding model towards greater transparency and activity-based accountability.

### 3. MBS Item 715: The Aboriginal Health Assessment

The most significant MBS-specific item for First Nations health is **MBS Item 715** — the **Annual Health Assessment for Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander People**. This is a comprehensive, structured health check for eligible patients of any age, typically lasting 45–60 minutes.

Key features of item 715:
- **Free** at ACCHOs and bulk-billing clinics (bulk billed at the schedule fee)
- Can be claimed **once every 9 months** per patient
- Covers full lifespan — from infants through to elderly patients — with age-specific components
- A 715 assessment can trigger **referrals for up to 10 MBS-supported allied health or Aboriginal health and wellbeing services per calendar year** (via the follow-on items linked to GPMP/TCA and now GPCCMP pathways)
- The item generates significant MBS revenue for ACCHOs and is a key entry point into chronic disease management

NACCHO's submission to the MBS Health Assessment Items Review (May 2025) argued for further reform of the 715 pathway, including extended frequency for high-need patients and increased rebate levels to better reflect the comprehensive nature of the assessment compared to standard MBS health assessment items (701–707).

Between July 2023 and June 2024, ACCHOs in Queensland alone conducted over **90,000 item 715 assessments** — illustrating the volume and revenue significance of this item for the sector.

### 4. Closing the Gap: A Framework Under Pressure

The **2020 National Agreement on Closing the Gap** is the current governing framework for Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander health and wellbeing policy. Unusually, it was negotiated as a **joint party arrangement** between the Commonwealth, states and territories, and the **Coalition of Peaks** — a group of over 50 community-controlled peak organisations — giving First Nations organisations formal co-design and accountability roles.

The National Agreement has **19 socio-economic targets** across life expectancy, education, employment, and other domains. The **Productivity Commission's Annual Data Compilation Report (July 2025)** presented a sobering assessment of progress:

- **Only 4 of 19 targets are on track** to be met by the 2031 endpoint
- **Four key targets are worsening**: suicide rates (Target 14), adult incarceration rates (Target 10), developmental outcomes for children (Target 4), and children in out-of-home care (Target 12)
- **Target 1 — life expectancy** (to close the gap within a generation): assessed as **off track**. The life expectancy gap of 8.8 years for males and 8.1 years for females has narrowed only slightly, and the pace of change is insufficient to close by 2031
- **Healthy birthweight** (Target 2): 89.2% of First Nations babies born at healthy weight; improving but still off track

The **Commonwealth Closing the Gap 2025 Annual Report and 2026 Implementation Plan** (tabled in Parliament, February 2026) acknowledged the insufficient pace of change and committed to record health and hospital investment: **$250 million in Commonwealth funding and $200 million from states and territories** for health and hospital system reform, plus **$144.1 million for ACCHO health infrastructure**.

### 5. The 2026–27 Budget: "Helpful, Yes. Transformational, No."

The **2026–27 Federal Budget** allocated approximately **$1.2 billion** to First Nations outcomes, with health being the largest component. Key health investments included:

- Extension of **Birthing on Country** culturally led maternity services: **$44.4 million** (see below)
- Extension of **renal dialysis services** in remote communities
- **$144.1 million** for ACCHO health infrastructure (as above)
- **$4.5 million** for the Coalition of Peaks secretariat and National Agreement on Closing the Gap work
- **$2.7 million over 3 years** for an additional cohort of First Nations Health Worker Traineeship students commencing January 2026

NACCHO's headline response to the Budget was pointed: **"Helpful, yes. Transformational, no."** The organisation noted that while the investments were genuine, the Budget failed to address the structural underfunding revealed by the $4.4 billion analysis — and notably **did not continue funding for the Culture Care Connect program** (see below).

### 6. Birthing on Country: A Model That Works

**Birthing on Country** services are culturally led maternity care models in which Aboriginal women are supported to give birth in their own Country with care from midwives, Aboriginal health workers, and Elders — rather than travelling to often-distant hospitals. They are typically delivered through ACCHOs.

The **$44.4 million** Budget 2026–27 extension supports approximately **1,100 women per year** who access these programs.

The clinical evidence is strong. A landmark trial of the Waminda Birthing on Country service (NSW) — the first ACCHO to launch a **24/7 Midwifery Practice with continuity of care from conception to 6 weeks postpartum** (launched 2024) — found:

- **50% reduction in preterm births** compared to standard care
- **75.6% exclusive breastfeeding** at discharge vs 63.3% under standard care
- Higher rates of antenatal appointment attendance
- Significantly lower rates of child protection involvement

A March 2026 rural Birthing on Country trial published in a peer-reviewed journal found similarly positive maternal and neonatal outcomes, reinforcing the model's effectiveness. The program is widely cited as one of the clearest examples of community-controlled healthcare producing measurably better outcomes than mainstream alternatives for the same population.

### 7. Culture Care Connect: The $53 Million Gap

The **Culture Care Connect** program is a community-led suicide prevention and mental health initiative delivered by NACCHO and its member ACCHOs. At scale, it operates from **31 regional sites** employing **up to 100 people** from their respective local communities. NACCHO describes it as "the largest ever Indigenous-led mental health and suicide prevention initiative" in Australia.

The program provides:
- Community-led suicide prevention activities
- Integrated cultural care and aftercare support
- Coordination of crisis response within communities
- A model of care blueprint that has been adopted for local and jurisdictional planning

Despite strong evidence of need — Target 14 (suicide rates) is one of the four worsening Closing the Gap targets — the **2026–27 Budget did not include continued funding for Culture Care Connect**. NACCHO identified this as the most significant gap in the Budget, raising concerns that the program's 31 sites could face defunding, dismantling a community-based infrastructure that is not easily reconstructed.

This represents a concrete illustration of the structural tension in First Nations health funding: time-limited grant programs are used to fund critical community health infrastructure, but without ongoing appropriations, the infrastructure cannot plan ahead or operate sustainably.

### 8. Rheumatic Heart Disease: A Preventable Epidemic

**Rheumatic heart disease (RHD)** is a condition almost eliminated from non-Indigenous Australia but still prevalent in Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander communities — primarily those in remote areas with overcrowded housing and limited primary care access.

AIHW data to 2024:
- **472 acute rheumatic fever (ARF) diagnoses** in 2024; **93% (467 people) were Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander** — in a population that represents approximately 3.8% of total Australians
- At 31 December 2024, there were **11,794 people on RHD registers**; 81% (9,496) were First Nations
- ARF rates have declined from a peak of 69 per 100,000 First Nations population in 2021 to 52 per 100,000 in 2024 — improvement, but still vastly above any comparable non-Indigenous rate
- The **RHD endgame strategy** aims to eliminate the disease by 2031, requiring sustained investments in primary prevention (housing density, environmental health) and secondary prevention (penicillin prophylaxis adherence)

RHD is paradigmatic of the payment-access disconnect in First Nations health: treatment and secondary prevention are available and effective, but structural drivers — housing, water, overcrowding — lie outside the health payments system entirely.

### 9. First Nations Health Workforce

Addressing the health workforce in First Nations communities requires not just attracting mainstream GPs (as covered in Report 012) but building a First Nations-specific clinical workforce. The **First Nations Health Worker Traineeship Program (FNHWTP)**, funded by the Commonwealth at **$54.3 million**, aims to certify up to **500 First Nations people as Aboriginal Health Workers or Aboriginal Health Practitioners** by 2027.

Broader workforce goals:
- As of mid-2024, **more than 300 people had enrolled** in the FNHWTP across the NACCHO network
- NACCHO has articulated a goal of adding **over 8,000 Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander health workers** across the sector — a recognition that the community health worker workforce, not just doctors and nurses, is a critical infrastructure gap
- The Budget 2026–27 allocation of **$2.7 million over three years** for an additional FNHWTP cohort was welcomed but described as insufficient to achieve the broader workforce goal

## Tensions and Open Questions

**1. Activity-based funding vs. comprehensive care.** The July 2024 shift to an activity-based IAHP funding model (50% client numbers, 50% episodes of care) creates a tension: ACCHOs delivering comprehensive care to complex, high-needs patients may generate fewer billable episodes per dollar of cost than those seeing higher volumes of lower-acuity patients. The model may inadvertently penalise ACCHOs serving the highest-need communities with the most complex social and cultural determinants.

**2. Structural vs. incremental approaches to the funding gap.** The NACCHO analysis puts the annual shortfall at $4.4 billion; the Budget 2026–27 delivered approximately $1.2 billion in total First Nations expenditure across all domains. The framing of annual incremental investments as "record" spending against an acknowledged structural gap of $4.4 billion represents a significant disconnect between the scale of the problem and the political will to address it.

**3. Time-limited grants vs. ongoing appropriations.** The Culture Care Connect defunding risk illustrates a systemic problem: the most innovative, community-controlled programs in First Nations health are often funded through time-limited grants rather than ongoing appropriations. When grants expire, organisations built on program staff and infrastructure are dismantled. The result is a policy environment where "successful pilots" are repeatedly shut down rather than scaled.

**4. MBS under-utilisation.** While ACCHOs increasingly use the MBS as a revenue stream — and item 715 in particular — First Nations Australians as a whole still under-utilise Medicare relative to their disease burden. Barriers include: workforce shortages (fewer services to claim), cultural unsafety in mainstream settings, geographic inaccessibility, and administrative complexity. The MBS Optimisation Program (funded by NIAA and delivered by NACCHO) exists precisely because MBS revenue is being left unclaimed by under-resourced ACCHOs.

**5. The Closing the Gap co-design challenge.** The 2020 National Agreement is genuinely more collaborative than its predecessors, with the Coalition of Peaks as joint parties. But four worsening targets — particularly suicide and incarceration — point to domains where health payment reform cannot reach the structural determinants. Housing, justice, education, and employment sit largely outside the health payments system and are where the health gap is primarily generated.
