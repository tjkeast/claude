# Aged Care Workforce Pipeline: Fair Work Wage Decisions, Care Minutes Compliance, and Australia's 110,000-Worker Gap

**Report 023 — 2026-06-29**

**Summary:** Australia's aged care sector is simultaneously experiencing its most significant wage reform in decades and its deepest financial crisis on record. The Fair Work Commission's Aged Care Work Value Case — which lifted minimum wages for care workers by up to 28.5% over two stages — was the right policy response to a chronically underpaid workforce, but the AN-ACC funding mechanism has not kept pace. Sixty-one per cent of residential aged care homes are now operating at a loss, a 110,000-worker shortage is projected by 2036, and the sector's new migration pathway is producing fewer overseas workers than a single mid-sized hospital ward.

**Why this follows from yesterday:** Report 022 documented how HTA reform stalled at the Budget stage despite years of expert consensus — a case of the right diagnosis and no funded cure. The aged care workforce crisis is the same structural pattern applied to a different domain: the government has committed to quality reform (Aged Care Act 2024, care minutes mandates, Support at Home) while the pricing mechanism that enables providers to deliver that quality has lagged badly, particularly in rural and remote areas.

---

## Background

Australia's aged care sector supports approximately 1.5 million older Australians through a mix of home care (now Support at Home), residential aged care, and transitional care programs. Government expenditure on aged care reached **$39.2 billion in 2024–25** — a 9.6% increase on the prior year — and the demographic pressure is accelerating: the first baby boomers turned 80 in 2026, and the number of Australians aged 80 and over is projected to increase 60% to more than 2 million by 2035.

The sector employs approximately **456,000–500,000 paid workers**, supported by around 79,000 volunteers. Personal care workers (PCWs), assistants in nursing (AINs), enrolled nurses, and registered nurses (RNs) form the direct care workforce. Allied health, leisure and lifestyle staff, administrative workers, and domestic staff make up the remainder.

For decades, care work was chronically underpaid relative to comparable roles in hospitals, disability services, and other feminised care sectors. The Aged Care Work Value Case (ACWVC) addressed that directly — but the resulting cost increase has landed on a sector already structurally dependent on government subsidy and ill-equipped to absorb a major cost shock.

---

## Key Findings

### 1. The Fair Work Commission Aged Care Work Value Case

The ACWVC was the largest sectoral work value case in Australia's industrial relations history. The process unfolded in three stages:

**Stage 1 (June 2023):** The Full Bench found a 15% interim minimum wage increase for direct care workers was "plainly justified by work value reasons." This applied from 30 June 2023 and covered approximately **400,000 aged care workers** across the Aged Care Award, the Nurses Award, and the Social, Community, Home Care and Disability Services Industry Award.

**Stage 3 (late 2024):** The Full Bench established new benchmark rates for personal care workers, assistants in nursing, and home care workers. When combined with the Stage 1 interim increase, the total award wage increase ranged from **13.3% to 28.5%** depending on classification level. Implementation was staged: 50% of the additional Stage 3 increase took effect from 1 January 2025; the remaining 50% from 1 October 2025.

**1 August 2026:** A further increase took effect following the 2026 Annual Wage Review (Fair Work Commission: 4.75%), with the government providing grants to supplement increased leave entitlement costs — 25% of the FWC increase to leave entitlements for residential providers; 50% for home and community care providers.

These wage decisions were the correct response to a structural equity problem. Care workers performing equivalent or more demanding work than hospital employees were paid substantially less. However, the timing and quantum of AN-ACC price adjustments to fund these increases have created an acute financial stress in the sector.

### 2. The AN-ACC Funding Gap

The Australian National Aged Care Classification (AN-ACC) is the primary funding mechanism for residential aged care. It replaced the older ACFI model in October 2022. The daily AN-ACC price increased from **$282.44 to $295.64 per resident per day** on 1 October 2025 — an increase of $13.20 (4.7%).

Of that increase, $6.25 was attributable to the Stage 3 FWC wage increases for care workers, and $1.88 to FWC increases for aged care nurses. The hotelling supplement (funding everyday living costs: cleaning, catering, laundry) increased from $15.60 to $22.15 per resident per day from September 2025.

Despite these adjustments, providers report that the **actual funding increase in effective terms was only 1.7–2.9%** — far below the labour cost increase generated by the Stage 3 wage decision. The structural problem is that AN-ACC price-setting is based on sector-average costs at a point in time, with a lag before the next repricing cycle captures actual wage cost increases.

### 3. Provider Financial Crisis: 61% Operating at a Loss

The StewartBrown Aged Care Financial Performance Survey — the sector's most comprehensive independent benchmarking exercise, covering 1,200 homes and 100,648 beds (46% of the sector) — provides the clearest picture of financial strain:

- **Average operating deficit:** $1.04 million per home (six months to 31 December 2025)
- **Per bed per day operating result:** –$9.80 per bed per day (compared to a +$1.56 surplus in the same period a year earlier)
- **Percentage of homes operating at a loss:** 61% (up from a near-break-even position in 2024)
- **Direct care margin collapse:** From +$19.08 per bed per day to +$6.15 per bed per day — the direct care margin is the surplus (or deficit) between AN-ACC funding and actual direct care labour costs

The sector requires a minimum return of approximately **$20,000 per bed per annum** to sustain capital maintenance and reinvestment. The current average is $4,582 — roughly one-fifth of what's needed. Ageing Australia (the peak body for aged care providers) describes the current funding and pricing settings as "not generating returns sufficient to encourage new entrants or further investment."

The government has made available an **AN-ACC Transition Fund** — extended for a further 12 months in 2026 — targeting providers in "thin market" areas (typically rural and remote communities) where geographic isolation compounds financial pressure. This is a targeted and necessary measure but does not address the systemic pricing gap facing the broader sector.

Separately, residential aged care is operating at approximately **94% capacity nationally**, with some capital cities reporting full occupancy. This is a structural capacity constraint compounding the demographic pressure: even if funding improved, there is insufficient bed stock to absorb growth.

### 4. Care Minutes: Compliance Lagging, Stakes Rising

The care minutes mandate requires each residential aged care home to deliver a minimum average of **215 minutes of direct care per resident per day**, of which **at least 44 minutes must be delivered by a registered nurse (RN)**. The mandate has been in force since 1 October 2024.

From **1 April 2026**, funding for non-specialised residential homes in metropolitan areas (MMM1) was formally linked to care minutes delivery. Homes failing to meet their care minutes targets can lose up to **$31.92 per resident per day** in funding through the care minutes supplement — a significant financial consequence.

National compliance remains inadequate:

- **2024–25:** 45.9% of residential homes met both the total care minutes and RN care minutes targets (up from 34.0% in 2023–24, but still less than half the sector)
- **For-profit homes in metropolitan areas:** Only 18% meeting both targets, versus 45% of not-for-profit homes in the same regions
- **Regional/remote areas:** Counterintuitively, compliance rates are higher outside metropolitan areas (MMM2-7) — likely because many rural homes are smaller and not-for-profit with stronger community governance

The compliance gap is predominantly a **RN availability problem**, not a personal care worker availability problem. Many homes can deliver sufficient total care minutes but cannot source the 44 RN minutes required. This is directly linked to the broader RN shortage: DoH&DA modelling projects a shortage of **17,551 FTE nurses in aged care by 2035** within a national nursing shortage of approximately 79,473 nurses.

The mandatory audited **Care Minutes Performance Statement** requirement — commencing with the 2025–26 aged care financial report — will make compliance data more granular and harder to dispute.

### 5. Workforce Shortage: Scale and Trajectory

The CEDA (Committee for Economic Development of Australia) Duty of Care report provides the definitive workforce shortage projection:

- **Annual shortfall (current):** At least 35,000 FTE direct care workers
- **2036 projection:** 110,000 FTE shortage — equivalent to losing one in five current workers
- **2050 projection:** 400,000 FTE shortage, encompassing residential, home, and disability care
- **Annual recruitment requirement:** At least 17,000 additional direct care workers every year for the next decade just to maintain current care standards

These projections reflect a structural demographic collision: the same baby boomer generation that represents the future clients of aged care also comprises a significant share of the current aged care workforce (personal care workers, administrative staff) who will retire over the same period.

The workforce is heavily feminised (~85%), predominantly employed part-time, and concentrated in metropolitan and inner-regional areas. Regional and remote areas face disproportionate shortages: a 2025 comparative analysis published in a peer-reviewed journal estimated that **492,416 additional care hours per week** (approximately 12,958 FTE workers) were needed in remote communities to reach metropolitan parity, with a further **95,342 FTE** needed in rural areas.

### 6. Migration: A Pipeline With Low Throughput

International recruitment is theoretically the fastest lever to address workforce shortfalls — faster than training pipeline expansion, which has a multi-year lag. Australia's migration settings have evolved considerably, but the actual throughput into aged care remains very low:

**Aged Care Industry Labour Agreement (ACILA):** The number of aged care labour agreements grew from 10 to **142 by June 2025** (company-specific and ACILA). However, the actual number of temporary migrants sponsored under these agreements across the entire sector at June 2025 was only **2,426** — fewer workers than a mid-sized metropolitan hospital employs. Critically, **90% of sponsored visas went to workers already inside Australia**, not new overseas entrants. Structural barriers include protracted negotiation processes, inconsistent approval timelines across states, and high visa costs deterring smaller providers.

Despite these low formal numbers, an estimated **70,000 personal care workers — roughly one in six of the total workforce** — are on some form of temporary visa. This reflects informal pathways (student visas, working holiday makers, partners) as much as formal labour agreements.

**Skills in Demand (SID) visa from 18 April 2026:** The replacement for the Temporary Skill Shortage (TSS/482) visa created a three-stream framework — Specialist Skills, Core Skills, and an **Essential Skills Stream** specifically targeting care workers, hospitality, and meat processing. For the first time, personal care workers in aged care have a formal, legislated pathway to permanent residency. Key changes include:

- Minimum salary thresholds for Core Skills stream: AUD $76,515 (rising to $79,499 from 1 July 2026)
- Essential Skills stream: for essential workers below Core Skills threshold; employer-sponsored; sector-specific
- Increased mobility: workers have 180 days to find a new sponsor after leaving a job
- Work experience requirement: reduced from 2 years to 1 year for many pathways

The Essential Skills stream represents an important structural improvement but its practical impact will depend on whether aged care providers — particularly smaller regional operators — can afford and navigate the sponsorship process, and whether visa processing times improve.

**Fee-Free TAFE:** For domestic workforce expansion, the government's Fee-Free TAFE program covers the Certificate III in Individual Support (CHC33021 — the primary entry-level qualification for personal care workers) without tuition fees for eligible workers. Take-up has been reasonable, but the training pipeline has an 18–24 month lag before new graduates can fill roles.

### 7. The Rural and Remote Compounding Problem

Rural and remote aged care sits at the intersection of every workforce pressure: higher care needs per resident, smaller provider organisations, thinner labour markets, higher travel costs, fewer training institutions, and lower attraction of RNs who prioritise hospital career pathways.

The care minutes mandate — designed with metropolitan workforce markets in mind — creates an especially acute tension for rural providers. Sourcing a registered nurse for a 44-minute daily minimum across a small facility in a remote town may require a full-time RN position that the facility's bed numbers cannot economically justify. The AN-ACC Transition Fund and geographic scaling of some supplements provide partial relief, but the core economic problem — that the cost of providing care in thin markets is materially higher than the price the AN-ACC pays — is not resolved by the current funding architecture.

## Tensions and Open Questions

**1. The wage-funding mismatch as a permanent problem.** The AN-ACC price-setting cycle is retrospective — it captures cost increases with a lag. If the Fair Work Commission continues to make significant wage decisions (not unlikely given ongoing work value proceedings in adjacent sectors), the funding gap will recur unless the price-setting mechanism is redesigned to track actual sector labour costs in closer to real time.

**2. The RN mandate as a care quality vs. access paradox.** The 44-minute RN mandate is clinically well-founded — RN involvement in care planning and clinical oversight improves outcomes. But in markets where RNs are structurally unavailable, the mandate creates a compliance-funding loop where homes are penalised financially for a workforce failure that is not within their control. Whether the mandate should be accompanied by a rural and remote workforce subsidy — rather than a pure funding penalty — is not resolved by current policy.

**3. Migration settings vs. wages.** A structurally underpaid workforce created the conditions for Australia's care crisis. Higher wages (the work value case outcome) are essential to attracting and retaining Australian-born workers. But higher wages also raise the cost of sponsoring overseas workers and make the sector's economics harder to sustain. The intersection of wage policy, immigration settings, and AN-ACC pricing has not been explicitly modelled as a coherent system.

**4. For-profit vs. not-for-profit compliance divergence.** The data consistently shows that for-profit providers in metropolitan areas have the lowest care minutes compliance rates (18% in MM1). This is not a workforce availability problem in those markets — it is a staffing model decision. The April 2026 funding-compliance link is designed to address this, but whether the financial consequence is large enough to change behaviour is unknown.

**5. The $1.7 billion bed construction incentive vs. the workforce to staff it.** Budget 2026-27 allocated $1.7 billion to incentivise construction of 5,000 new residential aged care beds per year. Building beds does not create the workers to staff them. Without a parallel workforce pipeline plan, new beds risk sitting vacant or being staffed inadequately — reproducing at scale the compliance failures already evident.

## Threads to Branch Into Next

1. **Residential aged care market structure** — the shift from not-for-profit to for-profit provider majority post-ACFI; how RAD retention, accommodation supplement restructuring, and care minutes compliance interact to shape market incentives; whether the $1.7 billion bed construction incentive will reach underserved areas or consolidate in profitable metropolitan markets

2. **Dementia and the hospital-to-aged-care boundary** — dementia is the single largest driver of residential aged care demand; the $224.3M Budget 2026-27 Hospital to Aged Care Dementia Support pathway; hospital avoidance and step-down care funding; intersects directly with RN shortages and care complexity

3. **Specialist training pipeline controls** — medical college training number restrictions across anaesthesia, ophthalmology, oncology, and psychiatry; how college-controlled supply limits perpetuate specialist fee power; link to HTA reform (drugs approved but no specialists to prescribe them)

4. **Aged care workforce innovation** — nursing assistant upskilling models; international nurse training compacts; Allied Health Assistants in aged care; technology substitution (monitoring sensors, medication dispensing robots); and whether the workforce crisis is ultimately solvable within current models

5. **Outcomes-based reimbursement in aged care** — AN-ACC is a classification-based funding model; whether an outcomes-linked component (hospitalisation avoidance, falls reduction, quality of life measures) could provide better incentives and better care; international models (UK's personal budgets, Netherlands' Buurtzorg home care)
