# Victoria's Mental Health Levy and Hypothecated Health Funding: Australia's Experiment with Dedicated Mental Health Finance

**Report 048 — 2026-07-24**

**Summary:** In 2022, Victoria introduced Australia's most ambitious health funding innovation in decades: a hypothecated payroll tax levy ring-fenced exclusively for mental health services, expected to raise over $1 billion per year by 2025-26. The levy provides the financial engine for implementing the 65 recommendations of the 2021 Royal Commission into Victoria's Mental Health System. At the five-year midpoint, the model offers important lessons: hypothecation generates both revenue and political durability for health investment, but Queensland's parallel levy experiment has already been audited and found "not effectively managed," and Victoria's own psychiatrists warn that reform is stalling on the most critical components — rehabilitation, housing, and specialist workforce — despite the money flowing in.

**Why this follows from yesterday:** Report 047 documented that Victoria's Royal Commission into its Mental Health System, funded by a dedicated payroll levy, represents the most politically significant state-level commitment to mental health reform in Australian history — and that psychiatrists are warning the implementation may be stalling. That raises the question this report examines: does the hypothecated funding model actually deliver? And could other states or the Commonwealth replicate it?

---

## Background

Australia's mental health system has a fundamental structural tension: the Commonwealth funds what it can measure (Medicare claims, PBS scripts) while states fund what is hardest to measure — the community mental health teams, crisis services, and inpatient beds that serve people with the most severe illness. As documented in Report 047, this has produced a $13.8 billion-per-year national mental health system in which the state community services layer ($3.5 billion in community ambulatory services alone) is perpetually under-resourced, with mental health competing for budget against hospitals, schools, and infrastructure in annual state budget processes.

Victoria's response to this structural problem was unprecedented in Australian health policy: hypothecation. Rather than funding mental health reform through general appropriation — a process vulnerable to year-to-year budget pressure — Victoria legislated that a new payroll tax surcharge would be ring-fenced by law, with 100% of its revenue dedicated exclusively to mental health services. The mechanism was Interim Recommendation 8 of the Royal Commission into Victoria's Mental Health System, and it became law on 1 January 2022.

### The Victorian Levy: Structure

The **Mental Health and Wellbeing Levy** operates as a surcharge on Victoria's existing payroll tax:

| Threshold (national payroll) | Levy rate on Victorian taxable wages |
|------------------------------|--------------------------------------|
| >$10 million | 0.5% |
| >$100 million | additional 0.5% (total 1.0%) |

The levy:
- Applies to fewer than **5% of Victorian employers** — those with substantial national payrolls, overwhelmingly large corporations and listed companies
- Is collected by the State Revenue Office alongside ordinary payroll tax
- Is **100% hypothecated by legislation** — the Mental Health and Wellbeing Act 2022 prohibits the revenue being used for any purpose other than mental health services
- Was projected to raise approximately **$600 million** in its first full year, **$900 million** by 2023-24, and **over $1 billion** from 2025-26

The model draws on an older Victorian precedent: the **VicHealth levy** (1987), in which a dedicated surcharge on tobacco products created a hypothecated fund for health promotion — widely regarded as the world's first sin-tax earmarked explicitly for health. Victoria has now applied the same logic at dramatically larger scale and to a direct service funding question.

### The Reform Mandate: The Royal Commission

The Levy provides the funding for implementing the **Royal Commission into Victoria's Mental Health System**, which released its final report in March 2021 after a two-year inquiry. The Commission made **65 final recommendations** (and 9 interim, for a total of 74), covering:

- The creation of a new community-first service architecture — Mental Health and Wellbeing Locals as area-based access points across Victoria
- A major expansion of inpatient capacity in general hospital settings
- A new legislated framework for mental health rights, including reduced use of restrictive interventions
- Substantial workforce development, training, and pipeline investment
- New governance structures — including a Mental Health and Wellbeing Commission and a Chief Officer for Mental Health and Wellbeing
- Funded rehabilitation services, supported housing, and community residential care
- Specific reforms for children and young people, Aboriginal communities, and people with dual diagnosis

The Government committed to the full 10-year reform agenda and allocated $3.8 billion in its immediate post-Commission budget response. The Levy was designed to make that commitment fiscally credible beyond a single budget cycle.

## Key Findings

### 1. Five Years In: What the Levy Has Built

As of mid-2026, at the **five-year midpoint** of the 10-year reform agenda, the Victorian Government reports work underway on **more than 85% of the 74 recommendations**. Key infrastructure delivered includes:

**Mental Health and Wellbeing Locals:** The centrepiece of the new community service model — area-based, free-at-point-of-care, multidisciplinary access points for adults not yet in crisis. The Royal Commission recommended up to 60 Locals across Victoria by the end of 2026. As of the 2026-27 Budget, **22 Mental Health and Wellbeing Locals are operating** — meaning the 60-centre target is under significant pressure with just months remaining. The first six opened in late 2022; a further nine commenced in December 2023; additional sites have been added progressively.

**Inpatient beds:** The 2025-26 budget alone committed **$289 million** to operate 82 inpatient mental health beds and 29 alcohol and other drug beds. Hospital in the Home beds for mental health received a further $12-30 million across successive budgets.

**Workforce investment:** $47 million in 2025-26 for Victoria's mental health workforce and training pipeline. The Mental Health and Wellbeing Workforce Strategy is a multi-year commitment covering nursing, psychology, peer workforce, and allied health training pathways.

**Legal and governance foundations:** The Mental Health and Wellbeing Act 2022 came into effect, replacing the Mental Health Act 2014, establishing new rights, and creating the Mental Health and Wellbeing Commission (MHWC) as an independent statutory watchdog with power to hold government accountable for implementation.

**Budget scale:** In 2026-27, Victoria will spend a record **$32 billion** on health — an extraordinary figure that reflects both the scale of the post-Royal Commission reform agenda and the broader political economy of health as the dominant state expenditure. The Levy contribution of over $1 billion per year represents approximately **3% of total Victorian health expenditure**, or roughly **$150-160 per Victorian person per year**.

### 2. The Stalling Warning: What the Levy Has Not Yet Built

Despite the substantial investment, the Royal College of Psychiatrists (RANZCP) Victorian Branch issued warnings through the 2025-26 and 2026-27 budget cycles that **reform may be stalling on the hardest components**.

Gaps explicitly identified by RANZCP and other clinical bodies include:

- **Rehabilitation services:** The Royal Commission identified rehabilitation as a missing pillar of Victoria's mental health system. Five years on, no new rehabilitation beds or programs have been funded at the scale the Commission envisaged. People with schizophrenia, severe bipolar disorder, and complex presentations who need step-down rehabilitation between acute hospital admission and community life have no new services to step down to.

- **Supported housing:** The intersection of mental illness and housing insecurity is the largest single determinant of revolving-door hospital admissions. The Royal Commission specifically recommended new supported housing pathways. These remain largely unfunded at scale.

- **Community-based alternatives to emergency departments:** As documented in Report 047, mental health ED presentations have grown at a rate of 156% over nine years — a direct consequence of community service capacity limits. The Locals model was partly intended to provide a walk-in alternative, but with only 22 of a planned 60 centres operational and limited after-hours capacity, this function is not yet delivered at scale.

- **Trauma-informed care:** The Commission's emphasis on trauma-informed care and alternatives to restrictive interventions (seclusion and restraint) was a major reform pillar. RANZCP notes there is **no statewide clinical strategy** underpinning this — individual service-level change without a systematic approach.

- **Rural and regional services:** The 22 Locals currently operating are concentrated in metropolitan Melbourne. Regional Victoria, where workforce shortages are most acute (as documented in Report 045 on rural mental health), has fewer centres and longer implementation timelines.

The RANZCP's 2025-26 statement that "Victoria's most vulnerable public mental health patients are overlooked" — referring specifically to people with severe and complex illness — captures a central tension: the Levy is generating substantial revenue, but the political and operational priorities of implementation have focused on the more accessible, lower-complexity end of the service spectrum (community access, early intervention, legislative rights) rather than the most resource-intensive rehabilitation and housing components.

### 3. The Queensland Comparison: A Cautionary Tale

Victoria was not the only Australian state to introduce a mental health levy. **Queensland introduced its own payroll tax surcharge on 1 January 2023**, structured as:

| Threshold (national payroll) | Levy rate on Queensland taxable wages |
|------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| >$10 million | 0.25% |
| >$100 million | additional 0.50% (total 0.75%) |

Queensland's levy has lower rates than Victoria's and, critically, was introduced without the same legislative ring-fencing and governance framework that anchors Victoria's model. This distinction proved significant.

In **May 2026**, the Queensland Audit Office (QAO) tabled a report — *Managing Funding from the Mental Health Levy* — that found Queensland's levy was **"not effectively managed as a whole."** Specifically:

> *"Effective governance arrangements for the levy were not established when it was introduced... There is no shared understanding between key entities about what services and infrastructure can be funded, or a whole-of-levy approach to planning, monitoring, or reporting on how funds are used."*

The QAO found no mechanism for tracking how levy revenue was being allocated across Queensland Health's multiple budget streams, no performance framework for measuring the mental health outcomes the levy was meant to achieve, and no clear accountability line between the levy revenue collected and the services ultimately funded.

The incoming Crisafulli Government's response was to allocate more than **$350 million in additional funding** from the levy to new and expanded initiatives in December 2025 — a significant injection, but one that cannot easily be tracked against the levy's original intent without the governance structures the QAO found absent.

The Queensland experience demonstrates that hypothecation alone does not guarantee effective spending. The ring-fence prevents diversion of funds to other purposes, but it does not automatically create a performance accountability framework for how ring-fenced funds are used within the mental health sector.

### 4. The Hypothecation Evidence Base: What International Experience Shows

The case for hypothecated health taxes rests on two arguments: they raise additional revenue that would not otherwise be appropriated, and they provide political durability for health investment by giving voters a direct link between a specific tax and a specific benefit.

The evidence on both is mixed.

**Public support:** Prior to Victoria's 1987 tobacco levy for VicHealth, surveys found 47% of respondents in favour of increased tobacco taxes; when those taxes were hypothecated for health, support rose to **84%**. This "hypothecation premium" in public support appears robust in multiple jurisdictions and applies to the 2022 Victorian mental health levy, which passed the Legislative Council with broad support including from crossbench members who would not ordinarily support payroll tax increases.

**Revenue additionality:** The critical question for health economists is whether hypothecated taxes genuinely add to health spending or simply displace general appropriation. The academic consensus (Health Foundation, World Bank, RAND) is that hypothecation is "unlikely to bring a sustained net increase in revenue due to offsetting" — Treasury departments tend to reduce general appropriation to health by roughly the amount raised through earmarked levies over time. Victoria's model partially addresses this risk by making the hypothecation statutory, but even statutory ring-fencing cannot prevent a government from reducing general health appropriation at the same time as the levy runs.

**Efficiency risks:** Hypothecation creates spending rigidities. If the mental health levy raises more than the system can absorb productively in a given year, that surplus must still be spent on mental health — potentially funding lower-priority initiatives rather than higher-priority non-mental-health investments. Conversely, if the system needs urgent investment in a non-levy-covered component (e.g., capital construction that takes time to flow through), the revenue continues accumulating without the corresponding service capacity to spend it on.

**International comparators:** Beyond VicHealth (1987) and the mental health levies, other hypothecated health tax examples include:
- **Italy:** A corporate tax on value added (IRAP) and regional income tax surcharges earmarked for health — a larger-scale, more established hypothecation model
- **Ghana:** National Health Insurance Levy — a 2.5% VAT levy hypothecated for health insurance, credited with substantially expanding coverage
- **United Kingdom:** While the NHS is general-budget funded, the 2022 National Insurance contribution increase for health and social care (subsequently reversed) was explicitly framed as a health hypothecation — and reversed under political pressure within 18 months, illustrating the fragility of non-statutory hypothecation

### 5. The Independent Oversight Model: Victoria's MHWC

One feature of Victoria's model that is largely absent from Queensland's levy is strong independent oversight. The **Mental Health and Wellbeing Commission (MHWC)** is a statutory authority created by the Mental Health and Wellbeing Act 2022 with the explicit function of:

- Monitoring and reporting on the performance, quality and safety of Victoria's mental health system
- Tracking and publicly reporting on implementation of the Royal Commission's recommendations
- Reporting on the use of restrictive interventions (seclusion and restraint)
- Holding the Secretary of the Department of Health and the government publicly accountable

The MHWC publishes annual reports and a recommendation implementation tracker visible to the public. This creates a degree of public accountability for levy spending that is absent from Queensland's model and that most Commonwealth health program spending also lacks.

The MHWC's 2024-25 annual report (published March 2026) noted progress on the legislative and structural reforms, while flagging continued concerns about the pace of community service development, the rehabilitation gap, and the workforce shortages that limit the system's capacity to absorb new investment.

### 6. The Workforce Constraint: Money Alone Cannot Hire Psychiatrists That Don't Exist

The deepest limitation of the hypothecated levy model is that it solves a funding problem in a context where the binding constraint may not primarily be funding. As documented in Report 047, Victoria faces a projected shortage of 74 FTE psychiatrists by 2025, rising to 124 FTE by 2030. No amount of levy revenue can produce a psychiatrist in less than the 12 years it takes to complete medical training and specialist training combined.

This is the core tension in the Levy's current implementation. The revenue is flowing. The mental health system has more money than at any prior point in Victoria's history. But:

- **Mental Health and Wellbeing Locals** cannot be fully staffed without clinical psychologists, mental health nurses, and psychiatrists — all of which are in shortage nationally (documented in Reports 045 and 047)
- **New inpatient beds** cannot be safely operated without adequate medical and nursing staff
- **Rehabilitation programs** require specialist occupational therapists, psychiatric nurses, and social workers in configurations that the workforce pipeline cannot supply on a 3-5 year timeframe

The result is that some of the most important components of reform — the ones requiring the highest-skilled workers — are the ones where implementation is slowest, regardless of the financial commitment.

## Tensions and Open Questions

**1. Is the hypothecation truly additional?** The Victorian Government has committed $3.8 billion (and more) in post-Commission funding, with the Levy providing a sustained revenue floor. But the quantum of general appropriation that would have flowed to mental health absent the Levy is unknown. If the Levy displaced $600-700 million in general appropriation that would have otherwise been allocated, the net additionality is much smaller than the headline revenue figure suggests. The absence of a pre-Levy mental health expenditure baseline that can be compared with post-Levy actuals makes this question largely unanswerable.

**2. Can the 60-Locals target be rescued?** With only 22 Mental Health and Wellbeing Locals operating by mid-2026 and the Royal Commission's deadline of 60 by end-2026 fast approaching, the government faces a choice between announcing a target miss, fast-tracking partial-service openings that may not deliver the full model, or quietly redefining the target. The 60-centre commitment was the centrepiece of the community service reform agenda. How the shortfall is handled will test the accountability mechanisms of both the MHWC and the political commitment to the Royal Commission's model.

**3. The rehabilitation and housing gap is the hardest to fix.** The reform components that have progressed most are the ones with clear structural deliverables: legislation passed, governance bodies established, new service categories created. The components that are stalling — rehabilitation, supported housing, trauma-informed care — require sustained operational investment in services that are hard to measure, politically invisible, and workforce-intensive. These are precisely the components that general-budget mental health funding historically neglected. If the Levy model cannot fund these effectively either, it is unclear what mechanism could.

**4. Should the Commonwealth adopt hypothecation for mental health?** The Commonwealth's response to the national mental health crisis has been an array of discrete programs — Better Access, headspace, Medicare Mental Health Centres, NDIS psychosocial supports — funded through general appropriation subject to annual budget decisions. The Productivity Commission's 2025 verdict that the National Mental Health and Suicide Prevention Agreement is "not fit for purpose" raises the question: should the Commonwealth consider a hypothecated funding mechanism for mental health? The Medicare Levy (2% of taxable income) is Australia's most significant national hypothecated health tax, though its revenue goes to the Consolidated Fund and is not separately tracked to health spending. A dedicated Commonwealth mental health levy — or a dedicated earmark within the Medicare Levy — would be a novel policy instrument, but one with the precedent of both Victoria and Queensland behind it.

**5. The Queensland audit warning.** The QAO's finding that Queensland's levy was "not effectively managed" provides a direct lesson for any government designing a hypothecated mental health tax: the governance and accountability architecture matters as much as the revenue mechanism. Revenue without a performance framework, a clear expenditure strategy, and an independent oversight body is just money — and money directed at a complex system without accountability infrastructure can be spent on the easiest, most visible activities rather than the most effective ones.

2. **NGO and community-managed organisations in mental health: the block grant sector** — the ~600 mental health NGOs that received state and Commonwealth funding for psychosocial programs were severely disrupted when the NDIS launched; with NDIS cuts from October 2026 and the Foundational Supports rollout not yet operational, this sector is the most exposed in Australia's mental health architecture

3. **Accommodation supplement equity in aged care** — persistently deferred from Report 043; the Commonwealth means-tested supplement paid to providers when residents cannot fund a RAD or DAP; adequacy in regional markets and whether rates reflect actual building/operating costs

4. **Medicare Levy and health hypothecation at the Commonwealth level** — the Medicare Levy raises ~$25 billion per year from 2% of taxable income, but its revenue is not separately tracked to health spending; whether a genuine dedicated Commonwealth mental health earmark could replicate the Victorian model nationally

5. **The Fifth National Mental Health and Suicide Prevention Plan (2021-2031): implementation failure** — the national framework within which the failed NMHSPA sat; what was committed, what was achieved, and what the successor framework must look like to be more than another set of coordination aspirations
