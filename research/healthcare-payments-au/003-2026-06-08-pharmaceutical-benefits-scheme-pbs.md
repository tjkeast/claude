# The Pharmaceutical Benefits Scheme: How Australia Prices and Subsidises Medicines

**Report 003 — 2026-06-08**

**Summary:** The Pharmaceutical Benefits Scheme (PBS) is a fundamentally different payments architecture from the Medicare Benefits Schedule — where the MBS administers a fee that tends to lag costs, the PBS uses competitive tendering, mandatory cost-effectiveness assessment, and an active price disclosure mechanism to continuously drive down what the government pays for medicines. In 2024–25 the government spent $19.1 billion subsidising 331 million prescriptions. But the system's rigorous gating mechanism also creates access delays averaging 591 days from TGA approval to PBS listing, and a rising tide of high-cost cancer biologics and gene therapies is testing the scheme's sustainability.

**Why this follows from yesterday:** Report 002 showed that the MBS Schedule Fee is an administered government price, set by formula, that has systematically lagged the real cost of delivering care. The PBS represents the government's alternative philosophy: rather than administer a price and let providers charge above it, the PBS gates entry through cost-effectiveness assessment, then actively negotiates prices down over time.

---

## Background

Australia's Pharmaceutical Benefits Scheme was established in 1948 — one year before the National Health Service in the UK — and is among the world's oldest national medicine subsidy programmes. Its core bargain: the government bears the majority of the cost of listed medicines, patients pay a fixed co-payment capped by regulation, and manufacturers gain access to a guaranteed public market in exchange for accepting a price set through negotiation and benchmarked against cost-effectiveness evidence.

This structure differs sharply from the MBS. There is no analogue to the MBS "Schedule Fee" — a fixed list price that providers may exceed at will. Under the PBS, the government-negotiated price is a ceiling, not a floor. Manufacturers cannot legally charge above it for listed indications, and price disclosure mechanisms ensure that listed prices fall toward actual transaction prices over time. The result is a scheme that has, by most international comparisons, delivered medicines at substantially lower cost than comparable systems in the United States, Canada, or Switzerland.

## Key Findings

### 1. Scale: $19.1 Billion and 331 Million Prescriptions

The PBS subsidises more than 5,600 listed medicine items (around 900 unique medicines) covering the vast majority of common therapeutic categories.

In **2023–24**, **331 million PBS and under-copayment prescriptions** were dispensed — roughly 13 scripts per Australian per year. Of those, 63.1% went to patients aged 60 and over, reflecting the scheme's heavy tilt toward chronic and age-related disease management. Government expenditure in that year was **$17.7 billion**; in **2024–25** it rose to **$19.1 billion** — a 7.9% increase, driven primarily by new high-cost biologics and expanded indications for existing medicines. (PBS Expenditure and Prescriptions Report 2024–25)

Patient contributions account for a small share of total cost. In 2023–24, concessional patients (pensioners, those with a Healthcare Card) accounted for **88% of prescriptions** but only **61% of government expenditure**, because concessional-rate items include many inexpensive, high-volume generics. The high-cost cancer biologics and rare disease therapies that dominate government expenditure are disproportionately dispensed to general (non-concessional) patients.

### 2. The PBAC: Cost-Effectiveness as the Gatekeeper

Every medicine seeking PBS listing must first be recommended by the **Pharmaceutical Benefits Advisory Committee (PBAC)** — an independent statutory body comprising doctors, health professionals, health economists, and two consumer representatives. No minister can list a medicine without a positive PBAC recommendation. (Under the National Health Act 1953, the Health Minister has discretion to list medicines not recommended by PBAC, but this power is rarely exercised and is politically costly.)

PBAC assessment takes **17 weeks per cycle**. The committee meets **three to six times per year**. Sponsors submit dossiers of clinical and economic evidence; these are independently evaluated by academic health technology assessment (HTA) units before the committee makes its recommendation.

The key question PBAC asks is: **is this medicine safe, clinically effective, and cost-effective compared to existing funded options?** Cost-effectiveness is quantified as the Incremental Cost-Effectiveness Ratio (ICER) — the additional cost per additional Quality-Adjusted Life Year (QALY) gained over the comparator.

PBAC does not publish a formal ICER threshold, but analyses of its historical decisions indicate that:
- Medicines with an ICER below **~$45,000–$50,000 per QALY** are almost always recommended
- Medicines above **~$70,000–$75,000 per QALY** face a high rejection risk
- Uncertainty around the ICER — especially for uncontrolled trials or surrogate endpoints — is a major rejection driver (ISPOR Asia 2022; Value in Health, 2018)

Between 2010 and 2020, PBAC positive recommendation rates ranged from **61% to 75% per meeting**. Applications made on a **cost-minimisation basis** (i.e., generic or biosimilar medicines shown to be therapeutically equivalent to an already-listed comparator) have substantially higher first-attempt success rates and shorter timelines (**488 days** average) than cost-effectiveness applications (**638 days** average). (Patient Access Gap Report, 2024)

### 3. The F1/F2 Formulary: Separating Patented from Generic Medicines

Introduced in 2007, the F1/F2 formulary structure separates how the PBS manages drug pricing based on competition:

| Formulary | Content | Price pressure mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **F1** | Single-brand (patented) medicines | No mandatory price cuts; price negotiated at listing |
| **F2** | Multi-brand, generic, and biosimilar medicines | Subject to statutory price reductions and price disclosure |

When a medicine's patent expires and a generic or biosimilar enters the market, the original brand is typically moved from F1 to F2, triggering an immediate **statutory price reduction**. For biosimilars, the reference biologic receives a **one-off 25% price reduction** on biosimilar entry, followed by ongoing price disclosure adjustments as competition increases. For standard generics, the first new brand entry triggers a separate reduction round.

### 4. Price Disclosure: Continuously Driving Prices Down

For F2 medicines, the PBS does not simply set a listed price and hold it. The **price disclosure** mechanism, operating since 2007, requires manufacturers to report their actual transaction prices (the weighted average disclosed price, or WADP) every six months. If the WADP is materially below the listed price, the listed price is reduced to match reality — and patient co-payments fall accordingly.

Price disclosure cycles run for 12 months each, comprising a **six-month data collection period** and a **six-month processing period**, with reductions taking effect on **1 April or 1 October** each year.

The most dramatic results have come from biosimilar competition in biologics. A study published in *Clinical Pharmacology & Therapeutics* (2025) found that TNF inhibitor biosimilars generated **ten years of PBS expenditure savings** for that drug class. Across biologics more broadly, reference product expenditure fell **38.3% within twelve months** of a biosimilar entering the market, even as total utilisation (reference plus biosimilar combined) increased slightly. (Value in Health Regional Issues, 2025)

### 5. Co-payments and Safety Nets in 2026

The co-payment structure sets the patient's contribution per prescription:

| Patient category | Co-payment (2026) | Safety net threshold | Post-safety-net cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| **General (Medicare card)** | **$25.00** | $1,748.20 | Concessional rate ($7.70) |
| **Concessional (pension/Healthcare Card)** | **$7.70** | $277.20 | Free (except premiums) |

The general co-payment was reduced from **$31.60 to $25.00 on 1 January 2026** — back to the level last seen in 2004. This was an Albanese Government election commitment, delivered in the 2025 Budget at a cost of **$689 million over four years**. The change saves patients up to **$6.60 per script** and is expected to benefit more than **400,000 Australians per week**. The concessional co-payment of $7.70 has been frozen until **2030**. (Department of Health, 2026)

Despite these protections, affordability remains a real problem. A 2025 survey found that **21% of Australians — approximately 3.75 million people — went without a prescription in the prior three years due to cost**, concentrated among those with chronic conditions and in lower socioeconomic groups. This figure is likely to improve following the January 2026 co-payment cut, but access gaps persist for medicines not listed on the PBS.

### 6. The Access Delay Problem: 591 Days from Approval to Listing

The PBS's rigorous gating mechanism has a direct cost: medicines that have been approved as safe and effective by the Therapeutic Goods Administration (TGA) can sit for years before they reach the PBS.

The **Australian Patient Access Gap Report (2024)**, which analysed every TGA-approved medicine between January 2021 and April 2024, found:
- The average time from TGA approval to PBS listing was **591 days** for novel medicines
- More than **80% of cost-effectiveness submissions** required multiple PBAC attempts
- More than **40 medicines** that had already received positive PBAC recommendations were still waiting for government listing more than **1,000 days** after the PBAC recommendation — stalled in pricing negotiations or Cabinet review
- PBS listing times had "blown out to almost two years" in some specialist categories (Medical Republic, 2025)

The bottleneck is partly structural: PBAC only meeting 3–6 times per year means a failed submission may wait six months just for a re-hearing. But pricing negotiations between the Department of Health and manufacturers are increasingly the long pole, especially for high-cost oncology medicines where the government seeks confidential rebates substantially below the listed price to bring the effective ICER within acceptable range.

### 7. The Life Saving Drugs Program: A Safety Valve for Ultra-Rare Diseases

For medicines that PBAC accepts as clinically effective but rejects on cost-effectiveness grounds — typically because they treat ultra-rare diseases where per-patient costs are extreme — the government operates the **Life Saving Drugs Program (LSDP)**. Eligibility requires:
- An ultra-rare disease with prevalence of **1 in 50,000** or fewer Australians (approximately 500 people)
- A positive PBAC clinical assessment but a negative cost-effectiveness recommendation

LSDP medicines are funded at **no cost to the patient**, outside the PBS framework. This is a deliberate carve-out: ultra-rare disease treatments cannot be assessed using standard cost-effectiveness methodology because the small patient populations produce extremely wide ICER confidence intervals, and the equity case for funding often outweighs the economic case.

## Tensions and Open Questions

1. **Sustainability vs. access for high-cost medicines:** At $19.1 billion and growing 7–8% annually, the PBS is increasingly dominated by high-cost oncology biologics and specialty medicines. The ICER threshold, which has not been formally updated in two decades, may no longer be calibrated to the actual cost of production and the real innovation value of new therapies.

2. **Access delays and patient harm:** A 591-day wait is a harm — particularly for cancer patients whose disease progresses while medicines commercially available in the US or EU sit pending PBAC review. Whether to move to an interim access model (as used in France and Canada) while full assessment proceeds is a live policy debate.

3. **Price disclosure and generic competition:** Price disclosure has successfully driven F2 prices toward competitive levels, but the mechanism depends on functioning generic and biosimilar markets. Supply chain disruptions — increasingly common post-COVID — have exposed the fragility of relying on the lowest disclosed price when manufacturing is concentrated offshore.

4. **The MBS-PBS asymmetry:** The PBS actively manages prices down; the MBS has no equivalent mechanism and prices tend to lag. This creates a structural inconsistency: patients are well-protected against the cost of listed medicines, but poorly protected against specialist fees and procedure costs that sit above MBS Schedule Fees.

5. **Unlisted medicines and private scripts:** Medicines not listed on the PBS — either because they failed PBAC assessment or have not yet been submitted — must be obtained by private script at the manufacturer's full price. For cancer patients or those with rare diseases, this can mean thousands of dollars per month, accessible only to the wealthy.

## Threads to Branch Into Next

1. **PBS access reform — interim listing and conditional reimbursement:** How Australia could close the 591-day gap by listing medicines on provisional terms while full evidence is gathered (analogous to Canada's CADTH interim listing model or France's ATU scheme)
2. **Biosimilar policy in depth:** Australia's biosimilar uptake strategies, prescriber switching incentives, and the experience with TNF inhibitors, oncology biosimilars, and insulin — and how PBS savings from biosimilars compare internationally
3. **High-cost medicines and the PBS sustainability question:** Projected PBS expenditure growth, the rising share of oncology biologics, and how Australia's ICER threshold compares to NICE (UK), CADTH (Canada), and PHARMAC (NZ)
4. **PHARMAC comparison — New Zealand's alternative model:** NZ's single national purchasing agency, its lower per-capita medicine costs, the equity/access trade-off, and what Australia has and hasn't adopted from the PHARMAC model
5. **Private health insurance and pharmacy benefits:** How private health insurance in Australia interacts (or fails to interact) with the PBS — including extras cover for medicines not on the PBS, hospital drug formularies, and the gap between insured and PBS coverage
