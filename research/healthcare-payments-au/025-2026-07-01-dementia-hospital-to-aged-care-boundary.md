# Dementia and the Hospital-to-Aged-Care Boundary: The $4.7 Billion System Failure and What the 2026-27 Budget Is — and Isn't — Fixing

**Report 025 · 1 July 2026**

**Summary:** Dementia is Australia's leading cause of death and second-largest disease burden, yet the system for moving people with dementia from hospital to appropriate care is chronically dysfunctional: ~3,300 Australians are stuck in hospitals awaiting discharge to aged care, consuming an estimated $1+ billion per year in unnecessary acute care costs, because BPSD (behavioural and psychological symptoms of dementia) renders them unacceptable to most mainstream residential aged care providers. The 2026-27 Budget's $224.3 million dementia package is a meaningful but structurally insufficient response to a problem embedded in the state-Commonwealth funding split itself.

**Why this follows from yesterday:** Report 024 documented how the residential aged care market is consolidating around for-profit providers, with compliance failures concentrated among the largest operators — the same providers who consistently refuse to accept high-acuity BPSD patients, directly fuelling the hospital bed-block crisis this report examines.

---

## Background

### Dementia as a system-defining condition

Dementia is not a single disease but an umbrella term for syndromes in which progressive neurodegeneration causes decline in memory, thinking, behaviour, and the ability to perform daily activities. Alzheimer's disease accounts for approximately 60-70% of cases; vascular dementia, Lewy body dementia, and frontotemporal dementia make up most of the remainder.

The scale of dementia in Australia is now system-defining:

- **446,500** Australians were estimated to be living with dementia in 2026 — up from 425,000 in 2024 — and this is projected to reach **1.1 million by 2065** as the baby boomer cohort ages through its eighth and ninth decades. (Source: Dementia Australia, AIHW)
- **29,000** live with *young onset dementia* (diagnosed before age 65).
- Dementia became Australia's **leading cause of death** in 2023, with 32,400 deaths attributed to or with dementia, representing 9.4% of all deaths. It is the leading cause of death for women (1 in 8 women die from dementia).
- In the **2024 Australian Burden of Disease Study**, dementia ranked second overall (behind coronary heart disease) responsible for 4.5% of the total disease burden — 262,000 DALYs lost nationally (6.5 DALYs per 1,000 people). For women, it was the single leading cause of burden.
- **54%** of people in permanent residential aged care have dementia, making it the dominant clinical condition in the sector — yet the AN-ACC funding tool that replaced ACFI in October 2022 does not capture clinical dementia diagnoses directly, creating a major data gap for policy.

### The $4.7 billion spend — and the hidden costs

The AIHW estimated that in 2022-23, **$4.7 billion** of the Australian health and aged care system's expenditure was spent directly on dementia:

- **$1.47 billion** on health care
  - $953 million on public hospital *admitted patient care* — 65% of all direct health care costs for dementia
  - The remainder on outpatient services, GP consultations, allied health, and diagnostic imaging
- **$3.2 billion** on aged care services and assessments
  - $2 billion (63%) on permanent residential aged care
  - The remainder on home care packages, CHSP, respite, and assessments

These figures significantly understate the real cost. They exclude the economic cost of informal care (family carers), which Dementia Australia estimates at billions more annually, and they use a prevalence-based methodology that captures only services coded to dementia as a primary diagnosis — excluding many hospital admissions where dementia is a contributing complexity.

## Key Findings

### 1. The hospital bed-block crisis: numbers and costs

The hospital-to-aged-care boundary is now one of the most costly structural failures in the Australian health system. As of December 2025, approximately **3,300 older Australians** were stuck in hospital waiting for discharge to residential aged care — a number described by FTI Consulting as representing "21 aged care homes' worth of patients."

State-level data reveals the scale:
- **NSW**: 776 older people waiting in hospitals as at March 2026 — a **158.7% increase** from 300 people in December 2023. Total waiting days in NSW hospitals rose from 11,943 (December 2023) to **44,487** by 2025. Separately, NSW hospitals reported more than **58,000 bed-days** consumed by patients past their discharge date.
- **Queensland**: 1,072 people waiting as at February 2026 — the highest of any state.
- **Tasmania**: 94 people waiting as at March 2026.

The systemic cost is substantial. Industry modelling suggests keeping older patients inappropriately in public hospital beds costs "**well over $1 billion per year**," since a public hospital bed day costs approximately $1,900-$2,500 (by comparison, a residential aged care bed under AN-ACC costs ~$296/day in government funding). When patients who are medically ready for discharge remain in hospital, they also face heightened risk of hospital-acquired complications — deconditioning, falls, delirium, and infections — that further extend their stays and worsen their dementia.

Dementia is a leading driver of this problem. Hospitalisations coded as *primarily* due to dementia exceeded **35,000 cases per year** by 2023-24, and are rising annually. People with dementia face specific barriers to discharge:
- **BPSD** (aggression, agitation, wandering, psychosis, sexual disinhibition) makes placement extremely difficult; mainstream aged care providers typically refuse to accept residents with active severe BPSD
- The residential aged care market, as documented in Report 024, is increasingly consolidated around for-profit providers who face financial disincentives to take on high-complexity, high-risk residents
- Assessment queues for aged care eligibility (ACAT/ACAS assessments) create additional delays

### 2. The state-Commonwealth cost-shifting trap

A structural problem underpins the crisis that no single Budget measure can fully resolve. Hospital care in Australia is funded jointly by Commonwealth and states under the National Health Reform Agreement (NHRA) — the 2026-2031 NHRA Addendum commits a record **$220.3 billion** from the Commonwealth over five years to states for public hospital services. Residential aged care, however, is funded almost entirely by the **Commonwealth alone**.

This creates a structural incentive misalignment:
- States bear significant cost when patients remain in hospital (state hospital operating budgets); but
- The *alternative* — the Commonwealth-funded aged care bed — is not under states' control to supply or accelerate.
- States therefore have strong incentive to demand that Commonwealth ensure adequate aged care capacity before accepting responsibility for discharge delays, but aged care providers (private operators) cannot be compelled to accept specific residents.

The result is that people with complex dementia and severe BPSD fall into the gap: too medically complex for mainstream residential aged care, too stable for acute care, and waiting in hospital at enormous cost to the system.

Dementia Australia and FTI Consulting have both argued that states should negotiate a specific clause in NHRA requiring Commonwealth cost-sharing for the cost of clinically ready patients awaiting Commonwealth-funded aged care placement — effectively a *delayed transfer of care* charge mechanism analogous to the UK model. As of July 2026, no such mechanism exists in the signed 2026-2031 NHRA Addendum.

### 3. The Hospital to Aged Care Dementia Support Program (HACDSP)

The most direct Commonwealth response to the hospital transition problem is the **Hospital to Aged Care Dementia Support Program**, operated by HammondCare's Dementia Support Australia. Launched 16 January 2025, the program was funded with **$56.8 million** in Budget 2024-25.

The program has three objectives:
1. **In-hospital wellbeing**: optimise care and engagement for dementia patients during their hospital admission
2. **Transition facilitation**: identify suitable aged care placements and actively support the discharge process — including managing BPSD with residential care providers to secure acceptance
3. **Post-discharge support**: provide three months of follow-up in the residential aged care setting to help stabilise the person and prevent readmission

Key program results from 16 January 2025 to 31 March 2026:
- **1,108 referrals** received across all jurisdictions
- **667 people** successfully transitioned from hospital to residential aged care
- **148 people** currently receiving active post-discharge residential support
- **244 people** in assessment or awaiting transition
- **<3% readmission rate** among those transitioned

The program operates across all eight states and territories, initially across **11 locations**. The **2026-27 Budget** commits to expanding it to **20 locations** as part of the $224.3 million dementia package.

These are encouraging early results. A readmission rate under 3% is significantly better than the broader aged care sector's hospital readmission patterns. However, 667 transitions over 14 months — roughly 48 per month — is a fraction of the 3,300 people stuck in hospitals at any given time, let alone the 35,000+ annual dementia-related hospitalisations. Scale remains the central challenge.

A formal evaluation is underway via HMA (Health Management Advisors), with an information bulletin published July 2025; results are expected later in 2026.

### 4. The Specialist Dementia Care Program (SDCP): the high-acuity answer

For people with the most severe BPSD — those who cannot be managed safely in mainstream residential aged care — the Commonwealth funds **Specialist Dementia Care Program (SDCP) units**: dedicated, high-staffing-ratio, purpose-designed "cottage-like" domestic environments within residential aged care homes.

The SDCP model provides:
- Person-centred, non-pharmacological behavioural management
- Multidisciplinary staffing (nursing, diversional therapy, personal care)
- Small-group living environments designed to reduce sensory overload and agitation
- An intended duration of approximately 12 months, after which residents are ideally transitioned to mainstream residential aged care with reduced behavioural complexity

The original policy target was **35 SDCP units nationally** — one per Primary Health Network region. Units are funded via a combination of Commonwealth grants and state/territory contributions under the National Health Reform Agreement (NHRA) Addendum. As of mid-2026, approximately 15 units are operational, with more in development.

A March 2025 review published in the *Australasian Journal on Ageing* (Atee et al., 2025) found preliminary evidence that SDCP units:
- Decrease the severity of BPSD
- Facilitate successful transitions back to mainstream residential aged care
- Reduce reliance on psychotropic medications
- Improve quality of life for residents

However, the review noted significant gaps: no randomised controlled trial evidence, heterogeneous outcome measurement across units, and limited long-term follow-up data. Unit costs remain opaque; per-place funding is substantially higher than mainstream AN-ACC rates but no public cost-effectiveness analysis has been published.

The **2026-27 Budget** allocates funding for **20 additional SDCP units** — more than doubling the existing network — as part of the $224.3 million four-year dementia package. An additional **$317.5 million** is committed from 2030-31 to 2035-36, signalling multi-decade intent.

### 5. The National Dementia Action Plan 2024-2034

The overarching policy framework is the **National Dementia Action Plan 2024-2034**, released in 2024 and aligned with the WHO's Global Action Plan on the Public Health Response to Dementia. Its eight action areas are:

1. Promote equity and human rights
2. Tackle stigma and improve awareness
3. Minimise risk and delay onset/progression (addressing the 43% of burden attributable to modifiable risk factors — physical inactivity, obesity, hypertension, smoking, depression, social isolation)
4. Improve diagnosis and post-diagnostic support
5. Improve treatment, coordination and support
6. Support carers
7. Build workforce capability
8. Improve data and maximise research impact

A key data finding from the Plan: the **average time from symptom onset to diagnosis is 3 years** (5 years for young onset dementia), and there is no single national data source for GP-level dementia diagnoses. This is a structural barrier to early intervention, prevention, and research; the 2023-2034 National Dementia Data Improvement Plan is attempting to build the data infrastructure from the ground up.

Budget 2024-25 separately invested $101.4 million in complex care readiness, $56.8 million on the HACDSP, and $7.7 million in 2025-26 for dementia training.

## Tensions and Open Questions

**1. Scale mismatch.** The HACDSP helped 667 people in 14 months. Australia has ~35,000 dementia-related hospitalisations per year and ~3,300 people stuck in hospital at any given time. Even expanding to 20 sites, HACDSP can plausibly serve ~1,000-1,500 people per year. The gap between program capacity and system need is roughly an order of magnitude.

**2. SDCP eligibility gatekeeping.** Access to SDCP units requires a formal "Needs Based Assessment" referral through Dementia Support Australia. In 2023-24, over 370 assessments were completed — a fraction of the people who might benefit. Eligibility criteria are stringent: only "very severe" BPSD qualifies. The people with moderately severe BPSD — too complex for mainstream aged care but not qualifying for SDCP — have no funded pathway.

**3. The workforce problem.** SDCP units require staff trained in non-pharmacological dementia behaviour management. As documented in Report 023, the aged care sector faces a 110,000-worker shortage by 2036, and dementia-trained workers are among the most scarce. Building 20 new SDCP units without a parallel workforce pipeline risks creating physical infrastructure without the human capacity to staff it.

**4. No national hospital wait-time reporting.** Unlike the UK, where NHS England publishes monthly delayed transfer of care data by diagnosis, Australia has no national reporting standard for hospital patients waiting for aged care placement. The figures cited above (3,300 nationally) are derived from aggregated state reports, not a national dataset. This makes it impossible to track trends or hold anyone accountable.

**5. The state-Commonwealth perverse incentive remains.** The 2026-2031 NHRA commits record hospital funding but includes no mechanism to share costs when clinically ready patients with dementia wait for Commonwealth-funded residential care. States retain the incentive to keep people in hospital and attribute delays to aged care capacity, rather than investing in community bridges.

**6. Dementia prevention as an underinvested lever.** The AIHW Burden of Disease Study 2024 estimates that 43% of dementia burden is due to six modifiable risk factors. This is the single largest addressable share of the burden, yet prevention funding is dwarfed by acute and residential care costs. The National Dementia Action Plan includes risk reduction as Action 3 but no major prevention investment was included in Budget 2026-27.

## Threads to Branch Into Next

1. **Specialist medical training pipeline: geriatricians and gerontologists.** Australia has approximately 600 geriatricians nationally — a severe shortage relative to an ageing population. Medical college training controls and long training pathways (12+ years from medical school to specialist certification) mean that any investment in specialist dementia care is constrained by the absence of specialist prescribers and assessors. This thread connects back to Reports 016, 022, and 024.

2. **Aged care quality and safety regulation (ACQSC under the Aged Care Act 2024).** The new rights-based Act creates enforceable quality standards for dementia care; the ACQSC must now enforce restrictive practice governance (physical restraint, psychotropic medication) in settings where workforce shortages make compliance difficult. How the regulator handles 636 providers with constrained resources is largely unexamined.

3. **Aged care and the Medicare Safety Net interface.** Older Australians with dementia face compounding out-of-pocket costs: MBS co-payments for specialist neurology/geriatric assessments, PBS co-payments for pharmacological management (memantine, donepezil), and aged care co-contributions. The financial toxicity of multi-system cost-sharing for this cohort is understudied.

4. **Dementia prevention and risk factor policy.** With 43% of dementia burden attributable to modifiable risk factors, the investment case for prevention (physical activity programs, hypertension control, hearing aid subsidies, social inclusion) potentially dwarfs the per-dollar return from acute care spending. No dedicated dementia prevention funding exists at Commonwealth level.

5. **Palliative care and end-of-life funding for dementia.** Dementia is now the leading cause of death; the majority of people with dementia die in residential aged care without formal palliative care plans. Palliative care is a long-deferred thread from this series and has a specific interface with dementia as a terminal illness.
