# Pharmacist Scope of Practice Expansion: Australia's Largest Primary Care Reform and the Payment System Scrambling to Keep Up

**Report 040 — 2026-07-16**

**Summary:** Australia is in the middle of its most significant expansion of pharmacist scope of practice in decades — with Queensland, Victoria, the Northern Territory, South Australia, and the ACT all introducing pharmacist prescribing rights for acute and chronic conditions since 2023, a national Extended Practice Authority framework from July 2025, and a $26.44 billion Eighth Community Pharmacy Agreement underpinning the pharmacy network. But the payment architecture has not kept pace: state governments pay as little as $20 per consultation, there are no Medicare Benefits Schedule items for extended-scope pharmacist services, and leading pharmacy bodies and the PBS subsidy system are still misaligned on how to fund medicines prescribed by endorsed pharmacists. The result is a workforce reform with compelling economics but a fragmented, underfunded business model.

**Why this follows from yesterday:** Report 039 documented Australia's dependence on international medical graduates to fill workforce gaps in general practice, rural medicine, and specialist care. One of the threads it opened was pharmacist scope of practice expansion as a mechanism to relieve GP pressure in Distribution Priority Areas — places where IMGs are often the only doctors, stretched thin across populations that need accessible care for common conditions.

---

## Background

### The Pharmacy Network Australia Is Building On

Australia's community pharmacy network is one of its most geographically distributed healthcare infrastructure assets. As of 2025, there are approximately **5,900 community pharmacies** across the country (PBS-approved, FY2023 data; the Pharmacy Guild estimates approximately 6,000 total), with at least one pharmacy in most rural and regional towns that lack a GP or medical centre. There are approximately **40,000 registered pharmacists** as of May 2025 (Pharmacy Board of Australia), up from 37,949 on general registration as of March 2024 — representing steady growth in the profession.

Under the **Eighth Community Pharmacy Agreement (8CPA)**, which commenced on 1 July 2024 and runs to 30 June 2029, community pharmacies receive a total of **$26.44 billion** in Commonwealth funding over five years. This represents a 22% increase over the 7CPA's $18.35 billion (2020–2025). The 8CPA's major components are:

| Component | Funding (5 years) |
|---|---|
| Dispensing remuneration (PBS prescriptions) | $22.5 billion |
| Additional Community Supply Support Payment | $2.1 billion |
| Pharmacy programs (Dose Administration Aids, MedsChecks, Staged Supply, regional allowances) | $1.05 billion |
| PBS co-payment freeze contribution | $484.4 million |
| **Total** | **$26.44 billion** |

The 8CPA's signatories are the Commonwealth Government and the **Pharmacy Guild of Australia** (which represents pharmacy owners). Notably, the **Pharmaceutical Society of Australia** — which was a co-signatory to the 7CPA for the first time in 2020 — was **not** a co-signatory to the 8CPA, a change that reflects the Guild's tighter control over commercial negotiations. The PSA instead entered a separate Strategic Agreement on Pharmacist Professional Practice alongside the 8CPA. The Guild's role as owner representative — rather than professional body — is central to understanding a structural tension in Australian pharmacy: the Guild's primary interest is the financial viability of the pharmacy business model, while the PSA advocates for pharmacists' clinical role and autonomy.

## Key Findings

### 1. The Extended Practice Authority — A National Framework from July 2025

Prior to 2025, pharmacist prescribing in Australia existed only as state-level pilots and in narrow circumstances (pharmacist-initiated supply of Schedule 2/3 medicines). From **1 July 2025**, a national **Extended Practice Authority — Pharmacists** framework took effect, enabling trained and endorsed pharmacists to provide clinical advice, diagnosis, and treatment — including Schedule 4 (prescription-only) medicine prescribing — for a defined range of acute and common conditions.

The conditions covered under the Extended Practice Authority (EPA-Pharmacists, version 8, July 2025) include:

- **Sexual and reproductive health**: Hormonal contraception (resupply and new initiation)
- **Urinary tract infections (UTIs)**
- **Skin conditions**: Impetigo, tinea, acne, eczema management (selected Schedule 4 treatments)
- **Respiratory**: Selected acute respiratory conditions
- **Vaccines**: Administration of National Immunisation Program and travel vaccines
- **Chronic condition management** (selected states only under extended authority)

The EPA is administered at the state level under Queensland Health's Extended Practice Authority framework, with other states either adopting equivalent instruments or transitioning toward alignment by 2026.

To prescribe under the EPA, pharmacists must complete a recognised prescribing training program — predominantly the **Pharmaceutical Society of Australia's Pharmacist Prescribing Training Program (PTP)** — and be authorised under their state's pharmacy legislation.

### 2. State-by-State Implementation: A Patchwork Going National

The pace and scope of pharmacist prescribing expansion varies significantly by jurisdiction, creating a national patchwork rather than a uniform model.

**Queensland** is the leader. Following pilot programs begun in 2021 (UTI pilot), the Queensland Government in March 2025 announced that expanded pharmacist scope — including 17 acute conditions plus hormonal contraception — would become **permanent from 1 July 2025**. More than 135 pharmacists participated in the pilots, delivering over 1,700 services before permanence. The Queensland model is funded through the state government at a rate embedded in the prescribing authority framework, not through MBS.

**Victoria** committed **$18 million** to fund expanded pharmacist scope under the Community Pharmacist Program. Victorian pharmacists receive a **$20 per consultation** payment from the Victorian Government per eligible service. From December 2025, the program expanded to **22 acute and chronic conditions**, including impetigo and two new hormonal contraceptive options. The pilot ran from October 2023 to July 2025, transitioning to a permanent model on 1 July 2025.

**Northern Territory** introduced an EPA covering **21 common conditions** allowing pharmacists to assess, diagnose, manage, and treat where clinically appropriate. This is significant in the NT context — the territory has some of Australia's most severe GP shortages, particularly in remote communities.

**South Australia** launched its expanded scope initiative with a new Graduate Certificate in Pharmacist Prescribing commencing Semester 1, 2026. Eligible pharmacists receive training reimbursement of up to $5,000 via the Pharmacy Guild's SA Branch.

**New South Wales** made UTI pharmacist prescribing permanent from May 2024, and OCP resupply permanent from 26 September 2024. Minor skin conditions (atopic dermatitis, herpes zoster, impetigo, psoriasis) were subsequently added. In March 2026, NSW expanded to include combined hormonal vaginal ring and depot medroxyprogesterone acetate (DMPA) injection. The NSW Health Minister announced in September 2025 that full acute condition scope aligned with Queensland would apply from 2026.

**ACT** enabled pharmacist prescribing for UTIs and OCP resupply from February 2025. By October 2025, the scope extended to musculoskeletal pain, reflux, rhinitis, acne, and nausea. From the 2026 flu season, pharmacists and intern pharmacists can administer vaccines to children from age 2.

**Western Australia** has planned a trial of Queensland pilot conditions from 2026.

**Tasmania** committed $5 million in state funding, with postgraduate scholarships subsidising training and pharmacist prescribing services expected to be available to patients within the next two years.

The overall trajectory is clear: the Queensland model — prescribing for 17–22 acute conditions, with pharmacist-held authority under state EPA — is becoming the de facto national standard, with federal government endorsement through the Pharmacy Board's framework and inclusion in the PSA's training architecture.

### 3. The Three-Body Problem: Guild, PSA, and the Federal Payment System

Australia's pharmacy governance involves three parties with partially aligned but ultimately distinct interests:

**The Pharmacy Guild of Australia** (owner representatives) strongly supports expanded scope as a revenue diversification mechanism — prescription dispensing margins are under pressure from PBS price disclosure, and the 8CPA's dispensing component ($22.5 billion) is largely a pass-through of PBS funding. The Guild's 2026 modelling estimated that pharmacist prescribing could:

- Generate **$1 billion in annual health system savings**
- Free up over **10 million GP appointments per year**
- Prevent **30,000 hospitalisations annually**

through diverting common-condition consultations (UTIs, contraception, skin conditions, asthma management) away from GP clinics and emergency departments.

**The PSA** (pharmacist professional body) advocates for an **autonomous endorsed pharmacist prescriber** model — a formal accreditation pathway allowing pharmacists who complete postgraduate prescribing training to prescribe independently across a broad scope, analogous to nurse practitioners. The PSA's preferred model includes full PBS access for medicines prescribed by endorsed pharmacists, with pharmacist prescriptions triggering PBS subsidies in the same way as GP prescriptions.

**AdPha (Advanced Pharmacy Australia)** (specialist pharmacists) jointly with the Guild and PSA submitted a framework in late 2025 proposing an **endorsed pharmacist prescriber** tier — requiring a graduate certificate in prescribing plus demonstrated clinical competency — that would enable autonomous prescribing within a defined and expandable scope.

The critical unresolved question for all three bodies is: **who pays?** Currently, state governments fund expanded scope consultations at rates ($20 in Victoria) well below the economic cost of a pharmacist clinical consultation. The Commonwealth contributes nothing directly — there are no MBS items for pharmacist prescribing consultations. PBS subsidies for medicines prescribed by endorsed pharmacists remain under discussion but are not yet established.

### 4. What the MBS Currently Funds for Pharmacists

Pharmacists have historically had limited direct MBS access. The primary mechanism for pharmacist-GP collaboration under Medicare is the **Home Medicines Review (HMR)**, funded under MBS Item 900, but the structure is revealing:

- Under Item 900, the **GP is reimbursed** by Medicare for coordinating the HMR referral and reviewing the pharmacist's report
- The **pharmacist's clinical work** is funded through the 8CPA Community Pharmacy Programs budget (~$1.05 billion over five years), not through direct MBS items
- From 1 July 2027, HMR access will require a GP chronic condition management plan as a prerequisite, tightening the GP-coordination model

This architecture — where the GP holds the MBS item and the pharmacist holds a program-funding line — reflects the historical policy position that pharmacists are clinical support staff rather than primary prescribers. The transition to expanded prescribing scope breaks this model, but no federal payment instrument has been designed to replace it.

The **National Immunisation Program (NIP)** vaccination expansion is the clearest example of federal payment for pharmacist services: the Commonwealth committed **$114.1 million over four years from January 2024** for pharmacists to administer NIP vaccines to people aged 5 and over — paid directly to pharmacies per vaccine administered. This is the closest analogue to what an MBS-style payment for pharmacist clinical services could look like.

### 5. The Safety Debate: A Cross-Sector Coalition Responds

The pace of scope expansion has triggered significant safety pushback from the medical establishment. A critical recent flashpoint is the **SAX Institute Report published 6 July 2026** — just ten days ago — titled *"Community pharmacist prescribing outcomes in Australia and beyond,"* prepared by the Leeder Centre for Health Policy, Economics and Data and commissioned by the RACGP. Its core finding: the bulk of conditions currently being treated by pharmacists have never been formally evaluated in robust clinical trials. Evidence in the field largely measures service uptake and prescribing *activity*, not patient safety *outcomes*. The RACGP used the report to publicly question whether Queensland made prescribing permanent without adequately evaluating either pilot. The Guild's counter-characterisation argued the same review "confirms that pharmacist prescribing improves access to medicines and care" — pointing to access metrics the report did contain, with both parties selectively citing the document.

More broadly, in June 2026, a cross-sector coalition issued a joint statement raising alarm. The signatories included:

- Royal Australian College of General Practitioners (RACGP)
- Australian Medical Association (AMA)
- Australian College of Nurse Practitioners (ACNP)
- Council of Presidents of Medical Colleges
- Choice Chemist and allied organisations

Their core argument is that **safe prescribing requires safe systems**, not merely skilled individual practitioners. Specifically:

1. **Episodic, isolated prescribing** — pharmacists treating a UTI without access to a patient's full history, current medications, or previous consultation records — risks missing early warning signs for more serious conditions (renal disease, STIs, diabetes)
2. **Separation of prescribing and dispensing** in the same premises creates a **commercial conflict of interest** — the prescribing pharmacist benefits financially from the medicines they prescribe, in a way GPs (who generally do not dispense) do not
3. **Inadequate evidence base** — the RACGP argues that high-quality, long-term clinical evidence for pharmacist prescribing for conditions such as asthma, COPD, or hormonal contraception outside a GP-led model is insufficient
4. **Continuity of care risk** — pharmacist-initiated treatment for a common condition that recurs or progresses may not be captured in any shared health record, leaving the GP without critical clinical history

The AMA stated in November 2025 that the Pharmacy Board was "ignoring evidence" in pursuit of a "risky prescribing agenda," arguing that Australia's model of restricting prescribing to medical practitioners "remains the safest and preferred model."

Pharmacy bodies counter that the evidence from UTI pilot programs, the UK's 18-year experience with pharmacist independent prescribing (since 2006), and Canada's Alberta model all demonstrate safety. They also point to **My Health Record** and the **Active Script List** (documented in Report 038) as infrastructure improvements that substantially reduce the information asymmetry concern.

### 6. International Comparison: The UK Model Shows What's Possible

The United Kingdom provides the most instructive international comparison. The UK introduced pharmacist independent prescribing in 2006, and on **31 January 2024** launched **Pharmacy First** — a £215 million program allowing pharmacists to prescribe prescription-only medicines (including antibiotics and antivirals) for seven conditions (sore throat, ear infections, impetigo, shingles, infected insect bites, sinusitis, and uncomplicated UTIs in women) without GP referral, with capacity for up to 12 million consultations per year. From 2026, independent prescribing will be integrated into the Community Pharmacy Contractual Framework as a formal extension of Pharmacy First and the Pharmacy Contraception Service. All newly graduating UK pharmacists will qualify as independent prescribers upon registration from 2026 onwards.

The UK framework uses a "standard of care" model — no conditions list, prescribe any licensed medicine within competence — compared to Australia's "protocol" model specifying permitted conditions.

**Canada** (particularly Alberta, where independent pharmacist prescribing has existed since 2006) uses a competency-based three-tier model: emergency prescribing; adapting an existing prescription; and Additional Prescribing Authorization (APA), allowing prescribing of almost any medicine except narcotics following a competency portfolio application. Alberta pharmacy clinics have been explicitly expanded since May 2024 to relieve GP and emergency department pressure. Canadian pharmacy prescribing is publicly funded — one of four countries (alongside the UK, Denmark, and France) where it is so structured.

**New Zealand** has seen a dramatic recent surge in pharmacist prescribers: from 73 in March 2024 to **297 as of March 2025 — a 307% increase in one year** — following the Medicines Amendment Act 2025, which expanded pharmacist prescriber rights to include unapproved medicines and 12-month prescriptions. NZ training pathways run through the universities of Auckland and Otago.

Australia's model, introduced in 2025, sits between the UK's broad standard-of-care model and the more restrictive Canadian/NZ frameworks — conditions-list-based, training-gate-kept, and with state-level rather than Commonwealth-level payment.

### 7. The Payment Gap — and Its Structural Consequences

The current payment model for pharmacist-extended-scope services has three structural problems:

**Problem 1: Under-pricing at state level.** At $20 per consultation in Victoria, the pharmacist consultation fee is well below the cost of a GP consultation (Item 23 MBS schedule fee ~$42.85; AMA recommended fee considerably higher). This signals that pharmacist clinical services are being priced as an administrative adjunct rather than a genuine clinical consultation, disincentivising uptake by pharmacists who must choose between extended-scope consultations and high-volume dispensing revenue.

**Problem 2: No PBS access for pharmacist-prescribed medicines.** When an endorsed pharmacist prescribes a PBS-listed medicine under the current model, the patient must pay full price — they cannot access PBS subsidies because PBS subsidy is linked to a prescription from a medical practitioner (or authorised nurse practitioner). For a UTI antibiotic, this might mean $15 vs $7.70 (concessional) or $31.60 (general). This is not a large amount for single episodes, but for chronic conditions the cumulative cost matters — and creates a perverse incentive to seek a GP referral anyway just to trigger PBS subsidy.

The joint pharmacy bodies' 2025 submission explicitly calls for **collaboration with governments to ensure patients can access PBS subsidies for medicines prescribed by endorsed pharmacists.** This would require an amendment to the National Health Act 1953 — politically contested territory.

**Problem 3: No federal infrastructure investment.** The 8CPA's $1.05 billion programs component is distributed across Dose Administration Aids, MedsChecks, Staged Supply, and regional allowances — all legacy programs predating the scope expansion. No 8CPA program funds clinical rooms, assessment equipment, or digital integration for expanded-scope consultations. Pharmacies are investing in clinical infrastructure (consulting rooms, point-of-care testing) without a federal payment to cover those capital costs.

## Tensions and Open Questions

**1. The co-prescribe/co-dispense conflict.** The separation of prescribing and dispensing is a bedrock principle of pharmaceutical governance in most health systems. Australia's model — pharmacist prescribing from within the dispensing pharmacy — tests this boundary in a way the UK and Canadian models largely avoid (UK independent prescribers frequently work in GP practices or specialist clinics, separated from dispensing). Whether financial conflict of interest matters in practice, or is a theoretical concern undermined by pharmacist professional obligations, is genuinely contested.

**2. The PBS access question is the reform's chokepoint.** A pharmacist prescribing antibiotic under state authority who cannot access PBS subsidies for the patient is providing an inferior service compared to a GP. Until PBS amendments follow, scope expansion will be of limited value for lower-income patients. This is a federal policy decision that has not been made.

**3. Continuity of care and My Health Record.** The safety coalition's concern about episodic prescribing improving as My Health Record and Active Script List become more complete is valid — but implementation rates matter. My Health Record opt-out penetration is high (~97% of Australians have a record) but uploading of consultation notes by GPs remains inconsistent, limiting the pharmacist's access to real-time clinical context.

**4. Grattan Institute's competitive market argument.** A 2024 Grattan Institute report on pharmacy argued that the location regulation system (the ACPA — Australian Community Pharmacy Authority) limiting pharmacy number and entry creates a non-competitive market that protects incumbent pharmacy owners at the cost of consumer access and price competition. The scope expansion debate is partly obscuring an underlying market structure question: if pharmacy entry were deregulated and competition increased, would expanded scope services be delivered more cheaply and more innovatively?

**5. Nursing profession response.** The ACNP's inclusion in the June 2026 safety coalition is significant — nurse practitioners (NPs) have held prescribing rights for over a decade and are concerned that pharmacist prescribing undercuts NP scope without equivalent training requirements. NPs undertake master's-level clinical training; pharmacist prescribers currently complete a graduate certificate. The scope and training hierarchy question has professional and funding implications.
