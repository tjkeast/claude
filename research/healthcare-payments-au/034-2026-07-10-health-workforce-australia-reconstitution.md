# Health Workforce Australia: The $200 Million Planning Body Australia Abolished — and the Cost of Ten Years Without One

**Report 034 — 2026-07-10**

**Summary:** Health Workforce Australia (HWA) was established in 2009 as the first national body dedicated to health workforce planning and clinical training reform. It was abolished in October 2014 by the Abbott Government as a "consolidation" efficiency measure. In the decade since, responsibility for health workforce planning has fragmented across at least 121 separate federal policies, seven inter-jurisdictional committees, and thirteen self-regulating medical colleges — all without a body capable of enforcing alignment between training supply and community need. The consequences are documented throughout this series: a projected shortage of ~70,700 FTE nurses by 2035, 9,000 GPs by 2048, 57% of required psychologists now absent, and a palliative medicine workforce that needs to double. The AMA, Grattan Institute, and a landmark 2025 Medical Journal of Australia study have all called for reconstitution of a national planning agency. Budget 2026-27 allocated $1 million over two years — for a design consultation on personal care worker registration. The planning gap remains unfilled.

**Why this follows from yesterday:** Report 033 documented how Australia's digital health infrastructure for aged care is fragmented, under-integrated, and advancing on a different timeline from the enforcement architecture it is supposed to support. The structural cause is the same one that runs through this series: decisions about training numbers, workforce distribution, and infrastructure investment are made in disconnected silos with no national coordination body responsible for alignment. Health Workforce Australia's abolition in 2014 is the institutional root of that fragmentation.

## Background

### What Health Workforce Australia Was

Health Workforce Australia was established in 2009 under the Rudd Government as a statutory authority under the Health Workforce Australia Act 2009. It was created in the context of a Council of Australian Governments (COAG) agreement that allocated $1.6 billion for health workforce reform — the largest Commonwealth health workforce investment to that point.

HWA had a broad mandate:
- **Workforce planning**: produce national supply and demand projections across all health professions; maintain the National Health Workforce Dataset (NHWDS)
- **Clinical training funding**: manage Commonwealth funding for clinical training placements in health services for medical, nursing, midwifery, and allied health students
- **Workforce reform**: identify and support scope-of-practice reforms, team-based care models, and workforce innovations
- **Indigenous health workforce**: fund and support programs to grow the First Nations health workforce
- **Data and research**: commission and publish health workforce research, including the foundational *Health Workforce 2025* report series

The *Health Workforce 2025: Doctors, Nurses and Midwives* report (published by HWA in 2012) was the most comprehensive national health workforce supply-and-demand modelling exercise Australia had attempted. It projected shortfalls decades into the future and provided the evidence base for both the GP training expansion and the medical school expansion that followed. It also identified nursing shortfalls — projections that have since been substantially confirmed by subsequent modelling showing a ~70,700 FTE shortfall by 2035.

At peak, HWA employed approximately 200 staff, was headquartered in Adelaide, and operated with an annual budget of approximately $50–80 million (excluding the clinical training funding it administered, which ran to hundreds of millions per year).

### The 2014 Abolition

Following the 2013 federal election, the Abbott Government established the National Commission of Audit (NCOA), chaired by Tony Shepherd. In its February 2014 report, the NCOA recommended consolidating five health portfolio agencies into the Department of Health: Australian Institute of Health and Welfare, National Health and Medical Research Council, Australian Health Practitioner Regulation Agency, HWA, and others.

The **Health Workforce Australia (Abolition) Bill 2014** was introduced to parliament in March 2014 and passed in June 2014. HWA was formally abolished on **8 October 2014**.

The rationale was efficiency and simplification: the Department of Health would absorb HWA's functions. In the parliamentary debate, the then-Health Minister Peter Dutton argued that the consolidation would remove duplication and reduce administrative overhead. Critics, including the ALP and health sector bodies, warned that the consolidation would produce a loss of capacity and independence in workforce planning.

What actually happened: the National Health Workforce Dataset was transferred to what is now the Health Workforce Data (HWD) website, maintained by the Department of Health. The clinical training funding programs were eventually restructured. But the independent modelling, research, and policy-development capacity was dispersed and substantially diminished. No equivalent statutory planning body replaced HWA.

## Key Findings

### 1. The Patchwork That Replaced a Planning Body

In place of HWA, Australia now has a layered advisory architecture that lacks the statutory independence, cross-professional scope, and enforcement authority that a genuine planning body would possess:

**Health Workforce Taskforce (HWT):** An inter-jurisdictional committee of Commonwealth and state/territory health officials. The HWT advises the Health Ministers' Meeting on workforce priorities and oversees implementation of various national workforce strategies. It publishes a **Workforce Intelligence Report** — the 2025 edition (released April 2026) providing insights into retention, attrition, and re-entry trends across the health workforce. The HWT has no authority over training colleges, medical schools, or universities, and no capacity to enforce alignment between training supply and projected community need.

**Medical Workforce Advisory Collaboration (MWAC):** Established around 2022 as part of the National Medical Workforce Strategy 2021–2031 implementation. MWAC provides advice to Health Ministers on medical workforce matters and oversees annual action planning for the strategy. It met in February 2025 and discussed doctor wellbeing, cultural safety, and First Nations medical workforce issues. MWAC covers only the *medical* workforce — nurses, pharmacists, allied health, and aged care workers fall outside its scope. It has advisory power only.

**Health Workforce Data (hwd.health.gov.au):** The successor to HWA's data function. The Department of Health has progressively published supply-and-demand modelling for specific professions — GP modelling published August 2024, nursing modelling 2023, psychiatry modelling June 2025. These are point-in-time reports rather than continuous monitoring. The data platform is useful but it is a research function, not a planning function with implementation authority.

**National Medical Workforce Strategy 2021–2031:** Developed collaboratively by governments and the medical sector. It contains 25 actions across 5 priority areas: collaboration, rebalancing supply, reforming training, building generalist capabilities, and ensuring flexibility. Implementation progress has been "inconsistent" (as documented in Report 028). Action 1 of the strategy — establishing a joint medical workforce planning and advisory body — has not been completed. Instead, the existing MWAC was strengthened modestly, and external assessment of MWAC's capacity against international comparators was still being finalised as at mid-2026.

### 2. What 121 Federal Policies Without a Plan Looks Like

A landmark peer-reviewed study published in the *Medical Journal of Australia* in 2025 (Topp et al., "Federal health workforce policy in Australia and its implications: a descriptive policy document review", MJA 223(9), 2025) reviewed the universe of current federal health workforce policy and found:

- **121 separate federal health workforce policy documents** currently active
- Significant fragmentation by profession: policies for specific medical specialties, nurses and midwives, allied health professions — but few cross-cutting policies
- **Limited coverage of allied health and pharmacy**: these professions, which constitute the fastest-growing segment of the health workforce (67% growth 2013–2022), receive disproportionately less policy attention than medical practitioners
- **Heavy reliance on short-term programs**: grants, incentive schemes, and time-limited funding dominate; long-term system design is absent
- **Inconsistent policy labelling and categorisation**: makes evaluation of cumulative policy impact practically impossible
- **No unifying national health workforce strategy**: the absence exacerbates fragmentation and undermines coordinated workforce planning

The study's conclusion: "the absence of a unified federal health workforce strategy exacerbates policy fragmentation and undermines coordinated workforce planning and equity."

This finding resonates throughout this research series. Each report has encountered a different set of workforce shortfalls that are individually documented but systemically unconnected: the GP desert (Report 009), the psychology undersupply (Report 011), the rural allied health gap (Report 012), the First Nations health worker pipeline (Report 013), the nursing shortage in aged care (Report 023), the palliative medicine workforce crisis (Report 026), the specialist training pipeline bottleneck (Report 028), the pharmacist shortage limiting the ACOP program (Report 031).

### 3. The Accumulated Workforce Shortfalls: A Ten-Year Deficit

The workforce shortfalls documented across this series can be read as the compounded result of a decade without coordinated national planning. The key figures:

| Profession | Projected Shortfall | Horizon | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| General Practitioners | ~9,000 FTE | 2048 | DoH GP Supply and Demand Study, Aug 2024 |
| Nurses (total) | ~70,707 FTE | 2035 | Nursing Supply and Demand Study 2023–2035 |
| Nurses in aged care | ~17,551 FTE | 2035 | DoH modelling |
| Psychologists | ~10,270 FTE (57.3% undersupply) | 2025 | Psychology Workforce Compendium, Apr 2026 |
| Psychiatrists | 600+ FTE now; 1,900+ FTE (2028); 6,100 FTE (2048) | 2024–2048 | Psychiatry Supply and Demand Compendium, Nov 2025 |
| Palliative medicine physicians | Need to double (from 358) | Immediate | AIHW workforce analysis |
| Aged care workers (total) | 35,000 annual shortfall; 110,000 total | 2036 | CEDA/DoH modelling |
| Rural allied health | 12,958 FTE (remote); 95,342 FTE (rural) | Now | Rural AH Workforce Report, 2025 |

These are not separate crises — they are facets of a single structural failure: no body with authority to set training targets, monitor delivery, and enforce course correction has existed since 2014.

The training pipeline lead time compounds the problem. It takes:
- 4 years to become a GP (from medical degree)
- 12+ years to become a specialist (medicine degree + internship + specialty training)
- 5 years to become a clinical psychologist (from 2026 integrated program)
- 18–24 months to become a personal care worker

This means that decisions not made in 2014–2019 cannot be remedied until 2026–2031 at the earliest. The shortfalls documented above are the direct consequence of training decisions — and non-decisions — made during the HWA-less decade.

### 4. The Self-Regulation Problem: Medical Colleges Without a Counterweight

A structural feature of Australia's specialist workforce planning (documented in Report 028) is that 13 specialist medical colleges control training capacity without any statutory obligation to align training numbers with projected community need. The colleges are autonomous self-regulating bodies — their legitimacy rests on professional standards, not public interest obligations.

Before HWA, there was at least a government counterweight engaging colleges on training numbers through the Prevocational Medical Education Forum and the Australian Medical Workforce Advisory Committee. HWA's abolition removed even that advisory-level counterweight.

**The National Health Practitioner Ombudsman (NHPO) review**: In November 2023, the NHPO published *"Processes for Progress: Part One"* — a review of specialist medical college policies and procedures for training site accreditation. The review issued **23 recommendations** to improve transparency, accountability, and consistency in specialist training accreditation. Crucially, Australian Health Ministers agreed with all 23 recommendations and issued **Ministerial Policy Direction 2023-01**, directing AHPRA and the Medical Board of Australia to require the Australian Medical Council (AMC) to work with colleges and jurisdictions on implementation. The AMC subsequently developed draft *Model Standards for Accreditation of Medical Training Sites* — outcome-centred and evidence-informed — with public consultation completing November 2024 and Health Ministers subsequently endorsing them. This is the most significant government intervention in college training-site governance since HWA's abolition, but it addresses *accreditation quality*, not *training numbers*.

The Grattan Institute's "Special Treatment" report (June 2025, covering specialist access — cited in Report 016) made a direct recommendation to address the numbers question: federal and state governments should establish a national health workforce planning body to set targets for specialist training, including the mix of specialists and rural training. Federal funding for the Specialist Training Program (STP) — which currently provides approximately $110,619 per FTE per year in Commonwealth salary contributions — should be **tied to these targets** and increased by $155 million per year.

The Grattan Institute also noted: if governments cannot agree to establish a new body, MWAC should be significantly strengthened, given ongoing funding for workforce modelling, and assigned a five-year work plan — with the expectation that this would serve as a bridge rather than a substitute.

### 5. The AMA's $182.6 Million Proposal

In its pre-budget submission for 2025-26, the Australian Medical Association called for a **federal government commitment of $182.6 million over four years** to establish an independent national health workforce planning agency.

The proposed agency would:
- Collate and analyse population demographics, healthcare trends, technological developments, and disease burden data to project future workforce requirements across all health professions
- Produce supply-and-demand modelling across the full health workforce (not just medicine) every two years
- Publish a national health workforce dataset with consistent methodology and coverage
- Make recommendations on migration and distribution policy settings, including District of Workforce Shortage and DPA designations
- Advise governments on training number targets by specialty and profession
- Report publicly and annually on implementation progress against targets

The proposal aligns with the most critical unimplemented recommendation of the National Medical Workforce Strategy 2021–2031: establishing a "joint medical workforce planning and advisory body with sufficient authority and expertise to advise and make recommendations on the size and structure of the medical workforce."

The AMA's press release at the time stated: "Without the right number of doctors in the right places, patients will continue to face unacceptable waits and rising out-of-pocket costs."

**Budget 2026-27 response:** The government did not fund the AMA proposal. The only workforce-planning-adjacent measure in Budget 2026-27 was **$1 million over two years** to progress the design of a national worker registration scheme for personal care workers in aged care — a consumer safety measure, not a workforce planning investment.

### 6. How International Comparators Do It

Australia's HWA abolition made it an outlier among comparable health systems that have retained or strengthened national workforce planning bodies:

**United Kingdom:** Health Education England (HEE) was established as a special health authority in 2012 (the same year HWA was reaching maturity) and was eventually merged into NHS England in April 2023, creating an **NHS England Workforce Directorate** with statutory planning functions, a budget of several billion pounds, and the authority to commission training places at medical schools and universities. In June 2023, NHS England published the *NHS Long Term Workforce Plan* — a 15-year strategy to expand the medical workforce by doubling medical school places (to 15,000 per year by 2031-32) and increasing nursing training places by 92%. This kind of long-horizon, funded target-setting is exactly what Australia lacks.

**New Zealand:** Health New Zealand Te Whatu Ora, established in July 2022 as part of the health system reform, incorporates a national workforce planning function within the integrated national health authority. NZ publishes Health Workforce Plans covering multiple professions. Australia's PHARMAC comparison (Report 007) was partly about institutional design — the contrast applies equally to workforce planning.

**Canada:** The federal-provincial structure makes national planning more complex, but the Canadian Institute for Health Information (CIHI) maintains comprehensive national health workforce data and publishes regular workforce reports with trend projections across professions.

**Scotland:** NHS Education for Scotland (NES) has a statutory workforce planning function for the NHS Scotland workforce, publishing annual Workforce Plan data and managing education and training commissioning.

The common feature of these international models is institutional permanence, cross-professional scope, and some form of leverage over training supply — typically through funding commission authority. Australia's MWAC has none of these features.

## Tensions and Open Questions

**1. Advisory versus executive authority.** The central debate is whether a reconstituted planning body should have advisory functions only — publishing projections and recommendations that governments choose to act on — or executive authority, such as the power to allocate clinical training funding contingent on training number commitments. The AMA proposal leans advisory but with public accountability. Grattan proposes using STP funding leverage — which is the closest to an executive mechanism available without legislating college training quotas. The medical colleges themselves are unlikely to accept any arrangement they perceive as threatening college autonomy.

**2. Scope: medicine only vs. all health professions.** The National Medical Workforce Strategy and MWAC both cover medical practitioners only. The MJA 2025 study found allied health and pharmacy are systemically under-represented in federal workforce policy despite being the fastest-growing segments. A credible reconstituted body would need to cover nurses, midwives, allied health (physio, OT, dietetics, social work, speech pathology), pharmacy, and aged care workers — professions regulated by nine separate AHPRA boards plus professional associations with their own interests.

**3. Commonwealth-state coordination.** Health workforce in Australia is a shared responsibility: the Commonwealth primarily funds training (STP, GP training, universities), while states employ and supervise most health workers. A national planning body must coordinate across jurisdictions with different political cycles, different labour markets, and different health system priorities. Queensland's nursing workforce challenges differ from those of the ACT. Any new body would need to be a COAG/National Cabinet creation, not a unilateral Commonwealth measure — which has historically proven slow and contentious.

**4. The self-regulating college problem.** Even a well-funded planning body cannot compel autonomous professional colleges to alter their training caps without legislative backing. Any arrangement short of legislation will leave college self-regulation intact. The only available lever is funding: withhold or condition STP grants. But this risks quality deterioration if colleges respond by accepting fewer trainees overall rather than redistributing them. International examples (UK) rely on a integrated state-commissioning model that does not apply in Australia's federated context.

**5. Time horizon vs. political cycle.** Health workforce planning operates on 10–30 year horizons; politics operates on 3-year cycles. HWA itself was a COAG creation funded to 2019, abolished in its fifth year. A reconstituted body would need cross-party political support, multi-year funding through forward estimates, and some form of independent governance to survive electoral cycles. The AMA's four-year funding ask may not be enough to establish durable institutional capacity.

**6. The IMG dependency safety valve.** Australia currently relies on International Medical Graduates (IMGs) to fill an estimated 42% of medical workforce growth through to 2048 (DoH modelling, from Report 028). IMGs, particularly those constrained to rural/remote DPA areas under Section 19AB, provide a pressure-relief valve that reduces the urgency felt domestically for training expansion. A national planning body might change this calculus by documenting the long-run risks of IMG dependency — particularly if global health workforce competition intensifies as aging populations in Europe and North America increase international demand for health workers.
