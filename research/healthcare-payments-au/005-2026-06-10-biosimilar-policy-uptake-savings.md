# Biosimilar Policy in Australia: The PBS Success Story That Isn't Quite Working

**Report 005 — 2026-06-10**

**Summary:** Australia's PBS biosimilar framework has generated AU$562 million in savings from just three TNF-inhibitor molecules over ten years — but biosimilar uptake in retail pharmacy sits at 35%, against an OECD average of 67% and Norway's 99%. The mechanism is working; the uptake is not. A June 2025 industry white paper estimates that policy reform could unlock an additional $75 million per biosimilar molecule, yet the government has not moved beyond a non-mandatory preferred-prescribing model.

**Why this follows from yesterday:** Report 004 documented Australia's 591-day average delay in listing new medicines on the PBS — a failure of the access system. Biosimilar policy is the counterpoint: it is an area where the PBS architecture has succeeded at reducing cost. But the same structural conservatism that delays novel medicines also limits how hard Australia leans on biosimilars — and the gap between potential and actual savings is now the central policy challenge.

---

## Background

Biosimilars are biological medicines — complex, protein-based drugs produced in living cells — that are highly similar to an already-approved reference biologic, with no clinically meaningful differences in safety or efficacy. They are not generic medicines in the conventional sense: because biologics cannot be exactly replicated through chemistry, biosimilars are approved through a dedicated regulatory pathway requiring comparative analytical, pre-clinical, and clinical studies.

The distinction matters for Australia's PBS because biologics are the single most expensive category of PBS-subsidised medicines. In 2020–21, biologics cost the Australian Government AU$2.41 billion — a figure that has grown every year since. In a programme costing $19.1 billion annually (2024–25), that is roughly 13% of PBS expenditure concentrated in a single medicine class. Biologics include the TNF inhibitors used in rheumatoid arthritis, psoriasis, and inflammatory bowel disease; the anti-HER2 agents used in breast cancer; growth hormones; insulins; erythropoietins; and the emerging wave of oncology checkpoint inhibitors.

When a biosimilar enters the market, two mechanisms generate savings for the government:

1. **The 25% statutory price reduction**: On the day the first biosimilar is PBS-listed, a mandatory one-off price reduction of up to 25% applies to all brands of the medicine — both the new biosimilar and the originator reference biologic. This is legislated under the National Health Act 1953 (Part VII, Division 3A) and was increased from 16% to up to 25% on 1 October 2018. It is automatic, unconditional, and applies regardless of how many patients switch.

2. **Ongoing price disclosure**: All medicines in F2 of the PBS formulary (which includes off-patent and biosimilar medicines) are subject to mandatory price disclosure twice a year. Sponsors must report actual market transaction prices; if the weighted average market price falls below the PBS listed price, the listed price is reduced accordingly. As more biosimilar brands enter, competitive dynamics push transaction prices down, and successive price disclosure cycles ratchet the government's purchase price progressively lower.

The combination of a guaranteed day-one price cut plus compounding disclosure reductions is the mechanism that, on paper, makes biosimilars a powerful tool for PBS sustainability.

## Key Findings

### 1. The TNF Inhibitor Case Study: $562 Million in Savings

The best empirical window into Australia's biosimilar savings comes from a 2025 study tracking PBS expenditure on the three major TNF inhibitors — adalimumab (Humira), etanercept (Enbrel), and infliximab (Remicade) — across the decade from their biosimilar market entry to 2025. (Yiu et al., *Clinical Pharmacology & Therapeutics*, 2025)

**Cumulative ten-year savings: AU$562 million.**

The savings were not evenly distributed:

- **Etanercept** contributed the largest absolute savings (~AU$341 million), due to its early biosimilar PBS listing in April 2017 and relatively rapid uptake in the hospital setting.
- **Adalimumab** biosimilars (Amgevita, Hadlima, Hyrimoz) were PBS-listed in April 2021, with further brands (Yuflyma in March 2023, Adalicip/Ciptunec in January 2024, Abrilada in August 2024) added subsequently. As of 2024, there are six adalimumab biosimilars on the PBS — more than any other molecule. The first price disclosure cycle for adalimumab ran in April 2024.
- **Infliximab** biosimilars achieved the highest uptake rate (62.4% in retail pharmacy), partly because infliximab is largely administered by infusion in hospital settings where institutional procurement decisions — rather than individual prescriber choices — drive switching.

The 25% statutory price reduction alone is estimated to have produced immediate savings of over AU$6 million in the year following etanercept biosimilar launch, before any uptake-driven savings. (AJMC, 2017 data)

### 2. The Scale of the PBS Biologic Budget

To understand why biosimilar uptake matters, the expenditure context is necessary:

- Biologics accounted for the majority of the most expensive PBS-subsidised medicines in 2020–21
- AU$2.41 billion in biologic expenditure in 2020–21 — growing each year
- At the current trajectory, biologic PBS costs could reach AU$5–6 billion annually within this decade
- Biosimilars now exist for a growing proportion of this spend: as of October 2024, 12 active ingredients have at least one biosimilar on the PBS, spread across 30 brands

The pipeline is also accelerating: the TGA registered 22 new biosimilars in 2024 alone — a record year — taking the total registered biosimilars in Australia to 65 (from just 2 in 2010). PBAC recommended nine biosimilars for PBS listing between March 2024 and March 2025.

### 3. The Uptake Problem: 35% vs 67%

Despite the structural pricing advantages, Australia's real-world uptake of biosimilars in the retail pharmacy setting lags significantly behind international peers.

| Country / Grouping | Anti-TNF biosimilar uptake | Key policy tool |
|---|---|---|
| **Norway** | ~99% (infliximab) | Annual competitive tender; hospital-level switching |
| **Denmark** | ~90% (trastuzumab after 3 months) | Mandatory hospital switching, national procurement |
| **OECD average** | ~67% | Varies; many use substitution incentives |
| **EU average** (30–40% biologic Rx) | 30–40% | Strong substitution + tender policies |
| **Australia** | ~35% (anti-TNF retail) | Non-mandatory preference; a-flag substitution |
| **Australia** (infliximab) | ~62% | Higher due to hospital infusion procurement |

(Sources: Yiu et al. 2025; Sandoz White Paper June 2025; OECD data; PubMed infliximab biosimilar comparison study)

Australia's 35% retail uptake figure represents a significant underperformance against the OECD average and, critically, a substantial opportunity cost: every additional percentage point of biosimilar uptake generates compounding savings through both the price effect (more competition in price disclosure) and the volume effect (government pays biosimilar price instead of originator price where any gap remains).

### 4. Three Structural Barriers to Higher Uptake

**A. No patient price incentive.** Under the PBS, patients pay the same co-payment regardless of whether they receive the reference biologic or a biosimilar — currently $7.70 for concessional patients and $31.60 for general patients (from January 2026, approximately). There is no financial reason for a patient to prefer a biosimilar, placing the entire burden of uptake on clinician behaviour and system-level policies. This is a deliberate equity design: the PBS is supposed to insulate patients from market pricing. But it removes the consumer-side pressure that in other markets (including the US) has driven biosimilar uptake.

**B. Complex prescriber administrative burden.** Many biologic medicines on the PBS require an Authority prescription — the prescriber must apply to Services Australia for approval before prescribing. When biosimilars were listed, the authority processes were not always streamlined to reduce the friction of switching. The Sandoz white paper (June 2025) specifically proposes removing PBS authority requirements once biosimilar uptake for a given medicine exceeds 70%, to reduce the administrative cost of switching established patients.

**C. No pharmacist financial incentive.** Unlike in some European systems, Australian pharmacists receive no additional remuneration for dispensing a biosimilar rather than a reference biologic. While pharmacists *may* substitute to an 'a-flagged' (substitutable) biosimilar when the prescriber has not indicated "no substitution", they have no financial reason to do so and face the risk of patient complaints if they substitute without thorough counselling.

Additionally, clinician education and patient trust remain barriers: some prescribers continue to hold clinical concerns about switching stable patients from a reference biologic to a biosimilar, despite regulatory bodies (TGA, PBAC) confirming equivalent safety and efficacy. The requirement for patient consent before substitution (where the prescriber has not blocked it) also means that poorly informed patients may decline.

### 5. Government Policy Tools: What Has Been Tried

Australia has applied several tools — but all are non-mandatory:

- **'a-flag' substitution**: Since the biosimilar framework was established, substitutable biosimilars are marked with an 'a' superscript in the PBS Schedule, enabling pharmacist substitution without a new prescription. This is effective only when combined with patient agreement and prescriber non-objection.
- **Preferred prescribing policy for treatment-naïve patients**: Biosimilar prescribing is officially *encouraged* (not mandated) for patients beginning biologic therapy for the first time. This is the single most important policy lever because switching established patients carries clinical complexity that new-start patients do not. The PBS Schedule includes administrative notes for medicines like adalimumab stating biosimilar prescribing is encouraged for treatment-naïve cohorts.
- **Biosimilar Awareness Initiative (2015–ongoing)**: The Department of Health and Aged Care has run clinician and consumer education campaigns through NPS MedicineWise and other channels. These campaigns address the most common misconceptions (biosimilars are not identical, but they are equivalent for regulatory and clinical purposes).
- **Streamlined authority processes**: In some therapeutic areas (dermatology), biosimilar prescribers have received streamlined authority codes, allowing immediate dispensing without submitting to Services Australia — reducing a friction point that discouraged switching.

### 6. What Reform Would Look Like: The June 2025 Sandoz White Paper

On 4 June 2025, Sandoz Australia published a white paper ("Pioneering Access for Patients Through Biosimilar Medicines") following a Canberra stakeholder summit held in February 2025. The paper represents the most detailed recent articulation of the reform case.

Key proposals:

1. **Mandate biosimilar prescribing for treatment-naïve patients** (removing the "encouraged" language and making it a prescribing requirement for new starts, with clinical exception pathways)
2. **Remove PBS authority requirements** once biosimilar uptake for a medicine exceeds 70%, reducing administrative friction for continuing and switching prescriptions
3. **Reinvest PBS biosimilar savings into a Chronic Disease Future Fund** — a hypothecated savings pool to fund new listings and disease management programmes
4. **Potential savings**: the paper estimates properly implemented reform could save the PBS **up to $75 million per biosimilar molecule**, compounded over the medicine lifecycle

The government has not formally responded to the white paper. The current Strategic Agreement with GBMA (the Generic and Biosimilar Medicines Association, representing biosimilar suppliers) and Medicines Australia (representing originators) runs until 30 June 2027 — providing a natural policy window.

### 7. International Models: What Works

**Norway's tender model** is the gold standard: the government runs an annual competitive tender for hospital-sector biologics and biosimilars. The lowest-priced product wins national supply for 12 months. In 2015, infliximab biosimilar (Remsima) won the tender at a price **72% below the originator's official list price**. This has driven near-total biosimilar uptake in Norway's hospital sector (99.4% for infliximab) and delivered enormous savings — but requires government willingness to select a single supplier and manage the risk of supply disruption.

**Denmark's switching mandate** drove 90% biosimilar trastuzumab uptake within three months of the first biosimilar launch — faster than any other European country — through a combination of national procurement policy, mandatory hospital switching, and clinician education aligned with switching.

Neither Norway's tendering nor Denmark's mandated switching has been adopted in Australia. The political economy of tendering for biologics is complex: originator companies and some clinician groups resist a model in which a single tender winner supplants all established brands, citing concerns about supply continuity, patient disruption, and prescriber autonomy.

## Tensions and Open Questions

**1. Forced switching vs clinical autonomy.** The strongest reforms — mandatory prescribing for new starts, potential switching mandates — conflict with Australia's medical culture of prescriber autonomy. Rheumatologists, gastroenterologists, and dermatologists who have carefully titrated a patient on a reference biologic for years may resist mandatory substitution even where evidence supports it. The clinical concern is immunogenicity risk on switching — a risk that regulators consider low but that individual specialists weigh differently than population-level data suggest.

**2. Who captures the savings?** The PBS savings from biosimilar uptake flow to the Commonwealth Budget, not to patients (whose co-payments are unchanged) and not to prescribers (who bear the administrative burden of switching). This creates a structural misalignment: the party that does the work (the clinician, who manages switching) receives no benefit, while the party that receives the saving (government) has limited ability to reward compliance. Hypothecated reinvestment (e.g., the Chronic Disease Future Fund proposal) attempts to address this.

**3. Oncology biosimilars: the next frontier.** Most of Australia's current biosimilar savings come from immunology (TNF inhibitors). The next wave — trastuzumab (breast cancer), bevacizumab (colorectal cancer), rituximab (lymphoma) — involves oncology, where clinician resistance to switching has historically been even stronger. TGA registrations of oncology biosimilars are accelerating (five denosumab biosimilar brands approved in 2024), but PBS listing and uptake strategies for oncology biosimilars will require more active management than immunology has needed.

**4. The 25% cap vs. competitive markets.** The mandatory 25% statutory reduction is a floor, not a ceiling on savings. In competitive biosimilar markets, price disclosure can drive the effective government purchase price far below that — but only if uptake is sufficient to generate real competition. With 35% uptake, manufacturers can price-discount without the full force of market competition applying. Lifting uptake to 67%+ would materially change the competitive dynamics and the price disclosure outcome.

**5. The safety communication challenge.** Despite regulatory confirmation of biosimilar equivalence, some patients and prescribers conflate "biosimilar" with "inferior" or "different." The framing matters: calling a biosimilar a "generic biologic" is technically incorrect but might improve public trust. Australia has not resolved the communication strategy to the satisfaction of all stakeholders.

## Threads to Branch Into Next

1. **High-cost medicines and PBS sustainability** — the next wave of biologics and gene therapies (CAR-T cells, mRNA platforms, gene-editing therapeutics) will dwarf today's biologic costs; what framework is Australia building to assess and fund these? The ICER threshold debate becomes existential here.
2. **The PHARMAC comparison** — New Zealand's single-buyer, tendering-based model represents the opposite end of the spectrum from Australia's approach; comparing per-capita biologic costs, patient access equity, and listing speed would directly illuminate the policy trade-offs Australia faces.
3. **Private health insurance and PBS interaction** — PHI hospital and extras covers interact with PBS in complex ways; for biologics in particular, patients in private hospitals may face different formulary access and different out-of-pocket costs than public patients.
4. **TGA reform and international regulatory alignment** — the 22 TGA biosimilar approvals in 2024 reflect a strengthening approval pipeline; if TGA can further align with EMA/FDA pathways, the biosimilar listing pipeline could accelerate further.
5. **GP practice economics and bulk billing viability** — still uncovered from earlier reports; the systemic pressures on general practice funding connect to how patients are channelled toward (or away from) specialist biologic prescribers, affecting who gets treated and where.
