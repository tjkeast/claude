# Rural and Remote Healthcare Payments: The Modified Monash Model, Workforce Incentives, and the $8.35 Billion Funding Gap

**Report 012 — 2026-06-18**

**Summary:** Approximately 7.7 million Australians (roughly 28% of the population) live outside major cities, yet the government spends $1,090 less per person per year on their health than on city residents — a total annual gap of $8.35 billion. The principal policy tool for addressing this is a geography-based classification and incentive system anchored by the Modified Monash Model (MMM), but payment incentives alone cannot solve a shortage that is fundamentally about workforce supply: when no psychologists, GPs, or allied health workers are available within driving distance, no rebate scale will conjure them.

**Why this follows from yesterday:** Report 011 documented a 57.3% undersupply in the psychology workforce — with the shortage concentrated most acutely in rural and remote areas — and noted that the session cap debate is partly moot when patients cannot find a psychologist at all. This report examines the payment architecture designed to address that geographic maldistribution across all health professions.

---

## Background

### The Modified Monash Model

The **Modified Monash Model (MMM)** is the Australian Government's primary geographic classification tool for health workforce and funding purposes. First introduced in 2015 to replace the Australian Standard Geographical Classification — Remoteness Areas (ASGC-RA) system, the MMM adds a population-size dimension on top of remoteness, producing a more granular seven-category scale:

| Category | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **MM 1** | Major city |
| **MM 2** | Inner regional (large regional cities: population >50,000) |
| **MM 3** | Large rural town (population 15,000–50,000) |
| **MM 4** | Medium rural town (population 5,000–15,000) |
| **MM 5** | Small rural town (population 1,000–5,000) |
| **MM 6** | Remote community (very small or remote) |
| **MM 7** | Very remote community |

The most recent version, **MMM 2023**, was published in March 2025 and is now the operative classification used by Services Australia, the Health Workforce Locator, and all MMM-linked payment programs. The classification determines eligibility and payment amounts for nearly every rural health financial incentive the Commonwealth operates.

### Distribution Priority Areas

Alongside the MMM sits the **Distribution Priority Area (DPA)** system, which specifically identifies locations with a shortage of GP services relative to a national benchmark. The DPA calculation combines the volume of Medicare-billed GP services actually received by an area's patients with demographic factors including age, sex, and socio-economic status (SEIFA).

The DPA has a direct regulatory function: **International Medical Graduates (IMGs) must practise in a DPA location to access Medicare billing rights**. This makes DPA a critical lever for directing overseas-trained GPs — who constitute a large share of Australia's rural medical workforce — to underserved areas. All MM 2–7 locations are automatically DPA; within MM 1 (major cities), DPA is determined by the GP-services benchmark. The most recent annual DPA update was released in March 2025.

### Who Lives Outside Major Cities?

The AIHW estimates approximately **7.7 million Australians** (around 27–28% of the population) live in regional, rural, or remote areas. They are not a marginal population: they represent more than one in four Australians, they have disproportionately high rates of chronic disease and injury, and they live in areas that systematically receive less healthcare funding and have fewer providers per capita than major cities.

## Key Findings

### 1. The Health Outcomes Gap Is Large and Getting Worse

The rural/urban health disparity in Australia is well-documented but insufficiently corrected. Key mortality statistics from the AIHW (2021–2023 data):

- The **age-standardised death rate in Very Remote Australia** is **1.6 times higher** than in Major cities — 779 deaths per 100,000 compared to 499 per 100,000
- **Potentially avoidable death rates** are dramatically higher in remote areas. For diabetes specifically: the rate in Very Remote areas is **6.7 times higher than Major cities** for males; for females in Very Remote areas, the diabetes PAD rate is **12 times higher**
- The leading causes of death in Remote and Very Remote Australia are largely preventable: coronary heart disease, lung cancer, diabetes, COPD, and suicide — a pattern that reflects inadequate chronic disease management infrastructure

The AIHW's 2025 rural and remote health report confirms the pattern holds across most major disease categories. Australians in rural and remote areas face lower life expectancy, higher rates of disease and injury, and substantially worse outcomes for conditions that respond well to primary care intervention when caught early.

### 2. The $8.35 Billion Funding Gap

The **National Rural Health Alliance's August 2025 "Forgotten Health Spend" report** — the most comprehensive recent analysis of rural health expenditure — put a precise figure on the disparity:

- The total gap in health expenditure between urban and non-urban Australians is **$8.35 billion per year** (2023–24 figures)
- This equates to **$1,090.47 less spent per person per year** on rural/remote Australians compared to city residents
- The gap has grown by approximately $1.8 billion since the equivalent 2020–21 analysis, and the per capita gap has increased by **$110.33 in real terms** even after adjusting for inflation

Where does the gap fall?

- **Public hospital funding** is the largest single driver: public hospitals in small rural towns receive approximately **70% less funding per capita** than metropolitan hospitals
- **Private hospital coverage** is absent in MMM 6 and 7 regions entirely — there are no private hospitals in the most remote parts of Australia
- **Private health insurance** benefits gaps follow: PHI hospital cover is essentially unusable in very remote areas, removing one of the two parallel funding streams that metropolitan patients access
- The NRHA called for a dedicated **rural health fund of $1 billion per annum** to enable coordinated, flexible state–federal–local funding as a structural response, rather than ad hoc incentive programs

### 3. MBS Bulk Billing Incentives Scale with Remoteness

Under the November 2025 reforms (documented in Report 009), the **Bulk Billing Practice Incentive Program (BBPIP)** provides a 12.5% loading on MBS benefits for practices that fully bulk bill. But this base rate is geographically scaled according to MMM classification:

- **MM 2 locations:** approximately **150%** of the metro (MM 1) incentive value
- **MM 3 locations:** higher again
- Through to **MM 7 locations:** approximately **190%** of the metro incentive value

This scaling acknowledges that the cost of operating in remote areas is higher (travel, locum cover, infrastructure) and that attracting GPs to fully bulk bill in those areas requires greater financial inducement. In practice, the MMM-based scaling of BBPIP incentives means that a rural GP practice at MM 7 who bulk bills all patients receives nearly double the base BBPIP incentive per service compared to a metropolitan practice.

Prior to BBPIP, separate **Rural Bulk Billing Incentives (RBBI)** applied for eligible rural patients; these were restructured under the November 2025 changes and folded into the unified BBPIP framework, with the geographic loading preserved.

### 4. The Workforce Incentive Program: Paying GPs to Go Rural

The most direct federal instrument for attracting GPs (and some allied health) to rural and remote locations is the **Workforce Incentive Program (WIP)**. It comprises two streams relevant to GPs:

**WIP Doctor Stream:** Provides annual financial incentives to eligible GPs based on their MMM location and years of continuous service in that location. Maximum annual payments range from **$0** at MM 1 to **up to $60,000** at MM 7, scaling progressively across years of service and remoteness level. The program is designed to reward sustained commitment — the incentive grows with the number of years a doctor remains in the location.

**WIP Rural Advanced Skills Stream:** Targets GPs who provide primary care *plus* emergency care and/or advanced clinical skills (obstetrics, anaesthetics, procedural skills) in MM 3–7 locations. Eligible doctors can claim **up to $21,000 per year**, claimable up to four times between 2023 and 2026.

**NSW Rural Health Workforce Incentive Scheme:** NSW Health separately offered incentive packages of **up to $20,000** for health workers relocating to rural/remote NSW positions, available to June 2026.

Beyond GPs, the **WIP Practice Stream** funds practices in MM 2–7 locations to employ nurses, midwives, and allied health professionals — providing a payment to the practice rather than directly to the health worker. This is intended to make it financially viable to add clinical staff who might not individually generate enough billings to justify their employment cost.

### 5. IMG Dependence and the DPA Mechanism

The Distribution Priority Area system has become a critical enabler of rural GP supply through **International Medical Graduates**. A significant and growing share of rural GP services is delivered by IMGs on Section 19AB restrictions — doctors who must work in DPA locations to receive Medicare assignment. The system has created a form of directed labour supply: IMGs who want access to the Australian healthcare market are effectively channelled to underserved locations as the price of entry.

This is both a pragmatic solution to an acute shortage and a source of structural fragility:
- IMGs on Section 19AB restrictions are typically required to work in DPA locations for **10 years** before restrictions are lifted
- Restrictions can be shorter for doctors with Fellowship of ACRRM or RACGP with a rural and remote qualification
- The system depends on continued international supply; if immigration conditions or overseas workforce dynamics change, the supply may not be reliable

The RACGP has raised concerns that DPA boundary changes can "take doctors away from rural areas" — when an area's DPA status is removed (because its GP service level meets the benchmark), it may lose the IMGs who were specifically permitted to work there, even if the benchmark was met only because of those IMGs. This creates a perverse circularity.

### 6. Rural Generalist Medicine: A New Specialty

In **September 2025**, all Australian Health Ministers formally approved **rural generalist medicine** as a recognised field of specialty practice within General Practice — a landmark reform after decades of advocacy by ACRRM and rural medical community stakeholders.

The recognition has practical consequences:
- Rural Generalists now have a **protected title** and are listed on the national register of medical specialties
- The **Rural Generalist Training Scheme (RGTS)** was consolidated into the Australian General Practice Training (AGPT) program from January 2026, absorbing 100 RGTS training places into the unified system for streamlined delivery
- The **John Flynn Prevocational Doctor Program** (which placed junior doctors in rural hospitals to give early rural exposure) became the **Australian Primary Care Prevocational Program** from 1 January 2026
- From 2026: an additional 200 funded AGPT commencing training places; from 2028, 400 additional places per year
- ACRRM will offer up to 374 Rural Generalist training places for 2027 — its largest intake on record

The recognition of rural generalism as a specialty matters for workforce supply because it makes a rural career pathway more legible, credentialed, and comparable to urban specialty pathways — addressing a long-standing concern that rural generalism was perceived as a career compromise rather than a distinct specialty practice.

### 7. Telehealth: The Digital Bridge and Its Limits

Telehealth has become a structurally important access mechanism for rural and remote Australians, and the evidence supports its use:

- **From 1 July 2025**, 23 new MBS telehealth items were introduced, covering 18 video consultation items and 5 telephone consultation items, primarily for chronic condition management
- **From 1 November 2025**, MyMedicare-enrolled patients are exempt from the 12-month face-to-face requirement to access telehealth, extending the usability of telehealth for enrolled rural patients
- AIHW data confirm telehealth usage increases with remoteness — use is highest in the most remote areas, where it partially bridges physical provider gaps
- Telepsychiatry specifically has expanded substantially following COVID-era telehealth rule relaxation; regions with fewer per-capita psychiatrists show greater telepsychiatry utilisation
- A systematic review of 20 studies found telehealth was a cost-saving option in 18 of them for maternal and child health in regional, rural, and remote settings

But telehealth has documented limits:
- **Connectivity**: poor phone and internet infrastructure in some remote areas makes video consultations unreliable
- **Scope**: many clinical needs require physical examination, procedures, or imaging unavailable remotely
- **Dementia and cognitive impairment**: telehealth is often inappropriate for patients with advanced cognitive disorders
- The Australian College of Nursing has flagged that some 2025 telehealth rule changes had "significant unintended consequences" for rural and remote patients

The government's own telehealth post-implementation review (2024) acknowledged that while telehealth expansion substantially improved access, it cannot substitute for local clinical capacity in high-acuity or procedural care.

### 8. The Allied Health Shortfall

Discussion of rural health workforce tends to focus on GPs, but the allied health shortage in rural and remote areas is equally severe and receives less policy attention.

- Allied health professionals — physiotherapists, occupational therapists, speech pathologists, dietitians, social workers, psychologists — are **heavily concentrated in major cities**
- While the national allied health workforce grew 67% between 2013 and 2022, this growth was unevenly distributed; rural and remote areas, particularly small rural towns, saw far lower per-capita growth
- A March 2025 report commissioned by ACDHS on rural and remote allied health workforce development identified persistent recruitment and retention barriers: professional isolation, limited career progression, inadequate clinical supervision, and income instability from thin local markets
- The **National Allied Health Workforce Strategy** was under development with expected completion in 2025 — its publication and implementation status is a key pending policy item

The allied health shortage is directly relevant to the Better Access findings in Report 011: the 57.3% national psychology workforce undersupply is concentrated in exactly the rural and remote areas where it is hardest to expand supply through financial incentives alone.

## Tensions and Open Questions

**1. Financial incentives are necessary but not sufficient.** WIP Doctor Stream and BBPIP geographic scaling can make rural practice more financially attractive, but they cannot address the non-financial deterrents: professional isolation, spouse/partner career constraints, school quality, social infrastructure, and safety. Evidence consistently shows that the strongest predictor of rural practice is rural origin — doctors who grew up rurally are far more likely to return. This argues for rural-origin pipeline investment (medical school selection, rural clinical placements) over payment incentives alone.

**2. The DPA/IMG system has fragility.** Australia's rural GP supply in MM 4–7 areas is substantially dependent on IMGs working under Medicare access restrictions. This is a managed dependency that works until it doesn't — policy changes to immigration, changes in source countries' healthcare needs, or geopolitical shifts could quickly disrupt supply in ways that financial incentives cannot rapidly repair.

**3. The $8.35 billion gap is structural, not marginal.** The NRHA figure is not driven by discrete payment anomalies but by the cumulative effect of hospital funding formulae, private health insurance inapplicability, allied health scarcity, and lower MBS utilisation from fewer providers. Closing it would require sustained, multi-decade investment across all health funding streams — not a program here and an incentive there.

**4. Telehealth and MMM: a classification problem.** The MMM classifies areas by population size and remoteness, but telehealth access depends on infrastructure (bandwidth, devices, digital literacy) that doesn't map cleanly onto MMM categories. A small rural town classified MM 5 may have better internet than a community classified MM 4. Payment policy that treats MMM as a proxy for digital access risk may be systematically miscalibrated.

**5. Rural Generalist recognition is symbolic and structural.** Formally designating rural generalism as a specialty sends an important signal, but the workforce pipeline runs on 4–6 year training cycles. The 2026 training reforms will not produce consultant-level rural generalists until 2030 at the earliest. The current workforce shortage is an acute problem; the training reforms are a long-run supply response.

## Threads to Branch Into Next

1. **Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander health payments** — The worst rural and remote health outcomes in Australia are experienced by Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander people, whose health funding structures go well beyond the MMM incentive system: Aboriginal Community Controlled Health Organisations (ACCHOs), the Closing the Gap funding framework, and the NDIS all operate in parallel with (and sometimes at tension with) the mainstream MBS/PBS architecture. This thread has been raised but not explored.

2. **Headspace and youth mental health block grants** — PHN-funded, not MBS fee-for-service; 12–25 age cohort; directly relevant to the rural access gap documented in Report 011.

3. **Medical Costs Finder Bill 2026** — Third deferral; specialist fee transparency legislation. The rural access gap adds a dimension: rural Australians who must travel to access specialists face both the out-of-pocket cost and the travel burden.

4. **NDIS and psychosocial disability in regional areas** — The "missing middle" identified in the Productivity Commission's November 2025 report is amplified in rural and remote areas where NDIS-funded psychosocial support services are even thinner on the ground.

5. **US trade pressure on PBS and Medicare pricing** — Deferred from Reports 007–011. With the NRHA data quantifying a $8.35 billion structural rural health funding gap, any PBS pricing pressure that reduced Commonwealth health expenditure capacity would disproportionately affect the programs and incentives designed for rural access.
