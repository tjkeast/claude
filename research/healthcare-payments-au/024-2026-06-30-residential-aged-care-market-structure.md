# Residential Aged Care Market Structure: For-Profit Growth, the RAD Capital Pool, and the Bed-Construction Gamble

**Report 024 — 2026-06-30**

**Summary:** Australia's residential aged care sector has undergone a quiet but significant ownership shift over the past decade, with for-profit providers now controlling about 40% of funded places. A $33.6 billion pool of residents' interest-free lump-sum deposits underpins the sector's capital base — a structural feature unique to Australia. With 61% of homes losing money, private equity consolidating its holdings at multi-billion-dollar valuations, and the government betting $1.7 billion on a bed-construction incentive that falls well short of demographic need, the sector is at a critical inflection point where market logic and care quality are pulling in opposite directions.

**Why this follows from yesterday:** Report 023 documented the aged care workforce crisis — the wage-funding mismatch, the care minutes compliance failure, and the migration pipeline's low throughput. The same financial pressure driving workforce understaffing also shapes who owns and operates residential aged care homes, how capital investment decisions are made, and whether the sector can build the 10,000 new beds per year that demography demands.

---

## Background

Residential aged care in Australia is a heavily government-funded but privately operated sector. The Commonwealth provides per-diem funding to approved providers via the Australian National Aged Care Classification (AN-ACC), while the Aged Care Quality and Safety Commission (ACQSC) regulates care standards. Unlike hospital care, residential aged care providers are not public entities: most are independently owned, and ownership type — for-profit, not-for-profit, or government — has become an increasingly contested variable in the quality and access debate.

The Royal Commission into Aged Care Quality and Safety (Final Report, March 2021) put it bluntly: the sector "has become dominated by large for-profit providers and lost focus on its values and social mission to provide safe and high quality care." The Commission recommended stronger regulatory oversight and provider-approval requirements, but stopped short of recommending ownership restrictions or profit limits — a contested gap in its reform agenda.

## Key Findings

### 1. Market Composition: For-Profit Now Controls 40% of Funded Places

As of 30 June 2024 (AIHW GEN Aged Care Data):

- **Not-for-profit organisations:** 57% of residential care *services* (by provider count); approximately 125,000 places — representing 55.8% of total places
- **Private for-profit organisations:** 35% of services; approximately **90,700 places — 40.5% of total funded places**
- **Government organisations:** 8% of services; approximately 8,300 places

The aggregate number of residential aged care places has grown from approximately 201,000 (2017) to **224,000 (2024)** — an 11% increase. For-profit providers have driven disproportionate share of that growth: private-sector places grew 14% over that period, against 12% for NFPs and a decline for government providers.

By state, Victoria has the highest proportion of private and government residential care services (41% and 20%, respectively). The Northern Territory and ACT have the highest NFP concentration (89% each) — reflecting the dominance of church-affiliated providers in smaller markets and the relative absence of for-profit capital in thin-market jurisdictions.

There are currently **636 residential aged care providers** operating in Australia (30 June 2025, Department of Health and Aged Care). This represents a 4.3% decline in the number of providers since FY21 — a sign of consolidation pressure, not market entry.

### 2. The Market Leaders: Private Equity's Dominance

The for-profit segment is heavily concentrated among a handful of large operators with private equity or institutional ownership:

**Opal HealthCare** (owned by Quadrant Private Equity/G.K. Goh): Australia's largest residential aged care operator with **142 homes** following its March 2024 acquisition of BlueCross and its 31 Victorian facilities. The BlueCross deal was the most significant market transaction of recent years and extended Opal's reach into the Victorian market for the first time.

**Estia Health** (~90 facilities): Bain Capital acquired Estia in 2022 (taking it private from the ASX at approximately $800M). In 2025–26, with the Regis merger proposal abandoned, Bain Capital agreed to sell Estia to a consortium led by Stonepeak (an alternative assets manager) and Axight Capital (Abu Dhabi) for approximately **$2.5 billion** — one of the largest transactions in Australian aged care history. The deal, expected to complete in H2 2026, signals ongoing institutional appetite for the sector as a long-duration asset.

**Regis Healthcare** (ASX-listed, ~68 homes): Remains publicly listed and has been acquisitive in 2025, purchasing multiple smaller operators. Regis had been in preliminary discussions about a merger with Estia under Bain before the Stonepeak deal was announced.

**Bolton Clarke** (NFP, ~2nd largest by homes): A notable counterexample — a large not-for-profit that has itself been acquisitive, purchasing Allity (43 homes) from Archer Capital for approximately $700M and Calvary's acquisition of Japara ($380M) represents similar NFP consolidation.

The M&A data reveals a sector being re-sorted into fewer but larger operators, with transaction multiples in the hundreds of millions to billions of dollars. IBISWorld estimates the residential aged care services industry revenue at **$38.7 billion in 2026**.

### 3. The RAD System: A $33+ Billion Interest-Free Capital Pool

Australia's residential aged care system has a structural capital mechanism unique globally: the Refundable Accommodation Deposit (RAD). When a resident enters an approved aged care home, they can pay a lump-sum RAD (equivalent to their accommodation cost capitalised at the Maximum Permissible Interest Rate, or MPIR). The provider holds the RAD interest-free and refunds it in full when the resident leaves or dies. The resident loses only the time-value of money on the amount; the provider gains access to a large interest-free loan for the duration of the resident's stay.

As of June 2021, the total value of RADs held by residential aged care providers was approximately **$33.6 billion** (Department of Health). This figure has likely grown since then as accommodation prices increased and new residents entered the system.

This pool functions as a de facto zero-cost bond market — providers borrow from residents to fund capital investment, property maintenance, and operating cash flow. It is a significant structural advantage for large operators with high occupancy: Opal and Estia, with thousands of residents, hold RAD pools in the billions. It also creates an inherent incentive to maximise the number of fully self-funded (non-means-tested) residents, whose higher RADs are more valuable to the provider than the government-capped Accommodation Supplement paid for supported residents.

**The maximum RAD** that can be charged without Aged Care Pricing Commissioner approval is currently capped at **$750,000** (indexed to CPI from July 2025 under the Aged Care Act 2024). The Commissioner must approve accommodation prices above an equivalent of approximately $165.44 per day.

### 4. RAD Retention: The New 2% Annual Deduction from 1 November 2025

The Aged Care Act 2024 introduced a mandatory RAD retention mechanism for residents entering aged care from 1 November 2025:

- Providers must deduct **2% per annum** of the resident's RAD balance (calculated daily)
- Deductions apply for a maximum of **five years** (up to 10% of the initial RAD over the full period)
- Deductions cannot reset if a resident moves between homes
- **Existing RAD holders** (entered before 1 November 2025) are fully protected — their RADs remain 100% refundable under the old rules

At a median RAD of roughly $400,000, the annual retention is approximately $8,000 — material for families, but modest relative to the time-value cost residents were already implicitly bearing. The policy objective is to provide providers with a more reliable capital contribution from residents, reducing their dependence on government capital subsidies. For providers, the 2% retention on a large RAD pool (e.g., $500M held by Opal) represents approximately $10M per year in non-refundable income — a meaningful supplement to operating cash flow.

Critics have noted this effectively makes the RAD a partially non-refundable accommodation bond, reversing the consumer protection introduced when the sector moved away from accommodation bonds in 2014.

### 5. Accommodation Supplement and the Low-Means Access Crisis

The Accommodation Supplement (paid by the government for residents with insufficient assets or income to pay the market accommodation price) has been structurally inadequate for years. As of 1 April 2026, the maximum Accommodation Supplement for newly built or significantly refurbished facilities is **$72.30 per resident per day** — a fraction of what providers can charge self-funded residents (whose RADs imply daily equivalents of $100–$300+).

This gap creates a structural market incentive: providers have a financial reason to prefer self-funded residents (higher RADs) over supported residents (supplement only). Geographic concentration follows: for-profit providers in metropolitan areas where property values are high and self-funded residents plentiful have less financial incentive to accept supported residents.

The **2026–27 Budget** attempts to address this with a **$1.1 billion** provisioned restructuring of the Accommodation Supplement, including:
- Increases to the supplement from 2027
- A new **premium payment for homes where more than 60% of residents are supported (low-means)** — targeting providers that take the hardest-to-serve populations
- **$348.4 million over four years** in capital subsidies, including a new **$30 per supported resident per day** payment and **$15 per supported resident per day** for existing homes undergoing significant expansion

The design — rewarding homes that take a high proportion of supported residents — is an attempt to counteract the market incentive to cherry-pick wealthier residents. Whether the quantum is sufficient to change provider behaviour in high-cost metropolitan markets remains to be seen.

### 6. Care Minutes Compliance: The For-Profit Governance Divide

The data on care minutes compliance provides the clearest evidence that ownership structure matters for care quality. As reported in Report 023, as of 2024–25:

- **For-profit homes in MM1 (metropolitan) areas:** Only **18%** meeting both the 215-minute total and 44-minute RN care minutes mandates
- **Not-for-profit homes in MM1 areas:** **45%** compliance — 2.5 times the for-profit rate

This gap is not a workforce availability problem in those markets — metropolitan areas have better nurse supply than rural regions. The gap reflects a **staffing model decision**: for-profit providers in metropolitan areas have been slower to invest in the additional registered nursing hours required, potentially because the cost reduces margin without direct revenue benefit.

The April 2026 funding-compliance link — where non-compliant homes can lose up to **$31.92 per resident per day** — is designed to change this calculus. Whether the financial consequence is large enough to alter staffing models at scale is unknown; early compliance data for metropolitan for-profit homes after April 2026 will be the key test.

### 7. The $1.7 Billion Bed Construction Gamble

Budget 2026–27 committed **$1.7 billion to incentivise construction of up to 5,000 additional residential aged care beds per year from 1 July 2027**, including:
- $606.5 million for new capital subsidies
- 20 additional Specialist Dementia Care units
- Expansion of the Hospital to Aged Care Dementia Support Program from 11 to 20 sites

The central problem is scale: Ageing Australia has stated that the sector needs approximately **10,000 new beds per year** to meet demographic demand — the Budget incentive targets half that. The sector is already at approximately **94% national occupancy**, with some metropolitan markets at effective full capacity.

The design of the capital subsidy also raises questions about distribution: capital-intensive investors (large for-profit chains with balance sheet capacity) are better positioned to access construction financing and execute at scale than smaller NFP operators or government-owned facilities. If the $1.7 billion incentive primarily stimulates construction by large for-profit chains in metropolitan markets, it may add capacity where occupancy and self-funded residents are plentiful while leaving the geographic gaps — rural and remote areas, regions with high supported-resident concentrations — unaddressed.

## Tensions and Open Questions

**1. Is the sector structurally ungovernable as a market?** Residential aged care has the features of a market — private providers, price signals, consumer choice — but not the conditions: residents lack information, choice, and often the capacity to switch. The Royal Commission identified this but recommendations stopped short of structural remedies.

**2. The RAD pool and systemic risk.** $33+ billion of residents' lump sums are held by providers as operational capital. If a large operator — an Opal or Estia equivalent — became financially insolvent, the refund obligation could trigger a crisis. The Aged Care Act 2024 requires monthly reporting of RAD balances, a prudential improvement, but there is no sector-wide insurance or guarantee scheme for RADs analogous to the Financial Claims Scheme for bank deposits.

**3. For-profit consolidation and the 2.5x compliance gap.** The combination of ongoing private equity consolidation and a persistent 18%/45% care minutes compliance gap in metropolitan areas suggests that scale and ownership type affect care quality in ways the current regulatory framework has not resolved. The April 2026 funding-compliance link may help, but if the penalty is absorbed as a cost of doing business, the structural problem remains.

**4. The construction incentive and geographic equity.** A $1.7 billion incentive targeting 5,000 beds (half of need) will likely favour markets where returns are highest. The government's stated focus on "protecting access for people with limited financial means" through the 60%-supported-resident premium may counteract this, but the mechanism is incomplete.

**5. Private equity as long-duration infrastructure investor.** The Stonepeak/Axight acquisition of Estia at $2.5 billion represents the sector as institutional infrastructure — a long-term cash-flow asset backed by government funding and demographic tailwinds. This is a legitimate capital market function. It also means the sector's strategic direction is set in part by investment committees whose primary obligation is returns to offshore institutional investors.

## Threads to Branch Into Next

1. **Dementia and the hospital-to-aged-care boundary** — dementia is now the leading driver of residential aged care demand and the primary clinical rationale for the new $224.3M Hospital to Aged Care Dementia Support expansion; how dementia care is funded across hospital, residential aged care, and community settings; the Specialist Dementia Care Program
2. **Specialist medical training pipeline** — medical college training number controls across oncology, haematology, psychiatry, and geriatrics; how supply restrictions perpetuate specialist fee power and limit access to specialist-intensive care like dementia management
3. **Aged care quality and safety regulation** — ACQSC enforcement powers under the Aged Care Act 2024; serious incident reporting; restrictive practice governance; the compliance-sanction pipeline; how a regulator polices 636 providers across 2,700+ services with constrained resources
4. **Outcomes-based reimbursement in aged care** — AN-ACC as a classification-based model; whether linking funding to outcomes (hospitalisation avoidance, falls reduction, quality of life) could shift incentives; international models (UK personalised budgets, Netherlands Buurtzorg)
5. **Aged care and the Medicare Safety Net interface** — older Australians in residential aged care still incur MBS co-payments (GP, specialist visits), PBS co-payments, and means-tested aged care co-contributions simultaneously; the compounding out-of-pocket burden is poorly studied
