# Voluntary Assisted Dying and the Healthcare Payment System: Australia's Newest End-of-Life Entitlement and Its Funding Fault Lines

**Report 027 · 3 July 2026**

**Summary:** Voluntary assisted dying (VAD) is now legal across all six Australian states and the ACT, and deaths by VAD rose 48% in 2024–25 to 3,329 — representing approximately 2% of all Australian deaths. Yet the payment architecture for VAD is arguably the most fragmented in Australia's healthcare system: there are no dedicated Medicare Benefits Schedule items, coordinating practitioners commit 6–8 hours per patient for which there is no adequate remuneration, a 2023 Federal Court ruling makes telehealth VAD consultations illegal under the Criminal Code, and state-based funding is patchy and inconsistent. VAD sits awkwardly in the gap between palliative care (which it is not), Medicare (which explicitly excludes it), and aged care (which must facilitate it but cannot initiate it). As the NT prepares to introduce its own bill and Victoria's landmark 2025 reforms await implementation, the central unresolved question is whether Australia can sustain a growing VAD system that relies on practitioners effectively working for free.

**Why this follows from yesterday:** Report 026 documented Australia's palliative care funding crisis — underfunded, hospital-concentrated, and poorly designed for dementia's slow terminal trajectory. Voluntary assisted dying is the legal alternative at the end of that trajectory, and understanding how — and whether — it is paid for reveals a different kind of systemic failure: one not of underfunding so much as of structural non-recognition by the Commonwealth.

## Background

### The legislative patchwork: from Victoria to the ACT

Australia's VAD laws are among the most thoroughly regulated in the world. Each state has built its own scheme, with different eligibility criteria, oversight structures, and practitioner training requirements. The legislation's spread from 2019 to 2025 has been rapid by Australian standards:

| Jurisdiction | Law commenced | Prognosis requirement | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Victoria | 19 June 2019 | 12 months (from April 2027) | Originally 6 months; extended by 2025 Amendment Act |
| Western Australia | 1 July 2021 | 6 months (12 months neurological) | Regional Access Support Scheme for travel costs |
| Tasmania | 23 October 2022 | 6 months (12 months neurological) | 100% practitioner dissatisfaction in 2025 VADANZ survey |
| Queensland | 1 January 2023 | 12 months (all conditions) | Highest death count in 2024–25 |
| South Australia | 31 January 2023 | 6 months (12 months neurological) | 100% practitioner dissatisfaction in 2025 VADANZ survey |
| New South Wales | 28 November 2023 | 6 months (12 months neurological) | Best-performing state for practitioner remuneration satisfaction |
| ACT | 3 November 2025 | No prognosis requirement | Unique nationally; nurse practitioners eligible to assess |
| Northern Territory | Bill expected mid-2026 | 12 months (proposed) | If passed, implementation likely early 2028 |

The common eligibility framework requires: Australian citizenship or permanent residency; the relevant state residency; a diagnosis of an advanced, incurable, and progressive disease; decision-making capacity; and that the condition is causing suffering that cannot be relieved in a manner acceptable to the person. All states require two independent medical assessments — by a coordinating practitioner and a consulting practitioner — each of whom must have completed mandatory training. In most states, the mandatory training takes 6–9 hours.

As of June 2025, approximately **14,000 Australians** have applied for VAD since Victoria's scheme began in 2019, and more than **7,200 have died** using a VAD substance.

### The Victorian 2025 Amendment Act

The most significant legislative change of the past year came from Victoria, where the parliament passed the **Voluntary Assisted Dying Amendment Act 2025** on 18 November 2025. The Act removes the "gag clause" — a prohibition on medical practitioners initiating discussions about VAD — allowing clinicians to proactively raise VAD options in end-of-life care conversations for the first time. It also extends Victoria's prognosis requirement from 6 to 12 months for all conditions (equalising with Queensland), and removes the 12-month residency requirement. These amendments will take effect from April 2027 following an 18-month implementation period.

The gag clause removal is particularly significant: it brings Victoria's framework into alignment with the evidence that early, proactive conversations about end-of-life preferences — including VAD — are associated with better outcomes. The 2025 amendment has been described as correcting a structural anachronism in which the Commonwealth can fund advance care planning (via MBS items for GP consultations) but state law previously prohibited the doctor from actually raising the dying option the plan might lead to.

## Key Findings

### 1. Demand is surging — 3,329 deaths in 2024–25, up 48%

VAD activity increased by approximately **48%** nationwide in 2024–25. In that year alone:

- **3,329 people died by VAD** — up from approximately 2,250 the previous year
- **Queensland**: 1,072 deaths (highest volume nationally; laws operative since January 2023)
- **New South Wales**: 1,028 deaths in its first full year of operation (28 Nov 2023 to 30 June 2025)
- **Victoria**: 371 deaths — the lowest per-capita rate despite being the longest-running scheme; attributed to the more restrictive 6-month prognosis requirement (now extended to 12 months from 2027)
- **Tasmania**: 209 deaths — a 73% year-on-year increase
- **Western Australia, South Australia**: also significant volumes (combined estimated ~550–600 for 2024–25 based on prior year data)

Nationally, VAD now accounts for approximately **2% of all Australian deaths** — including around **5% of cancer deaths** and an estimated **one in three Motor Neurone Disease deaths**. The ACT scheme (from November 2025) had its first deaths in early 2026.

The demographic profile is consistent across states: most VAD recipients are aged **70–79**, are **slightly more likely to be male**, and have a primary diagnosis of **cancer** (most common) or neurological disease (principally MND and Parkinson's Disease). In Victoria's 2024–25 cohort, 75% of applicants were already accessing palliative care when they first requested VAD — confirming that VAD and palliative care are complementary, not competing, end-of-life choices.

### 2. No Medicare items for VAD — the MBS exclusion

The most significant funding structural problem in Australia's VAD system is the absence of any dedicated Medicare Benefits Schedule (MBS) item.

The MBS General Explanatory Note GN.13.33 states:

> *"Euthanasia and any service directly related to the procedure does not attract Medicare benefits. However, services rendered for counselling and assessment about euthanasia will attract benefits."*

The practical consequence is that a coordinating practitioner conducting a formal VAD eligibility assessment — a structured, time-intensive, legally mandated clinical process — cannot bill a dedicated MBS item for that assessment. Individual practitioners are left to use their discretion about which general attendance items (if any) appropriately describe their work.

The most significant unremunerated time burden sits in activities that fall outside any plausible MBS claim:

- **Administrative and portal time**: Every state operates a mandatory digital portal (an "approved form" submission system overseen by the relevant Voluntary Assisted Dying Board). Completing these portal submissions is not a clinical service and attracts no MBS reimbursement.
- **Coordination and care navigation**: Liaising with consulting practitioners, specialists, pharmacies, aged care providers, and families.
- **Travel**: For practitioners serving regional or remote patients, travel time and costs are typically uncompensated.
- **Training completion**: The 6–9 hours of mandatory training required before a practitioner can provide any VAD service is unfunded.

In total, the coordinating practitioner's commitment to a single VAD patient from first request to death is estimated at **6–8 hours** — of which perhaps 1–2 hours may be claimable via general attendance MBS items for the direct clinical consultations, leaving 4–6 hours of work effectively unremunerated.

### 3. The VADANZ remuneration crisis — 63% of practitioners dissatisfied

The Voluntary Assisted Dying ANZ (VADANZ) 2025 Remuneration Survey is the most comprehensive data source on practitioner compensation in the VAD system. Conducted April–July 2025, with 170 respondents representing approximately 12% of the national VAD practitioner workforce, the survey found:

- **63%** of respondents felt their state's remuneration arrangements were inadequate
- **27%** reported providing unpaid VAD care
- Only **32%** received any state government reimbursement for their VAD work

The state-by-state variation in dissatisfaction was striking:

| State | Practitioner dissatisfaction |
|---|---|
| South Australia | 100% dissatisfied |
| Tasmania | 100% dissatisfied |
| Victoria | ~80% dissatisfied |
| Queensland | ~72% dissatisfied |
| New South Wales | ~49% dissatisfied (51% satisfied — only state where most practitioners felt adequately paid) |
| Western Australia | Significant dissatisfaction (precise figure not published separately) |

NSW's relative satisfaction appears to reflect a more developed state-based fee schedule and the shorter timeframe since scheme commencement (practitioner populations are self-selected early adopters still absorbing unremunerated costs, and the NSW scheme included more explicit funding provisions from the outset).

The survey's implications for service sustainability are serious. VADANZ's report identified a structural risk: the current VAD workforce is disproportionately composed of practitioners who absorbed financial losses as a personal commitment to patient rights. This cohort is not infinitely scalable as demand grows. As VADANZ stated in its January 2026 public statement: *"Without urgent reform to sustain voluntary assisted dying services, the system may not be able to keep up with demand."*

### 4. State-based reimbursement: patchwork solutions

In the absence of a Commonwealth MBS framework, states have developed ad hoc reimbursement arrangements that vary widely:

**Western Australia** has the most structured approach, with published **Practitioner Fee Guidelines** and the **Regional Access Support Scheme** — a fund that compensates practitioners for travel expenses and time when providing services to patients in regional and remote areas where no local VAD practitioner is available. The WA scheme also covers mandatory training costs in some circumstances.

**New South Wales** included practitioner payment provisions from the outset and has the highest satisfaction rate. The NSW government funds a centralised **VAD Care Navigator Service** which is free to patients and absorbs some of the coordination burden that would otherwise fall on individual practitioners.

**Victoria**, despite being the most experienced scheme, has the second-highest dissatisfaction rate — partly because the original 2019 framework predated recognition of the remuneration problem, and the state government has been slow to establish a catch-up fee schedule.

**South Australia and Tasmania** have effectively no systematic practitioner reimbursement beyond what can be claimed against the MBS for general consultation components — hence 100% dissatisfaction rates.

**Queensland** administers a **Queensland VAD Support and Pharmacy Service (QVAD-SPS)** which handles the storage and supply of VAD substances and provides some logistical support for practitioners, but does not systematically address the consultation remuneration gap.

### 5. The substance: state-funded, not PBS

The **VAD substance** — a compounded barbiturate preparation (typically pentobarbital-based, in proprietary state-specific formulations) — is provided **free of charge to approved patients** by state health authorities. This is a critical equity feature: unlike most medicines accessed through the healthcare system, there is no PBS co-payment and no risk of financial barrier to the substance itself.

Each state maintains an approved list of pharmacies authorised to compound and dispense VAD substances, and the substance is either dispensed to the patient for self-administration, or administered by a practitioner. The substance is **not PBS-listed** and is **not eligible for PBS subsidy** — it is funded entirely from state appropriations, outside the Commonwealth PBS framework.

The cost of the VAD substance is not publicly reported on a per-patient basis, but is understood to be several hundred dollars per prescription at compounding cost, fully absorbed by the state. This represents a deliberate policy choice: patients who qualify legally should not face a financial barrier to the substance once eligibility is confirmed.

### 6. The federal telehealth ban — a structural access block

The most legally complex dimension of VAD payment and access is a federal law problem with no MBS solution.

In November 2023, Federal Court Justice Wendy Abraham ruled that voluntary assisted dying constitutes **"suicide" under the Commonwealth Criminal Code**. The consequence: sections 474.29A and 474.29B of the **Criminal Code Act 1995** prohibit using a "carriage service" (telephone, video, email) to discuss or distribute "suicide-related material" — with penalties up to $220,000 in fines and potential criminal charges. This prohibition applies nationally, regardless of state VAD laws, and makes telehealth VAD eligibility consultations illegal across Australia.

The practical access consequences are severe:

- A terminally ill patient in a rural or remote area who cannot physically travel for multiple face-to-face VAD consultations is effectively denied access
- The Modified Monash Model areas (MM4–MM7) — home to approximately 7.7 million Australians — have the lowest density of trained VAD practitioners and the highest barriers to physical attendance
- Patients in the terminal phase of their illness, who may be physically incapable of repeated travel, must either be mobile enough to attend in person or go without access
- The 2026 Go Gentle Australia *State of VAD Report* identified the federal telehealth ban as among the **most significant barriers** to equitable VAD access nationally

The AMA has formally called on the Attorney-General to act, noting that an explicit legislative exemption for VAD from sections 474.29A-B would be a straightforward fix. As of July 2026, no such exemption has been enacted. The Albanese Government has been unwilling to legislate on VAD at the federal level, citing a political preference to leave VAD as a state matter — but the Criminal Code operates regardless of that preference.

### 7. Aged care — rights, duties, and the institutional objection problem

The intersection of VAD with residential aged care is one of the most contested payment and access dimensions of the system. As documented in Reports 025 and 026, approximately 54% of permanent residential aged care (RACF) residents have a dementia diagnosis, and dementia patients are among those with the most complex end-of-life care needs.

Under the **Aged Care Act 2024** and the **Aged Care Quality Standards** enforced by the ACQSC:
- Aged care residents have an **enforceable right** to choose VAD and have their choice respected
- Providers have an obligation to **inform residents** of their VAD rights and to **facilitate access** to VAD practitioners, even if the provider itself objects to participating
- Providers who choose not to participate must disclose this on their website, brochures, and intake documents
- Providers must allow practitioners to access residents on-site for all stages of the VAD assessment and administration process

In practice, the Go Gentle Australia 2025 Australian VAD Survey found **one in five respondents did not feel VAD was supported by healthcare facilities**. Specific reported issues include:
- Faith-based facilities (particularly Catholic and some Protestant organisations) refusing on-site VAD despite legal obligations
- Hospitals failing to facilitate practitioner access
- Aged care facilities discouraging residents from raising VAD

The ACQSC has jurisdiction to enforce provider obligations, but enforcement is reactive (complaint-based) and the regulator has limited inspection capacity across 636 providers. A NSW parliamentary proposal in late 2025 that would have made it easier for objecting residential facilities to prevent VAD on-site was defeated — maintaining the existing obligation on providers.

For dementia patients specifically, there is an additional clinical complexity: VAD requires **decision-making capacity** at the time of each assessment and at the time of requesting the substance. A person with advanced dementia who no longer has capacity cannot access VAD — unlike some European jurisdictions (Belgium, Netherlands) where advance directives for euthanasia are valid when capacity is later lost. This means the window for dementia patients to access VAD is the period of preserved capacity, typically in the early-to-moderate stages, which sits poorly with a prognosis-based eligibility system requiring death within 6–12 months.

## Tensions and Open Questions

**1. The Commonwealth-state architecture creates insoluble funding fragmentation.** VAD is state-legislated and state-administered, but the primary mechanism for funding medical practitioner time in Australia is the Commonwealth MBS. The two systems are incompatible: the MBS explicitly excludes VAD-related services beyond basic consultations, and the Commonwealth refuses to create dedicated items on the grounds that VAD is a state matter. The result is that practitioners bear the uncompensated coordination cost. Without federal action on MBS items or a structural Commonwealth-state transfer mechanism (analogous to the CPCiAC for palliative care), this will not resolve.

**2. The Criminal Code telehealth ban is a legal accident causing genuine harm.** The 2023 Federal Court ruling was not a deliberate policy choice to restrict VAD access — it was an interpretation of a criminal code provision designed to address online suicide promotion. The continued failure to pass a simple legislative exemption is a political choice that has material consequences for rural and remote patients with life-limiting illness. As VAD volume scales to 3,000+ deaths per year, this gap becomes harder to justify.

**3. Practitioner workforce sustainability is at risk.** The current ~1,400 trained VAD practitioners nationally are providing a service that costs them money to deliver. As VAD becomes an established part of the end-of-life care system and personal commitment to reform diminishes as a motivation, demand may outgrow a workforce that is effectively volunteering a portion of its time. The 48% year-on-year volume growth compounds this risk.

**4. The dementia-VAD eligibility mismatch is legally and ethically unresolved.** Australia's VAD laws require both terminal prognosis (6–12 months) and intact decision-making capacity. Dementia is progressively terminal and progressively strips capacity. The clinical consequence is that the window of joint eligibility (sufficient prognosis + sufficient capacity) may be narrow and unpredictable. The current system provides no mechanism for advance VAD directives — a gap that will intensify as Australia's dementia burden grows.

**5. The ACT's nurse practitioner model is a potential national reform lever.** The ACT's VAD Act (from November 2025) permits a nurse practitioner to serve as one of the two required assessors — the first Australian jurisdiction to take this approach. If nurse practitioners can expand the VAD workforce in the ACT with no documented safety deterioration, this provides an evidence base for other jurisdictions to follow. Given the 2025 psychology workforce data showing that allied health scope-of-practice expansion reliably increases access in constrained markets, the NP VAD model deserves close evaluation.

## Threads to Branch Into Next

1. **Specialist medical training pipeline: geriatricians, palliative medicine physicians, and geriatric psychiatry.** This thread has now been deferred from Reports 016, 022, 024, 025, and 026 — it is the series' most persistently deferred thread and connects directly to both palliative care and dementia care workforce gaps identified across the last four reports.

2. **Aged care quality and safety regulation under the Aged Care Act 2024 (ACQSC enforcement).** The new Act creates enforceable rights to palliative and end-of-life care, VAD access, and advance care planning — all of which require ACQSC to investigate complaints across 636 providers with limited inspection capacity. How the regulator is resourced and operationalised is largely unexamined.

3. **Advance care planning as a healthcare payment mechanism.** Research consistently shows that completed advance care plans reduce hospitalisation in the final year of life. The case for MBS-subsidised ACP as a cost-containment investment — not just an ethical measure — connects directly to both palliative care (Report 026) and VAD (this report), where advance directives are the missing link for dementia patients.

4. **Medicare Safety Net and the financial toxicity of multi-system OOP in late-life illness.** People with life-limiting conditions simultaneously face GP/specialist MBS gaps, PBS co-payments for symptom management medicines, and aged care co-contributions — creating cumulative out-of-pocket costs that have not been systematically studied. This thread has been flagged since Report 021.

5. **Private health insurance coverage in end-of-life care.** PHI covers hospital admission costs and some palliative care hospitalisation costs, but the interaction with community palliative care, Support at Home funding, and the uninsured costs of GP coordination in the final year of life is poorly understood. As PHI premiums reach their highest rate since 2018 (4.41% average increase in April 2026), what does PHI actually buy at end of life?
