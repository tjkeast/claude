# NDIS and Psychosocial Disability: The "Missing Middle", the $86,600 Average Plan, and the Race Against Time to Build Foundational Supports

**Report 017 — 2026-06-23**

**Summary:** Australia's psychosocial disability funding is bifurcated to the point of absurdity: 65,272 NDIS participants with mental illness receive an average of $86,600 per year in individualised supports, while an estimated 230,500–500,000 Australians with comparably severe conditions receive nothing — or a few thousand dollars through a patchwork of under-resourced programs. The Productivity Commission's November 2025 report declared the National Mental Health Agreement "not fit for purpose" and called on all governments to immediately build a pathway to meet this unmet need by 2030. The Federal Government's response — the NDIS Amendment (Securing the NDIS for Future Generations) Bill 2026 — introduces a new "foundational supports" funding stream but cuts participation support budgets from October 2026, while the replacement services remain undesigned, unfunded in practice, and not yet operational.

**Why this follows from yesterday:** Report 016 documented how specialist psychiatrists commonly charge $500–$1,000 upfront for a first appointment — a fee barrier that effectively excludes low-income Australians from clinical mental health care. The NDIS and its non-NDIS surroundings represent the non-clinical tier of mental health support: community services, recovery coaching, and daily living assistance that help people stay out of hospital. Understanding where that system fails — and for whom — is the direct complement to yesterday's analysis of clinical access barriers.

## Background

### What "Psychosocial Disability" Means in the NDIS Context

The NDIS uses the term "psychosocial disability" to describe the functional impairments arising from a mental health condition. It is not a diagnosis — it is a description of impact on daily life. A person may have schizophrenia, bipolar disorder, severe borderline personality disorder, or treatment-resistant depression; what matters for NDIS eligibility is whether the condition produces a *permanent or likely permanent* impairment that *substantially reduces* functional capacity in areas like self-care, social interaction, and community participation.

This "permanence" requirement is one of the structural tensions at the heart of psychosocial disability in the NDIS. Mental illness is episodic by nature: people can function well for months, then decompensate under stress, lose housing, or be admitted to hospital. Demonstrating that an episodic condition constitutes a "likely permanent" impairment requires evidence that not all clinicians are trained to compile — creating an access barrier at the front door of the scheme.

### The Pre-NDIS System and What Was Lost

Before the NDIS, psychosocial community support was delivered through state and territory mental health services and federally funded community mental health block grants. These services — social and community participation programs, supported accommodation, psychosocial rehabilitation — were modest but broad: they served large numbers of people at modest per-person cost.

When the NDIS launched from 2013, both Commonwealth and state governments progressively defunded these non-NDIS services, expecting the NDIS to absorb them. The problem was selection: the NDIS was designed for approximately 64,000 people with the most severe and permanent psychosocial disability. The remaining hundreds of thousands who had relied on block-grant services were left with nothing — the defunding had occurred faster than the NDIS could build capacity, and many simply did not meet the eligibility threshold.

## Key Findings

### 1. The NDIS Psychosocial Cohort: Who Gets In, and What It Costs

As at 30 June 2025, there were **65,272 active NDIS participants** with a primary psychosocial disability — 9% of total NDIS participants (761,442 at December 2025). This is roughly consistent with the original actuarial estimates of approximately 64,000 made when the NDIS was designed in the early 2010s.

Their average annual plan budget is **$86,600** (as at December 2024) — above the NDIS all-participant average of approximately $70,600 ($53.8 billion ÷ 761,442 participants). This reflects the high support intensity that severe, enduring mental illness typically requires: NDIS-funded psychosocial supports include:

- **Psychosocial Recovery Coaching** — a specialist role introduced in 2020 specifically for participants with psychosocial disability; coaches help with goal-setting, navigating services, building daily routines, and crisis prevention
- **Support workers** for daily tasks — cooking, cleaning, managing routines and medications, getting to appointments
- **Community participation supports** — funding to join social groups, maintain relationships, develop vocational skills
- **Support coordination** — navigating the NDIS plan itself, which for complex psychosocial needs is non-trivial
- **Supported Independent Living (SIL)** — shared or individual supported accommodation for those unable to live independently

Total NDIS spending on psychosocial disability was approximately **$4.25 billion in 2022–23** and has grown substantially since; at the 2025 average plan cost, the annual outlay exceeds **$5.6 billion**.

### 2. The Scale of What Was Not Built: 230,500–500,000 People with Unmet Need

The more damning number sits outside the NDIS. In August 2024, Australian Health and Mental Health Ministers released the *Final Report of the Analysis of Unmet Need for Psychosocial Supports Outside the NDIS* (commissioned following the 2022 NDIS Review). It found that in 2022–23, approximately **230,500 people aged 12–64 living with severe mental health challenges were unable to access any psychosocial supports** — not because they were fully recovered or had informal support, but because no funded services existed to serve them.

A broader estimate frequently used by advocacy organisations and the Productivity Commission is **500,000 Australians with moderate-to-severe mental illness** who fall outside the NDIS and do not have access to adequate psychosocial supports. The discrepancy between 230,500 and 500,000 reflects different thresholds for "severity" and "unmet need" — the 230,500 figure likely captures those with the most immediate and demonstrable need, while 500,000 includes those who would benefit from lower-intensity services they cannot access.

Either figure represents a profound policy failure. Even the conservative 230,500 is **3.5 times the NDIS psychosocial cohort size**.

### 3. The Commonwealth Psychosocial Support Program: A $136 Million Bridge for a $1+ Billion Gap

The Commonwealth Psychosocial Support Program (CPSP) is the federal government's primary non-NDIS psychosocial service. Funded through Primary Health Networks, it supports approximately **25,000 people** with severe mental illness who do not qualify for the NDIS. In the 2024–25 Mid-Year Economic and Fiscal Outlook (MYEFO), the Government extended the CPSP for two years to 30 June 2027, committing **$272.1 million** — approximately **$136 million per year** for 25,000 people, or **$5,440 per person per year**.

The funding intensity gulf is stark:

| Pathway | Participants | Govt expenditure | Per-person per year |
|---------|-------------|------------------|---------------------|
| NDIS (psychosocial) | 65,272 | ~$5.6 billion | **$86,600** |
| CPSP (non-NDIS) | ~25,000 | ~$136 million | **$5,440** |
| Ratio | — | — | **16:1** |

This is not simply a reflection of different severity levels; it reflects the structural failure to build a middle tier. People who narrowly miss NDIS eligibility may have equally debilitating conditions but receive support at one-sixteenth the funding intensity.

### 4. The Productivity Commission's November 2025 Verdict: "Not Fit for Purpose"

The Productivity Commission's *Mental Health and Suicide Prevention Agreement Review* final report was handed to government parties on 17 October 2025 and tabled in Parliament on **11 November 2025**. Its finding on psychosocial supports was unequivocal:

> State and territory governments should **immediately prioritise** addressing the gap in psychosocial supports outside the NDIS that is affecting approximately 500,000 people.

The report — 24 recommendations in total — found that the National Mental Health and Suicide Prevention Agreement, the key intergovernmental compact on mental health, was **"not fit for purpose"** and should be rewritten from scratch. The Commission called for:

- A **detailed national plan** to expand services and "fully meet this need by 2030"
- New **governance mechanisms** with defined roles for Commonwealth and states, and strong accountability
- Ongoing funding for psychosocial supports to be embedded in the **next national agreement**
- Co-design of any new policy architecture with people with lived experience

The Commission's framing was blunt: the problem is not unknown ("well known, but still shocking," as WAAMH summarised it), it has been documented in inquiry after inquiry since the original NDIS Review. What has been absent is coordinated action.

### 5. The 2026 NDIS Reform Package: Cuts Before a Safety Net Exists

The Federal Government's response came in the form of the **National Disability Insurance Scheme Amendment (Securing the NDIS for Future Generations) Bill 2026**, introduced to Parliament on 14 May 2026. The reform package has four pillars: quality services; clearer eligibility via standardised functional capacity assessments; community participation; and fraud prevention.

The fiscal headline is significant: the reforms are projected to reduce NDIS spending by **$37.8 billion over four years** against forecast trajectory — containing the scheme's growth to approximately **2% per year through 2030**, down from a trajectory tracking toward $53.8+ billion annually by 2025–26.

For psychosocial disability specifically, the reform's most immediate effect is a **cut to participation support budgets from 1 October 2026**. Community and social participation budgets — which for many psychosocial participants fund the support workers and group activities that sustain community connection and prevent hospitalisation — will be reduced from approximately **$33,000 to $26,000 per participant**, described by the government as a "reset to 2023 levels."

The structural danger is in the sequencing:

- **October 2026**: Participation support budget cuts begin
- **January 2028**: Standardised functional capacity assessments — the new eligibility framework — commence
- **By 2028–30**: Foundational Supports to be designed, agreed with states, and become operational

The government has committed **$3 billion over five years** for Foundational Supports (to be matched by states and territories, totalling approximately $6 billion), plus **$2 billion** for the Thriving Kids program and **$200 million** for an Inclusive Communities Fund. But as of mid-2026, Foundational Supports remain undesigned, unoperational, and unfunded at the service delivery level.

Disability advocates, mental health peak bodies, and the Grattan Institute have collectively raised the same concern: the NDIS is cutting before the safety net that is supposed to catch people exists. The Mental Health Coordinating Council specifically noted that "it will take time to rebuild the support services that were closed in NSW to fund the NDIS" — acknowledging that the original dismantling of the block-grant system is not easily reversed.

People with psychosocial disability are assessed by multiple advocacy organisations as bearing the highest risk from these cuts, precisely because their conditions are episodic and their community participation supports often constitute the difference between stable community living and a mental health crisis.

## Tensions and Open Questions

**1. Is $6 billion in Foundational Supports adequate?** If the government intends to close the gap for 230,500 people with unmet need, even at the very modest CPSP rate of $5,440/person/year, that implies an annual need of approximately $1.25 billion — and $600 million per year from a $3 billion Commonwealth allocation spread over five years falls short before state contributions. At anything approaching the per-person support intensity people actually need, the gap is enormous.

**2. Can functional capacity assessments handle episodic conditions?** The January 2028 shift to standardised functional capacity assessments is intended to create more consistent, evidence-based access determinations. But mental illness assessed on a good day looks very different from the same person assessed during a crisis period. The NDIS Review and multiple submissions have warned that standardised instruments must explicitly accommodate psychosocial disability's episodic and fluctuating nature, or the reforms will worsen access for this cohort rather than improve it.

**3. Who funds the transition period?** Between October 2026 (cuts) and 2028–30 (Foundational Supports operational), what happens to participants who lose participation support budgets and exit the NDIS? No interim support pathway has been confirmed. "No one should leave the NDIS without somewhere to go" was the headline from the Community Mental Health Australia submission — but as of June 2026 there is nowhere to go.

**4. State willingness to co-fund.** The $3 billion Commonwealth commitment assumes states match contributions. Mental health funding is a state responsibility in Australia's federated system, but states have been defunding community mental health services for a decade — that funding went to the NDIS. Rebuilding it requires political will in state budget cycles that are separately constrained. There is no binding mechanism requiring states to match the Commonwealth's commitment in any defined timeframe.

**5. Provider market viability.** The psychosocial support sector — recovery coaches, community participation organisations, supported accommodation providers — has grown substantially to serve NDIS participants. If budgets are cut and participants exit the scheme, provider viability becomes uncertain. Rebuilding a sector that has collapsed is considerably harder and more expensive than maintaining one.
