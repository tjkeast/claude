# Better Access and the Mental Health Rebate: Australia's Subsidised Psychology System — Costs, Caps, and the Equity Crisis

**Report 011 — 2026-06-16**

**Summary:** The Better Access initiative — Australia's primary mechanism for subsidising psychological therapy through Medicare — costs over $1.6 billion per year in MBS benefits, serves roughly 1.3 million Australians annually, and remains deeply contested: it is simultaneously the backbone of Australia's community mental health response and an inequitable system that largely benefits the already-advantaged, charges median out-of-pocket costs of ~$92 per session, and faces a projected 96.6% psychology workforce shortfall by 2038.

**Why this follows from yesterday:** Report 010 documented how MyMedicare is being used to drive continuity of care reform in general practice — and the November 2025 Better Access changes directly tethered GP-issued Mental Health Treatment Plans to the MyMedicare registration system, making mental healthcare a key test case for whether that infrastructure can serve all Australians equitably.

---

## Background

### The Program: What Better Access Is

Launched in November 2006 as part of the Howard Government's Mental Health package, the **Better Access to Psychiatrists, Psychologists and General Practitioners through the Medicare Benefits Schedule** initiative — universally known as **Better Access** — was the most significant expansion of Medicare's allied health coverage in the scheme's history.

The core design: a GP (or eligible psychiatrist or paediatrician) prepares a **Mental Health Treatment Plan (MHTP)** for a patient with a clinically diagnosed mental disorder. The MHTP gives the patient access to a capped number of MBS-subsidised sessions with an allied health professional — primarily psychologists, but also social workers and occupational therapists. The patient pays the provider directly; Medicare reimburses part of the cost.

For the past two decades, the GP has acted as the **gatekeeper** to psychological care: without an MHTP from a GP (or psychiatrist), the Medicare subsidy does not apply. This gatekeeper role has been both the program's greatest strength (it embeds mental healthcare in the primary care relationship, where most health needs are first identified) and its most significant structural weakness (it creates a double barrier — patients must first find and afford a GP, then find and afford a psychologist).

### The MBS Fee Structure

Under Better Access as of 1 July 2025, the key MBS items and rebates for psychology are:

| MBS Item | Description | Rebate (from 1 July 2025) | Provider |
|----------|-------------|--------------------------|----------|
| **80010** | Psychological therapy — individual session ≥50 min | **$145.25** | Clinical psychologists (Psychology Board endorsement required) |
| **80110** | Focused psychological strategies — individual session ≥50 min | **$98.95** | General (non-endorsed) psychologists, OTs, social workers |
| **80020** | Psychological therapy — group session | Lower rate | Clinical psychologists |
| **80120** | Focused psychological strategies — group session | Lower rate | General psychologists |

The distinction between **clinical** and **general** psychologists is important: clinical psychologists hold an additional Practice Endorsement from the Psychology Board of Australia and typically work with more complex presentations. The rebate differential ($145.25 vs $98.95) reflects this specialisation distinction, but it is contentious — both rates fall far short of market fees.

By comparison, the **Australian Psychological Society's recommended fee for 2025–26** is **$318 for a standard 46–60 minute consultation**. This reflects the true cost of practice: time for clinical notes, correspondence, supervision, indemnity insurance, practice administration, and overheads.

The arithmetic is stark:
- **Clinical psychologist gap:** $318 − $145.25 = **~$172 out-of-pocket per session**
- **General psychologist gap:** $318 − $98.95 = **~$219 out-of-pocket per session** (using APS fee as proxy)

## Key Findings

### 1. Scale and Expenditure

Better Access has become the largest single funding mechanism for mental health treatment in Australia outside hospital-based care:

- Approximately **1.3 million Australians** access therapy through Better Access each year
- Medicare-subsidised mental health MBS spending reached **over $1.6 billion per annum** (2024–25)
- **Psychologists** — including both clinical and general — accounted for **48% of national spending** on mental health-related MBS services, followed by psychiatrists (30%)
- Service volumes have grown substantially since 2006, with the COVID-19 pandemic accelerating demand and sustaining a higher utilisation base that persists in 2026

This scale makes Better Access a major Commonwealth fiscal commitment and explains why changes to the session cap (explored below) carry both significant budget implications and significant consequences for patient welfare.

### 2. The Session Cap: A Contested History

The most politically charged feature of Better Access has always been the **annual cap on subsidised sessions**.

**Original design (2006–2019):** 10 individual + 10 group sessions per calendar year. This was informed by the evidence base for short-term cognitive-behavioural therapies: most evidence-supported treatments for depression and anxiety can be delivered in 6–12 sessions.

**COVID expansion (2020–2022):** The Morrison Government temporarily doubled the individual session allowance to **20 sessions per calendar year** from 2020, as part of the pandemic mental health response. The additional 10 sessions were framed as a temporary measure for an extraordinary period of demand.

**The 2022 decision:** The independent **University of Melbourne evaluation of Better Access** (commissioned 2021, final report December 2022) delivered 16 recommendations. Its only recommendation not to be supported by the incoming Labor Government was the one recommending the permanent retention of 20 sessions. Health Minister Mark Butler announced in early 2023 that the temporary extension would not be renewed: **the cap reverted to 10 sessions from 1 January 2023**.

The decision was immediately controversial. Mental health advocates, the Australian Psychological Society, and thousands of patients argued that the cut would force people with chronic conditions — who often need more than 10 sessions annually — to pay full private fees or discontinue treatment. A public petition gathered significant signatures. The Minister's position was that the evaluation found the additional sessions had not produced measurable population-level mental health improvements and that the program's resources needed to be directed more equitably.

**The 2025 election and the 20-session debate resurfaced:** The Coalition — in opposition after the 2022 election and going into the May 2025 federal election — announced it would restore the 20-session cap if elected. Independent academic analysis of this proposal, published in March 2025, raised significant concerns:

- During the COVID period when 20 sessions were available, **most patients used 10 or fewer sessions**; the extra sessions primarily benefited patients already engaged in longer-term therapy
- Some evidence suggested the additional sessions *prevented new patients from accessing services*, as psychologists' books filled with existing patients receiving additional funded sessions rather than new referrals opening
- Researchers argued the more pressing reform is **needs-based access**: the number of sessions should be determined by clinical assessment rather than an arbitrary annual cap, applying equally at 5, 10, 15, or 20 sessions depending on the patient

As of June 2026, the **cap remains at 10 individual sessions + 10 group sessions per calendar year** under the returned Labor government.

### 3. The Out-of-Pocket Cost Crisis

The gap between the MBS rebate and market fees for psychology has produced a significant **out-of-pocket access barrier**:

- The **median out-of-pocket cost** per session increased from $81.41 in 2022 to **$92.04 in 2023** (most recent published data)
- **74.5% of Better Access treatment services** involve a co-payment — bulk billing by psychologists is now the exception, not the rule
- At ~$92 per session, a patient using all 10 annual subsidised sessions faces **~$920 in annual out-of-pocket costs** just from the funded sessions, before any unsubsidised sessions

**Why bulk billing has collapsed for psychology:** The economics are straightforward. At the APS recommended fee of $318, bulk billing (i.e., accepting the Medicare rebate of $145.25 for a clinical psychologist) means absorbing a ~$173 reduction per session. For a psychologist seeing 30 sessions per week, that represents foregone revenue of ~$5,190 per week, or ~$270,000 per year — the equivalent of a full salary. Unlike GPs, psychologists receive no Bulk Billing Practice Incentive Program (BBPIP) loading for bulk billing mental health sessions.

The result is a two-tier system: patients with the capacity to pay the gap fee access psychological therapy; patients without that capacity are either forced to use lower-quality bulk-billed services (where available), defer treatment, or access free-but-limited alternatives such as Headspace (for young people aged 12–25) or Medicare Mental Health Centres.

### 4. The Equity Problem

The 2022 University of Melbourne evaluation was explicit in identifying **systemic equity failures** in Better Access:

- Better Access disproportionately serves **higher-income, metropolitan, educated Australians** — the demographic most likely to afford the gap fee, most likely to have a regular GP who prepares MHTPs, and most likely to live in areas where psychologists practise
- People on **low incomes** were less likely to access treatment despite experiencing **higher rates of psychological distress** — the inverse of what an equitable system would produce
- **Rural and remote Australians** face the combined barriers of geography (psychologist availability) and cost (gap fees)
- **Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander people** remain underrepresented in Better Access utilisation despite facing substantially higher rates of mental health burden
- The 2022 evaluation found **32% of people who ceased treatment early** did so for reasons directly or indirectly linked to cost and access barriers

The equity finding is particularly pointed given Better Access was explicitly designed as a universal Medicare entitlement — the implicit promise was that anyone with a mental disorder could access subsidised therapy regardless of income. The rebate structure has not delivered on that promise.

### 5. The November 2025 Reforms: Continuity Over Volume

On 1 November 2025, the Federal Government implemented changes to Better Access that flowed directly from the 2022 evaluation's recommendations (excluding the session cap recommendation). The reforms focused on **continuity of care rather than volume**:

**GP billing streamlining:** Dedicated MBS items for MHTP review and mental health consultation were removed from the GP schedule. GPs now use general attendance items (the standard MBS consultation framework) to prepare and review MHTPs, rather than separate mental health-specific billing codes. The intent was to reduce administrative complexity and integrate mental health management into the standard care relationship.

**MyMedicare linkage:** From 1 November 2025, the preparation of an MHTP, referral for treatment, and MHTP reviews must be linked to a patient's **MyMedicare registered practice** (or their usual GP where MyMedicare is not used). Telehealth-based MHTPs must be conducted by the patient's MyMedicare GP or usual doctor — eliminating the previous arrangement where patients could access telehealth MHTP services from any provider.

This directly ties Report 010's MyMedicare architecture to mental healthcare: the same continuity-of-care logic that governs chronic condition management now governs the access gateway to psychological therapy. For the 90% of Australians not yet enrolled in MyMedicare (as at June 2025), the concept of a "usual GP" applies instead — maintaining access for non-enrolled patients while reinforcing the goal of relational care.

**Transition arrangements:** MHTP referrals prepared before 1 November 2025 remained valid until the treatment services specified were delivered, providing continuity for patients mid-course.

### 6. The Workforce Crisis Underlying Everything

No analysis of Better Access is complete without confronting the psychology **workforce shortage**, which is now the binding constraint on the program's ability to deliver on its promises regardless of rebate levels or session caps.

The April 2026 **Psychology Supply and Demand Compendium Report** (Department of Health, Disability and Ageing) provides the most comprehensive projection to date:

- **Current shortage (2025):** 10,269.9 FTE — a **57.3% undersupply** relative to estimated need
- **Projected shortage (2038):** 24,115.5 FTE — a **96.6% undersupply** if current trends continue
- The ageing psychology workforce compounds the problem: the proportion of FTE hours contributed by psychologists aged under 30 is projected to fall from 13.7% to 9.3%, and those aged 30–39 from 28.1% to 23.9%, between 2025 and 2038
- **Rural concentration:** The shortage is most acute in non-metropolitan areas, where psychologists are among the rarest specialist providers

The supply shortage produces a cruel paradox: even when Australians have a funded referral, wait times for psychology appointments can stretch for months, particularly outside metropolitan centres. Increasing the session cap to 20 would, in this supply-constrained environment, simply allow existing patients to extend their therapy while locking out new patients waiting for a first appointment.

The government has responded with training reforms: the Psychology Board of Australia is replacing the existing training pathways with a **single integrated five-year program from 2026**, designed to reduce training bottlenecks, embed practical placements earlier, remove the national psychology exam for domestic graduates, and create a potential new "psychology assistant" exit point at three years — widening the available workforce at a sub-specialist level.

### 7. The "Missing Middle": Beyond Better Access

Better Access is calibrated for people with mild-to-moderate mental health conditions — the target population for evidence-based short-term therapy. The **Productivity Commission's November 2025 report** on the Mental Health and Suicide Prevention Agreement exposed the system's failure for a different and larger cohort:

An estimated **500,000 Australians** live with moderate-to-severe mental illness but do not qualify for the NDIS (which was designed for only the ~64,000 most severely affected) and cannot be adequately served by Better Access alone. These patients fall into the "missing middle" — too unwell for the GP-psychologist primary care model, but not acutely unwell enough to access hospital-based services.

The Productivity Commission made 24 recommendations, with the most urgent being:
- Immediate prioritisation of **psychosocial supports** (non-clinical community services enabling independent living) outside the NDIS
- Explicit inclusion of psychosocial support funding in the next Mental Health and Suicide Prevention Agreement between Commonwealth and states/territories
- Reforms to improve coordination between the NDIS, hospital services, and community mental health

The report framed the situation as "well known, but still shocking" — a system failure that is extensively documented but has not been corrected.

## Tensions and Open Questions

**1. The rebate adequacy problem has no easy solution.** Raising the psychology rebate enough to make bulk billing viable would cost hundreds of millions annually. Without it, the out-of-pocket barrier persists. With it, the budget impact rivals the BBPIP in scale. The government has not announced plans to significantly increase the Better Access rebate.

**2. Session cap vs. clinical need.** The 10-session cap imposes an administrative constraint on clinical care. The evidence-based argument for 10 sessions works for mild-to-moderate depression and anxiety but breaks down for complex trauma, personality disorders, and chronic conditions. The evaluation's recommendation for needs-based rather than capped access remains unimplemented.

**3. GP gatekeeping creates compounding barriers.** To access Better Access, a patient must (1) have a GP, (2) attend a GP, (3) receive an MHTP, (4) obtain a referral to a psychologist, (5) find a psychologist who is accepting new patients, and (6) afford the gap fee. Each step is a potential dropout point. The system design selects for the most resourced patients at every stage.

**4. MyMedicare linkage: continuity gain, access risk.** Tethering MHTPs to the MyMedicare practice follows the continuity logic of Report 010's reforms — but the evidence from international patient enrolment models shows that continuity-driven designs can **reduce access** in capacity-constrained settings. If a patient's registered practice has limited mental health expertise, the linkage may push them toward a telehealth MHTP from a practitioner who knows them less well than a specialist.

**5. The workforce shortage makes every debate about session numbers partially moot.** A system running at a 57% undersupply of psychologists cannot simply absorb the demand that full equity and access would generate. Until the supply pipeline is fundamentally addressed, debates about 10 vs. 20 sessions are secondary to the question of whether patients can access even one session in a reasonable timeframe.

## Threads to Branch Into Next

1. **Rural and remote healthcare payments and the Modified Monash Model** — Flagged in Reports 009 and 010. How are GP, psychology, and other health services funded in rural and remote Australia? The Modified Monash Model (MMM) classifies geographic remoteness for payment purposes; incentive payments designed to attract providers to underserved areas are the direct policy response to the GP and psychology deserts documented in this report.

2. **Headspace and youth mental health funding** — Headspace centres are the non-Medicare funding stream for youth mental health (12–25 years), funded through PHNs with Commonwealth block grants rather than MBS fee-for-service. How does the Headspace model interact with Better Access? What does it cost per person served? Is it effective?

3. **Medical Costs Finder Bill 2026 and specialist fee transparency** — First flagged in Report 002 and repeatedly deferred. The Bill requires specialists to publish fees before appointments; the psychology gap fee crisis described in this report is the allied health analogue to the specialist out-of-pocket problem the Bill addresses. How far has it progressed?

4. **NDIS and psychosocial disability** — The Productivity Commission's November 2025 report identified 500,000 Australians in the "missing middle". The NDIS is the intended funding mechanism for the most severely affected. How does NDIS psychology/psychosocial support funding work? What are the cost and access dynamics?

5. **US trade pressure on PBS and Medicare pricing** — Deferred from Reports 007, 008, 009, and 010. With Australia's total health spend at $270.5 billion and the Medicare MBS a key target of US pharmaceutical and healthcare industry trade advocacy, this thread has grown more urgent as bilateral trade negotiations continue.
