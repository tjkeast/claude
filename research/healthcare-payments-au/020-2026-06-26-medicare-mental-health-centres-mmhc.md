# Medicare Mental Health Centres: Australia's Free Walk-In Mental Health Model — Block Grants, the Missing Middle, and the 92-Centre Ambition

**Report 020 — 2026-06-26**

**Summary:** Medicare Mental Health Centres (MMHCs) — rebranded from "Head to Health" in March 2025 — are Australia's network of free, walk-in, block-grant-funded mental health hubs designed to serve the "missing middle" between GP primary care and state-funded specialist services. Sixty-one centres are operational as of 30 June 2026, with a government target of 92 by 2027. The model is genuinely distinctive: no referral, no gap fee, no Medicare card needed, no session cap, no MBS billing — just salaried multidisciplinary teams. The challenges are equally distinctive: patchy coverage, limited evaluation data, a rebranding that confused the sector, and a fundamental tension between the walk-in model and a population that often cannot self-navigate to services.

**Why this follows from yesterday:** Report 019 documented CAMHS as Australia's chronically underfunded specialist tier for severe youth mental illness, with 100-day wait times, less than 1% coverage of under-18s, and no national data framework. The Albanese Government explicitly positioned MMHCs — and the parallel Medicare Mental Health Kids Hubs — as a new middle tier that absorbs demand below the CAMHS threshold. Whether this ambition is matched by design and resourcing is the subject of today's report.

---

## Background

Australia's mental health system has long been described as fragmented between a Commonwealth-funded primary care tier (Better Access, MBS psychology) and state-funded specialist services (CAMHS, public psychiatric units). The "missing middle" — people with complex, chronic, or episodic mental illness who need more than 10 MBS psychology sessions but do not meet the threshold for state inpatient or acute community services — has been a recognised policy gap since at least the 2011 National Mental Health Report.

The Head to Health initiative, launched under the Morrison Government in 2017 as an online information portal, was progressively expanded under the Albanese Government from 2022 onward into a network of physical walk-in centres. In the 2023–24 Budget, the government committed **$586.9 million** for mental health initiatives including the expansion of Head to Health centres to underserved communities.

On **31 March 2025**, the entire program was rebranded from "Head to Health" to **"Medicare Mental Health"**, explicitly connecting the centres to the Medicare brand and to the government's Strengthening Medicare agenda. The rebranding was accompanied by a new public website (medicarementalhealth.gov.au) and communications campaign — though it created temporary confusion within the sector about what, if anything, had changed in the service model.

## Key Findings

### 1. The Model: Block-Funded, Free, Walk-In — No MBS Billing

Despite the "Medicare" brand, MMHCs do **not** operate through the Medicare Benefits Schedule. Services are not billed item-by-item; there are no Medicare rebates and no gap fees. Centres are funded through **Commonwealth block grants**, with salaried multidisciplinary staff. This is the same structural logic as headspace (Report 015) — block-funded through Primary Health Networks — but with a broader target population (all ages rather than 12–25) and an explicitly more complex clinical cohort.

Key access features:
- **Free** at point of care — no cost to users
- **No referral required** — no GP Mental Health Treatment Plan needed
- **No Medicare card required** — accessible to people without Medicare enrolment, including those experiencing homelessness or new arrivals
- **Walk-in access** — no appointment needed (though some centres now accept appointments)
- **No session cap** — unlike Better Access, where MBS psychology is capped at 10 sessions per calendar year

The clinical model is multidisciplinary: psychologists (including provisional psychologists), social workers, peer workers, and integrated mental health workers form the core team, with **on-call access to psychiatrists and psychologists** funded from 2024–25. At two centres — **Midland (WA)** and **Darwin (NT)** — integrated vocational/employment support has been piloted as an optional co-located model, but as of mid-2026 only 2 of 61 centres deliver this component.

### 2. Rollout: 61 Centres Operational, 92 Targeted

As of **30 June 2026**, **61 Medicare Mental Health Centres** are fully operational nationally, having reached that milestone by the government's stated deadline. A parallel network of **17 Medicare Mental Health Kids Hubs** serves children (distinct service model from the adult centres, and the primary vehicle for Commonwealth engagement with younger cohorts below the CAMHS threshold). 

The government's medium-term target is **92 centres by 2027**, with a longer-term aspiration — stated in policy documents but not funded to that scale — that 4 in 5 Australians would live within a 20-minute drive of a Medicare Mental Health service.

State distribution remains uneven. The bulk of confirmed centres are in south-east Queensland, NSW, Victoria, and the ACT. Coverage in South Australia, Western Australia, and especially the Northern Territory remains thin, despite the NT having among the highest rates of mental health comorbidity and the lowest access to MBS-based mental health services nationally (as documented in the rural and remote context of Report 012).

More than **57% of clients** accessing MMHCs have been women — broadly consistent with the gender pattern in Better Access (Report 011) and mental health services generally, though adult men remain harder to engage through any voluntary mental health service.

### 3. Funding Trajectory: $586.9M → $361M → $78M

The funding profile across successive budgets tells an important story:

| Budget | MMHC-relevant allocation | Notes |
|--------|--------------------------|-------|
| 2023–24 | $586.9 million (mental health, including centre expansion) | Broad package; not disaggregated to centres alone |
| 2024–25 | $361.0 million (4 years), including $29.9M for on-call psychiatry/psychology | Major clinical upgrade |
| 2026–27 | **$78 million** | Narrow continuity funding; no structural expansion |

The 2026–27 Budget allocation of **$78 million** for MMHC operations raised concern among peak bodies: it represents continuation without expansion, while the government had separately committed **$225 million for 31 new or upgraded centres** as part of a broader **$1 billion election commitment** that also includes $500 million for 20 Youth Specialist Care Centres and $200+ million for 58 new or expanded headspace services. The election commitment pre-dates the 2026–27 Budget; it is not clear which of these elements have been formally funded versus announced as future commitments.

The **National Mental Health Commission** welcomed the budget continuity but cautioned that ongoing funding "underscores the need to define what comes next" — signalling that the sector views $78 million as a floor, not a ceiling.

### 4. The "Missing Middle" Fit — and Its Limits

MMHCs were explicitly designed to serve people who fall between:
- The **mild-to-moderate** tier served by Better Access (10 MBS psychology sessions, gap fees averaging $92/session)
- The **acute/severe** tier requiring state CAMHS or public psychiatric inpatient admission

The target population includes people with **personality disorders, eating disorders, and early psychosis** — conditions that overwhelm a 10-session MBS cap but typically do not meet acute hospitalisation criteria. The walk-in, no-referral, no-fee model is designed to remove the access friction that causes this group to fall through the gap: no need for a GP MHTP, no gap fees, no appointment waiting time in the acute moment.

However, three tensions complicate the fit:

**First**, the missing middle is not necessarily a walk-in population. People with severe personality disorders, complex trauma, eating disorders, or early psychosis frequently cannot self-navigate to a walk-in service in a crisis. The model assumes a degree of autonomous service-seeking that may not match the population's capacity. The lack of formal outreach or assertive community treatment (ACT) components in the current MMHC model is an identified gap.

**Second**, the block-grant structure means there is **no systematic national outcome data** — no MBS claims trail, no per-episode billing data that would allow analysis of who is being served, for how long, with what result. The **Black Dog Institute** (June 2026) explicitly called for a national standard of care across all MMHCs, and for evaluation and research to be embedded in every centre and funded from the $1 billion commitment. Without this, the program could scale to 92 centres without generating the evidence base needed to know whether it works.

**Third**, the scope of the missing middle — estimated at **500,000 Australians** with moderate-to-severe mental illness not qualifying for NDIS and inadequately served by Better Access — vastly exceeds what 61 (or even 92) centres can absorb. The Commonwealth Psychosocial Support Program (CPSP), which provides ~$5,440/person/year for ~25,000 people (Report 017), addresses one slice. MMHCs address another. Neither, alone or together, closes the gap.

### 5. Kids Hubs: The CAMHS Adjacent Tier

The 17 **Medicare Mental Health Kids Hubs** represent the most direct response to the CAMHS pressure described in Report 019. Unlike the adult MMHCs, Kids Hubs are explicitly positioned as serving children and adolescents with complex mental health needs below the threshold for state CAMHS admission.

The **Royal Australian and New Zealand College of Psychiatrists (RANZCP)** has recommended **$6 million annually** to embed child and adolescent psychiatrists in Medicare Mental Health Kids Hubs — currently, psychiatrist access in Kids Hubs relies on the same on-call telehealth model as adult centres. Given that child and adolescent psychiatry is among the most acute shortage subspecialties in Australia (56% demand-meet nationally, Report 019), the on-call model may reflect workforce reality rather than design preference.

### 6. Relationship to Other Tiers

| Tier | Service | Funding model | Age range | Cost to user | Referral? |
|------|---------|---------------|-----------|--------------|-----------|
| MMHC | Medicare Mental Health Centre | Block grant | All ages (adult-focused) | $0 | No |
| Kids Hub | Medicare Mental Health Kids Hub | Block grant | Children/adolescents | $0 | No |
| headspace | headspace centres | Block grant (PHN) | 12–25 | $0 | No |
| Better Access | MBS psychology/psychiatry | MBS fee-for-service | All ages | ~$92/session gap | Yes (GP MHTP) |
| CAMHS | State specialist services | State budget | Under 18 | $0 (public) | Yes |
| NDIS | Psychosocial disability supports | NDIS participant plans | Under 65 | $0 | NDIS eligibility |

The overlapping and partially competing layers — MMHCs, headspace, Better Access, and CAMHS — reflect the longstanding failure to design a coherent mental health system, instead layering programs onto one another across funding silos.

## Tensions and Open Questions

**1. Evidence base is near-absent.** The program has been operating since 2017 in some form, expanded significantly from 2022, and rebranded in 2025 — yet as of mid-2026, there are no published clinical outcome evaluations. The Black Dog Institute's call for embedded research signals that the sector knows this is a vulnerability.

**2. The rebranding cost.** Sector stakeholders and practitioners reported confusion following the March 2025 "Head to Health → Medicare Mental Health" transition. Referral pathways had been built around the Head to Health brand; patients and GPs had learned to navigate to those services. Rebranding without parallel clinical model change risks disrupting established pathways without clinical benefit.

**3. Workforce for 92 centres.** The government's target of 92 centres by 2027 requires a substantial increase in salaried multidisciplinary workforce at a moment when psychologists (57.3% FTE undersupply nationally), social workers, and psychiatrists are all in shortage. The block-grant funding model does not create the same supply-side incentives as MBS fee-for-service, which at least allows private practitioners to earn market-rate income.

**4. The $78M vs. $1B gap.** The 2026–27 Budget allocated $78 million for MMHC operations, while the government's election commitment totals $1 billion including $225M for 31 new centres. The relationship between these numbers — what is funded, what is promised, what is the timeline — is not publicly clear. The sector is watching closely.

**5. CAMHS overflow without CAMHS capacity.** MMHCs and Kids Hubs are intended to absorb demand that would otherwise reach state CAMHS. But if CAMHS continues to deteriorate (Report 019: 1 of 32 WA reforms implemented; 100-day wait times; 312 beds nationally), the pressure on MMHCs may increase beyond their designed scope.

## Threads to Branch Into Next

1. **Medicare Mental Health Kids Hubs in depth** — a focused report on the 17 Kids Hubs, their clinical model, workforce composition, and whether they are generating referral pathways that reduce CAMHS pressure; may warrant dedicated research given the CAMHS context.

2. **HTA Reform and the 2026–27 Budget failure** — the HTA Implementation Advisory Group's recommendations for faster PBS access pathways remain unfunded; compounds MFN-driven market de-prioritisation (Report 018) and the gene therapy and rare disease gaps (Reports 003–006); this thread has been deferred repeatedly.

3. **Aged care and the NDIS interface** — NDIS exit at age 65; psychosocial disability in older adults; the My Aged Care system and AN-ACC funding model; the only Report 001 thread entirely uncovered.

4. **Youth Specialist Care Centres (YSCCs)** — the $500 million arm of the $1B mental health commitment; 20 centres planned; designed for young people needing intensive sub-acute or early psychosis care above MMHC but below inpatient; rollout unclear as of mid-2026.

5. **Medicare Mental Health Check In (low-intensity CBT)** — new MBS item enabling GPs and nurses to deliver brief CBT-based interventions; different to MMHCs but part of the same Strengthening Medicare mental health suite; UNSW research (April 2026) examining uptake.
