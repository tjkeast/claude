# Aged Care and the NDIS Interface: Australia's $36 Billion System at a Turning Point — the Aged Care Act 2024, Support at Home, and the Age-65 Cliff

**Report 021 — 2026-06-27**

**Summary:** Australia's aged care system — encompassing residential facilities and home care — cost $36.4 billion in government expenditure in 2023–24, a 64% real increase over six years driven by the Royal Commission's reform agenda and a rapidly ageing population. Since 1 November 2025, an entirely new legislative and funding architecture has been in place: the Aged Care Act 2024 replaced the 1997 Act, and the Support at Home program replaced the Home Care Packages system with eight funding tiers and free clinical care. But the Inspector-General of Aged Care's September 2025 assessment found the sector's promised transformation "not yet achieved" — and the structural friction at the boundary between aged care and the NDIS remains a source of systemic failure for Australians with disability who age into the system.

**Why this follows from yesterday:** Report 020 documented the NDIS psychosocial disability "missing middle" — an estimated 500,000 Australians underserved by both the NDIS and Medicare's primary care tier. The NDIS-aged care boundary at age 65 is where that unmet need becomes structurally encoded: when NDIS participants with lifelong disability cross 65 and enter aged care, they transition from a needs-based entitlement to a means-tested, rationed service system with fundamentally different care logics. This has been the only major thread from Report 001's overview of Australian health financing that the series has not yet explored.

---

## Background

The Royal Commission into Aged Care Quality and Safety — which ran from 2018 to 2021 and delivered its final report in February 2021 — found Australia's aged care system to be chronically underfunded, understaffed, and structurally unfit for a rights-based conception of care. The Commission issued 148 recommendations and called for a fundamental transformation of the system — a phrase that became the yardstick against which every subsequent reform has been measured.

The Australian Government committed approximately **$17.7 billion over five years** in the 2021–22 Budget in direct response to the Royal Commission, with further investments in subsequent budgets. Between 2017–18 and 2023–24, **total government aged care expenditure grew from $22.1 billion to $36.4 billion** — a 64% increase in nominal terms.

This is not primarily a story of policy success. It is a story of how much money is required to move a large, complex, workforce-dependent service system from a low baseline — and how far that investment still falls short of the transformation envisaged.

## Key Findings

### 1. The Architecture: Three Tiers of Publicly Funded Aged Care

Australia's publicly funded aged care system has three main service streams:

**Residential aged care** (nursing homes and aged care homes): For people who can no longer live independently. The Commonwealth funds care costs via the AN-ACC subsidy; accommodation is separately charged via Refundable Accommodation Deposits (RADs) or daily payments; means-tested fees apply to ongoing care.

**Home care / Support at Home** (from 1 November 2025): For people living at home who need assistance. The Support at Home program (SaH) replaced the Home Care Packages (HCP) Program and the Short-Term Restorative Care Programme from 1 November 2025. The older Commonwealth Home Support Programme (CHSP) — a lower-intensity, lower-cost entry point — has been extended to 30 June 2027 and will transition into Support at Home no earlier than that date.

**My Aged Care** (assessment portal): The single entry point for all Commonwealth-funded aged care. Since the Aged Care Act 2024 commenced on 1 November 2025, assessment for any Commonwealth-funded aged care service now requires being aged 65 or over, or being an Aboriginal or Torres Strait Islander person aged 50 or over, or being homeless or at risk of homelessness.

### 2. Residential Aged Care: AN-ACC Replaces ACFI

Since 1 October 2022, residential aged care has been funded through the **Australian National Aged Care Classification (AN-ACC)** model, replacing the discredited Aged Care Funding Instrument (ACFI). AN-ACC classifies each resident into one of **13 care classes** based on clinical assessment, determining the daily care subsidy.

Key funding parameters (as at October 2025):
- **AN-ACC base price**: $295.64 per resident per day (increased from $282.44 on 1 October 2025)
- **Hotelling supplement** (covering cleaning, catering, laundry): $22.15 per resident per day (up from $15.60 from 20 September 2025 — reflecting IHACPA pricing advice)
- **AN-ACC Transition Fund**: available until 30 September 2026 to support providers in thin markets

From 1 October 2025, a **care minutes funding linkage** was introduced: non-specialised aged care homes in metropolitan areas must meet care minute targets to access full funding, with reporting now backed by mandatory external audit via a new Care Minutes Performance Statement.

**Mandatory care minutes**: 215 minutes of direct care per resident per day (sector-wide average), of which 44 minutes must be delivered by a **registered nurse**. A 24/7 registered nurse on-site requirement applies to all residential facilities. Meeting or exceeding these targets now determines Star Rating outcomes (3–5 stars for compliance; 1 star for failure to meet 90% of either metric).

**Accommodation payments** for residential care: residents not eligible for government accommodation support may pay up to $750,000 as a Refundable Accommodation Deposit (RAD) — indexed to CPI from 1 July 2025. Under the Aged Care Act 2024, RADs are now subject to **retention**: 2% per annum of the deposit balance is retained by the provider, up to a maximum of 5 years (i.e., up to 10% of the deposit may not be returned). This change, from 1 November 2025, was a significant policy shift creating an incentive for providers to attract residents who can pay large RADs.

**Residential care government spend**: $22.6 billion in 2023–24 (Commonwealth government), with residents contributing a further $6.1 billion — approximately 25% of total residential revenue.

### 3. Support at Home: The Most Significant Home Care Reform in Decades

The **Support at Home (SaH) program** launched on **1 November 2025**, replacing the four-tier Home Care Packages system that had been in place since 2017. The key structural changes:

| Feature | Old HCP system | Support at Home |
|---------|----------------|-----------------|
| Funding levels | 4 (L1–L4) | 8 classifications |
| Annual government funding range | $10,931–$63,440 | $11,795–$78,106 |
| Service categories | Undifferentiated | Clinical care; Independence; Everyday living |
| Budget period | Annual | Quarterly |
| Clinical care co-payment | Yes (means-tested) | **Free for all** |
| Personal care (showering, dressing) | Means-tested contribution | Free from October 2026 |
| Lifetime contribution cap | No | $135,318.69 (from Nov 2025) |
| Care management fee | Variable | 10% of quarterly budget |

The elimination of co-payments for **clinical care** — nursing, physiotherapy, occupational therapy, podiatry, speech pathology — is the most significant structural change. The government invested **$1 billion** in the 2026–27 Budget to fund the reclassification of showering, dressing and continence support as clinical care, extending the fee-free model to the personal care domain from October 2026.

A further **$389.8 million** in the 2026–27 Budget was allocated to accelerate Support at Home package release — responding to the 88,000 Australians waiting for their approved level of home care as at March 2025, with a median wait of nearly nine months.

**Critical concern** identified by the Inspector-General of Aged Care (September 2025 Progress Report): the co-payment design in Support at Home risks causing those with moderate needs to delay or decline essential in-home supports — potentially accelerating entry into residential care, the opposite of the policy intent.

### 4. The Age-65 Cliff: NDIS and Aged Care Cannot Coexist

The most structurally fraught aspect of Australia's care system for people with disability is the **age-65 boundary** between the NDIS and aged care.

**The rule**: An individual cannot enter the NDIS if they are aged 65 or over. If they are already on the NDIS, they can remain after turning 65 — but only while living in the community. **If an NDIS participant aged 65 or over permanently enters residential aged care, they must exit the NDIS.** Similarly, if they begin receiving permanent home care through the aged care system, they must leave the NDIS.

This creates a **funding cliff** for people with lifelong disability — acquired brain injury, cerebral palsy, intellectual disability, physical disability — who have spent decades building NDIS-funded support networks and then face an enforced transition to a different system with:
- Different assessment frameworks (functional capacity vs. means-tested need)
- Different funding quantum (NDIS average ~$70,600/year; Support at Home maximum $78,106; NDIS psychosocial average $86,600)
- Different support types (the NDIS funds reasonable and necessary disability supports; aged care funds personal and domestic assistance, clinical care)
- Different philosophical foundations (disability as rights-based entitlement vs. welfare-based rationed care)

**Population data**: As at March 2025, **37,000+ people over the age of 65** remained on the NDIS — representing 5% of the scheme, up from 2.4% in 2019 — at a total scheme cost of $3.7 billion. This proportion is projected to grow to **7.6% of NDIS participants (78,151 people) by 2034**, reflecting the ageing of the founding NDIS cohort. This is a group that governments have no clean plan to serve.

An Australian Journal of Social Issues 2025 paper (Howe et al.) identified the "interface age groups" of 60–64 and 65–69 as the most policy-exposed cohort: large enough in numbers to represent significant system risk, but without coherent policy frameworks to govern their transitions. Projections found the NDIS-eligible population aged 60–69 would grow nearly six-fold between 2016 and 2026 — from roughly 18,200 to over 104,000.

**The Aged Care Act 2024** (from 1 November 2025) made a specific structural change relevant here: it now **prevents people under 65 from entering residential aged care** except under very limited circumstances where it is their preference. This is the culmination of the Younger People in Residential Aged Care (YPIRAC) strategy, which has reduced the number of under-65s in nursing homes from **6,000 in 2017–18 to 880 in June 2025**. The success of YPIRAC is real — but it also illustrates how long intentional system pressure takes to produce outcomes when dealing with entrenched institutional patterns.

### 5. The Psychosocial Disability Problem at the Boundary

For people with **psychosocial disability** (severe mental illness), the aged care interface creates a particularly acute problem. As documented in Report 017, NDIS psychosocial participants currently average $86,600 per year in plan budgets. When they turn 65 and transition to aged care — whether residential or home-based — the supports available change fundamentally.

The aged care system was not designed to, and is not funded to, provide the psychosocial recovery coaching, community participation funding, and supported independent living that psychosocial NDIS participants rely on. The Support at Home program's categories (clinical care, independence, everyday living) do not map onto NDIS-funded psychosocial supports.

The NDIS Amendment (Securing the NDIS) Bill 2026 (from May 2026) cuts participation and social community budgets from October 2026 — the same period when the aged care interface is being redefined by the Support at Home reforms. The cumulative effect for people approaching or crossing the age-65 threshold with psychosocial disability is potentially severe.

Separately, the NDIS psychosocial disability eligibility rate dropped to just **23% in Q3 2024–25** — meaning fewer people with severe mental illness are being approved for NDIS in the first place. Those who are not approved are even more dependent on the aged care and community support systems that are themselves being reshaped.

### 6. Budget 2026–27: $3.7 Billion in New Measures — and Persistent Gaps

The government announced **$3.7 billion** in aged care measures in Budget 2026–27:

| Measure | Allocation |
|---------|-----------|
| Residential bed construction incentives (up to 5,000 beds/year) | $1.7 billion |
| Accommodation Supplement restructuring (low-means homes premium) | $1.1 billion (provisioning) |
| Free personal care (reclassification as clinical) | $1.0 billion |
| Support at Home package acceleration and affordability | $389.8 million |
| Quality, safety, and sector viability | $565.1 million |
| Dementia care (Hospital to Aged Care Dementia Support, 11→20 sites) | $224.3 million |

The residential bed construction incentive addresses a documented shortage: the first wave of baby boomers turned 80 in 2026, and the number of Australians aged 80 or over is projected to increase by approximately **60% to more than 2 million by 2035**.

**What the Budget did not fund**: direct response to the NDIS-aged care interface population, psychosocial support continuity for those turning 65, or the recommendations of the Inspector-General's 2025 report on Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander community-controlled aged care services.

### 7. Inspector-General Assessment: Transformation Not Achieved

The Inspector-General of Aged Care's **2025 Progress Report** (released September 2025) provided the most authoritative public assessment of where Australia stands on its Royal Commission reform agenda:

- **Main achievement**: passage of the Aged Care Act 2024, establishing a rights-based, person-centred legal framework — the structural foundation the Royal Commission called for
- **Overall finding**: "Australians cannot be confident that our aged care system will deliver the high-quality care and human rights envisaged by the new Act"
- **Critique**: reforms have been "disparate and sometimes disconnected" rather than driven by a coherent theory of systemic change
- **Specific concern**: the co-payment design in Support at Home risks perverse incentives that push people into residential care prematurely
- **Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander concern**: reforms risk a decline in community-controlled services, contrary to the Royal Commission's intent; Aboriginal elders with cultural connection to community-based care are particularly exposed

### 8. The Workforce Crisis: No Reform Survives Without People

The aged care workforce crisis threatens to undermine every structural reform:

- **Immediate**: an annual shortfall of approximately 35,000 aged care workers nationally as at 2025–26; 60%+ of providers report difficulty filling key roles
- **Short term**: direct-care worker shortage projected to reach **110,000 by 2036**
- **Long term**: **400,000 workers needed by 2050** unless dramatic structural change occurs
- **Nursing**: shortage of approximately **17,551 FTE nurses** in aged care by 2035
- **Wage costs**: the Fair Work Commission's 2023 and 2024 pay decisions added 15% and 28% respectively to care worker wages — the right policy direction, but adding cost pressure to an already stressed system
- **Home care wait**: 88,000 Australians were waiting for their approved home care level as at March 2025, with a median wait of **nearly nine months**

The care minutes mandate (215 min/resident/day, 44 min RN) creates compliance cost for providers in thin markets — particularly rural and remote facilities that are already operating at viability margins. The AN-ACC Transition Fund (to September 2026) provides temporary relief but not structural resolution.

## Tensions and Open Questions

**1. The means-testing paradox.** The aged care system is structured around means testing (ability to pay determines contribution), while the NDIS is structured around entitlement (demonstrated disability-related need determines support). The transition between these philosophies at age 65 is not a technicality — it represents a fundamentally different theory of how the state relates to people with care needs.

**2. The "no worse off" promise and its limits.** The government has committed that those who were in the aged care system before September 2024 will not be financially disadvantaged. But those transitioning from the NDIS have had no equivalent protection: they face moving from a scheme that funded $86,600/year in disability supports to a home care system with a maximum of $78,106/year — and in practice, they must start a new means assessment process that may result in lower-level classification.

**3. Is Support at Home large enough?** The highest Support at Home classification provides $78,106 per year. Complex care needs — the kind that keep people out of residential care — can require far more. The NDIS has consistently funded complex home-based disability support at well above this level. The gap creates pressure toward residential care.

**4. CHSP to Support at Home transition (2027).** The Commonwealth Home Support Programme — which serves the largest number of older Australians (estimated ~900,000 clients) at the lowest level of need — will not transition to Support at Home until at least 1 July 2027. During this period, two parallel systems operate with different assessment frameworks, different contribution models, and different provider obligations. Sector complexity remains very high.

**5. The data gap.** As with the NDIS psychosocial programs documented in Report 017, and the MMHC block-grant model documented in Report 020, aged care home care lacks a robust national outcomes dataset. Block-grant and subsidised programs generate less granular data than fee-for-service MBS billing, making evaluation difficult.
